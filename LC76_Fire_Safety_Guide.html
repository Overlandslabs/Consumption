What this guide is. Fire conduct, equipment and prevention for the vehicle and camp — extinguisher choice and placement, vehicle-fire response, fuel and gas handling, and bush- and camp-fire discipline. It owns prevention and equipment doctrine. It does not restate what other guides own: the in-the-moment vehicle-fire drill and core emergency procedures live in R10; extinguisher access and zero-dig stowage in the packing plan in R11; legal carry-requirements in R15; country fire bans and the fire season in the profiles. Security — checkpoints, corruption, theft and scams — is now R35.

1 · Extinguishers

The right type, enough of it, where your hand can reach it in seconds. Cross-ref R11 (access), R15 (legal requirement).

DecisionStandard
TypeDry powder (ABC / ABE) as the default — cheap, available everywhere, multipurpose across ordinary combustibles, flammable liquids and electrical fires, and non-conductive. An optional clean-agent unit (e.g. Halotron-type) leaves no corrosive residue on the engine or electronics, at higher cost.
How manyAt least two. One within the driver's reach, one in the rear/living area. Small 1 kg units empty in seconds and knock down very little — favour adequate size and a second bottle over one token extinguisher.
PlacementDriver-accessible in seconds — not buried under a drawer or behind a seat (R11 zero-dig access). The second within reach of the camp kitchen / rear.
MountingA secure metal bracket that survives corrugations; a metal pin preferred over plastic. A unit that rattles loose or leaks down is useless when you need it.
ServiceCheck the pressure gauge and security periodically; some countries legally require a serviceable extinguisher (R15).

2 · Vehicle Fire Response

Seconds matter, and the wrong instinct — flinging the bonnet open — makes it worse. Cross-ref R10, R26.

Why vehicles catch fire off-road: oil or transmission fluid sprayed onto a hot exhaust, an electrical short (engine bay, lighting, or the inverter/dual-battery wiring), fuel vapour from a cracked line near hot components, or dry debris trapped against the exhaust or turbo. Vibration loosens fittings and mud hides leaks, so these build unseen.

1
Pull over — onto bare ground, off dry grass — and switch the engine off. Get everyone out and back, roughly 50 m, upwind.
2
Do not fully open the bonnet. Releasing the catch and discharging the extinguisher through the gap starves the fire; throwing the bonnet wide feeds it oxygen and flares it into your face.
3
For an electrical fire, isolate the batteries — turn the M-series battery switch OFF (kills the house bank, MultiPlus included), then the hidden kill-switch / disconnect for the crank circuit. The MultiPlus inverter/charger and house-bank wiring are a real fire load (R26).
4
Aim low, at the base of the flames, in short bursts. A single small bottle knocks down only about a litre of oil fire — be quick and deliberate.
5
If it is beyond a bottle or two, get clear and stay clear. The vehicle is not worth your life; beware fuel and tyres. Account for everyone.

3 · Fuel & Gas Handling

The everyday fire risk is at the pump and the stove, not on the trail.

  • 1Refuelling disciplineEngine off, no smoking or open flame nearby, touch bare metal first to shed static. Decant the Desert Fox cells and jerry cans with a funnel, no spills, never near a hot exhaust.
  • 2Stove fuelStored sealed, ventilated, away from heat and sparks. Never refuel a stove that is hot or alight.
  • 3Gas, if carriedLeak-test connections with soapy water; store the bottle upright and ventilated; turn it off at the bottle when driving; never run a gas appliance inside a closed tent — fire and carbon-monoxide risk both.

4 · Bush & Camp Fire Discipline

A fire you start that spreads carries real legal and financial liability. Cross-ref R29.

Don't park a hot vehicle over dry grass. A hot exhaust, turbo or underbody sitting on dry veld grass is a genuine and common ignition source. In dry country, stop and park on bare ground, gravel or a road surface — not in long grass.
  • 1Campfires only where permittedContained in a ring or pan, well clear of tents and grass, never left unattended.
  • 2Fully extinguish before sleeping or leavingDrown it, stir it, drown it again until cold to the touch. Mind sparks carried on the wind.
  • 3Heed fire bans and the seasonHigh fire-danger days and seasonal bans are real — when in doubt, skip the fire. Check the country profiles for local rules.
  • 4Raised fire pan on no-dig terrainOn pans, salt flats, rock and sensitive ground, no fire touches the surface: the raised flat-pack fire pan is mandatory, with the fibreglass ground sheet laid beneath it — a raised pan still drops embers and radiant heat scars a pan crust. The ground sheet is a dedicated item; it is NOT the vehicle fire blanket, which stays mounted and accessible at all times (see §2).
  • 5Burn down, then kill it deadLast fire of the evening burns down to fine ash — no late wood. Douse, stir, douse again; confirm cold by hand. In wind, an ember bed under an ash blanket stays live overnight. Never decant warm ash; never leave a pan of ash standing where wind can lift it.
  • 6Ash disposal by terrainSensitive / no-dig terrain: pack out — cold, wetted ash into the sealed ash tin, disposed at the next town bin or a farm pit with the landowner's permission. Ordinary ground with landowner blessing: broadcast cold wood ash thinly over a wide area away from camp and water — scattered, never heaped. Established sites: use the fireplace or ash point. Note charcoal-briquette ash carries binder residues — pack it out rather than scatter.
