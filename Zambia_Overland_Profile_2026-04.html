01 Entry & Visas SA Passport · Visa on Arrival · KAZA UniVisa

South African passport holders are not visa-free for Zambia — a visa is required but is straightforwardly obtained on arrival at all major land borders and airports. The choice is between a standard single-entry visa (USD 50) or the KAZA UniVisa (also USD 50), which additionally covers Zimbabwe and is the better option for this route. All fees in crisp, post-2013 USD cash only — worn or older notes are refused.

KAZA UniVisa — what it covers The KAZA UniVisa allows unlimited crossings between Zambia and Zimbabwe for 30 days, and a single day trip to Botswana via Kazungula. For the planned route (Namibia Caprivi → Zambia → potentially Zimbabwe for Victoria Falls), the KAZA UniVisa is the obvious choice at identical cost to a single-entry Zambia visa. Ask specifically for the KAZA UniVisa at the border — officers don't always volunteer it.

02 Border Crossings From Namibia · Into Zimbabwe / Botswana / Malawi

The planned entry is from the Namibia Caprivi Strip at Katima Mulilo. Exit options depend on the onward itinerary — Victoria Falls (Zimbabwe), Botswana (Kazungula), or continue northeast toward Malawi. All border crossings close at 18:00 except Livingstone/Victoria Falls (which is 06:00–22:00).

— Entry from NamibiaPrimary Entry

Wenela / Sesheke (Namibia Caprivi → Zambia)

06:00–18:00 · GPS 17°28'S 24°17'E · Zambezi River crossing · KAZA UniVisa available

The standard overland crossing for travellers coming east through the Namibia Caprivi Strip. Crosses the Zambezi River at Katima Mulilo / Sesheke. The KAZA UniVisa is available at this border. From Sesheke, the sealed road runs east to Livingstone (approx 260 km, 3–4 hours).

  • Namibia exit: stamp out at Katima Mulilo, cancel Namibia documents
  • Zambia entry: stamp in, purchase KAZA UniVisa (USD 50 cash), obtain TIP
  • Carbon tax (road tax): payable at border in USD — ~USD 10
  • Third-party insurance: purchase at border — compulsory; ~USD 30/month
  • Time at border: typically 60–90 min
  • First fuel in Zambia: Sesheke town has a filling station — confirm before departing Katima Mulilo with enough fuel to cover the 20 km to Sesheke in case it's out of stock

03 Vehicle Documents TIP · Insurance · Road Tax · No Carnet Required

South African-registered vehicles do not require a Carnet de Passages for Zambia. A TIP is issued free at the border. Third-party insurance is compulsory and purchased at the border. The vehicle registration must be in the driver's name or accompanied by a letter of authority if registered to another person (Mary-Ann's registration — carry a letter of authority from her authorising Neil to drive the vehicle across borders).

04 Best Season & Timing Dry Season Only · Avoid European Peak

Post-European season. Extremely hot but game viewing is at its absolute peak as waterholes shrink to puddles. Dramatic concentrations at South Luangwa and Kafue.
Camp Closures
Jan – Mar (most)

Following on from Namibia (optimal April–May window), the May–June timing fits Zambia perfectly. Arriving in Livingstone in May means camps are open, prices are low compared to peak, tracks are drying but accessible, and game viewing is building toward the excellent mid-season window. The South Luangwa in May has green vegetation with wildlife concentrating around permanent river pools — a different experience from the parched September landscape, but excellent and crowd-free.

06 Roads Left-Hand Traffic · Potholes · 4×4 for Parks

Zambia drives on the left — same as South Africa, which is an immediate comfort after Angola and the Namibia highway. The main sealed arteries (T1 Lusaka–Livingstone, Great East Road Lusaka–Chipata, Great North Road Lusaka–Ndola) are generally serviceable but riddled with potholes. Zambia roads have dramatically improved since 2010 but maintenance lags construction. Night driving is strongly discouraged — pedestrians, cyclists, livestock, and vehicles without lights are constant hazards.

07 Security Low Risk Overall · Urban Caution · Safe for Overlanders

Zambia is one of the safest overlanding destinations in sub-Saharan Africa. It has been politically stable since independence, has no history of civil conflict or targeted crime against overlanders in remote areas, and the rural population is generally welcoming and non-threatening. The risk profile is primarily urban opportunist crime in Lusaka and Livingstone, and the universal African road safety risks of night driving and livestock.

Security Positives

  • Politically stable — peaceful since independence in 1964
  • Rural areas: zero reported armed crime against overlanders
  • Police professional, not systematically corrupt like some neighbours
  • Zambian people are exceptionally friendly and helpful
  • No landmine risk (unlike Angola)
  • No significant poaching-related security risk for tourists
  • Park areas are well-managed and safe within NP boundaries

Genuine Risks

  • Lusaka: bag snatching, vehicle break-ins, opportunist theft. Don't leave valuables visible in vehicle.
  • Livingstone: higher petty crime near tourist areas and Victoria Falls. Use guarded parking.
  • Rural breakdown risk: a broken-down vehicle on a quiet road can attract opportunists. Have insurance and satellite comms.
  • Road safety: potholes, pedestrians, livestock — the most real risk in Zambia for a driver
  • Tsetse fly in Kafue and Luangwa Valley — not a security risk but a real comfort issue requiring preparation

08 Health Malaria High Risk · Tsetse Fly · Bilharzia

Zambia has high malaria transmission throughout the country and year-round. Prophylaxis is non-negotiable. The Kafue and Luangwa Valley add tsetse flies to the hazard list. Bilharzia (schistosomiasis) is present in most standing and slow-moving water — do not swim in rivers, lakes, or pools that are not verified bilharzia-free. Victoria Falls is safe to view; do not wade or swim in the Zambezi above or below the falls.

09 Camp Safety & Wildlife Ground Tent · Unfenced · Hippos + Leopards

All of Zambia's significant national parks have unfenced camps. This is not a limitation — it's the defining character of the Zambian safari experience. Animals walk through camp at night. The three parks on this route present different wildlife-in-camp profiles: South Luangwa (leopards, elephants, hippos nightly), Kafue (lions, buffalo, elephants), Lower Zambezi (elephants, hippos, lions, potentially Cape buffalo). A ground tent in these environments is completely fine — it just requires different behaviour from a fenced SA national park.

Hippos, Crocs & Water HazardsRiver Camps

Hippos: All three parks have hippos in the adjacent river systems. Hippos leave water at dusk to graze on land and return before dawn. Their paths run through riverside camp areas — you may hear or see them at night. Never stand between a hippo and water; they panic and charge when they feel cut off. If you encounter a hippo on foot at night, stand completely still, identify which direction is away from water, and move that way slowly. Do not run unless directly charged. Crocodiles: The Luangwa, Kafue, and Zambezi rivers all have large croc populations. Do not wash in, wade, or collect water from any river — use the camp's water supply. The Luangwa River specifically has very large crocs that come onto the bank at night — the strip immediately adjacent to the river is croc territory after dark.

Park Rules — Zambia-SpecificWhat Differs from SA

Walking safaris: Zambia invented the walking safari — South Luangwa is the birthplace. Walking safaris are guided and armed-scout escorted. You cannot walk independently inside the park boundary. Night drives: South Luangwa allows night game drives — this is one of Zambia's most exceptional experiences and is not available in most SA parks. Book through your lodge/camp. Gate hours: Park gates typically 06:00–18:00. You must be back in camp before gate closing; driving inside the park after sunset is prohibited except on authorised night drives. Firewood: Collection is banned in all parks — buy from camp reception. Drone rules: See Section 10. Generally prohibited inside national park boundaries.

Snakes, Scorpions & Smaller HazardsBush Hazards

Zambia has a full range of venomous snakes including puff adders (most dangerous due to their sedentary behaviour), black mamba, Mozambique spitting cobra (common in the Luangwa and Zambezi valleys), boomslang, and various gaboon vipers. Shake boots every morning. Don't walk barefoot at dusk. Scorpions present but less prevalent than in Namibia's rocky terrain. The bigger issue in Zambia is tsetse flies in Kafue and Luangwa — they bite through thin fabric and are relentless during the day. Wear long sleeves of heavier weave for game drives; DEET on exposed skin. Avoid dark blue/black clothing which attracts them.

10 Communications SIM · Coverage Gaps · Drones

Zambia's mobile network (Airtel and MTN dominate; Zamtel is state-run and less useful) gives good coverage along main sealed corridors and in towns. Coverage drops immediately off main roads and is essentially absent inside all three major national parks. The Luangwa Valley and Kafue interior are dead zones. South Luangwa lodges and camps typically have wifi but this runs via satellite connections — variable quality.

Drones & GPSLegal Status

Drones: Zambia requires ZCAA (Zambia Civil Aviation Authority) drone operator and equipment registration. Flying drones inside national parks is prohibited without specific DNPW permission — which is rarely granted to tourists. Do not fly drones over wildlife. 11 Money & Currency ZMW · USD for Parks · Crisp Notes

Zambia uses the Kwacha (ZMW). ATMs are available in Lusaka, Livingstone, and other towns. USD cash is essential for park fees and border costs — all DNPW park gates charge in USD, cards are not accepted, and crisp post-2013 notes are required. The Kwacha is freely convertible and can be taken in and out of the country (unlike Angola's Kwanza).

SADC Resident TierSA Passport Discount

Zambia's DNPW applies a three-tier pricing structure for national park fees: self-drive international rate, standard international rate, and SADC/Zambia resident rate. As SA passport holders, you qualify for the SADC resident rate — which is materially cheaper than the standard international rate. Always present your SA passport at the gate and ask for the SADC rate. The self-drive rate applies regardless of tier if you're in your own vehicle (a premium for vehicles, which is separate from the per-person fee).

  • ATM strategy: Draw ZMW in Livingstone for daily Zambia expenses. Keep USD cash specifically for park gates and borders. Never mix the two purposes — you don't want to be short of USD at a park gate.
12 National Parks & Camping DNPW · USD Cash at Gates · Self-Drive Guide

Zambia's parks system is managed by the Department of National Parks and Wildlife (DNPW). For self-drive overlanders, the key booking distinction is that park entry fees are paid at the gate in USD cash — there is no advance booking or online payment for DNPW park fees. Camps and lodges inside or adjacent to parks are booked directly with the operator; the park entry fee is separate and always paid at the gate.

Booking System OverviewHow it Works
DNPW Park Entry
Pay at the gate in USD cash on arrival. No advance booking, no online system. Gate hours 06:00–18:00. A "day" runs 06:00–18:00 — each calendar day in the park you pay the fee regardless of when you entered. Carry enough USD for your planned nights plus one extra day as buffer.
Lodge / Camp Bookings
Book directly with each camp operator by email. Most accept EFT/card payment for accommodation. Park fees are always separate — paid at the gate, not included unless the camp explicitly says otherwise (all-inclusive lodges may include park fees, but verify). Budget camps do NOT include park fees.
USD Note Requirement
This is a hard requirement, not a guideline. Post-2013 series only. Clean, unfolded, unmarked notes. USD 50 and USD 100 bills are preferred. USD 1 and USD 5 bills are sometimes refused as "too small." Exchange in Lusaka or Livingstone before heading to any remote park gate.

13 Southern Zambia — Highlights Livingstone · Victoria Falls · Kafue

Southern Zambia is anchored by Livingstone and the Victoria Falls — the natural entry point from the Namibia Caprivi, Zimbabwe, or Botswana, and one of the most spectacular single natural sights in Africa. From Livingstone, the route branches west into Kafue National Park or northeast toward Lusaka and then east to South Luangwa.

Human History & ContextPre-colonial · Colonial · Modern
Pre-colonial peoples
Bantu migrations · Lozi kingdom · Bemba chieftaincy
Zambia's territory has been settled for tens of thousands of years — San hunter-gatherers in the southwest, followed by successive waves of Bantu-speaking agriculturalists from ~AD 300. By the 16th–19th centuries, powerful kingdoms had formed: the Lozi (Barotse) kingdom controlled the Zambezi floodplains of western Zambia, with an elaborate flood-season capital-moving ceremony (Kuomboka) still performed today. The Bemba chieftaincy dominated the northeast. The Tonga and Ila peoples settled the Kafue Flats and middle Zambezi corridor.
Colonial period
Northern Rhodesia · BSAC · British Crown Colony 1924
David Livingstone's explorations (1851–1873) — including his naming of Victoria Falls in 1855 — opened Zambia's interior to European awareness. The British South Africa Company (Cecil Rhodes) gained concessions in the 1890s; the territory became Northern Rhodesia in 1911. Copper mining transformed the economy from the 1930s — the Copperbelt became one of Africa's most productive mining regions. Northern Rhodesia was briefly part of the Central African Federation (with Southern Rhodesia and Nyasaland, 1953–1963), which collapsed under nationalist pressure.
Independence & modern era
Independence 1964 · Kenneth Kaunda · Copper dependency
Zambia gained independence on 24 October 1964 under Kenneth Kaunda and UNIP (United National Independence Party). Kaunda governed until 1991 under one-party rule; multi-party democracy was restored peacefully in 1991, when Frederick Chiluba won free elections — one of Africa's rare peaceful transitions. Zambia's economy has remained heavily copper-dependent; copper price collapses in the 1970s created lasting debt problems. Today Zambia is politically stable and democratic, with tourism a growing sector particularly around the parks.
Livingstone & the Zambezi
Explorer · Missionary · Anti-slavery
David Livingstone's legacy is complex and keenly felt in Zambia. He was genuinely motivated by opposition to the Arab slave trade, and his reports of its brutality galvanised British abolitionist sentiment. He named Victoria Falls (Mosi-oa-Tunya — "the smoke that thunders" — is the Tonga name, predating Livingstone by centuries) and died near Lake Bangweulu in 1873. His heart was buried in Zambia; his body returned to Westminster Abbey. Livingstone town is named for him.

>— Livingstone & Victoria Falls
★★★★★ Unmissable

Victoria Falls — Mosi-oa-Tunya

  • Scale: 1,708 m wide, up to 108 m high — the largest sheet of falling water in the world by volume (debated with Iguazu). The spray column is visible from 50 km away.
  • Zambia vs Zimbabwe side: Zambia (eastern side) gives excellent views of the main falls and the gorge. Zimbabwe (western side) gives the broader panoramic view. With the KAZA UniVisa, crossing for the Zimbabwean viewpoint is seamless.
  • Best season: April–June when river is at peak flow — the spray is extraordinary and the rainbow in the afternoon is iconic. July–September: lower water, clearer views. October–November: very low water (you can walk on parts of the basalt) but less impressive.
  • Activities: White-water rafting (Grades IV–V, spectacular), helicopter flights over the falls, sunset cruises on the Zambezi, bungee jumping from Victoria Falls Bridge, guided village walks
  • Entry: USD 20/person (Zambian side). SADC NP fee USD 5. Both payable on entry.
★★★★ Town

Livingstone

  • Zambia's tourist capital — well-stocked supermarkets, ATMs, fuel, vehicle services, good camp and lodge range
  • Maramba River Camp: excellent overlander camp with secure parking, good facilities, camping and chalets
  • Waterberry Lodge: upmarket but good value riverside option
  • Choppies supermarket for full provisions before heading north to parks
  • Last reliable ATM before Kafue (draw ZMW and confirm USD supply here)
  • Toyota dealer for any pre-park vehicle checks if needed
— Kafue National Park
★★★★★ Best of Kafue

Busanga Plains (Northern Kafue)

  • Seasonal floodplain in the far north of Kafue — one of Zambia's most spectacular landscapes
  • Famous for the Busanga lions — a population of lions that learned to climb trees (like Uganda's tree-climbing lions)
  • Lechwe, tsessebe, roan, sable in huge herds on the open plains
  • Cheetah and wild dog — some of Africa's best wild dog sightings in this area
  • Best season: June–October when the floodplain is dry enough to traverse
  • Access: rough 4×4 tracks from Kafue main road — guide recommended for first visit
★★★★ Southern Kafue

Southern Kafue — Itezhi-Tezhi & Kafue River

  • More accessible than northern Kafue — well-graded tracks from Kalomo or Lusaka
  • Lake Itezhi-Tezhi: massive reservoir with hippo, croc, diverse waterbirds; Musungwa Lodge overlooking the lake
  • Kafue River: excellent game viewing along the riparian strip
  • Easier self-drive than northern sector; high clearance 4×4 sufficient on main tracks
  • Good for a 2–3 day add-on between Livingstone and Lusaka
★★★★ Hidden Gem

Lochinvar National Park

  • Small, undervisited park on Kafue Flats north of Monze — endemic Kafue Lechwe in enormous herds
  • Outstanding birding: wattled crane, shoebill stork occasionally sighted, thousands of migrant waders
  • Fee: USD 7/person SADC, USD 10 international — excellent value
  • Access: turn off T1 at Monze, rough gravel track (~60 km). Basic facilities only — carry all supplies.
14 Eastern Zambia — Highlights South Luangwa · Lower Zambezi · Bangweulu

Eastern Zambia is where the country earns its reputation as Africa's walking safari heartland. The Luangwa Valley — cut by the Luangwa River running south to join the Zambezi — is one of Africa's last truly wild river systems. The South Luangwa is the epicentre. Lower Zambezi, while technically in the south-east, is most practically accessed from Lusaka and is grouped here.

Flora & Vegetation — ZambiaBiome · Dominant species · Seasonal change
Dominant biome
Miombo woodland · Mopane scrub · Riparian forest
Miombo woodland (Brachystegia and Julbernardia spp.) covers most of Zambia's plateau — the largest biome in southern Africa by area. Characteristic reddish new leaves in August–September create the "Zambian spring" before the rains. Mopane (Colophospermum mopane) dominates the hot, low-lying valleys — Luangwa and Zambezi — forming dense stands on heavy clay soils. Riparian forest (winterberry, leadwood, sycamore fig) lines permanent rivers.
Luangwa Valley signature trees
Leadwood · Ebony · Sausage tree · Marula
Combretum imberbe (leadwood) is the valley's architectural tree — enormously dense, slow-growing, used extensively by elephant as a scratching post. Diospyros mespiliformis (African ebony) provides critical shade in the dry season. Kigelia africana (sausage tree) — elephant eat the enormous fruit. Sclerocarya birrea (marula) — fruit ferments naturally and attracts elephant in October–November.
Kafue Flats & wetland vegetation
Floodplain grass · Wild date palm · Papyrus
The Kafue Flats seasonally flood over 6,000 km² — one of Africa's largest floodplain systems. Dominant grasses (Vossia cuspidata hippo grass, Echinochloa pyramidalis antelope grass) sustain massive lechwe herds. Wild date palm (Phoenix reclinata) and papyrus (Cyperus papyrus) line permanent channels. Ficus spp. mark permanent water sources in the drier surrounding woodland.
Seasonal vegetation change
Dry (May–Oct): open, tan, clear sightlines · Wet (Nov–Apr): dense, green, closed canopy
Miombo produces new leaves in August–September (before the rains) — a distinctive red-bronze flush. Mopane loses leaves in the dry season making it easier to spot elephant. Wet season: dense grass growth (2 m+ in floodplains) reduces sightlines dramatically and closes many tracks. Dry season grass is grazed short and burnt — maximum visibility for game viewing.
— South Luangwa Valley
★★★★★ Essential

South Luangwa National Park

  • Leopards: Arguably the best leopard sightings in Africa — habituated individuals that ignore game drive vehicles. Night drives are where most leopard encounters happen.
  • Walking safaris: Norman Carr invented the walking safari here in 1950. Guided walks with armed DNPW scout. You see the bush at ground level — tracks, insects, plants, context. One of Africa's signature safari experiences.
  • Night drives: Unique in that this park allows night drives until late — you'll see African wildcat, civets, porcupine, and often leopards on kills. Book through Flatdogs or any camp.
  • Wild dogs: Strong wild dog population in Luangwa Valley — sightings are common in the dry season. One of Zambia's best destinations for them.
  • Hippo pods: The Luangwa River has one of the highest hippo densities in Africa — drives along the ox-bow lagoons in the dry season are exceptional.
★★★★ Wildlife

Lower Zambezi National Park

  • Zambezi floodplain park across from Zimbabwe's Mana Pools — together they form one of Africa's great transboundary wilderness areas
  • Canoe safaris the signature experience — paddling past hippo pods and elephant herds from water level
  • Elephant concentration is extraordinary — hundreds of elephants on the river banks in the dry season
  • Tiger fishing (vundu, tigerfish) from camp or boat — the Zambezi is world-class fishing
  • The escarpment descent into the valley is a memorable drive in its own right
  • Access from Lusaka via Chirundu: ~130 km sealed, then challenging escarpment descent. Allow a full day from Lusaka.
★★★★ Specialist

Kasanka National Park

  • Small privately managed park in central Zambia (African Parks) — one of Africa's best kept secrets
  • The bat migration: October–December, 8–10 million straw-coloured fruit bats roost in Kasanka's mushitu forest — one of Africa's greatest wildlife spectacles, completely unknown to most visitors
  • Also: sitatunga (water antelope), puku, blue monkey, and excellent birding year-round
  • Wasa Lodge and Kasanka Camp for accommodation; camping available
  • Access from Lusaka: ~490 km north via T2. Good sealed road most of the way.
— Bangweulu WetlandsBlack Lechwe Capital

The Bangweulu Wetlands (African Parks managed) in northeastern Zambia are one of Africa's most extraordinary bird habitats and home to the shoebill stork — one of the most sought-after birds in Africa. Enormous herds of endemic black lechwe (100,000+) graze the floodplains. This is specialist, remote territory — allow 3–4 days minimum, fully self-sufficient, and plan carefully for the seasonal flooding. Best visited May–August. Getting there from Lusaka requires 2 full driving days. Not essential for a first Zambia trip but exceptional for those with the time and inclination.

Conservation & Environmental IssuesThreats · CBNRM · Ethics for travellers
Poaching pressure
Ivory · Bushmeat · Snaring
Zambia suffered severe elephant poaching in the 1970s–1990s, with the national herd declining from ~200,000 to under 20,000. Numbers have partly recovered under improved management, but snaring for bushmeat remains the most pervasive ongoing threat — particularly in the GMAs (Game Management Areas) that buffer national parks. Wire snares kill indiscriminately: eland, wild dog, and lion are all lost this way. Report snares found on tracks to DNPW or the nearest camp manager.
DNPW & park management
Department of National Parks & Wildlife
DNPW manages all national parks and GMAs. Capacity is stretched — many parks have limited ranger presence outside concession areas. African Parks now co-manages Bangweulu, Liuwa Plain, and Lower Zambezi under long-term agreements, bringing significant conservation investment. The South Luangwa Conservation Society (SLCS) runs community anti-poaching and wildlife monitoring programmes in the Luangwa Valley and is a reputable conservation partner — their work is worth supporting directly.
Community-based conservation (CBNRM)
Game Management Areas · Revenue sharing
Zambia's Game Management Areas (GMAs) surround national parks and are managed under a community-based natural resource management (CBNRM) model: wildlife is the property of the community, and tourism and hunting concession revenues are meant to flow back to community chieftaincy. In practice, revenue distribution is often contested. Choosing camps that demonstrably invest in community employment and anti-poaching (not just token CSR) is the most effective lever travellers have.
Traveller ethics
Stay on tracks · No off-road · Waste out
Stay strictly on designated tracks — off-road driving destroys vegetation root systems, creates new erosion channels, and disturbs ground-nesting birds. Carry all waste out of parks; no camp waste should be buried inside park boundaries. Do not approach wildlife on foot without a licensed guide. Never feed animals — habituated baboons and monkeys become aggressive and are destroyed. Buy local where possible: camp firewood, fresh produce, and guides from the nearest community.

Map planning principle — two separate layers When trip-planning, always treat political/administrative boundaries (national park borders, GMA boundaries, concession blocks, district lines) and ecological boundaries (floodplains, flood extents, river drainage basins, escarpment breaks) as separate layers serving different purposes. Political boundaries determine permits, fees, and legal access. Ecological boundaries determine travel safety, seasonal passability, and terrain. A route inside a legal concession may cross a flood-season floodplain that becomes impassable. The Luangwa Valley floor, Lower Zambezi escarpment descent, and Kafue Flats margins all require ecological assessment independent of administrative access. 16 Southern Zambia Route Guide Wenela → Livingstone → Kafue → Lusaka

The southern circuit connects the Namibia Caprivi entry point at Wenela/Sesheke with Livingstone (Victoria Falls), optionally through Kafue National Park, and on to Lusaka as the staging point for the eastern parks. Allow 8–12 days to do southern Zambia properly without rushing.

Route A — Southern Circuit with Kafue (10–12 days)

Wenela/Sesheke → Livingstone → Kafue → Lusaka
  • Day 1: Wenela/Sesheke border crossing. Fill fuel at Sesheke. Drive M10 east to Livingstone (~260 km). Arrive afternoon — camp at Maramba River Camp or similar.
  • Day 2–3: Livingstone / Victoria Falls. Day 2: Falls (Zambian side), afternoon at camp. Day 3: KAZA visa used — cross to Zimbabwean side for broader falls view, or book helicopter flight, or Zambezi sunset cruise. Reprovision fully at Choppies. ATM for ZMW and confirm USD supply.
  • Day 4: Livingstone → T1 north → Kalomo → Dundumwezi Gate into Kafue southern sector (~200 km). Or continue to Nalusanga Gate from Lusaka on a different day.
  • Day 5–7: Kafue NP — 3 nights at Mayukuyuku or Musungwa. Self-drive game drives daily. If time: overnight run to Busanga Plains (northern sector) — 2-day minimum. Tsetse fly precautions in force.
  • Day 8: Exit Kafue → T2 north → Lusaka (~250 km from Nalusanga Gate). Arrive Lusaka, Pioneer Camp for overnight.
  • Day 9: Lusaka admin day — vehicle check, ATM, Shoprite/Melmart for provisions, laundry, planning for eastern leg.

Route B — Express South (5–6 days)

For tighter schedules — Livingstone + skip or compress Kafue
  • Sesheke → Livingstone (Day 1–3: Falls, activities, rest)
  • Livingstone → T1 direct to Lusaka (Day 4 — 470 km, allow full day for potholes)
  • Lusaka (Day 5: admin, provision, prepare for eastern parks)
  • Kafue can be done as a 2-night add-on either between Livingstone and Lusaka, or skipped in favour of a longer South Luangwa stay

17 Eastern Zambia Route Guide Lusaka → South Luangwa → Lower Zambezi → Malawi

The eastern circuit departs Lusaka and follows the Great East Road toward Chipata and South Luangwa — the centrepiece of most Zambia overland trips. Lower Zambezi can be combined on the way back to Lusaka. Exiting northeast to Malawi via Chipata is a natural continuation for a longer Southern Africa circuit.

Route C — Full Eastern Circuit (12–14 days from Lusaka)

Lusaka → South Luangwa → Lower Zambezi → Lusaka (or exit to Malawi)
  • Day 1: Lusaka → Great East Road east → Luangwa Bridge Camp (~280 km). Overnight at the bridge camp — good rest stop, beautiful location on the Luangwa River. Affordable camping.
  • Day 2: Luangwa Bridge → Chipata (~320 km). Fill fuel at Chipata. Buy any remaining USD from Chipata ATM. Drive Chipata → Mfuwe main gate (~112 km). Arrive South Luangwa late afternoon. Camp Flatdogs or Wildlife Camp.
  • Day 3–6: South Luangwa — 4 nights. Morning game drive daily (06:00 departure). Afternoon rest. Night drive on at least 2 nights. Walking safari day. Hippo pod viewing from riverbank. Leopard watch at known territories.
  • Day 7: Depart Mfuwe → Chipata → back to Lusaka (700+ km — very long day, or break at Petauke). This is the main logistical challenge of the South Luangwa: it's a long way from anywhere.
  • Day 8: Lusaka (rest, vehicle, admin)
  • Day 9: Lusaka → T2 south → Chirundu → Lower Zambezi escarpment descent (~180 km total). Arrive valley camp (Mvuu, Elly's, or Chiawa camp area). Afternoon canoe safari.
  • Day 10–11: Lower Zambezi — 2 nights. Canoe safari, game drives along river, evening boat cruise. Elephant viewing from camp guaranteed.
  • Day 12: Ascend escarpment → Lusaka → rest
  • Continue to Malawi: Lusaka → Great East Road → Chipata → Mchinji border (~600 km). Exit Zambia into Malawi. SA passport: visa-free 30 days.

Route D — South Luangwa Only (7–8 days from Livingstone)

For tighter schedules — focus entirely on South Luangwa
  • Livingstone → Lusaka (Day 1)
  • Lusaka → South Luangwa (Day 2 — 2 stages with overnight at Luangwa Bridge)
  • South Luangwa (Days 3–6 — 4 nights)
  • Return to Lusaka/Chipata for onward routing (Day 7–8)
  • Skip Lower Zambezi to preserve time for South Luangwa. This is the right call if the total Zambia window is under 14 days.

18 Essential Phrases — Nyanja & Bemba English Official · Two Most Useful Languages

Zambia's official language is English — widely spoken in towns, at borders, in parks, and at all lodges and camps. Unlike Angola, you will have no communication barrier in English throughout Zambia. However, a few words of Nyanja (the language of eastern and central Zambia, including Lusaka and Luangwa) and Bemba (northern and Copperbelt Zambia) go a long way and are warmly received. — At the Border / Park Gate (English phrases)
SituationWhat to SayNotes
Requesting KAZA UniVisa at border"I'd like the KAZA UniVisa please — USD 50."Be specific — officers don't always volunteer it. Both people present passports and USD cash.
SADC rate at park gate"We're South African residents — can we have the SADC rate please?"Present SA passports. The officer will confirm. SADC rate is a meaningful discount.
Vehicle entry for foreign-plated vehicle"We have a South African registered vehicle — here is the registration document."TIP is issued at the border. At park gates, vehicle fee (USD 15) is separate from person fee.
Paying with USD at park gate"Here are our notes — I have USD 50 and USD 100 bills."Confirm they can make change before handing over large notes. Have small USD denominations for the exact fee if possible.
Night drive request at camp"We'd like to book a night drive for this evening — what time and what's the cost?"Night drives are typically USD 30–50 per person. Book by afternoon.
Walking safari booking"We'd like to book a morning walking safari for tomorrow — what time does it start?"Typically 06:00 departure, half-day, armed DNPW scout. Book day before. Safety briefing on arrival.
English is the language of Zambia Zambia has no language barrier for English speakers. Every border official, park ranger, camp manager, and most market traders speak workable English. The Nyanja and Bemba phrases above are for warmth and courtesy — Zambians are genuinely delighted when visitors make the effort. Zikomo kwambiri (thank you very much) gets a warm reaction everywhere.
