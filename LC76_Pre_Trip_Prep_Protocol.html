S1 Triage Framework Decision Logic · 300,000+ km
⚠ Expedition Standard vs Daily Driver Standard This vehicle is preparing for a two-year, 60,000+ km expedition across Southern and East Africa with no dealer support and limited workshop access in remote areas. The preparation standard must exceed what you would accept for a daily commute. Err on the side of replacement for any component where failure risk is elevated — the cost of replacement in Cape Town is a fraction of the cost of breakdown in the Luangwa Valley.
The Four-Bucket System Triage Logic

Every item on this vehicle falls into one of four buckets before departure. Work through each system using this logic before booking workshop time — it focuses spending and prevents missed items.

🔴 Replace Regardless
  • No service record and consequence of failure is high
  • Service interval exceeded by more than 20%
  • Rubber components — belts, hoses, seals on a 15+ year old vehicle
  • Interference engine timing belt with unknown history
  • Brake fluid (hygroscopic — absorbs moisture regardless of appearance)
  • Coolant (inhibitor depletion not visible — change on time)
  • Gearbox and diff oils with no confirmed change record
🔵 Test & Decide
  • Injectors — pop test gives measurable opening pressure; replace only if out of spec
  • Glow plugs — resistance test before replacement
  • Thermostat — test in hot water before assuming fault
  • Wheel bearings — check play and temperature after run
  • Viscous fan clutch — spin test and silicone leak inspection
  • Alternator — load test output before condemning
  • Clutch — measure free play; assess slip only under load
🟡 Monitor & Carry Spares
  • Injector pump — internal wear not diagnosable pre-failure; carry seal kit
  • Fuel lift pump — difficult to test definitively; carry spare
  • Radiator — inspect and pressure test; carry Stop Leak as emergency only
  • Water pump — check bearing play and weep hole; replace if any seeping
  • Power steering hoses — inspect at fittings; carry hydraulic repair kit
  • Suspension bushes — assess wear; known worn items replace pre-trip
🟢 Leave & Reassess Later
  • Recently replaced items within their service interval
  • Components with recent workshop sign-off (e.g. Terrain Tamer clutch Feb 2026)
  • Cosmetic items — body, trim, non-structural
  • OEM rubber recently replaced and inspected
  • Items with low failure consequence and easy field repair
  • Anything replaced < 20,000 km ago with no performance symptoms

S2 Engine & Fuel System 1HZ 4.2L Diesel · SAC Turbo
Timing Belt CRITICAL — Interference Engine
⚠ Catastrophic Failure Risk The 1HZ uses a rubber timing belt driving the camshaft. This is an interference engine — if the belt skips or breaks, pistons contact open valves. The result is bent valves, damaged pistons, and a full engine rebuild. There is no service record for this belt on this vehicle. Replace before expedition. No exceptions.
Timing Timing belt replacement — belt, tensioner, idler CRITICAL

Timing belt, tensioner, idler, and cover were replaced April 2025 at 273,023 km by PW Auto Centre. Current odometer ~305,000 km = approximately 32,000 km on the current belt — within interval. No replacement required before the expedition. Confirm the cam and crank front seals were inspected at that service; if no record, inspect now. Next due: approximately 363,000 km or April 2030.

Status: DONE — Apr 2025 @ 273,023 km · PW Auto Centre · Belt, tensioner, idler, cover all replaced · ~32,000 km ago · Next due: ~363,000 km / 2030

Timing Cam seal and crank front seal — inspect and replace CRITICAL

The timing belt was replaced April 2025. Confirm whether cam and crank front seals were inspected or replaced at that service — check the PW Auto Centre invoice. If no seals were replaced at that time, inspect both now for any weeping. At ~32,000 km on the current belt, an oil-contaminated belt situation would already show symptoms. If seals are dry, leave and monitor.

Check Apr 2025 invoice for seal replacement. If seals not replaced then: inspect for weeping. Dry = monitor. Any wet seeping = replace now.

Valve Clearances Priority — No Service Record
Note — Tight Clearances Burn Valves Valve clearances on the 1HZ tighten as valve seats wear. A tight exhaust valve that doesn't fully seat loses heat rapidly and burns. At 305,000 km with no recorded clearance check, this is a high-priority inspection. Tight is more common than loose and more dangerous.
Valves Valve clearance inspection and adjustment — all 12 CRITICAL

Check all 12 valve clearances (6 inlet, 6 exhaust) cold. Record each measurement before and after adjustment. If clearances are consistently tight, this suggests significant valve seat wear — flag to the workshop for further assessment. Combine with the timing belt job where possible — the rocker cover is removed for timing belt access.

Cold clearance spec: Inlet 0.20 mm / Exhaust 0.30 mm. Engine cold (below 35°C). Record all measurements pre and post adjustment.

Fuel Injectors Pop Test — Test & Decide
Fuel Injector pop test — all 6 cylinders

Remove all 6 injectors and have them pop-tested on a bench tester. This measures opening pressure, spray pattern, and atomisation. Worn nozzles dribble fuel rather than atomising it — leading to black smoke, carbon deposits, and increased fuel consumption. Return cleaned and re-shimmed to spec. Replace nozzles on any injector outside spec.

1HZ injector opening pressure: 14.2–15.4 MPa (142–154 bar / 2060–2240 psi). Dirk Du Plessis (Dup Diesel) for injection work.

Fuel Injection pump — external inspection and seal check

Assess the Denso VE-type injection pump indirectly: check for leaks at the governor cover and shaft seal. Check injection timing, smoke character on start and load, and fuel return volume. If leaking at any seal, have it rebuilt before departure. Carry a governor cover seal and shaft seal as spares regardless.

Check shaft seal and governor cover for weeping. Any concern — book Dup Diesel for assessment. Injection pump rebuild requires specialist equipment.

Fuel System — Pre-Filter & Lift Pump African Fuel Quality Protection
African Fuel Quality — Real Risk Diesel quality across Southern and East Africa varies significantly. Water contamination and particulates are common. The factory filter is the only protection on a standard system. A secondary pre-filter with water separator upstream of the lift pump adds critical protection and must be fitted before departure.
Fuel Secondary pre-filter with water separator — fit if absent CRITICAL

If a secondary pre-filter with water separator is not already fitted, install before departure. Fit upstream of the factory filter and lift pump — primary defence against contaminated fuel destroying the injection pump. Use a unit with a transparent bowl for field inspection and an easy drain valve. Carry 2 replacement filter elements.

Racor 500FG or equivalent. Fit between tank and factory filter. 10-micron minimum. Service every 10,000 km or when bowl shows sediment or water.

Fuel Fuel lift pump condition check

Inspect the mechanical lift pump for external leaks. Check diaphragm condition by briefly cracking the outlet — weak or intermittent flow suggests wear. At 305,000 km, replacement or rebuild is reasonable insurance. A failed lift pump strands the vehicle immediately — carry a complete replacement pump regardless of current condition.

Carry complete replacement lift pump as a spare. Field replacement is straightforward. Check at same time as factory filter.

Fuel Factory fuel filter replacement

Replace factory fuel filter before departure regardless of last change date. Inspect the old element for contamination, water, or unusual sediment. Drain water separator if fitted. Carry two spare factory filter elements for the expedition.

Replace every 30,000 km or annually. Drain water separator every 5,000 km in African conditions.

Glow Plugs Test & Decide
Electrical Glow plug resistance test — all 6

Disconnect the glow plug rail and test each plug with a multimeter. A working 1HZ glow plug reads approximately 0.6–1.5 Ω cold. Open circuit (OL) or shorted (0 Ω) means the plug has failed. If plugs have no replacement record, replace the full set — cheap relative to hard-start risk in cold highland conditions. Carry 2 spare plugs minimum.

Spec: ~0.6–1.5 Ω cold. Preheat time: 4–8 seconds at 12V. Carry 2 spare glow plugs minimum.

Electrical Glow plug relay and timer circuit test

Test the glow plug relay — the glow indicator light should illuminate on ignition and extinguish after the correct preheat period. A failed relay means no preheat and very difficult cold starts. Relay failure is common on high-mileage 1HZ vehicles. Carry a spare relay.

Preheat light should illuminate 4–8 sec at normal temperature. Shorter or absent preheat — check relay and timer unit.

Pressure Test Test & Decide
Cooling Cooling system and pressure cap test CRITICAL

Use a radiator pressure tester to pressurise the system to the cap rating and hold for 10 minutes. Any pressure drop indicates a leak — check all hose connections, radiator core, heater matrix circuit, water pump weep hole, and head gasket interface. A weeping head gasket at 305,000 km is a real risk — inspect carefully. Also test the pressure cap itself — a weak cap causes premature boiling.

System pressure: approximately 88 kPa (0.88 bar / 13 psi). Hold test minimum 10 minutes. Cap should open at rated pressure. Any drop — investigate fully before departure.

Thermostat Replace Regardless
Cooling Thermostat and housing gasket replacement

Replace the thermostat regardless of apparent condition. A stuck-closed thermostat causes rapid overheating. Stuck-open causes slow warm-up, increased fuel consumption, and reduced heater output. At this mileage and age, replacement is cheap insurance. Replace the housing gasket or O-ring at the same time. Combine with the coolant flush.

1HZ thermostat opening temp: 82°C. Full-open: 95°C. Toyota OEM preferred. Part: 90916-03099 or equivalent.

Hoses, Clamps & Water Pump Inspect — Replace if Suspect
Cooling All coolant hoses — squeeze test and clamp check CRITICAL

Inspect all coolant hoses cold — squeeze each one. It should feel firm but pliable, not cracked, hard, or spongy. Pull back the hose clamp to inspect the hose interior for cracking. Check clamps for corrosion and correct positioning. Bottom hose — check for internal collapse if the spring is weak. Any hose showing cracking or hardening: replace before departure. Any hose over 10 years old: replace regardless.

Replace any suspect hose. Toyota silicone hoses preferred for long-term reliability. Include bypass hose and heater hoses in inspection.

Cooling Water pump — bearing play and weep hole check

With engine cold, check for play in the water pump pulley — grip and rock it. No play should be detectable. Inspect the weep hole on the underside of the pump body — active weeping or drips indicate bearing seal failure and require replacement. If pump is original or has no replacement record at this mileage, replace proactively. Accessible during timing belt job.

No perceptible radial or axial pulley play. Weep hole: dried residue acceptable, active wet weeping means replace.

Viscous Fan Clutch Test & Decide
Cooling Viscous fan clutch — cold spin, wobble, and silicone check

Engine off and cold: attempt to spin the fan blade by hand — a serviceable cold clutch turns freely with minimal resistance. Check for silicone fluid leakage from the clutch body (wet or oily residue on fan or housing). Check for axial wobble in the clutch bearing. The critical failure mode is a clutch that freewheels hot — this removes cooling capacity exactly when you need it most. Any wobble, leakage, or hot freewheel test failure: replace.

Cold: spins freely. Hot (after highway run): resists rotation significantly. Silicone leak = replace. Axial play = replace.

Drivetrain Universal joint wear check — all propshaft UJs

S5 Electrical & Auxiliary Systems Alternator · Victron · Lights · Fuses
Note — LiFePO4 Charging Dependency The Victron Smart LiFePO4 200Ah battery charges primarily via the Orion-Tr Smart 12/12-30A DC-DC charger from the alternator. An alternator that cannot maintain 13.8V+ under full electrical load means inadequate auxiliary charging. A load test before departure is critical — not optional.
Alternator CRITICAL — Load Test Required
Electrical Alternator output load test CRITICAL

Test alternator output at idle and under full load — all accessories on: lights, ARB compressor, fan, charging loads. Voltage must remain 13.8–14.4V across all load conditions. A drop below 13.5V under load indicates a tired alternator struggling to support the LiFePO4 charging circuit. Also check for AC voltage ripple (exceeds 0.5V = failing rectifier diodes). A workshop battery/alternator tester can do this properly in under 20 minutes.

Idle, no load: 13.8–14.4V. Full load: minimum 13.5V. AC ripple: below 0.5V AC. 1HZ alternator: 80A standard. Check belt tension first — slippage produces identical symptoms to alternator wear.

Electrical Alternator belt tension and condition

Inspect the alternator / accessory belt for cracking, glazing, or fraying. Check tension — mid-span deflection 8–10 mm under moderate thumb pressure. A slipping belt mimics alternator failure and will also fail to drive the power steering pump and viscous fan bearing. Replace if any doubt. Carry a spare alternator belt.

Mid-span deflection: 8–10 mm. No cracking or glazing. Carry one spare alternator belt.

Starter Battery Load Test
Electrical Starter battery load test

Test the starter battery under load using a battery tester — not just a resting voltage check. A battery can show 12.6V at rest but fail under the cranking load of a cold 1HZ diesel. If the battery is over 4 years old, or if there is any doubt about cold cranking performance, replace it before departure. A failed start battery in Africa is not a minor inconvenience.

Load test to 50% of CCA for 15 seconds — voltage must not drop below 9.6V. Battery over 4 years old before a two-year expedition: replace.

Victron Auxiliary System Full Function Verification
Victron Victron system full function check

With vehicle running, verify the Orion-Tr Smart DC-DC charger is active and charging the LiFePO4 at the correct rate. Confirm SmartSolar MPPT is functional — connect Hardkorr blanket and confirm MPPT tracking. Check BMV-712 readings: voltage, SOC, and current flow all correct. Confirm Smart BMS 12/200 shows no fault flags. Run VictronConnect on all devices and confirm all Bluetooth connections are active and all parameters within spec.

Orion-Tr: charging at up to 30A from alternator. MPPT 100/30: bulk/absorption/float cycle confirmed. BMV-712: SOC, V, A reading. BMS 12/200: no fault LEDs. LiFePO4 200Ah: check cell balance via BMS app.

Victron All wiring, connections, and fuse holders — inspection

Inspect all visible auxiliary system wiring for chafing, heat damage, or loose connections. Check all fuse holders — corroded fuse clips cause resistance and voltage drop under load. Pull-test any crimped connections that appear loose. Check the main fuse at the battery for corrosion. Document which fuse serves which circuit. Carry a complete spare fuse kit covering all ratings in the system.

All connections tight and corrosion-free. Wiring routed away from heat and sharp edges. Spare fuse kit must cover all ratings fitted to the vehicle.

Lights & Circuits Full Check
Electrical All vehicle lights — full circuit test

Test every light circuit: headlights (low and high beam), fog lights, indicators (all four corners), hazards, brake lights (including third brake light), reversing light, interior lights, number plate lights, and any auxiliary driving lights. A non-functional brake light is both a safety risk and a cause of problems at border crossings — inspectors check these. Carry spare globes for all standard fittings. Check earth connections for corrosion — most lighting faults on older vehicles are earth-related.

All lights operational. Carry spare globe kit. Check and clean all lighting earth points at this service.

S6 Workshop Visit Protocol How to Brief · What to Ask · Sign-Off
Context Not all preparation work can or should be done at home. A diesel specialist with 1HZ experience adds significant value — particularly for timing belt, valve clearances, injector work, and injection pump concerns. This section covers how to brief a workshop effectively and what a pre-expedition sign-off should include.
Documents to Bring Workshop Briefing Pack
Bring to the Workshop
  • Service history printout from R8 (LC76_Service_History.html) — the mechanic needs to see what has and hasn't been done
  • This document (R23) — bring the full prep list or the relevant sections as a workshop brief
  • R1 Reference Card (LC76_Reference_Card.html) — fluid specs, oil grades, tyre pressures, torque specs
  • Current odometer reading and target expedition departure date
  • SAC turbo service documentation — reconned August 2023 at 224,406 km (~80,600 km ago)
  • Any recent noise, smoke, or performance concerns noted in daily use
  • Photo record of any visible leaks, damage, or wear observed during your own pre-inspection
How to Brief the Mechanic Key Instructions
Priority Brief Points
  • This is an expedition vehicle preparing for 2 years in Africa — not a routine service. The standard is higher than a daily driver. Do not take shortcuts.
  • Timing belt has no service record — treat as if never replaced. Replace belt, tensioner, idler, and inspect cam and crank seals while cover is off.
  • Valve clearances have never been recorded — inspect and set to spec. Record all 12 measurements before and after adjustment.
  • Injectors: remove all 6 and pop-test on the bench. Return cleaned and shimmed. Replace nozzles on any injector outside spec. Do not clean and return without testing.
  • Ask to be shown the drain plug magnets from gearbox, transfer case, and diffs before refilling — you want to see what came out.
  • Do not change anything without informing you first — this is a learning exercise as well as a service.
  • Document everything — km at repair, parts fitted (brand and part number), before/after specs where applicable.

Questions to Ask About Findings Workshop Intelligence
Ask the Mechanic Directly
  • "What did you find on the drain plug magnets — how much debris?" (quantify the wear level)
  • "What condition were the valve clearances in — which cylinders were tightest?" (tightest exhaust = most wear)
  • "Were there any signs of oil contamination on the timing belt or behind the timing cover?"
  • "What was the condition of the old thermostat — any sign of sticking?"
  • "What were the injector opening pressures before and after service?" (ask for the actual numbers)
  • "Did anything surprise you that wasn't on the brief?"
  • "Given what you've seen on this vehicle — is there anything you'd be concerned about for a two-year expedition?"
  • "What would you want checked at 350,000 km if the vehicle is in the field when it gets there?"

Namibia Toyota Namibia (CFAO) Windhoek (main), Swakopmund, Walvis Bay, Oshakati Good parts availability in Windhoek. Supplied from TSAM Boksburg parts hub
Botswana Toyota Botswana (CFAO) Gaborone (main), Francistown, Maun Maun is a key staging point for Okavango/Chobe expeditions — Toyota dealer present
Zimbabwe Toyota by CFAO Zimbabwe · Croco Toyota Harare (main), Bulawayo, Victoria Falls Croco Toyota is the authorised dealer. Parts availability variable — call ahead for 1HZ-specific items. Victoria Falls location useful for Zambia border prep
Zambia Toyota Zambia (CFAO Mobility) Lusaka (multiple branches), Kitwe, Livingstone, Solwezi, Kabwe Lusaka has multiple branches on Cairo Road. Livingstone branch useful near Zim border. Parts ordered through CFAO network
Malawi Toyota Malawi (CFAO) Blantyre (main), Lilongwe Limited specialist diesel knowledge — use for genuine parts sourcing rather than complex engine work
Tanzania Toyota Tanzania Ltd Dar es Salaam (main), Arusha, Mwanza, Dodoma Dar es Salaam has the main workshop. Arusha is the key access point for northern route (Serengeti, Ngorongoro). Land Cruiser extremely common — independent 1HZ knowledge widely available
Kenya Toyota Kenya Ltd (CFAO) Nairobi (main — multiple locations), Mombasa, Eldoret, Kisumu Land Cruiser assembly was in Kenya from 1977. Strong 1HZ expertise. Nairobi Industrial Area has numerous independent LC specialists alongside the main dealer
Uganda Toyota Uganda (CFAO) Kampala (main) Kampala dealer. Independent Land Cruiser workshops widely available — LC76/78 is the dominant vehicle for NGO and government fleets
Mozambique Toyota Mozambique (CFAO) Maputo (main), Beira Maputo main dealer. Parts availability in northern Mozambique (Pemba, Nampula) limited — carry critical spares before entering
Eswatini Toyota Eswatini (TSAM network) Mbabane, Manzini Served directly from TSAM South Africa parts hub
Field Reality Check Dealer presence does not guarantee 1HZ part availability on the shelf. Injector nozzles, timing belt kits, and injection pump seals may need to be ordered from Johannesburg or Nairobi. For any complex diesel work away from South Africa, call the dealer in advance, describe the specific part needed (with part number from R1 Reference Card), and allow 3–7 days for sourcing. Independent diesel specialists in Nairobi and Lusaka often have better 1HZ stocks than franchise dealers.
