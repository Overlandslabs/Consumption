What this guide is. A single home for the two safety domains the audit found scattered or thin: personal & vehicle security (checkpoint conduct, corruption, theft prevention) and fire (extinguishers, vehicle fire, fuel and bush-fire discipline). It owns conduct and prevention protocols. It does not restate the things other guides own — border process, visas and cash mechanics live in R15; insurance, theft cover and medevac in R29; core emergency procedures in R10; country-by-country security colour in the profiles. This guide points to those rather than competing with them.
PART A · SECURITY

1 · Awareness & No-Go Judgement

Most security problems are avoided, not handled. The work is mostly done before you arrive.

Plan
Plan The Day
Know the route, the distance, and where you'll sleep before you set off. Don't improvise into unknown towns at dusk.
Daylight
Arrive In Daylight
Avoid driving at night — it multiplies every risk (crime, animals, breakdowns, bad roads). Be set up before dark.
Profile
Keep It Low
Don't display cash, cameras or kit. Modest, friendly, unremarkable. A loaded expedition truck already draws attention — don't add to it.
Intel
Use Local Knowledge
Other overlanders, lodge staff and locals know the current picture — which roads, which checkpoints, which areas. Ask, and trust your instincts.

Conditions vary sharply by country and change over time. Checkpoint density and corruption are heavy in some places and minimal in others; some regions have banditry or conflict zones to route around entirely. Treat the country profiles as the current-state source and re-check close to travel — this guide is the how, the profiles are the where.

2 · Documents & Money Security

The single best defence at a checkpoint or after a theft is good paperwork and distributed cash. Cross-ref R15, R29.

Carry & Copy
  • 1Originals on handPassport, vehicle registration, driving licence + International Driving Permit, carnet/temporary import permit, insurance, and any required police clearance/letter (see R15 and the country profiles).
  • 2Vehicle ownership proofThe vehicle is registered to Mary-Ann West — carry clear proof of ownership and, where she is not present, written authority to drive it across borders.
  • 3Colour copies, kept separatelyA full set of copies stored apart from the originals, plus digital copies in the cloud and on a phone. Hand a copy over first wherever an official will accept it.
  • 4Cash, distributedNever one wallet. Spread cash across several stashes; keep small-denomination USD accessible for legitimate fees (R15); keep the main reserve hidden and separate.
  • 5A decoy walletA cheap wallet with a modest amount of local cash and an expired card — the thing you hand over in a robbery so your real reserve stays hidden.
The passport stays on you. Keep the original passport on your person and offer a copy first. Don't surrender originals unless genuinely required — and never leave a passport as "security" for a fee.

3 · Checkpoints & Roadblocks

Calm, polite, well-documented and unhurried. Most checkpoints are routine and end in seconds.

There are two kinds: a formal roadblock (several officers, signage, every vehicle stopped) and a roadside check (a single officer waving cars over). Both can lawfully ask for your licence and the vehicle's papers, and can issue a valid fine for a real offence. Knowing this — and behaving like a relaxed, properly-documented private traveller — is what keeps you off the "easy target" list.

1
Slow early, indicate, stop where directed. Hazards on. Keep your hands visible.
2
Window down, engine off, greet the officer politely — a few words of the local language go a long way. Stay in the vehicle unless asked to step out.
3
Have your document copies ready. Answer simple questions plainly. Don't volunteer extra, don't lecture them on the law, don't get aggressive.
4
Patience is the tool. Friendly, unflustered and in no hurry. If you're genuinely in the wrong (a real traffic offence), accept the official process.
Filming is country-specific. In South Africa you are legally entitled to film or photograph a roadblock. Do not assume this holds anywhere else. At foreign checkpoints, photographing police, soldiers or "strategic" infrastructure can be treated as a crime and will escalate the encounter — default to not filming once you cross a border.

4 · "Spot Fines" & Solicited Bribes

A demand for immediate cash with no paperwork is, in nearly every case, a solicited bribe. Handle it calmly — and put safety above principle.

Police generally cannot lawfully demand on-the-spot cash. The default posture is simple and effective: don't offer and don't pay as a first move. Play the patient, polite, slightly-bewildered foreigner who would love to comply properly.

1
Ask, politely, what the offence is and to see the regulation.
2
Ask for an official written ticket or receipt payable at a police station or post. A great many "fines" evaporate the moment paperwork is requested.
3
Offer to follow them to the station to pay properly. Stay friendly, slow, and a little confused — time and good humour are on your side.
4
If it's a formal roadblock and a bribe is being solicited, you can ask to speak to the supervisor on scene; note the location and officer details if it's safe to do so.
Never escalate. No anger, no accusations of corruption, no threats, never physical resistance. Aggression turns a shakedown into a real problem. Confusion, courtesy and patience defuse far more than confrontation.
Safety overrides principle. If you are genuinely threatened, isolated, or your personal safety is at risk, getting out of the situation matters more than the point of principle. Paying a small "fine" from your accessible cash to leave a bad encounter is a legitimate judgement call — not a policy, a last resort. Keep small notes reachable for exactly this; never reveal your main reserve.

5 · Urban Crime & Driving

Towns and traffic are where opportunistic crime concentrates. Simple habits remove most of it.

  • 1Doors locked, windows up in towns and trafficBags and valuables out of sight — smash-and-grab happens at traffic lights and in stop-start traffic.
  • 2Leave an escape gap at stopsStop far enough behind the car ahead to see its rear tyres — room to pull away if you need to. Watch your mirrors at lights.
  • 3Don't stop for staged problemsBe wary of flagged-down "help", pointed-out flat tyres, or distractions while a second person approaches.
  • 4ATMs in daylight, busy and secureCover the keypad, refuse "helpers", be aware of who is watching. Draw cash inside a bank or mall where possible.
  • 5Park secure, leave nothing visibleGuarded parking where available; the vehicle locked and bare. Tip the car guard — local goodwill is cheap security.
If you are hijacked, comply. Hands visible, no sudden moves, no resistance — give up the vehicle. It is insured (R29) and replaceable; you are not. Get clear, then report.

6 · Vehicle & Camp Theft Prevention

Opportunistic theft of gear and the vehicle itself. Cross-ref R28 for camp siting.

The Vehicle
  • 1Steering lock + hidden isolator/kill-switchA concealed battery isolator or fuel cut is a cheap, effective theft deterrent and doubles as the electrical-fire isolation (§9).
  • 2Lock external gearCable-lock jerry cans, recovery gear and the spare wheel. These walk easily when you're not watching.
  • 3Close up when you leave itWindows up, doors locked, nothing on display — even for a short stop.
The Camp
  • 4Site with judgementEstablished sites, near other travellers, or with the community's knowledge and permission on their land. See R28 for the siting trade-offs (visibility vs exposure).
  • 5Bring valuables into the tentDon't leave gear, documents or electronics out overnight. Pack down before dark.
  • 6Locals are your best securityGreet people, ask permission, be courteous. Goodwill protects a camp better than any lock; rudeness invites trouble.

7 · If It Goes Wrong

Robbery, hijack or worse. Cross-ref R29 (claims), R10 (emergency procedures).

COMPLY→ Hands visible · no resistance · give the decoy→ Get to safety→ Report & claim

In a robbery or hijack, comply fully — keep your hands visible, make no sudden movements, hand over the decoy wallet and the vehicle if demanded. Property is replaceable; your safety is the only thing that matters in that moment.

Afterwards: get to a safe place, then report to the police and obtain a case number / police report — you will need it for any insurance claim (R29). For injuries or a medical emergency, work the R10 emergency procedures and R29 medevac chain. Keep emergency and insurer contacts written down, not only in a phone that may be taken.

PART B · FIRE

8 · Extinguishers

The right type, enough of it, where your hand can reach it in seconds. Cross-ref R11 (access), R15 (legal requirement).

DecisionStandard
TypeDry powder (ABC / ABE) as the default — cheap, available everywhere, multipurpose across ordinary combustibles, flammable liquids and electrical fires, and non-conductive. An optional clean-agent unit (e.g. Halotron-type) leaves no corrosive residue on the engine or electronics, at higher cost.
How manyAt least two. One within the driver's reach, one in the rear/living area. Small 1 kg units empty in seconds and knock down very little — favour adequate size and a second bottle over one token extinguisher.
PlacementDriver-accessible in seconds — not buried under a drawer or behind a seat (R11 zero-dig access). The second within reach of the camp kitchen / rear.
MountingA secure metal bracket that survives corrugations; a metal pin preferred over plastic. A unit that rattles loose or leaks down is useless when you need it.
ServiceCheck the pressure gauge and security periodically; some countries legally require a serviceable extinguisher (R15).

9 · Vehicle Fire Response

Seconds matter, and the wrong instinct — flinging the bonnet open — makes it worse. Cross-ref R10, R26.

Why vehicles catch fire off-road: oil or transmission fluid sprayed onto a hot exhaust, an electrical short (engine bay, lighting, or the inverter/dual-battery wiring), fuel vapour from a cracked line near hot components, or dry debris trapped against the exhaust or turbo. Vibration loosens fittings and mud hides leaks, so these build unseen.

1
Pull over — onto bare ground, off dry grass — and switch the engine off. Get everyone out and back, roughly 50 m, upwind.
2
Do not fully open the bonnet. Releasing the catch and discharging the extinguisher through the gap starves the fire; throwing the bonnet wide feeds it oxygen and flares it into your face.
3
For an electrical fire, isolate the batteries — turn the M-series battery switch OFF (kills the house bank, MultiPlus included), then the hidden kill-switch / disconnect for the crank circuit. The MultiPlus inverter/charger and house-bank wiring are a real fire load (R26).
4
Aim low, at the base of the flames, in short bursts. A single small bottle knocks down only about a litre of oil fire — be quick and deliberate.
5
If it is beyond a bottle or two, get clear and stay clear. The vehicle is not worth your life; beware fuel and tyres. Account for everyone.

10 · Fuel & Gas Handling

The everyday fire risk is at the pump and the stove, not on the trail.

  • 1Refuelling disciplineEngine off, no smoking or open flame nearby, touch bare metal first to shed static. Decant the Desert Fox cells and jerry cans with a funnel, no spills, never near a hot exhaust.
  • 2Stove fuelStored sealed, ventilated, away from heat and sparks. Never refuel a stove that is hot or alight.
  • 3Gas, if carriedLeak-test connections with soapy water; store the bottle upright and ventilated; turn it off at the bottle when driving; never run a gas appliance inside a closed tent — fire and carbon-monoxide risk both.

11 · Bush & Camp Fire Discipline

A fire you start that spreads carries real legal and financial liability. Cross-ref R29.

Don't park a hot vehicle over dry grass. A hot exhaust, turbo or underbody sitting on dry veld grass is a genuine and common ignition source. In dry country, stop and park on bare ground, gravel or a road surface — not in long grass.
  • 1Campfires only where permittedContained in a ring or pan, well clear of tents and grass, never left unattended.
  • 2Fully extinguish before sleeping or leavingDrown it, stir it, drown it again until cold to the touch. Mind sparks carried on the wind.
  • 3Heed fire bans and the seasonHigh fire-danger days and seasonal bans are real — when in doubt, skip the fire. Check the country profiles for local rules.
  • 4Raised fire pan on no-dig terrainOn pans, salt flats, rock and sensitive ground, no fire touches the surface: the raised flat-pack fire pan is mandatory, with the fibreglass ground sheet laid beneath it — a raised pan still drops embers and radiant heat scars a pan crust. The ground sheet is a dedicated item; it is NOT the vehicle fire blanket, which stays mounted and accessible at all times (see §9).
  • 5Burn down, then kill it deadLast fire of the evening burns down to fine ash — no late wood. Douse, stir, douse again; confirm cold by hand. In wind, an ember bed under an ash blanket stays live overnight. Never decant warm ash; never leave a pan of ash standing where wind can lift it.
  • 6Ash disposal by terrainSensitive / no-dig terrain: pack out — cold, wetted ash into the sealed ash tin, disposed at the next town bin or a farm pit with the landowner's permission. Ordinary ground with landowner blessing: broadcast cold wood ash thinly over a wide area away from camp and water — scattered, never heaped. Established sites: use the fireplace or ash point. Note charcoal-briquette ash carries binder residues — pack it out rather than scatter.
