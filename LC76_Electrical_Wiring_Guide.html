§1 System Overview & Topology As fitted · July 2026

The LC76 runs a dual-chemistry battery system: a LiFePO4 house battery (Enertec Power+ 210Ah) for accessories, and a separate lead-acid crank battery (Willard 650) for starting. A Victron Orion Smart 12/12-50A non-isolated DC-DC charger charges the house from the alternator — its output capped to ~40A to protect the 1HZ alternator — while a SmartSolar MPPT and a MultiPlus add solar and mains/shore charging. The house battery’s internal BMS manages cell protection; a SmartShunt 500A tracks true state of charge by counting amp-hours.

§3 How the Dual-Battery System Works Orion DC-DC logic · SmartShunt readings
DC-DC Charging (Orion Smart)Alternator → house, current-controlled

The Victron Orion Smart 12/12-50A is a DC-DC charger, not a relay. It takes 12V from the crank battery (charged by the alternator) and delivers a proper multi-stage LiFePO4 charge to the house battery. Unlike the old VSR it does not simply parallel the two batteries — it isolates them for charging purposes and controls the current, so the house always receives a correct charge profile regardless of alternator voltage.

Orion DC-DC operating behaviour
StateTriggerWhat happens
CHARGINGEngine running / input ≥ ~13.1VOrion detects the alternator running (input voltage rises) and charges the house up to the configured limit.
CURRENT-CAPPEDOutput limited to ~40AOutput is capped in VictronConnect to ~40A to protect the 1HZ alternator, even though unit and battery could accept more.
STANDBYInput < ~12.8V (engine off)Orion stops drawing from the crank battery, protecting it from discharge by house loads.
BMS CUTHouse BMS signals stopIf the house BMS blocks charging (cell full, or temperature < 0°C) the Orion pauses until conditions clear.
Key implication: the crank is protected, the house always charges correctly The Orion charges on engine-run detection (input voltage), not the ignition switch. Because it is a true DC-DC charger, the house receives a full LiFePO4 profile even when the alternator only reaches ~14.0V — something the old VSR could never do. The crank battery is never loaded by house accessories.
Reading the SmartShuntBattery monitor — coulomb counting

The Victron SmartShunt 500A is fitted on the house battery negative and reports via Bluetooth in VictronConnect (there is no fixed display). It measures every amp in and out and reports true state of charge by counting amp-hours — this is the primary house-battery reading and your in-field electrical status check.

On LiFePO4, resting voltage does NOT tell you state of charge A LiFePO4 battery holds almost the same voltage (~13.2–13.4V) from roughly 20% to 90% charge — the discharge curve is nearly flat. You cannot read SoC from a voltmeter the way you can with lead-acid. Always use the SmartShunt’s amp-hour count / SoC %. Voltage is only a coarse sanity check at the extremes.
House battery (LiFePO4) — SmartShunt interpretation
SmartShunt readingMeaningAction
SoC 100% · ~13.6–14.4VFull / chargingFully charged. 210Ah nominal, ~168Ah usable to 80% DoD.
SoC 30–90% · ~13.2–13.4VNormal working rangeFlat voltage is normal — trust the SoC %, not the voltage.
SoC 15–20% · ~12.8–13.0VApproaching planned floorRecharge soon. Staying above ~20% preserves cycle life.
Voltage falling fast / < 12.0VNear-empty — BMS cutoff imminentThe BMS disconnects at ~9.6V to protect the cells. Reduce load and recharge; do not rely on reaching cutoff.
The crank battery still reads by voltage The crank battery (Willard 650) is lead-acid — its resting voltage does indicate SoC (12.6–12.8V healthy). Check it at the terminals or in VictronConnect if monitored. Do not apply lead-acid voltage rules to the LiFePO4 house.
§4 Charging Paths Alternator/DC-DC · MultiPlus mains · SmartSolar
Alternator → DC-DC ChargingPrimary source — engine running

On the road the 1HZ alternator charges the crank battery, and the Orion Smart DC-DC charges the house battery from the crank. The Orion delivers a full multi-stage LiFePO4 profile regardless of alternator voltage, so the house reaches a genuine full charge on a long enough drive — unlike a lead-acid aux on a VSR, which could only bulk-charge.

Orion output capped to ~40A — protect the 1HZ alternator The house battery can accept far more than 40A, but the 1HZ alternator is not sized to deliver a continuous 50A to the house on top of the vehicle’s own loads. The Orion output is capped to ~40A in VictronConnect. Confirm the cap is set before departure and after any firmware reset. At ~40A a deeply drawn 210Ah house takes roughly 3–4 hours of driving to refill — verify with the SmartShunt, not by voltage.
MultiPlus — Mains / Shore ChargingCampsite / home use

The Victron MultiPlus 12/500/20-16 is a combined inverter and mains charger. On 230V shore power it charges the house battery (20A charger) with a full LiFePO4 profile and simultaneously passes AC through to the vehicle’s outlets. It replaces both the old mains charger and the inverter. Shore-power hardware and transfer behaviour are detailed in §11.

One box does both jobs The MultiPlus charges from mains and inverts to supply 230V from the house battery (~430W continuous). There is no separate mains charger and no separate inverter to fault-find — a single unit, configured in VictronConnect.
Hardkorr 200W Portable SolarSupplementary — stationary use

The Hardkorr 200W portable panel feeds the Victron SmartSolar MPPT 100/30, which charges the house battery directly with an MPPT LiFePO4 profile. Output is weather and angle dependent — on a clear southern-African day expect roughly 50–80Ah over 6–8 hours of good sun, enough to offset moderate fridge and lighting loads while stationary. Solar runs independently of the engine and the MultiPlus, so it keeps the house topped up in camp.

Solar charges the house directly The MPPT connects to the house battery, not the crank — solar energy goes where it is most useful. All three charge sources (alternator/Orion, solar/MPPT, shore/MultiPlus) can run together; each respects the house BMS.
§5 Fault-Tracing Method Multimeter-based · systematic isolation
Know your limits This section covers basic multimeter-based diagnosis. It is not a substitute for a qualified auto-electrician or 12V specialist. If a fault is beyond basic diagnosis, do not improvise with high-current circuits — a short on a main battery cable can cause a fire. Diagnose and manage; seek professional repair at the next opportunity.
Multimeter SetupFour essential tests
Multimeter tests — settings and use
TestMeter settingProbesWhat you're measuring
DC voltageDC V (20V range)Red → +ve · Black → −ve/chassisBattery state, charging voltage, presence of supply at a fuse or terminal
ContinuityΩ / beep modeOne probe each end of wire/fuseWhether a wire or fuse is intact (continuous). Beep = good.
Voltage dropDC V (low range)Across a terminal, connector, or section of cableResistance in the circuit under load. >0.2V across a connection = poor connection.
Parasitic drainDC mA (or A)In series: disconnect one battery cable, insert meter between cable end and postCurrent drawn with engine off and everything switched off. <50mA is normal; >100mA sustained = drain fault.
Systematic Isolation MethodAlways start here
  1. Confirm the symptom precisely. "Battery flat" is not enough — is it the crank, the aux, or both? What was the last known state? Does the SmartShunt show a voltage and SoC?
  2. Measure battery voltages first. Crank and aux — note readings. This tells you immediately whether you have a battery problem or a wiring/supply problem.
  3. Check the obvious first. Loose terminal, blown fuse, tripped isolator. These cause most field failures. Do not skip to complex diagnosis.
  4. Work from the source toward the load. Battery → fuse → switch → load. Confirm voltage is present at each stage. Where voltage disappears, the fault is between that point and the previous one.
  5. Isolate before probing. For continuity tests, the circuit must be de-energised (battery disconnected). Never test continuity on a live circuit.
  6. Document what you find. Note the voltage readings, which fuses you pulled, and what changed when. This matters if a specialist has to continue the diagnosis later.

§7 Field Fixes & Workarounds Terminal care · DC-DC failure · jump-start · limp options
Clean and Tighten TerminalsMost common field fix

Corroded or loose terminals are the single most common cause of intermittent electrical faults in the field. Vibration on corrugated roads works terminals loose over time. Include a terminal check in every pre-departure inspection.

Inspect all battery terminal clamps
Check for white/blue powdery corrosion (sulphation). Clean with terminal brush or baking-soda solution. Ensure clamps are tight — cannot rotate by hand. Apply petroleum jelly or terminal grease after cleaning.
Check earth straps
Battery negative to chassis, engine block to chassis. Tug-test each strap — should be solid. Clean contact points if corroded.
Re-torque after corrugated sections
After 200+ km of corrugated road, re-check all terminal clamps and main earth connections.
If the DC-DC (Orion) FailsCharging workaround — no direct bridging

If the Orion DC-DC fails, the house battery simply stops charging from the alternator. The other two charge paths still work, so this is rarely a stranding fault.

Never bridge the crank directly to the LiFePO4 house The old lead-acid trick of jumping crank-to-aux does not apply here. Directly paralleling the 12V crank to the LiFePO4 house bypasses the DC-DC and the BMS current control — the inrush can trip the BMS, stress the cells, or damage cabling. If the Orion is dead, charge the house through the paths below instead.
  1. Use solar. The SmartSolar MPPT charges the house independently of the Orion — deploy the Hardkorr panel in good sun.
  2. Use shore/mains. If a 230V source is available, the MultiPlus charges the house directly.
  3. Reduce house loads to stretch capacity until the Orion is repaired or replaced. The crank battery still starts the engine normally.
  4. Log the fault and check the Orion’s input/output fuses and VictronConnect status when safe.
Jump-Starting with the ARB PackFlat crank battery
ARB jump pack — mandatory cable order Connect in this order only. Reverse polarity will destroy electronics. Do not connect to a frozen battery.
  1. Red (positive) → flat crank battery positive terminal.
  2. Black (negative) → chassis earth point — NOT the flat battery negative terminal. Connecting to the battery negative can ignite hydrogen gas near the battery.
  3. Switch on the ARB pack and wait 60 seconds.
  4. Attempt engine start. If no start after 2–3 attempts, wait 2 minutes before retrying (pack needs to recover).
  5. Once running, disconnect black (negative) first, then red (positive).
  6. Drive for at least 30–60 minutes to charge the crank battery before switching off.
Limp OptionsWhen a full fix is not possible

§10 Safety Disconnect order · arc risk · LiFePO4 & lead-acid
Battery disconnect order — always follow this sequence Disconnecting: negative (black, −) first, then positive (red, +).
Reconnecting: positive (red, +) first, then negative (black, −).

Reversing this sequence risks a short circuit: if the positive terminal is disconnected first and the spanner bridges to the chassis while the negative is still connected, the short occurs through the spanner. Disconnecting negative first eliminates this risk.
Short-Circuit and Arc Risk

A 12V battery — lead-acid or LiFePO4 — can deliver very high current into a short circuit, enough to instantly vaporise a spanner, ignite fuel or insulation, and cause a severe burn. The risk is low in a well-maintained system but becomes real during any wiring work.

Remove rings, watches and metal jewellery before working near batteries
Metal jewellery bridging battery terminals causes immediate severe burns.
Insulate all tools before working in the battery bay
Wrap spanner handles in electrical tape or use insulated tools.
Never lay tools across the battery terminals
Keep spanners and screwdrivers away from the battery top while working on adjacent components.
Flooded Lead-Acid (FLA) — Crank BatteryWillard 650
Hydrogen gas and acid — flooded LA specific The Willard 650 crank battery is a flooded lead-acid (FLA) unit. Charging generates hydrogen gas, which is flammable. Acid electrolyte can cause burns.
  • Do not charge in an enclosed space without ventilation.
  • Do not connect or disconnect the battery (or jump leads) while the battery is actively gassing (bubbling) — a spark can ignite hydrogen.
  • If electrolyte contacts skin or eyes: flush immediately with large quantities of water for 15 minutes. Seek medical attention for eye exposure.
  • Keep the crank battery upright. If tipped, acid can spill from the vent caps.
LiFePO4 — House BatteryEnertec Power+ 210Ah
LiFePO4 hazards differ from lead-acid The Enertec Power+ house battery is LiFePO4 with an internal BMS. It does not gas hydrogen in normal use and needs no ventilation for charging, but it has its own rules:
  • The BMS blocks charging below 0°C — do not force-charge a cold battery; let it warm first. Never charge a frozen battery.
  • Respect the 105A continuous discharge ceiling; sustained overload trips the BMS and, if abused, stresses the cells.
  • Do not use a battery that is swollen, pierced, or physically damaged — a damaged cell can vent. Isolate and replace it.
  • It can deliver very high fault current — the short-circuit and jewellery precautions above apply equally.
  • Keep terminals clean and torqued; the case is IP65 but connections still corrode.
§11Shore Power, AC & EarthingMultiPlus mains · EZ-Lock inlet · common-negative earth
Shore Power & ACMultiPlus 12/500 · 16A EZ-Lock inlet

Mains power enters through a 16A EZ-Lock stainless inlet via a 15 m 16A lead to the Victron MultiPlus 12/500/20-16. The MultiPlus does two jobs: it charges the house battery (20A charger, full LiFePO4 profile) and it inverts from the house to supply 230V to the vehicle’s AC outlets (~430W continuous).

Transfer behaviour — pass-through when on shore When shore power is connected the MultiPlus passes AC straight through to the AC loads and charges the house at the same time (up to a 16A transfer limit). When shore power is removed it seamlessly inverts from the house battery to keep the AC outlets live — UPS-style. Keep continuous AC draw within ~430W; brief peaks are tolerated but sustained overload will fault the unit.
Before connecting shore power Check the lead and inlet for damage, confirm the site supply is 230V, and seat the EZ-Lock inlet fully. Set the MultiPlus input current limit in VictronConnect to match the campsite breaker.
EarthingNon-isolated common-negative star-earth

The system uses a non-isolated, common-negative star-earth: a single earth reference. The crank negative, the house negative, and all load returns meet at one common star point tied to the chassis. Because the Orion DC-DC is the non-isolated type, it shares battery negative between input and output — input and output grounds are common by design.

One earth reference — avoid earth loops Do not create a second, separate earth path or a long parallel negative run that bypasses the star point — multiple earth references cause circulating currents, false SmartShunt readings, and noise. The SmartShunt 500A sits in the house negative at the star point and must see all house current, so nothing may earth downstream of it.
