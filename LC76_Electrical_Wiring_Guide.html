§1 System Overview & Topology As fitted · June 2026

The LC76 runs a lead-acid dual-battery system. A Victron Cyrix VSR (voltage-sensitive relay) automatically isolates or combines the two batteries based on measured voltage — protecting the crank battery from discharge by accessories while allowing the alternator to charge both.

§3 How the Dual-Battery System Works Cyrix VSR logic · BM2000 readings
Cyrix VSR LogicVoltage-sensitive relay

The Victron Cyrix 120A is a voltage-sensitive relay (VSR) — not a simple switch. It monitors the crank battery voltage and automatically combines or separates the two batteries based on that voltage.

Cyrix operating thresholds
StateVoltage thresholdWhat happens
COMBINED≥ 13.7V for 30 secRelay closes — both batteries connected. Alternator or mains charger charges both simultaneously.
HOLD COMBINED≥ 13.0VRelay stays closed while voltage holds above this level.
SEPARATING< 13.0V for 30 secRelay opens — batteries isolated. Crank battery protected from accessory discharge.
SEPARATED< 12.5VBatteries fully isolated. Engine must be running (or mains connected) to re-combine.
Key implication: the Cyrix does not combine on engine ignition alone The Cyrix triggers on voltage, not the ignition switch. The relay will only close once the alternator has raised crank voltage to ~13.7V — typically 30–60 seconds after start-up at idle. Until then, the aux battery is isolated and not receiving charge. This is normal behaviour.
Reading the BM2000Dual-battery monitor

The BM2000 displays voltage and/or state-of-charge for both batteries. Use it as your primary in-field electrical status check.

BM2000 readings — interpretation guide (lead-acid)
ReadingCrank (Willard 650)Aux (Deltec 1250)Action
Healthy resting12.6–12.8V12.6–12.8VNormal — record as your baseline.
Charging (alternator)13.8–14.4V13.5–14.2VNormal. Aux may be slightly lower due to Cyrix voltage drop.
Low resting12.2–12.5V12.2–12.5VPartially discharged. Charge before use.
Flat / discharge< 12.0V< 12.0VBattery significantly discharged. Do not attempt engine start until charged.
Critical< 11.8V< 11.8VBattery deeply discharged — potential damage. Charge immediately, assess condition.
Surface charge masking Immediately after charging, a lead-acid battery can show a falsely high voltage (surface charge). For an accurate resting voltage reading, leave the battery at rest with no load or charge for at least 2 hours after the last charge. Overnight is better.
§4 Charging Paths Alternator · DEFA mains · Hardkorr solar
Alternator ChargingPrimary source — engine running

The 1HZ alternator is the primary charge source on the road. Once the crank battery voltage reaches ~13.7V and the Cyrix combines (after ~30–60 sec at idle), both batteries charge in parallel. The alternator's regulated output (~13.8–14.4V) is sufficient to bring both batteries through bulk charge. It cannot complete the absorption and float stages that a smart charger provides — full charge requires mains or extended driving.

Alternator charge time — rough guide A 50% discharged 200Ah combined bank (crank + aux) requires approximately 3–4 hours of highway driving to reach ~80% state of charge. The alternator is not efficient at the top 20% — it tapers off as battery voltage rises. Never assume a battery is full after even a long drive. Verify with the BM2000.
DEFA SmartCharge — Mains ChargingCampsite / home use

The DEFA SmartCharge provides multi-stage (bulk / absorption / float) charging from a 220V mains supply. It is connected to the crank battery. When the DEFA raises crank voltage to ~13.7V, the Cyrix combines and the aux battery charges through the relay.

Known quirk: aux battery may not reach 100% via DEFA The aux (Deltec 1250) sits below the DEFA's regulation point by the voltage drop across the Cyrix relay and cabling — typically 0.1–0.3V. This means the aux may complete bulk charge but may never fully complete the absorption stage, and can chronically rest at ~90–95% even after an overnight DEFA charge. Field check: after a full overnight DEFA charge, read both battery voltages on the BM2000 with engine off. If the aux reads 0.2V or more below the crank, the aux is not reaching full charge via the DEFA path. Record the result here and in Facts.txt §8.

Hardkorr 200W Portable SolarSupplementary — stationary use

The Hardkorr 200W portable panel provides supplementary charge when stationary. It is typically connected directly to the aux battery (verify your wiring). Solar output is weather and angle dependent — on a clear day in southern Africa, expect 50–80Ah of usable charge over 6–8 hours of good sun. This is adequate to offset moderate fridge and lighting loads but will not recover a significantly discharged bank.

Solar connection point Aux battery or crank? [ verify and record during pre-departure audit ]
If solar is connected directly to the aux (bypassing the Cyrix), the crank battery does not benefit from solar charging. This is the recommended configuration — it protects the crank and directs solar energy where it is most useful.
§5 Fault-Tracing Method Multimeter-based · systematic isolation
Know your limits This section covers basic multimeter-based diagnosis. It is not a substitute for a qualified auto-electrician or 12V specialist. If a fault is beyond basic diagnosis, do not improvise with high-current circuits — a short on a main battery cable can cause a fire. Diagnose and manage; seek professional repair at the next opportunity.
Multimeter SetupFour essential tests
Multimeter tests — settings and use
TestMeter settingProbesWhat you're measuring
DC voltageDC V (20V range)Red → +ve · Black → −ve/chassisBattery state, charging voltage, presence of supply at a fuse or terminal
ContinuityΩ / beep modeOne probe each end of wire/fuseWhether a wire or fuse is intact (continuous). Beep = good.
Voltage dropDC V (low range)Across a terminal, connector, or section of cableResistance in the circuit under load. >0.2V across a connection = poor connection.
Parasitic drainDC mA (or A)In series: disconnect one battery cable, insert meter between cable end and postCurrent drawn with engine off and everything switched off. <50mA is normal; >100mA sustained = drain fault.
Systematic Isolation MethodAlways start here
  1. Confirm the symptom precisely. "Battery flat" is not enough — is it the crank, the aux, or both? What was the last known state? Does the BM2000 show a voltage?
  2. Measure battery voltages first. Crank and aux — note readings. This tells you immediately whether you have a battery problem or a wiring/supply problem.
  3. Check the obvious first. Loose terminal, blown fuse, tripped isolator. These cause most field failures. Do not skip to complex diagnosis.
  4. Work from the source toward the load. Battery → fuse → switch → load. Confirm voltage is present at each stage. Where voltage disappears, the fault is between that point and the previous one.
  5. Isolate before probing. For continuity tests, the circuit must be de-energised (battery disconnected). Never test continuity on a live circuit.
  6. Document what you find. Note the voltage readings, which fuses you pulled, and what changed when. This matters if a specialist has to continue the diagnosis later.
§6 Common Faults & Diagnosis Symptom → cause → check → fix
Symptom Likely cause Check Field fix / action
Aux not charging after long drive Cyrix not combining; blown Cyrix fuse; bad connection on Cyrix terminals Is crank voltage ≥13.7V? Check Cyrix fuse (if fitted). Check voltage at Cyrix input and output terminals under load. Clean Cyrix terminals. Replace fuse if blown. If Cyrix faulty, aux will need direct-charge workaround (see §7).
Cyrix not engaging (engine running) Alternator output low; Cyrix fuse blown; Cyrix failed open Measure alternator output voltage at crank battery terminals (engine running). Should be 13.8–14.4V. If low, suspect alternator or charge circuit. If alternator output is good but Cyrix still open: replace Cyrix (carry spare if possible) or bridge with a direct cable for aux charging (supervised only).
Cyrix stuck closed (aux draining crank) Cyrix relay welded; internal fault With engine off and no charger, both batteries should show independent voltages. If crank is draining toward aux level overnight with nothing on, Cyrix may be welded closed. Disconnect the Cyrix cable on the aux side to isolate. Seek replacement at next opportunity.
Flat crank — won't start Battery deeply discharged; poor terminal contact; battery failed BM2000 or multimeter: crank voltage <12V? Check terminal clamps are tight and clean. Jump-start with ARB pack (see §7). Once running, drive to charge. If battery fails to recover, replace at next opportunity.
Parasitic drain — battery flat with no load Accessory left on; faulty switch or relay; wiring fault With everything off, measure current draw in series on the battery cable (mA mode). >100mA sustained = drain. Pull fuses one at a time — when drain drops, that circuit is the culprit. Isolate faulty circuit (pull fuse or disconnect). Do not leave batteries connected if drain is significant and vehicle is parked for days.
Blown fuse (circuit dead) Overload; short circuit; loose fitting causing arc Locate fuse for that circuit (§2 fuse map). Inspect visually — blown fuses show a broken wire or blackening inside. Replace with same-rating fuse (never higher). If new fuse blows immediately, there is a short — do not replace again until fault is found. Isolate circuit.
Bad earth / intermittent fault Corroded earth strap; loose chassis connection Voltage drop test across earth strap under load (>0.2V = high resistance). Inspect earth points — battery negative, chassis straps, body earths. Clean earth connections with terminal brush, tighten. A temporary extra earth strap can confirm the fault.
Winch no power Blown inline fuse; tripped solenoid; poor crank terminal contact Check inline winch fuse first (likely near battery). Check solenoid — does it click when you activate the winch switch? Measure voltage at solenoid input. Replace fuse if blown (verify correct rating — do not oversize). If solenoid fails to click, check winch control circuit fuse and wiring to switch.
Inverter low-voltage cutout Aux battery low; high load exceeds available capacity Check aux voltage on BM2000. Inverter typically cuts out at ~10.5–11V to protect the battery. Reduce load. Allow aux to charge. Do not override the cutout — it protects the battery from deep discharge damage.
Fridge cutting out Aux voltage low; fridge low-voltage protection triggering; blown fridge fuse Check aux voltage — below 11.8V will trigger most compressor fridge protection. Check fridge inline fuse. Allow aux to charge before resuming fridge use. If voltage is adequate but fridge still cuts: check fridge fuse and terminals.
Solar not charging aux Panel disconnected; reverse polarity; blown solar fuse; bad connection Is the panel plugged in and in sun? Measure panel output voltage (open circuit, in sun, should be ~20V+ for a 12V panel). Check inline fuse on solar lead. Measure voltage at battery connection point. Clean and reseat connectors. Replace fuse if blown. If panel output is present at battery but battery is not rising, check polarity and connection integrity.
§7 Field Fixes & Workarounds Terminal care · manual combining · jump-start · limp options
Clean and Tighten TerminalsMost common field fix

Corroded or loose terminals are the single most common cause of intermittent electrical faults in the field. Vibration on corrugated roads works terminals loose over time. Include a terminal check in every pre-departure inspection.

Inspect all battery terminal clamps
Check for white/blue powdery corrosion (sulphation). Clean with terminal brush or baking-soda solution. Ensure clamps are tight — cannot rotate by hand. Apply petroleum jelly or terminal grease after cleaning.
Check earth straps
Battery negative to chassis, engine block to chassis. Tug-test each strap — should be solid. Clean contact points if corroded.
Re-torque after corrugated sections
After 200+ km of corrugated road, re-check all terminal clamps and main earth connections.
Manually Combining to Charge AuxCyrix bypass — workaround only

If the Cyrix has failed open and the aux battery cannot charge via the normal path, the batteries can be temporarily bridged using heavy-gauge jumper cables to allow the alternator to charge the aux. This is a workaround, not a permanent solution.

Manual combining — safety rules Only do this with the engine running. Never bridge a fully charged battery to a deeply discharged one with the engine off — the current surge can damage both batteries or cause a fire. Monitor both batteries continuously while bridged. Remove the bridge cable before switching off the engine.
  1. Start the engine and allow it to run at a fast idle (~1500 RPM).
  2. Connect a heavy-gauge jumper cable from the crank battery positive terminal to the aux battery positive terminal. Do not connect negative-to-negative — both are already earthed to chassis.
  3. Monitor with the BM2000. Aux voltage should begin rising. Expect slow recovery — allow 30–60 minutes of driving to bring aux to an acceptable level.
  4. Remove the cable before switching off the engine. Disconnect the aux (+) end first, then the crank (+) end.
Jump-Starting with the ARB PackFlat crank battery
ARB jump pack — mandatory cable order Connect in this order only. Reverse polarity will destroy electronics. Do not connect to a frozen battery.
  1. Red (positive) → flat crank battery positive terminal.
  2. Black (negative) → chassis earth point — NOT the flat battery negative terminal. Connecting to the battery negative can ignite hydrogen gas near the battery.
  3. Switch on the ARB pack and wait 60 seconds.
  4. Attempt engine start. If no start after 2–3 attempts, wait 2 minutes before retrying (pack needs to recover).
  5. Once running, disconnect black (negative) first, then red (positive).
  6. Drive for at least 30–60 minutes to charge the crank battery before switching off.
Limp OptionsWhen a full fix is not possible
ScenarioLimp option
Cyrix failed — aux cannot chargeManual bridge (see above) or accept limited aux capacity; prioritise load reduction (no inverter, fridge on minimum).

§10 Safety Disconnect order · arc risk · lead-acid hazards
Battery disconnect order — always follow this sequence Disconnecting: negative (black, −) first, then positive (red, +).
Reconnecting: positive (red, +) first, then negative (black, −).

Reversing this sequence risks a short circuit: if the positive terminal is disconnected first and the spanner bridges to the chassis while the negative is still connected, the short occurs through the spanner. Disconnecting negative first eliminates this risk.
Short-Circuit and Arc Risk

A 12V lead-acid battery can deliver thousands of amps into a short circuit — enough to instantly vaporise a spanner, ignite fuel or insulation, and cause a severe burn. The risk is low in a well-maintained system but becomes real during any wiring work.

Remove rings, watches and metal jewellery before working near batteries
Metal jewellery bridging battery terminals causes immediate severe burns.
Insulate all tools before working in the battery bay
Wrap spanner handles in electrical tape or use insulated tools.
Never lay tools across the battery terminals
Keep spanners and screwdrivers away from the battery top while working on adjacent components.
Flooded Lead-Acid (FLA) — Crank BatteryWillard 650
Hydrogen gas and acid — flooded LA specific The Willard 650 crank battery is a flooded lead-acid (FLA) unit. Charging generates hydrogen gas, which is flammable. Acid electrolyte can cause burns.
  • Do not charge in an enclosed space without ventilation.
  • Do not connect or disconnect the battery (or jump leads) while the battery is actively gassing (bubbling) — a spark can ignite hydrogen.
  • If electrolyte contacts skin or eyes: flush immediately with large quantities of water for 15 minutes. Seek medical attention for eye exposure.
  • Keep the crank battery upright. If tipped, acid can spill from the vent caps.
Sealed Lead-Acid (SLA) — Aux BatteryDeltec 1250

The Deltec 1250 is a sealed (valve-regulated) lead-acid battery. Under normal conditions it does not vent gas. However, overcharging or reverse charging can cause pressure build-up and electrolyte venting. Keep away from open flame and do not overcharge beyond the rated charge voltage. If the battery case is swollen or distorted, do not use it — replace immediately.
