01 Geology, Ecology & The Great Paradox Not Actually A Desert

The Kalahari is the world's largest continuous sand mass — a fossil sand sea covering approximately 900,000 km² across southern Africa, stretching from the Orange River in the south to the Congo Basin in the north, touching Botswana, Namibia, South Africa, Zimbabwe, Zambia, and Angola. It is ancient beyond reckoning: the dunes were laid down by wind approximately 60,000 years ago during an ice-age arid period, and before that the underlying geology dates to the Precambrian — some of the oldest exposed rock on Earth.

The central paradox of the Kalahari is that it is not technically a desert. A true desert receives less than 250mm of rainfall per year; the Kalahari receives 150–500mm annually depending on location. The sand does not form a true erg (open dune sea) — much of it is stabilised by vegetation. There is a substantial underground water table, accessed by deep-rooted trees like the camel thorn acacia and shepherd's tree. The Kalahari is correctly described as a fossil desert or semi-arid savannah — the dunes are real, the aridity is real, but life has found footholds throughout.

Total Area
900,000 km²
Larger than France and Germany combined. Spans 6 countries. The LC76 expedition will cross it multiple times from multiple directions.
Annual Rainfall
150–500mm
South: ~150mm (Kgalagadi). North: ~500mm (CKGR northern edge). Seasonal: November–April. All falls on sand — no surface rivers, no standing water except pans after rain.
Dune Age
~60,000 Years
Aeolian (wind-deposited) dunes formed during the last glacial period. Most are now fixed and vegetated. Active dunes in the southwest (Kgalagadi) and around the Makgadikgadi pans.
Underground Water
Present But Deep
A vast underground aquifer underlies most of the Kalahari. Accessible via deep-rooted trees and boreholes — not accessible to people on foot. Pan water (if present) is often alkaline and undrinkable.
Name Origin
Makgadikgadi
The name Kalahari derives from the Setswana word Kgalagadi — "great thirst" or "the waterless place." The Makgadikgadi (the vast salt pan system) shares the same root. The name is not merely poetic; it is a survival warning.
Soil & Sand Colour
Red to White
Southern Kalahari (Kgalagadi): distinctive deep red sand — iron oxide-stained. Northern pans (Makgadikgadi): brilliant white salt. CKGR interior: pale golden sand. The colour shift is a reliable geographic orientation cue.

The vegetation is equally paradoxical. The Kalahari supports camel thorn acacia (Vachellia erioloba) — trees up to 17 metres tall with root systems reaching 40+ metres to underground water. Shepherd's tree (Boscia albitrunca) provides the only year-round shade in many areas. Tsamma melon (Citrullus lanatus) grows wild across the sands — a critical water source for both wildlife and San people. Hoodia cactus, wild raisins, buffalo thorn, and dozens of grass species complete an ecosystem that is sparse to the eye but biologically rich underfoot.

The "desert" framing misleads travellers People arrive in the Kalahari expecting barren emptiness and find grass, trees, birds, and abundant wildlife. The real dangers are not the absence of life but the absence of permanent surface water, the heat extremes (45°C+ in summer), and the sheer scale — distances that are easy on a map become lethal miscalculations when sand slows progress to 30 km/h and fuel consumption doubles.

Map layers — two separate tasks When trip planning, always keep political and ecological layers separate. Political layers (country borders, district boundaries, park concession lines) determine which permits you need, which operator you book with, and which crossing point applies — they are the permit map. Ecological layers (dune fields, flood extents, pan margins, fossil valley floors, vegetation types) determine travel safety, tyre pressures, fuel consumption, and wildlife expectations — they are the terrain map. Overlaying both on the same display creates confusion; use T4A for political/track data and satellite imagery (Google Earth / NASA MODIS seasonal layers) for ecological assessment.
— Climate & Diurnal PatternsWind · Temperature Swing · Dust · Lightning

The Kalahari has a climate defined by extremes of temperature swing, not just extremes of heat. The diurnal range — the difference between daily maximum and nightly minimum — can exceed 30°C in a single day. A June day in the CKGR that reaches 28°C may drop to −3°C before dawn. This range stresses vehicle systems, affects human performance, and requires a different gear strategy for each part of the day.

Summer Days (Dec–Feb)
40–46°C
Physiologically dangerous above 38°C for sustained activity. Heat exhaustion onset: 2–3 hours of exposed work. Enforce shade rest 10:00–16:00. Vehicle: check coolant every morning. 1HZ oil temperature will run high — watch for overheating on long sand sections.
Winter Nights (Jun–Jul)
−5 to +5°C
Frost is common in the southern Kalahari (Kgalagadi, Mabuasehube). The 1HZ glow plugs are critical — allow 30–45 seconds pre-heat before cranking on cold mornings. Warm sleeping gear mandatory. Ground sleeping (OzTent) requires an insulating mat under the sleeping bag — cold from the ground is significant.
Diurnal Swing
Up to 30°C
A 28°C day can precede a 0°C dawn. This is not unusual — it is the norm in the dry Kalahari winter. Plan clothing and camp setup for both extremes in the same 24-hour period. Tyre pressure rises 0.2–0.3 bar from cold morning to hot midday — check at a consistent temperature.
Wind Pattern
SE Prevailing
Prevailing winds are from the southeast — this moves dust in predictable directions. Camp orientation: put your tent opening away from the prevailing wind. On pans: vehicle tracks stir dust that follows you. Drive into the wind on open pans to keep dust away from the camp.
Dust Storms
Haboobs — Oct/Nov
Pre-rain season (Oct–Nov) produces haboobs — walls of dust 100–300m high that can reduce visibility to zero in minutes. Stop driving immediately if a haboob approaches. Close all windows and vents. A haboob typically passes in 15–30 minutes. Do not attempt to drive through one — the dust can clog air filters critically.
Lightning — Pans
Significant Risk
The flat salt pans are the highest-risk lightning environments in the Kalahari. A vehicle or a person on an open pan is the tallest object for kilometres. If a storm approaches while on a pan: return to the vehicle (a metal vehicle offers partial Faraday cage protection), stay inside, do not use handheld electronics. Do not shelter under isolated trees — they attract strikes.
02 The San People 100,000 Years · Oldest Culture on Earth · 2006 Land Rights

The San people of the Kalahari carry the oldest continuous cultural lineage on Earth. Genetic analysis published in major research journals indicates that the San lineage diverges from all other human populations approximately 100,000–150,000 years ago — meaning they have been present in this landscape since anatomically modern humans first emerged in Africa. Every other culture on Earth descends from a population that later diverged from theirs. They are not a remnant or a fragment; they are the root.

The San are hunter-gatherers of extraordinary competence. They tracked prey across the Kalahari using the persistence hunting method — following an animal at a trot for hours in the heat, reading tracks and predicting behaviour, until the prey collapsed from heat exhaustion. They located water by finding tsamma melons, following gemsbok trails to buried moisture, and digging at specific geological signs. They navigated 900,000 km² without maps or compass. Their pharmacopoeia — derived entirely from Kalahari plants — included treatments for pain, infection, and snakebite that are still being studied by researchers today.

Genetic Divergence
~100,000 Years
The San lineage separates from all other human populations approximately 100,000–150,000 years ago. The oldest continuously inhabited cultural tradition on Earth. They have occupied this landscape since before the Kalahari dunes were laid down.
Current Population
~90,000
Across southern Africa. Approximately 55,000 in Botswana (including Basarwa/Bushmen), 27,000 in Namibia (including ‡Khomani San), and others in SA, Zimbabwe, Zambia. Most no longer live as traditional hunter-gatherers.
2006 CKGR Land Ruling
High Court Victory
Botswana High Court ruled in 2006 that the forced relocation of CKGR San communities was unlawful and unconstitutional. The ruling affirmed their right to return to ancestral land inside the park. Implementation has been partial and contested. The CKGR today is both a wildlife reserve and contested indigenous homeland.
Cultural Encounters
Proceed with Care
Touristic San "experiences" vary enormously in authenticity and benefit. The best encounters are community-run and involve fair compensation. Avoid operations where San people are presented as exhibits. New Xade community (CKGR relocation village) and D'Kar village near Ghanzi both have legitimate community-run visitor programmes.

The 2006 Botswana High Court ruling on the CKGR is one of the most significant indigenous land rights decisions in African legal history. The court found that the government's removal of the San from the CKGR between 1997 and 2002 was an unconstitutional deprivation of their rights. Despite the ruling, the reality on the ground remains complex: access to the reserve for CKGR San residents is bureaucratically difficult, and most San communities remain in resettlement villages like New Xade outside the park boundary. When travelling through the CKGR, you are on land that has been inhabited and tracked by human beings for an unbroken 100,000+ years. That is not a romantic abstraction; it is a geological and genetic fact.

04 Zone 1 — Kgalagadi Transfrontier Park South Africa + Botswana · Black-Maned Lions · Wilderness Trails
ZONE 1 Kgalagadi Transfrontier Park 3.6M ha · SA + BWA · Upington Gateway

The Kgalagadi Transfrontier Park is the southern anchor of the Kalahari ecosystem — 3.6 million hectares of red dunes, dry riverbeds (Nossob and Auob), and open scrub spanning the Northern Cape of South Africa and southwestern Botswana. It is the most accessible Kalahari destination from South Africa, the most photographed, and in many ways the definitive Kalahari experience: red dunes against blue sky, enormous black-maned lions lying in camel thorn shade, leopards scanning from tree branches, and a silence that requires no description to those who have heard it.

The park was officially proclaimed in 2000 as a transfrontier conservation area, unifying the former Gemsbok National Park (Botswana, proclaimed 1938) and the South African Kalahari Gemsbok National Park (proclaimed 1931). There are no internal fences — animals migrate freely across what was once an international border. The concept of a "transfrontier" park, allowing wildlife to move according to ecological rather than political logic, was pioneered here before it became common practice elsewhere in Africa.

Total Park Area
3.6 Million ha
SA side: ~1 million ha. Botswana side (Gemsbok NP): ~2.5 million ha. No internal fences — one unified ecosystem.

05 Zone 2 — Mabuasehube SW Botswana · Remotest Kalahari Camping · DWNP + Intertourism
ZONE 2 Mabuasehube Pan Complex Botswana · DWNP / Intertourism · Tsabong Gateway

Mabuasehube is the Botswana side of the Kgalagadi Transfrontier Park — the same ecosystem as the SA section but without the infrastructure. Where the SA side has paved approach roads, marked tracks, and three main camps, Mabuasehube has soft sand tracks, six isolated pan campsites, and essentially no services. This is genuine wilderness camping: no fences, no neighbours, no phone signal, no help. What it offers in exchange is an experience of the Kalahari that is impossible to find on the SA side — complete solitude, lion-dominated pans, and night skies that have no competition in southern Africa.

Access
Tsabong
Gateway town in far SW Botswana. ~130 km of sand track from Tsabong to the Mabuasehube entrance. Fuel in Tsabong (confirm availability before departure — small town). The approach road is deep Kalahari sand; 4×4 from Tsabong.
From SA (Kgalagadi)
Via Twee Rivieren
Cross from Kgalagadi into Botswana at the Two Rivers/Mabuasehube gate — you exit SANParks jurisdiction and enter DWNP. The gate hours and procedures must be confirmed with DWNP before relying on this crossing.
Fee (SADC)
BWP 200 / pax / day
Standard DWNP rate for non-CKGR parks. Vehicle: BWP 90/day. Campsite fees paid separately to Intertourism Group for privately managed sites.
Key Pans
6 Major Pans
Mabuasehube, Lesholoago, Monamodi, Mpayathutlwa, Khiding, Bosobogolo. Each has a basic campsite. Mabuasehube Pan is the most visited; Bosobogolo the most remote. All are lion and brown hyena territory.
Mabuasehube — two-vehicle rule The Mabuasehube area is one of the most isolated vehicle-accessible destinations in southern Africa. A mechanical failure here — even a flat tyre requiring a jack in soft sand — can become a serious situation rapidly. Two vehicles strongly recommended. Satellite communicator mandatory. Inform someone outside the park of your entry and expected exit.
06 Zone 3 — Khutse Game Reserve Southern CKGR Gateway · 240 km from Gaborone · BigFoot Tours
ZONE 3 Khutse Game Reserve Botswana · DWNP / BigFoot Tours · Letlhakeng Gateway

Khutse Game Reserve is contiguous with the southern boundary of the CKGR — the same sand, the same predators, the same silence — but only 240 km from Gaborone, making it the most accessible genuine Kalahari experience for visitors entering from South Africa. It is less dramatically remote than the CKGR interior but operationally more forgiving: closer to help, shorter drives between campsites, and slightly less demanding sand.

The classic trans-Kalahari route enters at Khutse's southern gate (from Letlhakeng) and exits at CKGR's Matswere Gate in the northeast, traversing the full north-south length of both reserves — approximately 500 km of Kalahari sand over 4–6 days. This is one of the definitive overland routes in southern Africa and currently has no single reference document that maps it end to end. This guide is that reference.

2,500 km²
Contiguous with CKGR to the north — combined protected area approaches 55,000 km². Wildlife moves freely between both reserves.

Letlhakeng
~190 km west of Gaborone. Small town — basic fuel and provisions. Last services before entering Khutse. Fill completely here.
Campsites

All five managed by BigFoot Tours. Khutse 1–3 near the entrance pan cluster; Khutse 4 and 5 further north toward CKGR boundary. Basic facilities — bush toilets, no water.
Wildlife

Lion, cheetah, brown hyena, black-backed jackal, bat-eared fox. Gemsbok, springbok, hartebeest in numbers on the pans. Brown hyena encounters at campsites are common after dark.
07 Zone 4 — Central Kalahari Game Reserve 52,800 km² · Most Isolated Vehicle-Accessible Park · Black-Maned Lions
ZONE 4 Central Kalahari Game Reserve Botswana · DWNP / BigFoot Tours · Larger than Switzerland

The Central Kalahari Game Reserve is the most remote vehicle-accessible national park in southern Africa and one of the largest in the world. At 52,800 km² it is larger than Switzerland. The interior is accessible only by 4×4 on deep sand tracks; there are no sealed roads, no permanent water, no fuel depots, and no cell towers. A breakdown in the deep interior could mean 24–48 hours before any help could realistically arrive.

What the CKGR offers in exchange for this inaccessibility is extraordinary: the Kalahari in its most complete, least compromised form. The black-maned lions of the Deception Valley are the most studied wild lion population in Africa after Serengeti — their manes grow unusually long and dark due to the interaction of high testosterone levels and the relatively cool nights at this latitude and altitude. Seeing a coalition of five black-maned males on the open valley floor at sunrise is one of the genuinely unrepeatable wildlife experiences in the world.

Area
52,800 km²
Larger than Switzerland. One of the largest protected areas in the world. The interior is as close to unmodified Kalahari as exists anywhere on Earth.
Water
Zero Inside Park
There is no water anywhere inside the CKGR. No waterholes (except seasonal pans after rain), no boreholes accessible to visitors, no camp water supply. Carry minimum 5L/person/day plus 20L vehicle reserve.
Fuel
Zero Inside Park
No fuel inside the CKGR or within 200+ km radius. Matswere Gate (east): last fuel at Rakops, ~95 km from gate. Xade Gate (west): last fuel at Ghanzi, ~170 km from gate. Plan fuel for the full interior circuit plus 20% buffer.
Sand Consumption
14–15 L/100km
Deep Kalahari sand significantly increases 1HZ fuel consumption. Plan at 14–15L/100km for all CKGR driving. A 350 km Deception Valley loop requires minimum 53L on top of gate-to-gate distance.
Gates
4 Gates
Matswere (NE, main entry — from Rakops); Xade (W — from Ghanzi); Tsau (NW); Southern Gate (connects to Khutse GR). Most overlanders enter via Matswere and exit via same or Xade for a loop.
Fee (SADC)
BWP 200 / pax / day
DWNP SADC rate (2026). Vehicle: BWP 90/day. Campsite fees separate to BigFoot Tours — approximately BWP 500–700/night for 2 people SADC. Cheaper than Chobe/Moremi but still significant over a multi-day circuit.

CKGR: The minimum-two-vehicle rule The Botswana DWNP does not formally mandate two vehicles, but the reality of the terrain makes a solo vehicle the wrong decision in the CKGR interior. — Wildlife Corridors & Seasonal MovementMigration · Green-Up · Corridor Integrity

The Kalahari wildlife movements are driven by a single variable: where the grass has greened after rain. In the dry season, animals concentrate at permanent water — the Boteti River, the Nossob/Auob waterholes, and boreholes. When the rains arrive (November–April), they disperse across the sand sea to follow the flush of annual grasses. Understanding this pattern tells you where to look at any given time of year.

Zebra Migration
Nov – Apr
25,000–30,000 Burchell's zebra move from Makgadikgadi pans eastward to the Boteti River following seasonal rains — the second-largest zebra migration in Africa. In the dry season they concentrate on the Boteti. Green-up pulls them back onto the pans. Plan visit around which phenomenon you want to see — they are opposite seasonal events.
Gemsbok Dispersal
All Year
Gemsbok are resident throughout all Kalahari zones and do not migrate in the conventional sense — they range freely across hundreds of kilometres following grass. After rain, herds of 200–500 appear on open pans that were empty a week before. Their presence on a pan is a reliable indicator of safe crust strength.
Corridor: CKGR ↔ Makgadikgadi
Unfenced Connection
The CKGR's northern boundary and the Makgadikgadi ecosystem are connected by an unfenced wildlife corridor through the Nxai Pan NP buffer zone. Lion, cheetah, and wild dog move freely between the two. This corridor is one of the longest remaining unfenced wildlife connections in southern Africa — its integrity depends on the cattle farms along its edges remaining undeveloped.
Fencing Threat
Ongoing Pressure
The veterinary cordon fencing (Buffalo Fence) and private farm fencing progressively fragment the Kalahari. Fence removal is active conservation work in several areas. When you see a fence line across a sand track, this is typically a DWNP/veterinary boundary — crossing without authority is an offence in Botswana. Check your route for fence crossings before entering.
— Tsamma Melon: The Kalahari Survival PlantField Identification · Context

The tsamma melon (Citrullus lanatus) — the wild ancestor of the domestic watermelon — is the Kalahari's most important survival plant for both wildlife and humans. It grows in sandy soil across the southern and central Kalahari, producing fruits 15–30cm across that are approximately 90% water. San people historically used tsamma as their primary water source during dry-season hunts, estimating up to 2L of liquid per large fruit. The rind is bitter; the flesh and seeds are both edible. In the CKGR and Kgalagadi, you will see tsamma vines growing across dune flanks and open areas from January onwards. They are a reliable indicator that animals are finding moisture here, even when no surface water is visible. Do not harvest tsamma in any national park — it is a protected plant and a critical wildlife water source.

11 Kalahari Wildlife Guide Signature Species · By Zone

The Kalahari wildlife list is long but defined by a set of signature species that cannot be reliably seen anywhere else. The black-maned lion and the brown hyena are the two most uniquely Kalahari — both found elsewhere, but nowhere in the concentration, accessibility, and distinctiveness that the Kalahari provides.

Kalahari Black-Maned Lion

★★★★★ SIGNATURE SPECIES · ZONES 1, 2, 4

The males of the CKGR and Kgalagadi lion populations grow unusually long, dark manes — the result of high testosterone levels and the cooler night temperatures at this latitude. This makes them visually distinctive from any other lion population in Africa. The mechanism is well understood: cooler temperatures allow the mane to grow longer without the heat penalty that reduces mane size in equatorial populations. Best sightings: Deception Valley (CKGR) and Nossob Valley (Kgalagadi) early morning and late afternoon.

Brown Hyena

★★★★★ GLOBALLY RARE · ZONES 1, 2, 4

The brown hyena is globally rare — total wild population estimated at fewer than 10,000. The Kalahari holds the world's highest density. Nocturnal and solitary, they are difficult to find elsewhere; in the CKGR and Kgalagadi they reliably visit campsites after dark and patrol pan edges at dusk. Distinguishable from spotted hyena by the shaggy brown coat and relatively small size. An encounter with a brown hyena is one of Africa's rarest wildlife sightings — the Kalahari makes it routine.

Gemsbok (Oryx)

★★★★★ ABUNDANT · ALL ZONES

The gemsbok is the Kalahari's defining ungulate. Perfectly adapted to arid conditions — the nasal blood-cooling system allows core body temperature to be managed independently of ambient heat; they can survive on the moisture in vegetation alone. Groups of 40–100 animals are common on open pans; solitary bulls patrol the dunescapes. The long straight horns, black-and-white facial mask, and grey body make them one of Africa's most visually striking animals. Herds on the red dunes of Kgalagadi at sunset are among the great photography subjects of southern Africa.

Cheetah

★★★★ GOOD SIGHTINGS · ZONES 1, 3, 4

The open pan and valley floors of both Kgalagadi and the CKGR provide ideal cheetah hunting terrain. The flat visibility allows cheetahs to hunt by sight rather than ambush — early morning and late afternoon are the active periods. Coalition males (2–3 brothers) range large territories in Kgalagadi; solitary females with cubs occasionally den near campsites. The Kalahari has a healthy cheetah population — sightings are frequent by African safari standards.

Springbok

★★★★★ ABUNDANT · ZONES 1, 2, 3, 4

The Kalahari's most numerous antelope — herds of 200–500 are common throughout all zones. Famous for pronking (the stiff-legged vertical bouncing behaviour), which functions as both predator-confusion and display. The springbok's white dorsal crest is raised during pronking. In wet seasons, large herds congregate on greening plains; in the dry season they concentrate near remaining vegetation. Excellent cheetah prey — watching a cheetah chase through a springbok herd is one of the Kalahari's most dramatic sequences.

Black-Footed Cat

★★★ NOCTURNAL · SOUTH KALAHARI

The smallest wild cat in Africa — and pound-for-pound one of the most effective predators on Earth, with the highest kill rate of any wild cat species (approximately 60% of hunts successful, vs lion ~20%). Found across the southern Kalahari, particularly in the Kgalagadi area. Nocturnal and secretive — spotlight drives after dark are the best opportunity. Rarely seen but reliably present if you spend nights in the right habitat. The CKGR also has records but southern Kgalagadi has the highest confirmed density.

Meerkat (Suricate)

★★★★★ ACCESSIBLE · MAKGADIKGADI EDGE

Meerkats are present throughout the Kalahari but the most famous habituated groups are near the Makgadikgadi pans (Planet Baobab / Jack's Camp area). The habituation process means meerkats treat humans as elevated vantage points — they will stand on your head to scan for raptors at dawn. This behaviour is instinctive, not trained; you are being used because you are tall. Outside the habituated groups, meerkats are common around pan edges and in scrubby dune areas throughout the CKGR.

Sociable Weaver

★★★★★ UNMISSABLE · ALL ZONES

The sociable weaver builds the largest communal bird nests in the world — massive haystacks up to 7 metres long and 2 metres high in camel thorn acacia trees, housing hundreds of nesting pairs in a thermal-regulated apartment block. A single nest can be maintained by the same colony for 100+ years. They are everywhere in the Kalahari and unmissable — but pause to look closely: pygmy falcon (Africa's smallest raptor) nests parasitically in sociable weaver colonies, and cape cobra regularly raids the outer chambers. The nest is a food web in a tree.

Cape Cobra & Puff Adder

⚠ HAZARD · ALL ZONES

Both species are present throughout the Kalahari. The puff adder is the cause of more snakebite fatalities in Africa than any other species — slow-moving, cryptically coloured, and reluctant to move when stepped on. In camps: shake boots every morning, check sleeping gear, watch where you put your hands on rocks and logs. Cape cobras are more alert and active — particularly common near sociable weaver nests (they raid them regularly). Antivenom is not available inside any Kalahari park; serious envenomation requires evacuation.

Many community members have had their images used commercially without benefit — they have every reason to be cautious. Monuments & Relics Act — Botswana 2001 Any archaeological object found in Botswana — including San tools, ostrich eggshell beads, bones, pottery, or rock art — is the property of the state. Removing, collecting, or disturbing any such object is a criminal offence. If you find something unusual: photograph it in situ, record the GPS coordinate, and report it to DWNP or the National Museum of Botswana on exit. Do not move it.

15 Water & Fuel Self-Sufficiency Matrix Zone-by-Zone · No Assumptions

The Kalahari is unique among southern African overland destinations in that water and fuel planning are not optional refinements — they are the primary survival calculus. This matrix covers all six zones. Use it before committing to any interior circuit.

Water: minimum 5L per person per day in all Kalahari zones This is not comfortable-margin water. It is survival-minimum in 35–45°C conditions, accounting for drinking, basic hygiene, and cooking. 17 Environmental Issues & Conservation What is Threatened · What Overlanders Can Do

The Kalahari is under pressure from several directions simultaneously. Understanding the issues makes you a better-informed visitor — and informs decisions about where you camp, what you do with waste, and which operators you support.

— Key Conservation Pressures
Veterinary Fencing
Migration Fragmentation
The Buffalo Fence and other DWNP veterinary cordon fences block historical wildlife migration routes across the Kalahari. In the 1980s, wildebeest migrations of 250,000+ animals moved freely between the CKGR and the Okavango — the fences ended this. Active conservation debate: fencing protects the critical cattle industry (Botswana's second-largest export) but fragments ecosystem connectivity. Fence removal is ongoing in some areas but contested by ranchers.
Overgrazing — Park Margins
Edge Degradation
Communal cattle grazing on park boundary areas creates heavily degraded vegetation strips 5–15 km wide around CKGR, Kgalagadi, and Khutse. This is visible on satellite imagery as brown bands adjacent to park fences. The degraded strips reduce buffer zone function and push wildlife further into park interiors.
Pan Desiccation
Climate Change Signal
The Makgadikgadi pan system is showing increased desiccation stress — the Boteti River has experienced longer dry-season interruptions over the past 20 years. The Okavango inflow (which feeds the Boteti via the Thamalakane River) is sensitive to upstream catchment rainfall in Angola. Angola deforestation → reduced catchment yield → reduced Okavango inflow → reduced Boteti flow → reduced zebra migration extent.
Rhino Poaching
Botswana — Active Threat
Botswana experienced a sharp rise in rhino poaching 2018–2022 that coincided with reduced anti-poaching enforcement under a changed government policy. Khama Rhino Sanctuary and private rhino custodians are active. If you see any suspicious activity near a rhino sanctuary: do not intervene, record details and report immediately to Botswana Police (tel: 999) or DWNP anti-poaching hotline (+267 391 2354).
Off-Road Impact
Tyre Tracks Persist
A vehicle track in Kalahari sand persists for decades — the compaction alters drainage and vegetation recovery for 20+ years. In all Kalahari parks: drive only on established tracks. Do not create new tracks to reach a campsite or a better photography position. This applies to both park rules and basic field ethics.
Light Pollution
Currently Minimal
The CKGR interior has some of the darkest skies in the southern hemisphere — Milky Way core visibility is extraordinary. Maintain this by using red-light torches at camp (not white light), keeping vehicle headlights off when stationary, and using low-intensity lanterns rather than floodlights. The darkness is part of the experience.
— What Overlanders Can Do
IssueField Action
Track creationNever leave established tracks inside any park. If you find a track ends or is unclear, return to the last known junction and reassess — do not push into untracked sand to create a shortcut.
FirewoodDo not collect firewood inside national parks — it is illegal in all Kalahari parks. Bring charcoal or a gas stove. Dead wood in the Kalahari is habitat for insects, aardvark, and small mammals.
WasteCarry out all waste. Human waste: cat holes minimum 15cm deep, minimum 100m from any pan, track, or water source. Toilet paper: burn or carry out — it does not decompose in the Kalahari's dry conditions.
Wildlife disturbanceDo not approach lion, cheetah, or wild dog on foot inside any Kalahari park. Do not follow a cheetah hunt in progress — vehicle pursuit forces the animal to abort hunts and is a documented conservation problem in Kgalagadi. Observe from a stationary position.
San community benefitSupport community-run operations at D'Kar and New Xade rather than commercial operators who use San cultural imagery without community benefit. Buy direct from San craftspeople where possible.
ReportingRecord and report: injured wildlife (DWNP), poaching activity (DWNP anti-poaching +267 391 2354 / Botswana Police 999), illegal off-road driving by other visitors (park gate on exit), abandoned or damaged infrastructure.

San languages — appreciate the context, don't attempt the words The San languages of the Kalahari use click consonants (represented as !, ǀ, ǁ, ‡ in IPA transcription) that require years of practice to produce correctly. Attempting them without training produces garbled sounds that may be interpreted as mockery.
