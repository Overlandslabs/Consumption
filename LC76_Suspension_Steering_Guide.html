Suspension & Steering Architecture

The HZJ76R-RKMRS rides on solid axles at both ends. The front is coil sprung on twin leading arms with a panhard rod for lateral location and a front stabiliser bar. The rear is leaf sprung — multi-leaf packs clamped to the axle with U-bolts, pinned at the front eye and shackled at the rear, locating the axle fore-aft, laterally, and vertically from the leaves alone. No rear control arms, no panhard, no A-frame. Steering is hydraulically-assisted recirculating ball, with a single drag link from the pitman arm to the right knuckle and a tie rod between the knuckles. Everything is mechanical, repairable, and inspectable with hand tools — the same defining strength as the engine.

Why this matters in Africa: Solid axles with coil front and leaf rear are the most field-serviceable suspension layout on any modern touring vehicle. Every bush, every shackle, every leaf can be inspected with a torch and a pry bar, and most can be replaced with hand tools. But at 307,000 km, the original components are well past their design life — what is on the truck now is the second or third generation of nearly everything that wears. This guide is structured around that reality.
Mechanical simplicity is both shield and liability. A 76 will keep going with a leaking shock, a torn bush, a missing U-bolt nut, or a wallowing steering box — and the handling degrades gradually enough that the driver normalises it without noticing. The failure point then moves to the next weak link. The whole point of inspection is to catch creep before it becomes a roadside event. Trust the tape measure, the torque wrench, and the pry bar — not the seat of your pants.

Rear track is 95 mm narrower than front. Every 76 SW drives a wheel path where the rear wheels track inside the front. On cambered roads this shifts load distribution slightly, and in mud or deep sand the rear wheels do not run in the front ruts. It is a known characteristic and not a fault — do not try to diagnose it as wear. Options for narrowing the gap (offset rims, spacers, TrueTrac) are tracked in the Reference Card but not fitted.
Suspension & Steering Components

Each component, its role, its dominant wear mode, and where it sits in the system. Tags: ACTIVE works under load, PASSIVE structural, WEAR consumable, PLANNED upgrade or future replacement.

Front Suspension
Front Coils — OME 859 ACTIVE
OME Medium-rate coils, 400 kg constant load. Matched to a permanently-loaded touring 76 with bullbar and winch — stiffer than stock but not harsh unloaded. Sag over time is the main wear mode: measure ride height annually and compare against baseline, not against "looks right." A single sagged or broken coil shows as one low corner.
Front Shocks — OME Nitrocharger+ N181 WEAR
Fitted April 2023 at 221,000 km (G-Tec). At 307,000 km they have ~86,000 km of mixed touring on them — inside useful life but approaching the half-way mark of a realistic touring envelope. Heat fade on long corrugations is the single most common touring failure: the shock still moves but stops damping. Replace as a pair, never individually.
Front Leading Arms L & R PASSIVE
Twin leading arms locate the front axle fore-aft and resist brake torque. The arms themselves rarely fail; the bushes at each end (chassis and axle) are the wear item. The chassis-end support bracket on the front diff housing was plated and welded in June 2025 (Suspension City, Conrad) after historic stress cracking — that repair is a specific inspection point on every wheel-off service.
Leading Arm Bushes WEAR
Four bushes total (two per arm). Crush, split, and dry out with age and heat. Lower-arm control bushes replaced during the 2023 front knuckle/hub work at 245,370 km. Pry test each bush individually; carry a spare kit as a bush spare. Worn bushes give vague steering, brake-dive that takes the axle with it, and clunks over speed bumps.
Front Panhard Rod PASSIVE
Single bar from chassis to axle that locates the front axle laterally. With a 2" lift the panhard angle shifts the axle slightly off-centre — usually accepted. Bushes at both ends are wear items; the rod itself is structural and should be inspected for bending after any underbody impact.
Front Panhard Bushes WEAR
The bush at each end of the panhard rod takes the full lateral load of the front axle. When they wear, the front end "walks" laterally over corrugations and on cambered roads. Pry test with a bar between rod and bracket — any visible movement is too much.
Front Stabiliser Bar ACTIVE
Anti-roll bar across the front axle, reducing body roll in corners. The bar itself is structural; the bushes and end links are the wear items. Front stabiliser bush kit replaced April 2025 at 273,023 km (PW Auto Centre). Knocks over small bumps when the bushes wear — listen for metallic chatter from the front end on rough roads.
King Pins (upper & lower) ACTIVE
Tapered roller / trunnion bearings that carry steering pivot loads at the top and bottom of each swivel hub. Preload shim-adjustable. Symptoms of wear: vague centre, knock when going over a bump while turning, uneven tyre wear across the front. Front knuckle/spindle overhaul kit fitted February 2025 at 271,200 km (Cape Transmissions) — reset the baseline.
Swivel Hub / Swivel Ball PASSIVE
The chrome swivel ball is the spherical surface the inner seal rides on. Must be smooth and uncorroded — pitting destroys the inner seal in short order and diff oil migrates into the swivel housing or out onto the brake disc. Inspect for rust, pitting, and seal track wear at every wheel-off service.
Swivel Hub Seal / Sideshaft Seal WEAR
Felt-and-rubber seal keeping swivel hub oil in and dust out. Left-front sideshaft seal has been a recurring problem — replaced August 2025 at 278,675 km (Northam Toyota) and gasket replaced again January 2026 at 293,949 km (PW Auto Centre). Inspect left front inside wheel and brake disc for oil weep at every service. Cross-reference R20 Seal & Gasket Atlas for part numbers; carry a seal kit as a bush spare.
Rear Suspension
Rear Leaf Packs — OME CS058R ACTIVE
Multi-leaf packs clamped to the axle with U-bolts. Carry load, provide springing, AND locate the axle fore-aft and laterally — there is no separate panhard or control arm to share the load. Fitted January 2026 at 294,500 km (4×4 Megaworld) after a broken leaf on the original pack. The entire rear suspension state is effectively baselined to that service date.
Rear Shocks — OME Nitrocharger+ 63111 WEAR
Fitted January 2026 at 294,500 km with the new leaf packs. Rear shocks take a harder life than front shocks on a touring 76 because the rear carries the heavier constant load and runs hotter on corrugations. A failed rear shock shows as porpoising over undulations, "skipping" rear end on washboard, and accelerated rear tyre wear.
U-Bolts ×4 PASSIVE
Four U-bolts per side (two per leaf pack) clamp the pack to the axle. New U-bolts fitted January 2026 with the new leaf packs. These are critical — a loose U-bolt lets the axle shift on the spring pack under load and can destroy the leaf pack within a single heavy trip. Re-torque after the first 500 km of trip use, then at every 5,000 km interval for the first 20,000 km, then at every service.
Greasable Shackle Bushes WEAR
Greasable shackle bushes fitted January 2026. Unlike standard rubber bushes, these have zerks and must be field-greased at every service interval — this is the whole point of the greasable design. Last regreased March 2026 at 296,000 km (Suspension City) after a squeak developed. Squeaks = add to the grease schedule, do not ignore.
Front Spring Eye & Hanger PASSIVE
Fixed mount — the front of each leaf pack is pinned to a chassis hanger. No moving parts in normal service, but the pin wears and the hanger can crack under extreme loads. Inspect the hanger-to-chassis weld, the pin head and nut, and the bush inside the spring eye at every wheel-off service.
Leaf Pack Centre Bolt PASSIVE
The centre bolt passes through every leaf in the pack and locates the pack on the axle spring pad. If the centre bolt shears or the nut loosens, the pack can walk on the axle and alter rear-end geometry. Inspect visually for a snug head and nut at every service — any gap or rust-streak between leaves near the bolt head means it has moved.
Stone Guard Kit PASSIVE
Fitted January 2026 with the new leaf packs. Protects the leaf pack from stone strikes — on African gravel, a stone hit to a leaf can chip the leaf surface and start a stress crack that grows into a broken leaf over thousands of km. Inspect guard for damage after any significant gravel run.
Axle Bump Stops PASSIVE
Rubber stops that limit upward axle travel and protect the shock body from over-compression. Inspect for cracking, splitting, and missing pieces at every wheel-off service. With the 2" lift, bump stop clearance has shifted from standard — any further loss from spring sag accelerates bump-stop impact on the shock.
Steering
Steering Box (recirc. ball) ACTIVE
Hydraulically assisted recirculating ball box bolted to the chassis. Internal slack accumulates with km and corrugation. Adjustment screw on top can take up some slack, but only after confirming no internal damage — over-adjustment binds the box on lock. Most 76s past 250,000 km benefit from a recon or replacement eventually. Symptom: dead spot at centre, vague return-to-centre. Free play was adjusted at 202,066 km (Toyota Malelane) — next adjustment or recon to be assessed as slack creeps back.
Pitman Arm PASSIVE
Steel arm splined to the steering box output shaft, connecting the box to the drag link. Rarely fails, but the splines can loosen under repeated heavy loads and the taper at the drag link end must be confirmed tight. Check pitman nut torque at every wheel-off service.
Drag Link ACTIVE
Single rod from the pitman arm to the right knuckle steering arm. Two ball joints, one each end, both critical and both wearing. A worn drag link end is the most common cause of "death wobble" precursor symptoms on the 76. Carry one spare drag link assembly as a bush spare, or at minimum the ball joint ends.
Drag Link Ends (×2) WEAR
Sealed ball joints at each end of the drag link. Test by gripping the link near the joint and trying to rock it vertically — any perceptible movement is failure. Boot torn? The grease is gone and the joint is on borrowed time. These are the highest-priority steering wear items on a high-mileage 76.
Tie Rod ACTIVE
Rod between the two front knuckles, sets toe and transmits steering input from the right knuckle to the left. Two ball joints (one per end), both wear items. A bent tie rod from rock impact is a common fault on hard tracks — inspect for straightness after any underbody hit. Tie rod end free play was adjusted at 202,066 km (Toyota Malelane) — baseline for future wear tracking.
Tie Rod Ends (×2) WEAR
Same construction and same test as drag link ends. Tear in the boot is a fast countdown to failure. Carry tie rod ends as bush spares — these are simple roadside replacements with a tie rod end puller.
Power Steering Pump ACTIVE
Engine-driven hydraulic pump that feeds the steering box. Failure modes: belt slip (replace belt at intervals), seal leaks (check reservoir level monthly), pump whine (low fluid, air ingress, or pump end-of-life). Without power assist the 76 is steerable but heavy at low speed.
Steering Damper WEAR
Horizontal damper between axle and tie rod (or drag link, depending on fitment) that absorbs road feedback and damps oscillation. Critical for predictable steering on corrugation. Test off the truck: should resist movement firmly in both directions. Replace if it moves under hand pressure.
Wear & Failure Modes — 76 Series at 300,000+ km

What fails first on a touring 76 living on African roads, ranked by frequency and consequence. Risk tags reflect the cost of ignoring the issue.

Leaf Spring Breakage (Rear)
HIGH
The failure mode that took out the original rear pack in January 2026. A chipped leaf surface starts a stress crack that grows slowly until the leaf shears. Usually one of the main leaves, usually under load, usually on corrugation. The truck remains drivable but geometry is compromised and the remaining leaves carry double load. Stone guard kit reduces the risk; visual inspection of leaf edges and faces confirms the state. Onset: variable · Current pack: fresh as of Jan 2026 @ 294,500 km
U-Bolt Loosening
HIGH
New U-bolts stretch slightly on first load cycles and must be re-torqued after the first 500 km of hard use. If this is missed, the pack walks on the axle, centre bolt shears, and the axle shifts. Early symptom is a fresh rust-streak around the U-bolt base; confirmed with a torque wrench. First re-torque is a non-negotiable item for any new leaf fitment. First re-torque: 500 km after fitment · Then every 5,000 km until 20,000 km stable
Shock Fade & Heat-Cycle Failure
HIGH
OME shocks work hard on a loaded touring rig, and long African corrugation runs heat-cycle them past their design envelope. A faded shock still moves but no longer damps — the axle skips, tyres wear, the truck "floats" over undulations. Replace as a pair, axle by axle, never individually. Front pair is from April 2023 (~86,000 km on them); rear pair is fresh (Jan 2026). Touring life: 60,000–100,000 km depending on terrain and load
Drag Link & Tie Rod End Wear
HIGH
Sealed ball joints with grease for life — and on a 76, "life" is much shorter than the rest of the steering hardware. Worn ends are the most common cause of vague centre, "death wobble" precursor symptoms, and a steering wheel that doesn't quite return to centre. Carry spares; replacement is a roadside job with a separator. Touring life: 80,000–120,000 km · Boot tear = immediate end of life
Leaf Pack Bush Wear (Front Eye & Shackles)
MED
Front spring eye bush and shackle bushes accumulate wear. With greasable shackles fitted Jan 2026, the shackle end is maintainable — grease at every service, and any squeak means "add grease," not "replace yet." The front eye bush is a solid bush, non-greasable, and fails silently. Pry test during inspection. Greasable shackles: grease every 5,000 km · Squeak = immediate regrease
King Pin Bearing Slack
MED
King pin trunnion bearings carry steering pivot loads and accumulate slack with km. Symptoms: knock from the front end when turning over a bump, vague centre, accelerated inner-edge tyre wear. Front knuckle/spindle overhaul at 271,200 km (Feb 2025) reset this. Next reset assessment is by symptom, not mileage. Last reset: Feb 2025 @ 271,200 km (Cape Transmissions)
Steering Box Internal Slack
MED
Internal wear in the recirculating ball mechanism produces a dead spot at centre — the wheel turns 5–10° before the front wheels respond. The top adjustment screw can take up some slack, but only on a healthy box; over-adjustment binds the box on full lock. Last free-play adjustment was April 2021 at 202,066 km (Toyota Malelane) — so ~105,000 km since then. Common reset/recon window: 250,000–350,000 km
Left-Front Sideshaft Seal (Recurring)
MED
The left-front sideshaft oil seal has been a repeat issue on this truck — replaced August 2025 at 278,675 km (Northam Toyota) and axle gasket replaced again January 2026 at 293,949 km (PW Auto Centre). Inspect the inside of the left front wheel and disc for oil weep at every wheel-off service. Do not assume the last repair was permanent. History: Aug 2025 (278,675 km) + Jan 2026 (293,949 km)
Coil Spring Sag (Front)
MED
Front coils sag with age and load but typically slower than a rear pack because the front carries less constant mass. First symptom is reduced bump-stop clearance, second is altered steering geometry as front-rear balance shifts. Annual ride-height measurement against baseline is the only reliable way to catch it. Onset: from 100,000 km · Replacement trigger: measured sag >15 mm
Front Stabiliser Bush Wear
LOW
Stabiliser bar bushes and end links clunk over small bumps when worn. Stabiliser bush kit replaced April 2025 at 273,023 km (PW Auto Centre). Easy roadside replacement if a spare kit is carried. Touring life: 50,000–80,000 km
Steering Damper End-of-Life
LOW
Damper fails and the truck develops a wobble or oscillation under braking on rough roads. Cheap to carry as a spare, easy to replace roadside. Test by hand off the truck — should resist movement firmly in both directions. Touring life: 60,000–100,000 km on corrugation-heavy routes
Bump Stop Cracking
LOW
Rubber bump stops crack and crumble with UV exposure and repeated heavy compression. Missing bump stops let the shock take the over-travel load directly — fastest path to a blown shock body. Check at every wheel-off service. Inspection: visual at every brake service
Inspection Methodology

Where, when, and with what tools. Suspension and steering inspection on a high-km 76 is not optional — it is the only way slack stays measured rather than guessed.

Inspection Intervals
IntervalWhat to check
Daily — touringWalk-around: visual on shocks (any new wetness?), leaf packs visible & unbroken, tyre pressures, no obvious sag or lean.
Weekly — touringFresh oil weep at swivels (especially left front), panhard rod and bush visual, drag link and tie rod ends (visual + pry test if anything looks suspect), shackle grease condition.
Every 5,000 kmPry-bar test on all bushes. Pry test on drag link and tie rod ends. Grease the greasable shackles. Ride height check (four corners) against baseline. Steering linkage lubrication (grease nipples).
Every 10,000 kmWheel-off inspection both front: king pin freeplay, swivel seal condition, brake hose chafing. Front-end alignment sanity check against tyre wear pattern. Visual leaf pack inspection — centre bolt, U-bolt tightness, stone guard condition.
Every 20,000 kmFull suspension bolt torque check — including U-bolts, leaf pack hanger bolts, panhard bolts, leading arm bolts, steering box mount, pitman arm nut. Shock inspection for oil weep or body damage.
Annually or pre-tripFull bush, shock, and bearing audit. Replace any bush past visual or pry test. Verify swivel hub oil level. Verify leading arm bracket weld condition on the front diff housing (historic repair point).
Tools Required
  • 1
    Pry bar — long, 600 mm minimum.The single most useful suspension diagnostic tool. Lever between bush and bracket, between rod and chassis, between knuckle and arm. Movement = wear.
  • 2
    Tape measure / steel rule.Ride height measurement against baseline. Record values, do not eyeball.
  • 3
    Torque wrench, 10–200 Nm range + larger for U-bolts.U-bolts and leaf hangers need specific torque figures, not "tight enough." Leaf pack U-bolt torque is critical after any new fitment.
  • 4
    Tie rod end / ball joint separator.Pickle fork or screw-type separator. Carry one. Without it, replacing a tie rod end roadside is misery.
  • 5
    Grease gun + grease cartridges.Greasable shackle bushes need service grease at every 5,000 km interval. Without the gun, the greasable design is pointless.
  • 6
    Wheel chocks & jack stands.Both axles need to be off the ground and secured for any meaningful pry-bar test. Never trust the trolley jack alone.
  • 7
    Torch & mirror.For inspecting bushes and seals you cannot reach by hand. Half the suspension is hidden until you light it up.
The Pry-Bar Bush Test
1
Vehicle on flat ground, axle weighted (wheels on ground or on stands under the axle, not the chassis).
2
Insert the pry bar between the suspect arm or rod and its mounting bracket — chassis end first, axle end second.
3
Apply firm, controlled leverage in the direction the bush is loaded under road use (vertically for leading arms, laterally for the panhard, vertically/laterally for leaf shackles).
4
Watch the bush itself, not the arm. A healthy bush deforms slightly and returns. A worn bush opens visibly, shows daylight, or moves with no resistance.
5
Repeat for every bush in the system. Note any that fail or look marginal. Do not partially replace — buy the kit, do them all.
Ride Height Baseline
Set a baseline now and record it. Measure the distance from the centre of each wheel hub to the corresponding wheel arch lip, with the truck on flat level ground, fuel and water tanks at a known state (full or empty — record which), and no people or kit in the cab. Record all four values to the nearest mm. Re-measure annually or before any departure. A 15 mm drop on either rear corner is a leaf pack wear warning. A drop on one front corner only is a settled coil.
Field Diagnosis — Symptom to Cause

Tap any symptom to expand. Each card lists the likely causes ranked by probability for a 76 at this mileage on African roads, with a recommended check sequence.

Steering Symptoms
Vague centre / dead spot in the steering wheel
CAUTION ▼
  • 1
    Worn drag link or tie rod ends.Most common cause on a 76 at 300k+ km. Pry test each ball joint individually. Movement = replace.
  • 2
    Steering box internal slack.Test: with engine running and wheels straight, ask a helper to rock the steering wheel ±5°. The pitman arm should move with the wheel, not lag behind it.
  • 3
    King pin freeplay.Wheel off, grip top and bottom of tyre, rock vertically. Any perceptible movement = king pin or bearing wear. Knuckle overhaul done Feb 2025 @ 271k — should be tight.
  • 4
    Worn front panhard bushes.Pry between rod and bracket. Lateral movement = bush replacement.
Order of investigation: drag link / tie rod ends first (cheap, quick, common), then king pins, then steering box. Do not adjust the steering box top screw to compensate for ball joint wear — you will mask the real fault and bind the box at full lock.
Steering wheel does not return to centre after a turn
CAUTION ▼
  • 1
    Caster incorrectly set.Historic issue on this truck — originally mis-set by First Alignment (Johan), corrected June 2025 by Conrad at Suspension City. If self-centring degrades, retest caster with a trusted shop. Conrad is the trusted contact.
  • 2
    King pin bearings over-tight or seized.Jack the front off the ground and turn each wheel by hand. Should turn freely with light resistance. Notchy or heavy = king pin issue.
  • 3
    Power steering pump fluid low or air-bound.Check reservoir, top up if needed, bleed by lock-to-lock cycling with engine running.
High-frequency wobble at speed (death wobble precursor)
STOP ▼
Stop driving above the speed where the wobble starts. Solid axle wobble is self-amplifying — once it starts, it does not fade until you slow down. It will damage steering components and can cause loss of control. This is a fault to fix at the next opportunity, not at the next service.
  • 1
    Worn drag link or tie rod end (any one of four).Single most common trigger. Inspect all four ball joints by hand and pry test.
  • 2
    Failed steering damper.Test off the truck — should resist firmly in both directions. Movement under hand pressure = replace.
  • 3
    Out-of-balance front wheel or buckled rim.Inspect rims, check tyre wear pattern, rebalance if any doubt.
  • 4
    Worn front panhard bush or rod.Pry test. Bent rod can develop after a heavy underbody hit — visual check.
  • 5
    Loose pitman arm nut.Torque check at the pitman arm-to-box splines. Loose = immediate retorque.
Heavy / notchy steering at low speed
MONITOR ▼
  • 1
    Power steering fluid low.Check reservoir, top up with correct fluid. Inspect for leaks at pump, hoses, box.
  • 2
    Power steering belt slipping.Visual check, tension check. Replace if glazed or cracked.
  • 3
    King pin preload over-tight.Notchy quality especially at low speed off-road = king pin shim error or seized bearing. Workshop call.
  • 4
    Steering box top screw over-adjusted.If the screw has been wound in to chase a dead spot, it may now be binding the box on lock. Back off slightly and retest.
Truck pulls to one side on a flat road
CAUTION ▼
  • 1
    Tyre pressures unequal L/R.Always check tyre pressures first — cheapest and most common cause.
  • 2
    Caster drift — especially pulling LEFT.This exact symptom is the one that triggered the June 2025 Suspension City repair (original First Alignment castor correction was wrong). Conrad at Suspension City is the trusted workshop for re-checking caster.
  • 3
    Front wheel alignment — toe out of spec.Inspect inner-edge tyre wear pattern. Heavy feathering = toe error.
  • 4
    Brake drag on one front wheel.After a road run, hand-test wheel temperature. Hot one side = sticking caliper or hose.
Suspension Symptoms
Visible broken leaf in the rear pack
STOP ▼
Reduce speed immediately and find a safe place to inspect. A broken leaf does not usually disable the truck, but it shifts the load onto the remaining leaves, alters ride height, and can allow the axle to shift under the pack. Continued hard driving will take out the rest of the pack.
  • 1
    Confirm which leaf is broken and where.Main leaf (top) = critical, drive slowly to workshop. Secondary leaf = reduce speed and distance. Any break = do not load the truck further.
  • 2
    Check U-bolt torque and centre bolt.A broken leaf can have started from a loose U-bolt letting the pack walk on the axle. Retorque before continuing.
  • 3
    Reduce load if possible.Offload water, fuel, non-essential kit if the situation allows. Less load = less stress on the remaining leaves.
  • 4
    Plan the route to a workshop.Avoid corrugation, avoid fast highway speeds. Moderate gravel at reduced speed is usually safer than fast tar.
Skipping / hopping rear axle on washboard
CAUTION ▼
  • 1
    Faded or failed rear shocks.Most common cause on a touring 76. Current rear shocks are fresh (Jan 2026 @ 294,500 km) — if skipping starts within that life, suspect other causes first.
  • 2
    Tyre pressures too high for the surface.Drop rear pressures by 0.2–0.3 bar and re-test before condemning the shocks.
  • 3
    U-bolts loose — pack walking on axle.Early leaf-pack fault. Torque check all four U-bolts immediately.
  • 4
    Shackle bushes worn or dry.Greasable shackles — add grease first, reassess. If squeak persists, inspect bush condition.
Squeak from rear suspension
MONITOR ▼
  • 1
    Dry shackle bushes.This exact fault presented March 2026 at 296,000 km — regreased by Suspension City. Greasable shackles are designed to be field-greased; squeak = grease gun time, not a replacement decision.
  • 2
    Dry front spring eye bush.Non-greasable — if grease does not cure the squeak, the front eye is suspect.
  • 3
    Leaves rubbing dry against each other.Normal on a leaf pack if the silicone/slip pads have dried out. Not a fault, but add it to the inspection note.
Clunk under power on / power off (front or rear)
CAUTION ▼
  • 1
    Worn front leading arm bushes.Most common source on a 76. Pry test all four leading arm bushes (chassis end and axle end).
  • 2
    Loose U-bolt or shackle.Rear-end clunks on torque reversal. Torque-check all four U-bolts and both shackle bolts.
  • 3
    Worn front stabiliser bushes.Usually a metallic chatter rather than a clunk, but worth a pry test. Replaced Apr 2025 @ 273k.
  • 4
    Loose pitman arm nut.Front end clunk under steering load. Immediate retorque.
Cross-check against R13 Drivetrain Training before condemning suspension bushes — a worn UJ or diff input flange can mimic the same clunk.
Truck leans noticeably to one side at rest
CAUTION ▼
  • 1
    Broken leaf (rear) or settled coil (front).Measure ride height all four corners against baseline. The low corner identifies the affected spring.
  • 2
    Loose U-bolt — rear axle shifted on pack.Visual: is the axle still centred on the spring pad? Has the centre bolt moved? Torque-check the U-bolts.
  • 3
    Asymmetric load in the kit.Eliminate before condemning the spring — relocate kit and re-measure.
Porpoising over undulations at speed
MONITOR ▼

    Faded or failed shocks — usually rear first.Classic shock failure symptom. The springs cycle freely with no damping. Current rear shocks are fresh — suspect fronts first (older pair from Apr 2023).
  • 2

Oil weeping down the inside of a front wheel

  • 1
    Failed swivel hub or sideshaft seal — especially left front.Known recurring issue on this truck — left-front sideshaft seal replaced Aug 2025 and axle gasket replaced Jan 2026. Inspect swivel hub oil level and the inside of the brake disc for contamination.
  • 2
    Cracked swivel housing.Rare but happens after impact. Visual inspection.
  • 3
    Brake fluid leak from caliper or hose.Confirm fluid identity — diff oil is dark and gear-smelling, brake fluid is clear/amber and astringent.
Brake disc contaminated with diff oil = no useful front braking on that wheel. Clean disc and pads with brake cleaner, replace the seal at the next opportunity, and reduce expectations of front braking until the seal is replaced.
Visible shock body damage / leak / dent
STOP ▼
A dented shock body cannot be safely used. The shaft will bind or seize at the dent, the seal will fail, and the shock can lock under compression — a serious handling event. Replace the affected shock and its pair as soon as possible. In the field, if no spare is available, run on the remaining three but reduce speed and avoid heavy off-road use.
  • 1
    Underbody impact — rock, log, kerb.Check the shock mounts for cracks at the same time. Any crack = workshop repair before further use.
  • 2
    Seal failure from heat-cycling.Look for fresh oil mist around the seal. Wet shock body with light oil staining = end of useful life.
Post-Impact Inspection Protocol

Run this protocol after any significant underbody hit, deep washout, side impact, or hard landing off a step. Do not rely on "feels OK" — bent and cracked components fail later, not at the moment of the hit.

Definition of "significant impact": any hit hard enough that you said anything out loud, any hit that left a visible mark on the underbody, any hit that altered the steering feel even briefly, any landing off a step where you felt the shocks bottom out, any side scrape against a rock or stump that touched the steering arms or links.
1
Stop in a safe place. Do not push on if anything in the steering feels wrong.
A small change in steering feel after an impact is a signal, not a tolerance. Stop, inspect, decide.
2
Visual inspection — walk around the truck.
Look at all four corners. Anything obviously bent, hanging, leaking, or out of place? Take photos of anything suspect for later comparison.
3
Underbody inspection — get under the truck.
Tie rod and drag link straightness (sight along each rod), panhard rod straight, all four shock bodies for new dents or wetness, all suspension mounts for cracks at the welds, leaf packs unbroken, U-bolts still tight, leaf hangers and shackles intact.
4
Steering box and leading arm bracket inspection.
Steering box bolts to chassis rail — inspect for cracks, deformation, or paint flaking. The leading arm support bracket on the front diff housing is a specific inspection point (historic repair weld from June 2025) — inspect the weld and surrounding metal for fresh cracks. A cracked steering box mount or a cracked leading arm bracket is a stop-and-fix fault.
5
Tyre and rim inspection.
Bent rim? Sidewall damage? Bead seal compromised? A damaged rim from a hard impact often only shows up as a slow leak hours later — mark it now and re-check pressures in the morning.
6
Slow test drive — straight line, light steering inputs.
Find a flat clear area. Drive forward 50 m, brake gently, reverse 20 m, brake gently, turn lock-to-lock at low speed. Listen and feel for new noises, new pulls, new clunks, new vibration.
7
Speed test — incrementally up to road speed.
Build up gradually: 30, 50, 70, 90 km/h. Any new wobble, vibration, or pull = stop and re-inspect. Do not push through a new symptom.
8
Re-check 24 hours later.
Tyre pressures, fresh fluid leaks, shock body weeping, suspension bolts still tight, U-bolt torque. Some impact damage manifests as slow leaks or loosening over a day of driving.
9
Log the event.
Add to the inspection log at the end of this document and to R8 Service History. Record location, terrain, what you hit, what you found, what you did. Future inspections rely on knowing where the truck has been hurt.
Tie rod and drag link are the highest-priority impact items. Both are exposed, both are made from relatively soft tube, both can bend without obviously breaking, and a bent rod alters the toe and self-centring behaviour. Sight along each rod from one end against a known straight reference. If in doubt, remove and check on a flat surface.
Load & Travel Limits

The OME Medium (400 kg constant) kit is rated for the GVM, not above it. Overloading does not just stress the springs — it eats bump stop clearance, accelerates shock fade, alters geometry, and shortens bush life.

GVM (manufacturer)2,850 kg
Kerb mass (estimate)~2,160 kg

Going over GVM is the fastest way to wreck the suspension. Leaf packs sag, U-bolts loosen faster, bump stop clearance disappears, shocks take direct over-travel hits, and bush wear accelerates. There is no safe margin above 2,850 kg on this truck — what fits in the budget is the budget. Cross-reference R11 Packing Optimisation Guide for category-by-category mass control.
What Overloading Looks Like
Reduced bump stop clearance
HIGH
First and most reliable indicator. Measure clearance at all four corners against baseline. If the rear has lost more than 15 mm, the truck is either overloaded or the rear leaves are sagging — both bad, both fixable.
Shock body running hot
HIGH
After a corrugation run, hand-test the shock body — warm is normal, too-hot-to-touch is the shock working past its envelope. Overload accelerates fade. A shock that runs hot under load will not last a long expedition.
U-bolts loosening early
HIGH
New U-bolts on a well-loaded 76 should be stable within 5,000 km. If you are having to re-torque them at 1,000 km intervals, something is wrong — either the load is too high, the leaves have already settled abnormally, or the initial torque was wrong.
Front-end light at speed
MED
If the rear is overloaded, the front lifts, geometry shifts, and steering goes vague at speed. Re-balance the load before re-tuning the suspension.
Bush wear accelerating
MED
Bushes have a service life measured in load-cycles, not just kilometres. Heavy load doubles or triples the cycle load and halves the bush life. Catching this means catching it in inspection — by the time bushes are visibly torn, they have been working overloaded for thousands of km.
Leaf pack sag
MED
Leaf packs settle with load-cycles, not just age. A new pack with a heavy constant load sags faster than a lightly-loaded older pack. The ride height drop is the measurement that matters — not how the rear "looks."
Travel & Bump Stop
With the 2" lift, suspension travel is recalculated against the new ride height. Bump stop clearance and the shock travel envelope are workshop-verified items. Confirm at the next workshop service whether the bump stop spacers (if fitted) and shock lengths are correctly matched to the lift height — mismatched components are the most common cause of premature shock failure on lifted trucks.
Steering Geometry Checks After Heavy Corrugation

Long stretches of corrugation pound the front-end alignment out of spec — toe drifts, components shift on their mounts, and tyre wear changes within hundreds of km if alignment is wrong. After any 500+ km of heavy corrugation, run these checks before the wear becomes expensive.

The Three Geometry Numbers That Matter
SettingWhat it controlsField check
ToeHow parallel the front wheels are. The most likely setting to drift on corrugation. Wrong toe = fastest tyre wear of any geometry error.String-line method or measure between front-wheel rim faces, front and rear of the wheel, at hub height. Difference is the toe value. Compare against baseline.
CasterHow far forward the steering axis tilts. Determines self-centring. Reduced after a lift if no correction was applied. This is the known problem area on this truck — mis-set by First Alignment, corrected June 2025 by Conrad at Suspension City.Workshop measurement only. In the field, the symptom is the test: clean return to centre after a turn = caster acceptable. Pull to the left under cruise = revisit Conrad at Suspension City.
CamberVertical tilt of the wheel. Solid axle 76: camber is fixed by the axle. Should not change.If camber appears wrong on a 76, suspect a bent axle housing or knuckle, not a wandering setting. Workshop call.
Field Toe Check — String Line Method
1
Truck on flat level ground, wheels straight ahead, no driver in the cab.
2
Run a string line along each side of the truck, parallel to the centreline, just touching the front and rear tyres at sidewall mid-height.
3
Measure the gap between the string and the front edge of the front tyre, then the gap to the rear edge of the front tyre, on each side.
4
If the front-edge gap is smaller than the rear-edge gap, the wheel is toed in. If larger, toed out. Both wheels should match, and total toe-in should be within factory spec (a few mm in).
5
If toe is significantly off, suspect the tie rod (bent or moved on its threads). Adjustment is by loosening the tie rod clamps and turning the rod itself — quarter turn at a time, re-measure each time.
The string method is not a workshop alignment. It is a sanity check that catches gross errors before they destroy a set of tyres. For a proper alignment, use a trusted shop: Conrad at Suspension City (Maitland) is the trusted contact after the First Alignment caster correction went wrong in 2025. Any future full geometry work goes through Conrad.
Tyre Wear as a Geometry Indicator
  • 1
    Inner-edge wear, both fronts.Toe-out, or excessive negative caster, or both. Check toe first.
  • 2
    Outer-edge wear, both fronts.Toe-in. Less common after corrugation than toe-out.
  • 3
    Feathering across the tread (sharp-edged ridges).Toe error of either direction — the angle is wrong, the tyre is being scrubbed sideways every metre.
  • 4
    One-sided wear on one wheel only.Bent component on that side — tie rod, knuckle, or steering arm. Visual inspection.
  • 5
    Cupping or scalloping on either front.Failed shock or king pin slack — not a geometry issue. Check shocks and king pins.
Pre-Departure Suspension & Steering Checklist

Run this in full before any major trip. Items here are non-negotiable for a high-km 76 about to take on African roads. Tick nothing on memory — confirm each item physically.

  • 1
    Ride height measured at all four corners and matched against baseline (within 15 mm) Recorded in the inspection log. Any corner low = investigate before departure, do not depart hoping it is fine.
  • 2
    All four shocks visually inspected — no leaks, no dents, no fresh oil mist A shock that is wet around the seal is at end of life, regardless of km. Current fronts are from Apr 2023 (~86k km on them) — under extra scrutiny.
  • 3
    Rear leaf packs visually inspected — all leaves intact, no fresh cracks Walk both sides of the vehicle with a torch. Look at the edges and faces of every leaf, especially the main (top) leaf. Any chip or crack = workshop call before departure.
  • 4
    Leaf pack U-bolts torque-checked to spec — all four Non-negotiable. Loose U-bolt = pack walks on axle = broken leaf. Torque figures confirmed from 4×4 Megaworld fitment spec.
  • 5
    Shackle bushes greased — both sides, greasable zerks Grease gun on each zerk until clean grease emerges. Any squeak after this = further inspection, not more grease.
  • 6
    Leaf pack centre bolts checked — no gap between leaves at the bolt head Visual only — any gap, rust streak, or movement at the centre bolt = the pack has started walking. Workshop call.
  • 7
    Front stone guard kit intact and undamaged Fitted Jan 2026 with the new leaves. Inspect for damage after any heavy gravel run and before any major departure.
  • 8
    Pry-bar test on every front bush — leading arms, panhard, stabiliser Four leading arm bushes (both ends × two arms), two panhard bushes, stabiliser bar bushes and end links.
  • 9
    Drag link and tie rod ends — pry test all four ball joints Boots intact, no movement under hand pressure, no tear or weep. Any failure = replace before departure.
  • 10
    King pin freeplay test — both fronts off the ground Grip top and bottom of tyre, rock vertically. Knuckle overhaul was Feb 2025 @ 271k — should still be tight. Any perceptible movement = workshop call.
  • 11
    Left-front swivel / sideshaft — inspect for oil weep on disc and inside wheel Recurring fault point on this truck. Top up swivel oil if low, check brake disc for contamination.
  • 12
    Leading arm support bracket weld (front diff) inspected Plated and welded June 2025 by Conrad at Suspension City. Inspect the weld and surrounding metal for any new crack or paint flake.
  • 13
    Steering box mounting bolts torqued to spec All bolts at the chassis-rail mount. No fresh paint cracks at the mounting bracket weld.
  • 14
    Pitman arm nut torque verified Loose pitman = catastrophic steering loss. Confirm torque, do not just visually check.
  • 15
    Steering damper functional — hand test off the truck Should resist firmly in both directions through full travel. Replace if it moves under hand pressure.
  • 16
    Power steering fluid level correct, no leaks at pump or box Inspect hoses for chafing or sweating. Test for whine on cold start — air ingress is a developing leak.
  • 17
    Wheel alignment confirmed — caster & toe within factory spec String check minimum for toe. Last alignment: Hi-Q Paarden Eiland (Jerome) — Jan 2026 @ 294,500 km.
  • 18
    Bump stops F & R inspected — no cracks, no missing chunks Visible from underneath at each wheel. Cracked or missing = order replacements before departure.
  • 19
    Bush spares packed — leading arm kit, panhard kit, drag link / tie rod ends, stabiliser bush kit Cross-reference R3 Spares & Tools. Carry the kit, not single bushes — partial replacement is wasted effort.
  • 20
    Tie rod end / ball joint separator tool + grease gun packed Separator with spares, grease gun with consumables. Without these tools the spares kit is useless in the field.
  • 21
    Inspection log set up and reviewed Last entry reviewed, baseline ride height confirmed, any open items closed before departure.
