Upgrades commonly required for a turbocharged 1HZ that this vehicle already has. Included for completeness and cross-reference — no action required unless noted.

Safari Snorkel DONE

Raises the air intake point above the bonnet line. Two critical functions: allows safe wading depth beyond the stock intake position, and draws cleaner air above the dust cloud at wheel level. On a turbocharged engine, unfiltered air at compressor speed acts like a sandblaster on the compressor wheel — the snorkel is the first line of protection. Inspect the entire snorkel body, A-pillar mounting, and rubber joints for cracks at every service. Any leak bypasses all downstream filtration.

EGT Gauge (Madman Pyrometer) DONE

Post-turbo exhaust gas temperature sensor with dashboard display. Alarm set at 600°C post-turbo. The single most important monitoring tool on a turbocharged 1HZ — without it, the primary cause of catastrophic engine failure (overheating from sustained high EGT) is invisible to the driver. Add ~150°C to post-turbo readings to estimate true pre-turbo manifold temperature.

Boost Compensator DONE

Mounted on the injection pump governor housing, connected to the intake manifold post-intercooler. Matches fuel delivery to available boost pressure — prevents over-fuelling at idle (black smoke, high EGT) and under-fuelling under boost (flat power). Pump bench-recalibrated by Udel with the compensator integrated (June 2026; not dyno re-tested since the March 2025 dyno verification).

The stock clutch cannot reliably handle the additional torque from a turbo long-term. This is the 4th clutch in the vehicle's life — the current Terrain Tamer 4WD HD unit is the appropriate specification for the power level and intended use (loaded expedition vehicle, sustained grades, regular 4WD engagement). Installed Feb 2026 @ 296,500 km. ~12,000 km.

Gearbox Rebuild + 5th Gear Upgrade DONE

Full R151F rebuild Feb 2025 @ 271,200 km. Input shaft, cluster gear, 3rd/4th hub upgrade, mainshaft and transfer input shaft upgraded, 5th gear set upgrade, thrust washer and hardened spacer. The R151F's 5th gear is a known weak point under turbo torque loads — the upgraded set addresses this directly.

Injection Pump Calibration (Bench-Recalibrated) DONE

The mechanical Bosch VE (Denso-built) injection pump on the 1HZ must be calibrated specifically for a turbo application — fuel delivery, boost compensator integration, and timing must be set by a specialist who understands the setup. A generic pump shop job risks over-fuelling (high EGT, cracked head) or under-fuelling (flat power). Current calibration: the pump was bench-rebuilt and recalibrated by Udel in June 2026; it has not been dyno re-tested since. The last dyno verification (March 2025) recorded 118 kW / 373 Nm — figures not re-confirmed post-rebuild. Compression test passed.

Cambelt + Tensioner + Idlers DONE

The 1HZ is a rubber timing belt interference engine — belt failure causes valve damage and potential piston contact. On a turbocharged engine where combustion pressures are elevated, this is a survival-critical item. Replaced Apr 2025 @ 273,023 km. Next due ~373,000 km or Apr 2030 (100,000 km / 5-year interval).

Anti-Corrosion Treatment DONE

Heavy duty marine grade underbody anti-corrosion treatment applied April 2024 at 252,800 km by Anthony AntiCorrosion. Marine grade formulation provides long-term protection against the combined effects of coastal salt air, river crossings, and the corrosive red laterite soils common in East Africa. Inspect underbody at each major service interval — re-treat any exposed bare metal before the 2028 expedition departure.

Free-Flow Exhaust System (76mm 409 Stainless) DONE

Why it matters: The standard 1HZ exhaust is adequate for the naturally-aspirated engine but restrictive with a turbo fitted. Lower back pressure means the turbine spins more freely, exhaust gases exit faster, and less residual heat remains in the cylinder. The result: lower EGT under sustained load — the exact operating condition of a loaded expedition vehicle climbing at altitude.

What to fit: A 75mm (3”) stainless steel mandrel-bent system replacing everything post-turbo — downpipe, mid-pipe, and muffler. Going larger than 75mm on a low-boost setup yields diminishing returns and can hurt low-rpm torque. A quality muffler rather than a straight pipe is appropriate for a two-year expedition through populated areas.

Status — DONE: Fitted 20 May 2026 @ 309,968 km by Powerflow Exhausts (Bellville). 76mm bore, 409-grade stainless, full post-turbo system. Came in under estimate at R8,500. Re-baseline the Madman EGT readings — expect modestly lower EGT under sustained load and quicker cool-down. Watch for any change in peak boost on the low-boost setup; boost subsequently confirmed at 0.6 bar (~8.7 psi), Udel Jun 2026.

3 — Evaluate Before Expedition

Upgrades commonly performed on 1HZ turbo builds that this vehicle does not currently have. Each needs a decision before the 2028 departure.

Secondary Fuel Pre-Filter (Racor) PLANNED — NOT INSTALLED

Why it matters: Diesel quality in East Africa (Tanzania, Ethiopia, northern Mozambique, Angola) is inconsistent. Water, sediment, and microbial contamination are common. The OEM fuel filter is the only protection your injectors and injection pump have — and it was not designed to handle this level of contamination alone. A Racor in-line filter/water separator sits upstream of the OEM filter, filters to 10 microns, and separates ~98% of suspended water. Clear bowl for visual inspection and petcock drain.

What to fit: Racor LC70 Series kit — mounting bracket, filter element, spare element, fittings. Installation is straightforward for any diesel mechanic. Already documented in R4 (Section 5B.4) as a planned modification.

Rear Disc Brake Conversion NOT DONE

Why it matters: The vehicle currently runs front vented discs and rear drums. Rear drums were replaced Jan 2026 @ 293,949 km — the system is functional. However, rear drums trap water after crossings, take longer to dry than discs, and the active rear hub seal leak risks contaminating brake shoes. Contaminated shoes cannot be cleaned — only replaced.

Counter-argument: Current rear drums are fresh (Jan 2026) and the factory configuration is proven and simple. Drum brakes are more effective as a parking/handbrake and more tolerant of mud ingress than exposed disc/caliper setups. This is an upgrade, not a repair — evaluate at the Snyman 4×4 assessment.

Diff Breather Extensions VERIFY IF DONE

Why it matters: Standard Toyota diff breathers are inadequate for water crossings deeper than ankle height. When diffs heat up during driving and then cool rapidly in a water crossing, the contracting air inside creates negative pressure that pulls water past the seals. Extended breathers route the vent points for front diff, rear diff, and transfer case to a high point in the engine bay.

What to check: Verify whether breather extensions are already fitted. If not, this is one of the cheapest and most effective overland modifications. Kits are available from multiple suppliers.

Est. costR300–R800 (DIY)
PriorityHIGH — verify, then fit if absent

4 — Not Required / Not Applicable

Upgrades that were researched and ruled out for this vehicle and use case. Documented here to prevent revisiting settled decisions.

Oil Catch Can NOT REQUIRED

Research conclusion: The primary purpose of a catch can is to prevent oil vapour from mixing with EGR soot in the intake manifold, creating carbon buildup. This vehicle has no EGR. Without EGR recirculating sooty exhaust gas into the intake, there is no mixing of oil and soot to create the problematic deposits that catch cans are designed to prevent. On a non-EGR 1HZ, a catch can is engine bay clutter with no functional benefit.

PCV routing (resolved): The crankcase breather should vent pre-turbo (between airbox and turbo inlet), not post-turbo — post-turbo routing can pressurise the crankcase and push oil past the top-end seals, which was the leading hypothesis for the cylinder-head oil seepage noted Apr 2026. That seepage resolved by Jun 2026 and is no longer an open item, so no PCV routing change is required. Retained here as background only, not an action.

ECU Remap / Chip Tuning NOT APPLICABLE

The 1HZ uses a mechanical Bosch VE injection pump — there is no engine control unit (ECU) to remap. Fuel delivery is controlled mechanically via the pump and boost compensator. The pump was bench-recalibrated by Udel in June 2026 (not dyno re-tested); the last dyno verification, March 2025, recorded 118 kW / 373 Nm, not re-confirmed since. ECU tuning applies to common-rail diesel engines with electronic injection — not relevant for the 1HZ.

Larger Turbo / Higher Boost NOT RECOMMENDED

The SAC low-boost setup at 0.6 bar is deliberately conservative for long-term reliability on an expedition vehicle. Going to a larger turbo or higher boost requires engine internal upgrades and dramatically increases risk on a two-year remote trip. The 1HZ has no oil-cooled pistons — sustained high combustion loads at higher boost accelerate piston and head gasket wear. Not appropriate for the use case.

Engine Internal Upgrades (Billet Rods, Pistons) NOT APPLICABLE

Only relevant for builds targeting 200+ HP (150+ kW). The current 118 kW setup with stock internals and low boost is within the safe operating envelope for the 1HZ bottom end. The philosophy is reliability over performance — "eco not ego."

5th Gear Ratio Change (0.711:1) DEFER

The Terrain Tamer 0.711:1 5th gear reduction ratio kit is available and documented in R1. It reduces RPM at cruise and improves fuel economy versus the current OEM 0.838:1 ratio. However, the gearbox was fully rebuilt with upgraded components Feb 2025 — re-opening it for a ratio change is not justified at this stage. Revisit only if fuel economy at highway cruise becomes a concern after the tyre upgrade.

Boost Gauge NOT REQUIRED

Decision — not fitting: Peak boost was confirmed at 0.6 bar (~8.7 psi) by Udel's diagnostic during the Jun 2026 fuel-injection overhaul. With boost verified and the Madman EGT gauge already monitoring the critical thermal limit, a permanent boost gauge adds dashboard clutter without changing any field decision. Eco not ego — keeping the install simple.

If a question ever arises: boost can be re-checked at any diesel workshop with a temporary gauge tee on the boost-compensator port — no permanent install required.
