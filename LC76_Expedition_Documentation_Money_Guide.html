PART A · THE PAPER THAT CROSSES BORDERS

1 · Carnet de Passages en Douane

The vehicle's passport — the single document that lets the LC76 cross a border without leaving a cash bond at each one, and the one with the longest lead time and the sharpest teeth if mishandled.

A Carnet de Passages en Douane (CPD) is an internationally recognised customs document that guarantees the import duties on the vehicle: the issuing body stands surety that the vehicle will be re-exported, so foreign customs let it in without you paying — or risking — a cash deposit at every frontier. In practice it also speeds crossings, because it is a document officials recognise on sight.

Is it required for our route?

The honest answer is partly, and the practice shifts. Kenya — the northern terminus — is where a Carnet is effectively expected; Tanzania too. Across the SADC leg (Mozambique, Malawi, Zimbabwe, Zambia) vehicles registered in the region have long crossed on border-issued Temporary Import Permits (TIPs) without a Carnet. Reports conflict and rules change year to year. The clean decision for an expedition that reaches Kenya is to carry a Carnet for the whole route: it covers every country, removes the cash-bond risk everywhere, and smooths the crossings — then verify each country's current entry rule close to travel via R15 and the profiles.

Issuer, deposit & value
  • 1Issuer — AASAThe Automobile Association of South Africa is the only authorised FIA Carnet issuer for South African citizens and residents. Apply well ahead — allow several weeks; this is the longest-lead document of the trip.
  • 2Refundable deposit / bank guaranteeA refundable cash deposit (or bank guarantee) is lodged with the issuer, scaled to the declared value of the vehicle and the countries on the itinerary. It is held in a non-interest-bearing account until the Carnet is discharged. Declared value: R VALUE · Deposit lodged: R DEPOSIT.
  • 3Declare a realistic valueThe deposit tracks the vehicle's declared value — a well-used 2009 vehicle sits in the lowest bracket. Declare an honest, defensible value; do not inflate it (it only raises your deposit) and do not understate it (the value must be justifiable).
  • 4Documents for the applicationID/passport copy, the vehicle registration papers, and an itinerary of countries to be visited. One Carnet per vehicle — no trailer is carried, so one booklet. Because the vehicle is registered to Mary-Ann West, the application and the owner-authority interlock (§3).
Using it — and the trap

At every border the customs officer stamps the entry (importation) voucher on arrival and the exit (re-exportation) voucher on departure. Before you drive away from any post, physically confirm the exit stamp is in the booklet.

A missed exit stamp is the classic Carnet disaster. If a country has no re-export stamp, the vehicle looks as though it was never taken out — and the issuer (and ultimately you) can be billed the full import duties for that country. The holder is always the party finally liable if the vehicle is not properly re-exported — through a missed stamp, theft, or a write-off. Guard the booklet like the vehicle's passport, and check every stamp.
Validity, renewal & the split-trip constraint

A Carnet is valid for a maximum of 12 months from date of issue, typically renewable once for a further year (not guaranteed — arrange before it expires). For a 2028–2030 expedition that may be split around the SA vehicle-licence renewal, this 12-month life is a hard planning constraint.

This drives an EX-9 decision. Whether the vehicle overwinters abroad (Carnet renewed on the road, or transferred to a fresh Carnet at a crossing) or returns to RSA (discharge and re-issue a fresh Carnet for the second leg) has to reconcile with the 12-month Carnet clock. Carry this into the route & licence-break workshop — see R38 §4.
Discharge on return

On returning the vehicle to South Africa, have the Certificate of Location (the last page of the booklet) endorsed by SA Customs — or a police station — as proof the vehicle is home. Return the discharged Carnet to the issuer; the deposit is then refunded or the bank guarantee released. Do not lose the booklet on the way home: no endorsement, no refund.

List high-value carried spares on the Carnet. A Carnet covers the vehicle and its listed equipment — and a customs officer who finds an undeclared boxed alternator, winch, or major electronics assumes commercial import, not a spare. When applying, declare the significant spares and equipment carried (spare alternator and starter kit per RA-4, recovery winch, Victron electronics, sat comms hardware) with serial numbers where they exist. Ten minutes on the application form prevents a duty dispute at a land border where you hold no cards. Keep the carried-spares list current against the R3 ledger — what is listed should match what is in the vehicle.

2 · Cross-Border Vehicle Insurance

Third-party motor cover is compulsory in every country you enter. You either show valid cover at the border or buy it on the spot — this section is the inventory of which instrument covers where.

The COMESA Yellow Card — five of our six countries

The COMESA Yellow Card is a regional third-party motor-insurance card recognised across thirteen Eastern and Southern African states. For this route it covers Malawi, Zambia, Zimbabwe, Tanzania and Kenya in one document. It provides the minimum compulsory third-party liability cover, plus a limited third-party property-damage benefit and an emergency medical expenses benefit. It can be issued in South Africa before departure (via a participating insurer's business-to-business arrangement) or bought at the first COMESA border. One card, five countries — the efficient instrument for the East African leg.

Mozambique is the exception. Mozambique is not a COMESA Yellow Card territory. On entry you buy Mozambican compulsory third-party insurance (seguro obrigatório) at the border — a cheap, short-term certificate issued on the spot. Budget for it and do not assume the Yellow Card covers you there.

Any Botswana or Namibia transit on a return leg (if EX-9 routes that way) is a separate SADC arrangement — buy border third-party as applicable; resolve with the route.

Third-party only. The Yellow Card and border certificates protect other people — they do not cover your own vehicle for damage or theft. Own-damage, theft and comprehensive cover, travel/medical insurance, and medevac are the policies of record in R29. This section owns only the cross-border third-party inventory; it does not duplicate R29.
  • 1Confirm the home policy's reachCheck whether the SA comprehensive policy extends any cover cross-border, or arrange a rider — this is an R29 action. Own-damage abroad is the gap the Yellow Card does not fill.
  • 2COMESA Yellow Card before departureArrange it in SA to cover Malawi, Zambia, Zimbabwe, Tanzania, Kenya. Policy No.: COMESA POLICY No. · Issued: ISSUE · Expires: EXPIRY.
  • 3Budget the Mozambique border seguroBought on entry; carry local currency or small USD for it. Keep the certificate with the vehicle papers.
  • 4Carry proofPaper original plus a certified copy of every cover document, in the vehicle-papers wallet (see §5 redundancy).

3 · Authority to Drive a Vehicle Not in Your Name

The LC76 is registered to Mary-Ann West. When Neil drives — a vehicle not in his name, across borders — officials need proof of authority, or a routine stop becomes a suspected-theft problem.

The requirement is concrete and well established. A driver who is not the registered owner of a vehicle taken across an international border should carry a police-certified affidavit from the registered owner authorising them to take the vehicle abroad, together with the vehicle registration papers. A certified copy of the registration is accepted at most border posts; keep the original secure and carry certified copies.

  • 1Owner's affidavit / letter of authorityFrom Mary-Ann West (registered owner), authorising Neil to drive and take the vehicle across borders. Commissioned at a police station (SAPS commissioner of oaths). It should name both crew, identify the vehicle (make, model, VIN, engine number, registration), and state the travel dates and countries. Affidavit dated: AFFIDAVIT DATE. Pre-drafted template: Owner's Authority Affidavit (R39 supplement) — print, complete in ink before commissioning (blanks filled after stamping can void the attestation; ID numbers are deliberately blank in the library copy — complete from the physical documents).
  • 2Vehicle registration certificateOriginal kept secure; carry several certified copies for the border wallet and the redundancy stash (§5).
  • 3Both crew namedSo either Neil or Mary-Ann may drive lawfully at any point — consistent with the no-single-point-of-failure crew doctrine in R37/R38.
  • 4Finance checkIf the vehicle were financed, a lienholder letter of authority would also be required. Confirm the vehicle is owned outright so this is not needed — record the confirmation.
Keep the affidavit current. The authority should cover the actual travel window. If dates slip, or a split expedition returns to RSA and departs again, commission a fresh affidavit for the second leg so the authorised dates always bracket the travel. Border officials also commonly treat a certified affidavit as current for only ~3 months from commissioning — and re-certification is SAPS-only, impossible abroad. How a two-year no-return trip lives with that window is an open EX-9 question (OI-30).
The registration disk must be valid the whole time abroad. An expired SA vehicle licence while the vehicle is out of the country is a genuine problem at borders and on discharge. This is the licence-renewal driver behind the EX-9 licence-break question — see R38 §4. Do not let the disk lapse mid-expedition.
Disc renewal doctrine (resolved 13 Jul 2026). SA allows early licence renewal, and early renewal resets the renewal cycle from the new date (pro-rata paid) — so the disc no longer constrains the departure date: renew in the final week before departure, whichever month that is, and leave with a full twelve months on the windscreen. Current disc expires 1 July 2027 — one ordinary renewal falls in Jul 2027, then the final-week early renewal at the 2028 departure. The 12-month disc life then produces one mid-expedition renewal event at ~month 12 (~2029). Handle it by proxy: a nominated agent in RSA (not either crew) renews on the NaTIS / online channel under Mary-Ann's letter of authority + certified copies, then couriers the disc to a reliable route address — Windhoek, Arusha or Nairobi are the workable destinations on the Option-A calendar. Fix this waypoint into the route plan at EX-9.

4 · Personal Documents

The people-paperwork. Per-country visa detail lives in the profiles (007–010); this is the cross-cutting inventory that applies whatever the exact route.

Passports

Both crew: machine-readable passports, valid for at least six months beyond the intended end of travel, with two to three blank pages per country — a multi-country trip eats passport pages in stamps faster than people expect. For a trip that may run to 2030, check that expiry covers the whole window and renew early if it is tight. Neil — No.: PASSPORT No. · Expires: EXPIRY. Mary-Ann — No.: PASSPORT No. · Expires: EXPIRY.

Visas — overview only

SA passport holders are visa-free or visa-on-arrival for some of the route and need advance visas for others; regional instruments such as the KAZA UniVisa (Zimbabwe/Zambia) and East African arrangements may apply. The per-country visa requirements, fees and application timing are owned by the country profiles (007–010) and verified close to travel — they are deliberately not duplicated here. This guide simply flags that visas are part of the pre-departure document set.

Yellow fever certificate (ICVP)

Get the yellow-fever vaccine and the International Certificate of Vaccination or Prophylaxis ("yellow card") before departure. The reason is specific to this route: the itinerary includes Kenya, which is on the WHO yellow-fever-risk list. That means once the crew has been in Kenya, the certificate is triggered for onward crossings and — importantly — for re-entry to South Africa. Tanzania and Zambia are not themselves risk-listed, but the Kenya leg makes carrying the certificate effectively mandatory for the whole trip. One dose gives lifetime protection; have it administered at an approved centre at least 10 days before it is needed, and carry the original — photocopies and phone images are not accepted at most borders. Neil dated: YF DATE · Mary-Ann dated: YF DATE.

Vaccination medicine is R17's. Malaria prophylaxis, hepatitis A, typhoid and the rest of the health picture are owned by R17 — this section records only the yellow-fever certificate, because it is a border document, not just a health measure.
Driving — International Driving Permit

Each driver carries an International Driving Permit (IDP) — the AASA-issued translation of the SA licence, valid 12 months. The IDP is only valid alongside the original SA driving licence; on its own it is worthless, so carry both. Both crew hold one. As with the Carnet, the 12-month life means timing the issue to the departure — a split trip may need a re-issue for the second leg. Neil IDP: IDP No. · Mary-Ann IDP: IDP No..

Card validity chain (CORRECTED 17 Jul 2026 — supersedes the 13 Jul note). An IDP can never outlive the SA licence card it is issued against. Exact dates off the physical cards: Mary-Ann's card expires 27 May 2029 — inside the expedition window (~month 9–11), so her pre-departure early renewal is MANDATORY; a SA card cannot be renewed from abroad or by proxy (in-person biometrics), and the fallback is a fly-home renewal ~Apr/May 2029 clustered with the OI-25 disc waypoint. The Western Cape position is that renewal is allowed at any time before expiry, with the new card valid 5 years from the renewal date — confirmed in writing by the WCG Contact Centre, 17 Jul 2026, ref 260717-000921 (OI-27 closed). 5 · Document Redundancy

The same no-single-point-of-failure doctrine R38 applies to crew, applied to paper: losing or having a document wallet stolen must not end the expedition.

Every critical document exists in three layers, in three places, so no single loss or theft is fatal:

Layer 1
Originals
Carried securely, and shown at borders only where a certified copy will not do. Split the critical originals between two locations in the vehicle/on the crew so a single theft never takes the whole set at once.
Layer 2
Certified copies
A full certified-copy set — passports, registration, Carnet ID page, licences & IDPs, yellow-fever certificate, insurance — stashed separately in the vehicle, away from the originals (per R11 packing and R35 concealment). A second full set held by the home anchor (R38).
Layer 3
Encrypted digital
Scans of everything in encrypted cloud storage plus an offline encrypted copy on a device or USB. Access details known to both crew and lodged with the anchor. Retrieval depends on connectivity — see R16.
  • 1Two certified-copy setsOne in the vehicle (separate from originals), one with the anchor at home (R38).
  • 2Encrypted cloud + offline copyBoth crew know the access; the anchor holds the master reference.
  • 3Photograph stamps on arrivalPhotograph each country's entry stamp and Carnet vouchers as you go — a dated record that resolves later disputes.
Certify before you leave. Certified copies are made and certified before departure (SAPS or a commissioner of oaths) — you cannot easily certify documents on the road. Do it once, thoroughly, and make spares.

PART B · THE MONEY THAT KEEPS YOU MOVING

6 · Money & Forex Strategy

Upgrading the “thin” grading from the June 2026 audit. A multi-country expedition crosses several currencies, patchy card acceptance, and long stretches with no ATM. The whole strategy is one word: redundancy — never a single point of failure for access to money.

Currencies on the route

Rand at home, then Mozambican metical, Malawian kwacha, Zimbabwe (US dollars are widely used there), Zambian kwacha, Tanzanian shilling and Kenyan shilling. Across the whole region US dollars are the universal fallback — but carry clean, newer-series notes: worn, marked, or older-series US bills are frequently refused at banks and bureaux.

The three money instruments
Instrument 1
Cards
At least two cards on different networks (a Visa and a Mastercard), ideally from different accounts/banks, so one block or fraud-freeze cannot strand you. Notify both banks of the travel dates and countries. Cards work in towns, fuel stations and lodges — and are useless in the bush.
Instrument 2
USD cash
The primary fallback and the currency for borders, informal fuel, and anywhere cards fail. Carry a mix of denominations — small notes for borders, tips and the Mozambique seguro; larger notes for fuel and lodging. Clean, newer series only.
Instrument 3
Local currency
Draw modest amounts at each country's ATMs or bureaux for daily spend. Don't over-convert — you lose on the exchange out again. Keep small local notes for markets, tips and informal purchases.
Card-failure fallback

Assume a card will fail somewhere — a block, a dead network, a swallowed card, a village with no machine. The rule: carry enough USD cash to complete any leg with no working card at all. Keep a backup card physically separate from the everyday wallet. Know the banks' emergency and fraud numbers, and lodge them with the anchor (R38) alongside the R29 policy details. Emergency USD float carried: USD FLOAT · Banks notified: DATE NOTIFIED.

Cash concealment & the split

Never keep all the cash in one place. Split it: a day-wallet with modest cash for checkpoints and daily spend; separate concealed stashes distributed in the vehicle (per the R35 security doctrine); and a small body-carried reserve. Both crew know every location. At a checkpoint you show a modest wallet, never a full one — how to conduct yourself with solicited “fees” and bribes is owned by R35; this section owns only the money you carry and where.

ATM-desert planning

Long remote legs have no ATM for days, and the one machine in a small town is often out of cash or offline. Before any remote stretch: top up local currency and USD to cover the whole leg plus a margin, and note the last reliable bank/ATM town on the route — a datum that feeds directly into the country profiles' serviceability notes. Never rely on a single town's single ATM working on the day you arrive.

Keep a running cash log. A simple ledger of what was spent, where, and in what currency — it costs nothing on the road and feeds the post-trip review (R23): actual spend versus plan, and a real forex budget for the next leg.
