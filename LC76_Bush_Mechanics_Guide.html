1 — Mindset

The difference between a field repair that gets you home and one that strands you permanently is almost never about tools or parts. It is about the state of mind you bring before you touch anything. These eight principles apply to every roadside situation, from a flat tyre to a blown head gasket.

Principle 01
Stop · Breathe · Think
The first impulse after a breakdown is to act. Resist it. Stop the vehicle safely. Get out and walk around it. Give yourself three minutes before touching a single bolt. Adrenaline makes you miss the obvious and creates new problems faster than it solves old ones. The truck isn't going anywhere.
Principle 02
Diagnose Before You Touch
Identify the fault with confidence before dismantling anything. What system is it? What is the symptom — smoke, noise, leak, loss of function? What changed just before the failure? A wrong diagnosis strips threads, loses irreplaceable clips, and wastes time. One correct diagnosis saves five wrong repairs.
Principle 03
Never Make It Worse
The golden rule of field mechanics. If your repair attempt might cause a second failure on top of the first — a stripped thread, a cracked housing, a contaminated circuit — stop. A drivable vehicle with a leaking hose clamp is infinitely better than an immovable vehicle with a cracked manifold you created trying to fix it.
Principle 04
Slow Is Fast
Field repairs done in a hurry take three times as long as field repairs done carefully. Torque fasteners. Clean surfaces before sealing. Lay out removed parts in order. The 20 minutes you spend doing it properly saves the three hours you'd spend redoing it 50 km down the road — after dark, in the rain.
Principle 05
Document Everything
Photograph the fault before and after. Write down what you did, what materials you used, how long it took, and what the vehicle's condition was when you drove away. This record is your transfer note to a workshop and your evidence that the repair was sound. Use the Fault Log at the end of this guide.
Principle 06
Two Sets of Eyes
Before you reassemble anything, have Mary-Ann walk the repair with you. A second pair of eyes will find the thing you missed — the hose not fully seated, the clip not clicked home, the wire routed over a hot surface. This is not optional. It has prevented stranding more than once on long trips.
Principle 07
Temporary Means Temporary
A bush repair is a field fix to reach the next workshop, not a permanent solution. Know what it is. Write "TEMP" in the Fault Log. Inform the workshop exactly what was done. A JB Weld patch on a cracked housing buys you time — it does not replace the housing. Do not convince yourself otherwise.
Principle 08
Know What You Cannot Fix
A cracked block, a snapped crank, a shattered transfer case, a seized turbo bearing, a burnt valve. Know this list before you leave. Not every failure has a field fix, and attempting a repair beyond your skill level with inadequate tools turns a recoverable situation into a recovery. The Stop & Call protocol (Section 6) applies here.
Australian bush mechanic tradition: The Australian outback overlanding culture — from which much of African overlanding inherited its approach — is built on one rule: be methodical, not heroic. A methodical driver with a set of spanners and a roll of wire has extracted more vehicles from the desert than any amount of enthusiasm with no plan.
2 — Triage

Triage is the discipline of deciding — quickly and correctly — what category of response a failure demands. There are three outcomes: Fix Here, Limp Out, or Stop and Call. The decision tree below and the Three Questions test give you a repeatable framework. Use them before emotions take over.

The Three Questions Test

Answer all three honestly before you start any field repair. One "No" shifts the outcome.

Q1
Do you know exactly what is wrong?
YES → Proceed to Q2.
NO → Diagnose first. Do not start dismantling on a guess. Review R18 Troubleshooting Guide.
Q2
Do you have the materials to fix it?
YES → Proceed to Q3.
NO → Check Section 3 for improvisation options. If nothing works, assess limp or stop.
Q3
Do you have the skill and tools to do the repair safely?
YES → Fix it. Use Section 4 technique cards.
NO → Limp strategy (Section 5) or Stop & Call (Section 6).

3 — Universal Repair Materials

These 15 materials live permanently in the vehicle. Each one earns its weight by solving multiple problems across multiple systems. Know their capabilities and — critically — their limits before you reach for them under pressure.

JB Weld (Original)STRUCTURAL
Two-part steel-reinforced epoxy. Bonds metal, plastic, ceramic. Cures to ~280°C max (normal cure). Full strength at 24 h; working strength at 4–6 h.
✕ Not for head or block cracks under pressure. Not for brake or steering hydraulics. Not a substitute for welding structural joints. Temperature limit matters — sump and manifold use ExtremeHeat variant only.
Pratley Steel PuttySTRUCTURAL
Two-part epoxy putty. Hand-kneadable, sets hard in 30–45 min. Fills voids, patches cracks. Can be drilled, tapped and filed when cured.
✕ Not for high-cycle vibration joints. Not for head gasket or injector seat area. Limited heat tolerance — keeps it away from exhaust manifolds.
Self-Amalgamating TapeSEAL
Silicone-based tape that fuses to itself under tension with no adhesive. Waterproof, fuel-resistant, rated to ~200°C. Works on hoses, wiring, and irregular shapes. Apply stretched to 50% of original length for full seal.
✕ Temporary fix only on pressurised hoses — especially coolant under boost. Not suitable for oil under pressure. Inspect every 200 km after application.
Duct TapeBIND
Cloth-reinforced pressure-sensitive tape. Air ducting, bodywork, cable bundling, dust sealing, boot patching. Works on dry surfaces only.
✕ Not fuel, oil, or coolant rated. Fails rapidly in heat above 60°C. Not a structural fix. Not a seal under fluid pressure.
Cable Ties (Assorted)BIND
UV-rated nylon, 100 mm through 400 mm lengths. Bundle wiring, secure hoses, replace broken clips, lash loose body panels, hold radiator overflow hose. Carry 50+ of each size.
✕ No structural load. UV grades degrade over time — inspect annually. Do not run over hot surfaces — use a heat shield if routing near exhaust.
Fencing Wire (3 m)BIND
Galvanised mild steel wire. The original bush fix — it can lash, bind, substitute for a broken clip or bracket, hold a cracked exhaust pipe, and keep a loose heat shield quiet. Twist with pliers for tension.
✕ Conduct hazard near wiring — insulate any contact points with tape. Not a replacement for a structural fastener. Vibrates loose — check at every stop.
Hose Clamps (Assorted)SEAL
Stainless worm-drive clamps, 20 mm through 80 mm. Secure hoses, compress self-amalgamating tape patches, clamp split hoses, hold improvised splices. Carry 4–6 of each size.
✕ Overtightening cuts rubber hoses. Not for rigid pipe joints without rubber buffer. Check torque after first heat cycle following installation.
Ratchet Straps (×2)BIND
25 mm × 5 m, 750 kg WLL each. Secondary leaf spring lashing if a spring breaks, body panel retention, load control, towing anchor. Dual use: recovery and repair.
✕ Not a substitute for structural leaf spring replacement — emergency only, limited to walking pace. Do not exceed rated WLL. Never cross-load hooks.
Thread Lock + Anti-SeizeCHEMICAL
Loctite 243 (medium thread lock) and Copaslip anti-seize paste. Secure reinstalled fasteners against vibration. Anti-seize on exhaust studs, suspension bolts, caliper guide pins — prevents future seizure in dissimilar metals.
✕ Thread lock on fasteners that may need future field removal — use sparingly. Anti-seize on brake disc or drum mating surfaces compromises torque retention.
Soap (Bar)SEAL
Ordinary hard soap applied to a slow coolant hose leak or a weeping joint will swell in contact with water and seal temporarily. Also acts as tyre bead lubricant during a field tyre swap. Carry a 100 g bar.
✕ Temporary and low-pressure only. Washes out once coolant flow resumes fully. Inspect repair at every stop. Replace with correct hose at next workshop.
Gun Gum / Exhaust PuttySEAL
High-temperature exhaust repair compound (up to 1,000°C). Seals cracks, holes, and joint leaks in the exhaust system. Apply to clean metal, wrap with aluminium tape for reinforcement, cure by running engine progressively hot.
✕ Does not bond to oily surfaces — degrease first. Vibrates loose on flex joints. Temporary fix for pinhole cracks only — does not bridge large structural breaks.
Wire + Crimp TerminalsELECTRICAL
2.5 mm² and 4 mm² automotive wire, assorted crimp terminals, butt splices, heat-shrink sleeves. Repair broken harness wires, replace blown inline fuses, bridge corroded connectors. Carry a crimping tool.
✕ Solder joints over crimps if possible — crimps in high-vibration areas work loose over time. Route repairs away from heat and sharp edges. Label all new wires.
Brake CleanerCHEMICAL
Non-residue solvent. Degreases surfaces before JB Weld, Pratley, or exhaust putty application. Cleans electrical contacts. Removes fuel from air filter if accidentally over-fuelled. Evaporates without trace.
✕ Highly flammable — never use near a flame or hot exhaust. Attacks rubber — keep off hoses, seals, and painted surfaces. Do not inhale.
Spare Fasteners (Assorted)STRUCTURAL
M6, M8, M10, M12 hex bolts and nuts in stainless, plus a set of split washers and flat washers for each. U-bolt nuts (rear leaf spring size). Carry in a labelled zip-lock bag. Critical — lost hardware is the most common stranding cause after flat tyres.
✕ Match thread pitch and grade to original. Do not replace high-tensile structural bolts (suspension, steering, propshaft) with inferior hardware.
Rags (Clean)GENERAL
Cotton or microfibre rags for surface cleaning before sealing, oil absorption, improvised padding or insulation, and general cleanup. A clean work surface is the single most effective tool for preventing a second fault during a repair.
✕ Oil-soaked rags are a fire risk — store separately and dispose of at next fuel stop. Never use synthetic fabrics near the exhaust system.

4 — Field Repair Techniques

Tap any card to expand. Each technique is specific to the 1HZ / 76 Series platform. Cards are organised by system — Cooling, Fuel, Suspension & Steering, Drivetrain, Electrical, Body & Chassis. Badge colours indicate urgency: RED = stop immediately, AMBER = monitor and manage, GREEN = continue with care.

Cooling System
Split or leaking coolant hose
AMBER — Monitor ▼
Before you start: Let the system cool completely. Opening a hot cooling system cap causes severe steam burns. Wait 30 minutes minimum after engine off.
1
Locate the leak visually. Check upper hose (thermostat to radiator), lower hose (radiator to pump), and both turbo intercooler hoses. Wipe dry and watch for the drip source — don't assume.
2
If it is a pinhole or small split: dry the area thoroughly, apply self-amalgamating tape directly over the split under tension. Wrap 50 mm either side. Add a hose clamp over the tape at the split point to compress it.
3
If the hose is split near a fitting or has fully failed: if you carry a spare hose, replace it. If not, cut the damaged section out cleanly, bridge the gap with the closest diameter hose section from your spares, and double-clamp each end.
4
Refill with water (not coolant — label the level so workshop can correct mix ratio). Bleed air by running engine with cap off until thermostat opens and coolant flows freely. Top up. Replace cap.
5
Run engine and watch repair. Check level again after 5 minutes running. If level drops rapidly — stop. The repair has not held.
Limp: Carry extra water. Stop every 20 km to check level. Keep load light and speed below 80 km/h. Avoid steep climbs. Target nearest workshop within 150 km.
See alsoR22 Cooling System Guide
Radiator core leak or damage
RED — Stop Risk ▼
Do not continue driving with an uncontrolled radiator leak. The 1HZ has no temperature warning light — you will find out you have overheated from white steam and a cooked engine, not from an alarm.
1
Identify the leak location — top or bottom tank (usually plastic on the replacement radiators common in SA), core tubes (aluminium), or end header seam.
2
For a leaking plastic tank seam or small tank crack: Dry thoroughly. Knead Pratley Steel Putty and press firmly over the crack. Smooth to a dome shape so it is under compression against water pressure. Allow 30–45 min cure. Do not pressurise until set.
3
For a bent or fractured aluminium core tube: if a row of tubes is crushed (stone impact), you can block that row entirely by squeezing both ends with pliers — losing ~10% of cooling capacity but stopping the leak. Not ideal; acceptable as a temporary measure.
4
Top up with water. Run carefully with cap off to check leak is contained. Replace the cap only once you are satisfied the leak is sealed.
Limp only if leak is confirmed sealed and level stable. Absolute maximum 100 km at reduced speed. Do not climb steep hills. Watch temp gauge continuously — the 1HZ has no overheat warning alarm; install a temperature alert if you can.
SA radiator quality warning: Many replacement radiators sold in SA for the 1HZ use inferior plastic tanks that crack under normal operating pressure. If you have fitted a non-OEM radiator without verifying origin, carry Pratley Putty as non-negotiable insurance.
Thermostat failure (stuck open or closed)
AMBER — System dependent ▼
1
Determine failure mode. Stuck open: engine runs cold, heats slowly, never reaches full operating temp — drives but fuel economy and power suffer. Stuck closed: rapid overheating within 10–15 min of driving — dangerous, stop immediately.
2
If stuck closed (overheating): once cool, remove thermostat housing (two bolts, top of engine at front, accessible with basic tools). Remove thermostat. Reinstall housing with paper gasket substitute (cut from a fuel box or cereal box — JB Weld can seal the joint). Run without thermostat.
3
Running without a thermostat: the engine runs cool and takes longer to warm up. This is acceptable for short-term limping. The lack of thermostat means the water pump sees slightly reduced flow efficiency — but it is a safe, drivable condition.
4
If stuck open and no overheating risk: simply drive. Monitor fuel consumption increase. Replace thermostat at next workshop.
Without thermostat: fully drivable for several hundred km at normal speed. Carry a replacement (82°C rated for the 1HZ) — a thermostat weighs 80 g and costs R50.

Fuel System
Fuel tank slow leak (stone strike or crack)
AMBER — Fire risk ▼
Fuel leaks near a hot exhaust are a fire risk. Check the leak location relative to the exhaust path before attempting any repair.
1
Identify the exact leak — drip or seep? Location on the tank? If it is near a weld seam or drain plug, assess whether the tank can be drained to a safe level before repair.
2
For a pinhole or stone impact crack on a plastic or metal tank: clean and degrease thoroughly with brake cleaner. Allow to fully dry. Knead JB Weld or Pratley Putty and press firmly over the crack. Build up a thick dome — you need compressive mass.
3
Allow full cure (minimum 4 h for JB Weld). Do not fill or start engine during cure.
4
After cure, fill slowly and watch the patch for seep. If it holds, drive. If seep restarts, do not continue — you need a workshop.
If the repair holds: run with reduced tank level (less head pressure). Inspect patch every stop. Do not use aux cells if the main tank leak is unrepaired.
See alsoR24 Fuel System Guide
Bad or contaminated fuel (water / dirt ingestion)
AMBER — Drivability ▼
1
Symptoms: loss of power, rough running, black or white smoke change, hard starting, injector rattle. Sudden onset after a fuel fill = contaminated source.
2
Check the Racor pre-filter bowl — if it shows water or sediment, drain it. Open the drain tap at the bottom of the bowl, drain until only clean fuel runs. Reclose. If the bowl is more than 50% contaminated, replace the filter element.
3
If the engine has stopped and will not restart after filter drain: you may need to bleed the system. Crack the bleed nipple on the injection pump, prime the lift pump by hand (small lever on the side of the pump) until fuel runs bubble-free. Retighten. Attempt restart.
4
If you have clean fuel in aux cells, dilute the contaminated main tank by adding clean fuel. The larger volume reduces concentration. Do not drain the main tank in the field unless you have sufficient clean replacement.
Post-bleed, engine should restart and improve within a few km as clean fuel washes through. Continue to workshop. Replace both filter elements at next opportunity.
See alsoR24 Fuel System Guide — Bad-Fuel Protocol
Lift pump weep or failure (no fuel pressure)
RED — Engine will stop ▼
1
The 1HZ lift pump is engine-driven (camshaft-actuated diaphragm). Failure symptoms: gradual power loss, engine stumbles and cuts under load, hard starting. Weep from pump body = diaphragm cracking.
2
If the pump has failed and you carry a spare: replace it. Two bolts, disconnect inlet and outlet lines (plug outlet immediately to prevent injection pump air ingress), fit new pump, bleed system as above.
3
If no spare pump: gravity feed is possible on a flat road if the tank is above the injection pump height and lines are clear. Remove lift pump body and bridge the inlet to outlet with a short piece of hose and two clamps. This only works with fuel above tank level — it will not overcome a negative head.
4
Carry a spare lift pump — they cost under R400 and weigh 400 g. It is one of the most cost-effective spares on the 1HZ.

Suspension & Steering
Broken rear leaf spring (leaf fracture)
AMBER — Drivable with care ▼
1
Identify: vehicle corners low, metallic creak or grind under load, or visible leaf separating from the pack. The OME CS058R rear pack has multiple leaves — a single broken leaf usually still leaves the vehicle drivable at reduced load.
2
Remove broken leaf if it is protruding and risk contact with tyre or wheel arch. Use a jack and axle stand to safely relieve spring pressure, undo the U-bolt if needed to extract the fragment.
3
Carry two full-length 38 mm × 8 mm flat steel ratchet straps or a spare leaf. Thread the strap under the leaf pack and over the axle housing — this provides a compressive substitute. A good ratchet strap arrangement can carry 200–300 kg at walking pace.
4
Redistribute payload to the opposite corner to minimise asymmetric load. Carry water on the opposite side if possible.
Limp: max 30 km/h, no corrugations, no heavy payload on the affected corner. Carry spare U-bolts (new ones were fitted January 2026 — have a set of the same size).
See alsoR25 Suspension & Steering GuideR3 Spares & Tools
Sheared or loose U-bolt
AMBER — Axle shift risk ▼
A loose U-bolt allows the axle to shift on the spring pack. This causes dog-tracking (vehicle crabbing sideways) and uneven braking. Do not drive fast with a loose U-bolt.
1
If you carry spare U-bolts: replace. Jack the axle, remove the cross-member plate, slide out the old U-bolt, replace, torque to 90 Nm (use a torque wrench or estimated half-arm leverage with a 600 mm breaker). Re-torque after 50 km.
2
If no spare U-bolt: do not remove the failed one. Bind the spring pack to the axle housing with two ratchet straps crossed diagonally. This is a very short-term fix — 20 km maximum at walking pace. You need a replacement.
3
Check axle alignment after any U-bolt failure: stand 5 m behind the vehicle. Rear axle should be perpendicular to the vehicle centreline. If it has shifted, you need to reposition it before driving any distance.
See alsoR25 Suspension & Steering Guide
Tie rod or drag link damage (bent or joint failure)
RED — Steering compromised ▼
A failed drag link or tie rod = uncontrolled steering. This is a Stop condition. Do not attempt to drive on a failed steering component.
1
Assess: is the rod bent (vehicle cannot track straight) or has the ball joint failed completely (wheel has unpredictable movement under load)?
2
Bent rod: if you carry a spare tie rod or drag link (strongly recommended at 305,000 km), replace it. If not, a bent tie rod can sometimes be straightened by rolling under a vehicle tyre with progressive weight — imprecise but may restore enough function to walk pace.
3
Failed ball joint with separation: if the rod end has pulled from the taper, you cannot continue. Even if you reconnect it temporarily, the taper is damaged and will re-separate under cornering load. Stop & Call.
4
Tie rod ends and drag link ends on the 76 were new at 271,200 km (Feb 2025). They should still have significant life. But at 305,000 km on African roads, inspection is mandatory at each service.

Broken or failed shock absorber
GREEN — Drivable ▼
1
Symptoms: excessive bounce on corrugations, fluid-wetted shock body, clanking from suspension on washboard. A blown shock is uncomfortable but not immediately dangerous.
2
If the shock body has separated from the mount or is dragging: remove it entirely. The vehicle will handle poorly but will drive. This is preferable to a dragging shock that contacts the tyre.
3
With a blown shock fitted: reduce speed significantly on corrugations and rough surfaces. The spring pack still supports the vehicle — the shock simply no longer damps oscillation. Excessive speed will fatigue the leaf pack or coil spring faster.
Fully drivable without the shock. Limit speed to 60 km/h on rough roads. Do not exceed 100 km/h on tar. Replace at next main workshop.
Drivetrain
Broken propshaft (universal joint failure)
RED — Drive loss ▼
1
Symptoms: sudden severe vibration with metallic impact. Rear propshaft failure is survivable (front diff still drives via front prop). Front propshaft failure: you still have rear drive. Total loss of drive = both propshafts or transfer case failure.
2
If the broken propshaft is still attached but flailing: it must be removed before driving. It will destroy the tunnel, diff flange, and potentially rupture the fuel tank. Jack, disconnect flanges at both ends, remove. Store the prop in the roof rack.
3
With rear propshaft removed: front axle 4WD remains operable. Engage 4H and drive on front wheels only — a legitimate limp-out on the 76's part-time system. Do not exceed 80 km/h. Avoid tight turning circles (diff windup).
4
With front propshaft removed: rear 2WD remains. This is your normal highway mode anyway. Drive normally until workshop.
Single propshaft loss is a recoverable situation. The 76 Series part-time 4WD architecture makes this one of the more field-friendly drivetrain failures.
See alsoR13 Drivetrain Training Guide
CV joint or front wheel bearing failure
AMBER — Speed limited ▼
1
Symptoms: clicking on full lock in 4WD (CV wear), grinding growl on the move (wheel bearing), or visible grease expulsion from a torn CV boot (imminent joint failure).
2
A worn CV boot with exposed joint: clean the joint, pack with as much grease as possible, wrap the boot area in self-amalgamating tape to retain grease. This is temporary — the joint will fail within 500–1,000 km without a proper boot.
3
A failed CV joint: if the vehicle is in 4WD mode, switch to 2WD to remove load from the front axle. Lock hubs to free if equipped. Reduce speed and avoid harsh steering lock. The front axle on the 76 has traditional trunnion / king pin setup — not a modern independent suspension — so bearing replacement in the field with a hub puller is possible but takes 2–3 hours with the right tools.

Electrical System
Charging fault — alternator or DC-DC failure
AMBER — Manage loads ▼
1
Diagnosis: BMV-712 showing negative amperage while driving = alternator not charging. Check battery voltage at engine bay with multimeter — should be 13.8–14.4 V at idle with charging. Below 12.8 V = alternator fault or connection loss.
2
Check the alternator belt — if it has snapped or slipped, the alternator is not turning. 1HZ uses a simple V-belt. Replacement: release tensioner, remove old belt, fit spare (carry one). Takes 20 minutes.
3
If belt is intact but charging is absent: check alternator output terminal connection (positive post, usually 10 mm bolt). Corrosion here is common in Africa's humidity. Clean with brake cleaner and sandpaper. Reconnect firmly.
4
Orion-Tr Smart DC-DC failure: the LiFePO4 secondary bank will not charge from the alternator, but the vehicle's starter battery is unaffected — the Orion is isolated. Reduce secondary bank loads (fridge, lighting). Solar continues charging. Drive normally.
Without alternator: 12V starter battery will drain within 2–4 hours of driving (electrical loads). Reduce all non-essential loads. The 200Ah LiFePO4 bank via Victron BMS is completely isolated — it will not drain the starter battery. Prioritise reaching a workshop with an alternator specialist.
See alsoR5 Battery Training GuideR7 LiFePO4 System Report
Broken wiring or connector (harness damage)
AMBER — Trace first ▼
1
Trace before you cut. Which circuit is affected? Identify by what stopped working. On the 1HZ, the engine has almost no ECU-controlled circuits — if the engine runs, most electrical faults are accessory or secondary bank issues only.
2
Check fuses first. The 76 has main fuse box under the bonnet and a second block behind the glove box. Carry 5 A, 10 A, 15 A, 20 A, 30 A spare fuses of each type (blade and glass). A repeatedly blown fuse means a short — find it before you replace again.
3
For a broken wire: identify both ends, strip 10 mm of insulation, join with a crimp butt splice, heat-shrink the joint. If the wire is inaccessible, run a temporary bridge wire in parallel using your spare wire — tape it away from any moving or hot parts.
4
For a damaged connector: inspect the pin contacts. Corroded pins can often be cleaned with brake cleaner and a fine pick. Bent pins can be straightened with a jeweller's screwdriver. A terminal that has pulled from a connector housing can be re-inserted by depressing the locking tab.
Starter motor or solenoid fault (no-crank)
RED — Engine will not start ▼
1
Distinguish: click-click = solenoid engaging but no motor rotation (dead starter or bad earth). Single heavy click = solenoid failing to hold. Silence = solenoid not receiving signal.
2
Check battery voltage first (multimeter). If below 12.2 V, the battery may simply be discharged — charge or jump from the 200Ah LiFePO4 bank via the Orion, or jump from another vehicle.
3
Check all cable connections at the starter motor: positive (heavy cable from battery), earth to block, and the thin solenoid trigger wire. Corrosion on any of these will prevent starting. Clean and reconnect.
4
If the R151F is in gear and the clutch is not fully depressed, the starter inhibit switch prevents cranking — check the clutch is fully depressed to the floor.
5
Bump/push start: the 1HZ can be compression-started in 2nd gear at walking pace on a slope. With four people pushing or a slight downhill, this is a realistic field option and confirms the engine itself is functional.
Bump-start success + electrical fault diagnosis = drive to nearest town. Have the starter bench-tested on arrival.
Engine Oil Leaks
Valve cover / rocker cover gasket leak
AMBER — Fire risk with turbo ▼
On the turbo 76: the exhaust manifold runs directly below the valve cover and reaches 600°C under load. Any valve cover oil seep drips onto this manifold and burns. This is a fire risk — not cosmetic. Do not tolerate any seep on a turbocharged 1HZ.
1
Symptoms: oil smell burning on engine bay heat, blue smoke at idle from the top of the engine, oil residue on the top of the block or on the intercooler pipework above.
2
Clean the top of the engine with brake cleaner. Dry it fully. Run for 10 minutes and identify exactly where the oil is originating — valve cover gasket seam, individual bolt grommet, or filler cap O-ring.
3
Field fix for gasket seam seep: clean the mating surface area thoroughly. Apply a thin bead of RTV silicone (grey or black, high-temp rated) along the seam from the outside. This is a surface seal, not a structural fix. Allow 1 h cure before running.
4
For a leaking filler cap O-ring: wrap the cap threads with PTFE tape as a temporary measure. Replace the entire cap at the next workshop (O-rings are not sold separately).
5
Carry a valve cover gasket set (gasket + bolt grommets) — it weighs under 100 g and takes 20 minutes to replace on the 1HZ. The job is accessible with standard tools.
Fully drivable with RTV field fix applied. Inspect at every stop. Replace gasket properly at next workshop — do not carry an unaddressed valve cover seep into a remote leg.
See alsoR20 Seal & Gasket Atlas §1.6R21 Turbo Operations Guide
Sump gasket or sump plug weep
GREEN — Monitor rate ▼
1
Identify the source. Sump gasket leaks appear as a film of oil on the flat bottom surface of the sump. A weeping sump plug shows as a defined drip from the drain hole. Do not confuse with rear main seal (oil at the back of the sump where the gearbox mates to the engine).
2
Sump plug weep: tighten carefully — the sump on the 1HZ is cast aluminium and the thread strips easily. If the plug is weeping rather than dripping, a new copper crush washer is usually all it needs. Carry 4× M14 copper crush washers in the spares bag.
3
If the sump plug thread has stripped: JB Weld on a stripped aluminium thread buys one or two more drain cycles. For a field fix only, clean the thread, apply JB Weld to the plug threads, reinsert — this is one-time-use only. Workshop must helicoil the thread on return.
4
Sump gasket seam seep: clean and dry the seam area. Apply high-temp RTV silicone externally along the seam. Allow 1 h cure before filling with oil or running.
5
Monitor oil level at every stop — carry 2 L spare engine oil minimum. Top up to maintain level in the safe zone on the dipstick. Never run the 1HZ below the minimum mark — particularly critical with the turbo, which depends on clean oil supply to the bearing housing.
A slow seep (losing less than 100 ml per 200 km) is manageable with monitoring. A drip that increases = stop and re-examine. Running low on oil with the SAC turbo is a very expensive failure — the turbo bearing destroys itself within minutes of oil starvation.

Front crankshaft oil seal leak (oil thrown onto timing belt)
RED — Belt contamination risk ▼
This is the most dangerous oil leak on the 1HZ. Oil contamination on the rubber timing belt causes the belt to slip and fail. The 1HZ is an interference engine — a failed timing belt bends the valves and destroys the head. A front crank seal leak is a Stop & Workshop condition, not a limp-and-monitor condition.
1
Identify: oil flung onto the front of the engine, down the front of the block, and onto the radiator core. Often mistaken for a sump gasket leak. The distinction is height — crank seal oil appears on the front of the engine at crank pulley height, not just at the bottom.
2
Inspect the timing belt access cover. If you can get to the belt: look for oil sheen, glazing, or darkening of the belt surface. Any oily belt = the belt is compromised. It must be replaced.
3
If the seal is weeping but the belt is confirmed dry and the leak is slow: clean the crank nose, apply Pratley Putty around the seal lip as a temporary dam. This buys limited time — perhaps 200 km at reduced speed — to reach a workshop. No more.
4
There is no safe field replacement for a crank seal in bush conditions — it requires the crank pulley to be pulled, which needs a puller tool. If you carry a puller and the replacement seal, the job is possible on the 1HZ, but time-consuming (2–3 h). Replace the timing belt at the same time — do not put a new seal behind a potentially contaminated belt.
HZJ76R-RKMRS has no valve clearance service on record at 305,000 km. A bent valve from timing belt failure on top of this service gap = very expensive head rebuild. The front crank seal is a direct threat to the belt. Inspect it at every service.
See alsoR20 Seal & Gasket Atlas §1.1R4 Engine TrainingR18 1HZ Troubleshooting
Injection pump side-cover O-ring fuel leak
AMBER — Fire + fuel loss ▼
1
The Denso VE rotary injection pump on the 1HZ has two side-cover O-rings which are the single most common pump leak on high-mileage engines (250–300k+ km). Symptoms: diesel weep on the side of the pump body, fuel smell in the engine bay, drips on the front diff housing underneath.
2
Field fix: clean and degrease the area. Apply self-amalgamating tape tightly wrapped around the pump body at the side cover seam. This is a containment measure only — it will not stop the leak but will slow it. Suitable for 100–200 km to a workshop.
3
Do not attempt to remove the side covers in the field. The injection pump interior requires a clean environment — any dirt ingress destroys precision components. This is a workshop O-ring replacement only.
4
Monitor fuel level carefully — a weeping pump can lose meaningful fuel volume over a long day. Check the dipstick area under the pump at every stop.
Drivable with tape containment. Reduce exposure time — prioritise reaching Dirk (Dup Diesel) or any Denso-trained fuel injection specialist. The O-ring kit is inexpensive and the job is under 1 h for a specialist.

1HZ — Additional Known Failures
Viscous fan clutch failure (overheating at low speed)
RED — Overheat risk ▼
This is the most common cause of 1HZ overheating. The viscous fan clutch is a silicone-fluid coupling that should lock the fan blade to near-full pulley speed when the engine is hot or at low vehicle speed. When it fails, the fan freewheels and provides almost no cooling at low speed — exactly when you need it most in sand, technical terrain, or crawling traffic.
1
Test (engine off, key out, fully cold): grip the fan blade and spin it by hand. A healthy clutch offers significant resistance — the silicone fluid is engaged. A failed clutch spins freely with one finger. Any freely-spinning fan = replace before the next remote trip.
2
A failed viscous clutch cannot be repaired in the field — the silicone fluid is sealed inside. There is no field fix. If you discover this on trip, your only option is to manage engine temperature through behaviour.
3
Behavioural mitigation with a failed clutch: avoid prolonged low-speed work (sand, technical rock). Stop and idle at higher RPM (1,500 RPM) with park brake on if temperature rises — the fan works better at higher engine speed. Keep coolant level full. Avoid towing. Maximise airflow through the grille (remove any bull bar-mounted accessories that block airflow).
4
If temperature gauge rises even on highway: pull over, idle engine at higher RPM with bonnet open until it drops. If it won't recover, Stop & Call.
A 1HZ with a failed viscous clutch driving slowly on a sand track in 35°C heat will overheat. This is a pre-departure inspection item — test the fan before every remote trip, not after you have already left.
See alsoR18 1HZ Troubleshooting — OverheatingR22 Cooling System Guide
Radiator blocked externally (mud, insects, seeds)
AMBER — Common on bush tracks ▼
1
The 76 Series intercooler sits directly in front of the radiator. In African bush conditions, the gap between the two fills rapidly with seed pods, dried grass, insects, and compacted mud. The result is a progressive reduction in airflow — the engine runs hotter on each successive day until it overheats without any mechanical failure.
2
Inspect the radiator/intercooler gap at every stop on bush tracks. Look from the side — can you see daylight through the fins? If not, it needs clearing.
3
Field clearing: remove the intercooler-to-radiator bridging panel if fitted. Use compressed air (12V tyre compressor works) from the engine side to blow debris out toward the front of the vehicle. A soft brush on a long handle can dislodge compacted material. Do not use high-pressure water on a hot radiator.
4
If no compressed air is available: a gentle flush with a container of water from the engine bay side, with the radiator at ambient temperature, will clear loose debris.
Once cleared, coolant temperatures should return to normal immediately. This is a maintenance task, not a repair — build it into the daily walkaround. A blocked core that goes unnoticed compounds with a marginal viscous clutch to produce overheating very quickly.
See alsoR22 Cooling System Guide
Glow plug failure (hard cold starting)
GREEN — Warm conditions ▼
1
The 1HZ has six glow plugs (one per cylinder). In warm African conditions (above 15°C ambient), the 1HZ will cold-start on three or four functioning glow plugs. Below 5°C, all six are needed. Symptoms: slow to start in the morning, white smoke that takes longer than usual to clear, rough idle when cold.
2
Test each plug: disconnect the harness bus bar (one bolt), use a multimeter on continuity or resistance across each plug to chassis earth. A good plug reads 0.5–2 Ω. An open circuit = failed plug. A short to earth = also failed. Note which positions have failed.
3
Failed plugs cannot be field-repaired — they require replacement. However, on the Southern African trip corridor, cold starts below 10°C are rare. In a warm climate, two or three failed plugs may not noticeably affect starting. Use a longer glow period (hold the key in position II for 10–15 s) to compensate for a partial set.
4
If all six plugs have failed and it is cold: ether (cold start fluid) sprayed briefly into the intake will start the engine. Carry a small can. Use sparingly — ether is hard on the 1HZ on repeated use. This is a one-time measure, not a habit.
One to two failed plugs in warm conditions: continue and replace at next workshop. Three or more failed plugs: replace before any high-altitude or cold-weather section of the route.

Power steering pump or box weep (fluid loss)
GREEN — Monitor level ▼
1
Symptoms: light whine or groan on full steering lock, increasing steering effort, fluid drips on the front crossmember. The 76 Series uses an engine-driven hydraulic pump feeding a recirculating ball steering box. Seal weeps are age-related on this platform and rarely sudden.
2
Identify leak point: pump body (output shaft seal), high-pressure hose connections, steering box input shaft, or steering box output shaft (pitman arm seal).
3
Field fix for a weeping hose fitting: tighten the banjo bolt (check for a copper washer — replace if available). For a weeping hose body, wrap with self-amalgamating tape and a hose clamp. Power steering hoses are high-pressure — a proper field patch will hold for 200–300 km.
4
Top up the PS reservoir with ATF Dexron III (the 76's PS system uses ATF, not specific PS fluid). Carry 500 ml. Check level at each stop.
5
If PS fluid is completely lost and pump starts groaning: reduce steering lock usage to protect the pump. At near-zero fluid the pump will fail — if that happens, steering becomes heavy (manual) but the vehicle remains steerable. Drive slowly to the nearest town.
A slow weep managed with top-ups is a long-term situation that does not prevent driving. Replace the seals at the next suitable workshop. Carry 500 ml ATF Dexron III as standard.

Body & Chassis
Cracked or broken chassis mount (bracket, tab, or boss)
AMBER — Load-dependent ▼
1
Identify what the mount supports. A cracked body mount (non-structural) is far less serious than a cracked suspension or diff mount. Assess load path before deciding response.
2
For a broken body mount or panel bracket: clean, apply JB Weld to both surfaces, clamp with a G-clamp or cable ties if possible. Support with a backing plate (cut from aluminium or steel sheet) if the joint will be in tension.
3
For a cracked suspension pickup point (the forward leading arm chassis bracket on this vehicle was plated and welded at Suspension City in June 2025 — inspect this mount at every departure): this requires welding. A temporary fix is to triangulate with a ratchet strap or wire as a tension brace and immediately reduce load and speed. Do not continue in this condition beyond the nearest workshop.
The front suspension leading arm chassis bracket on HZJ76R-RKMRS was repaired at 275,000 km. It is a known inspection point. Any sign of movement or cracking here = Stop & Call immediately.

Broken or cracked exhaust (pipe or manifold joint)
GREEN — Nuisance, manage ▼
1
Symptoms: loud ticking or popping from exhaust, increased exhaust noise. An exhaust leak is not normally a safety emergency — it is a nuisance and long-term engine inefficiency issue.
2
Carbon will often self-seal minor leaks over time as deposits form at the crack. If the leak is audible but small, continue and inspect at each stop.
3
For a larger leak or joint separation: clean the area (the exhaust must be fully cold), apply Gun Gum putty over the crack or joint, wrap with aluminium exhaust tape, and cure by running the engine at idle for 15 minutes then driving gently for 30 minutes.
4
Fencing wire can lash a separated mid-pipe temporarily. Route it over the pipe and around the nearest chassis hanger point. This is a noise fix, not a structural fix.
Do not route exhaust repairs near the turbo downpipe — the SAC turbo operates at very high temperatures at that junction. Gun Gum is rated for these temperatures but requires correct cure before high-boost running.

5 — Limp Strategy

A vehicle that can move, even slowly, is vastly better than one that cannot. Limp mode is a structured decision — not a desperate gamble. Define your parameters before you drive. Know what condition will force you to stop completely before you get in.

Limp mode requires three defined limits before you move: (1) Maximum speed. (2) Maximum distance between inspection stops. (3) Exit condition — the observable symptom that forces you to stop immediately. If any exit condition is triggered, you stop, you do not continue.

Temperature and pressure rise under load. A repair that held at idle may fail under boost on a steep climb. All of these stress a field repair harder than flat highway cruising.

6 — Stop & Call

Some failures cannot be fixed in the field and cannot safely be limped out. This is not failure — it is correct judgement. The following conditions require you to stop the vehicle, secure it and the crew, and activate the communications sequence.

Immediate Stop Conditions — Do Not Drive
  • ✕
    Uncontrolled coolant loss — active overheating.The 1HZ does not have an overheat alarm. Steam from the bonnet, temperature gauge pegged high, or coolant loss that does not respond to the patch = stop now. An overheated 1HZ can bend the head and destroy the engine in minutes.
  • ✕
    Steering loss or significant freeplay on a moving vehicle.A failed drag link or ball joint that produces wheel wander at speed = stop immediately. Pull over gently without sudden inputs.
  • ✕
    Brake failure or significant pedal fade.If the brake pedal goes to the floor or you have asymmetric braking causing the vehicle to pull hard under braking, stop. Use engine braking and handbrake to stop if service brakes are lost.
  • ✕
    Propshaft still attached and flailing.A propshaft oscillating under the vehicle will destroy the tunnel, rupture the fuel tank, and can fracture brake and fuel lines. Stop and remove before driving.
  • ✕
    Cracked block or head (white steam from exhaust with coolant loss).White sweet-smelling steam from the exhaust combined with coolant level drop = head gasket or crack. This is terminal until a workshop rebuild. Stop immediately.
  • ✕
    Fire — any smoke from the engine bay or chassis that is not steam.Stop, kill ignition, all out, extinguisher if accessible and safe, then distance from vehicle. Do not re-enter. Activate emergency beacon.
  • ✕
    Structural fracture at a suspension or steering pickup point.The forward leading arm bracket on this vehicle (known repair point, June 2025) or any welded joint at a diff mount showing active movement = Stop & Call. Driving on a fractured suspension pickup causes total wheel departure.
Communication Activation Sequence
1. Confirm position (GPS)

2. Safety check — crew, fire, hazard

3. Attempt phone / satphone
→

Before activating an emergency beacon: Confirm the situation genuinely exceeds your self-rescue capability. A PLB activation initiates a SAR (Search and Rescue) response that costs significant resources. The test: can you solve this in the next 24 hours using your own kit, skills, and communications? If yes — solve it. If no — activate.
Leave a note. If you leave the vehicle to walk out, write on paper: GPS coordinates, your heading and destination, number of people, vehicle registration, date and time, and your medical status. Tape it to the inside of the windscreen. This is standard overland practice.

7 — What Not To Do

These are the repairs and decisions that routinely turn a recoverable situation into a permanent stranding. Most are well-documented across African and Australian bush mechanics communities — and most happen to experienced drivers who should know better.

  • ✕
    Do not open the cooling system cap when the engine is hot.Even a system that "looks" settled can spray boiling coolant. Wait a minimum of 30 minutes after shutdown. Place a rag over the cap and turn slowly to release pressure before removing.
  • ✕
    Do not add cold water to an overheated engine.Thermal shock on a hot cast-iron 1HZ block can crack the block or head. If you must add water, idle the engine and add slowly in small increments while running.
  • ✕
    Do not keep driving on an overheating engine.Five minutes of over-temperature on the 1HZ bends the head. The 1HZ has no electronic overheat warning. If the temperature gauge moves into the danger zone, you have seconds to pull over — not minutes.
  • ✕
    Do not assume a fuse failure is random — find the short first.Replacing a blown fuse without finding the short will blow the replacement. Replacing with a higher-rated fuse will melt the wire and potentially start a fire. A 10 A circuit gets a 10 A fuse, no exceptions.
  • ✕
    Do not remove the injection pump cover in the field.The Denso VE rotary injection pump requires specialist tools and a clean environment to service. Any contamination introduced during a field attempt will destroy the pump. If the pump has failed, Stop & Call.
  • ✕
    Do not apply JB Weld or Pratley to a wet or oily surface.Either compound will not adhere. Degrease with brake cleaner, dry completely, then apply. A poorly bonded repair is worse than no repair — it will fail under load and take the surrounding material with it.
  • ✕
    Do not drive on a SAT propshaft that is still physically attached but vibrating.A vibrating propshaft will fatigue-crack the diff flanges, punch holes in the tunnel, and sever brake lines. Remove it entirely before driving. Store it properly — you may need it again.
  • ✕
    Do not over-torque a fastener on a cold engine or in aluminium.Aluminium heads and brackets are soft. African field mechanics tend to overtighten when they're nervous. Use a torque wrench. If you are guessing, err slightly under and recheck when warm.
  • ✕
    Do not mix brake fluid types.The 76 uses DOT 3/DOT 4. Mixing with DOT 5 (silicone) will contaminate the entire system and destroy seals. If topping up, use the same spec that is already in the system. If in doubt, do not top up and drive conservatively until you can confirm.
  • ✕
    Do not improvise a hydraulic brake or clutch line repair without a proper flare kit.A hose clamp on a brake line will not hold. A JB Weld patch on a brake line will not hold. A brake line leak is a Stop condition — the vehicle cannot be safely driven until the line is replaced or professionally repaired.
  • ✕
    Do not drive in 4WD on dry tar for more than a few hundred metres.The 76 has a part-time 4WD system with no centre differential. On hard, high-traction surfaces, drivetrain windup will stress the transfer case, front diff, and propshafts. On tar: 2WD only unless you are specifically navigating a section of 4WD terrain.
  • ✕
    Do not leave a field repair unmonitored on the next drive.Stop 10 km after any field repair and inspect it. The first heat-and-vibration cycle is when repairs fail. Catching a failed repair early at 10 km is far better than discovering it at 100 km.
8 — Field Documentation

A field repair that is not documented is a ticking clock. Workshop mechanics cannot properly diagnose what they cannot see, and a temporary repair that is forgotten becomes a permanent risk. Good documentation also builds your own fault pattern awareness over time.

What to Record for Every Fault Event
  • 1
    Date, location (GPS coordinates), and odometer reading.The minimum addressable record. Cross-reference to trip log for route context.
  • 2
    What the fault was — system, symptom, and your diagnosis.Distinguish between what you observed and what you concluded. "Coolant level dropped 200 ml — suspected hose split at lower radiator inlet" is more useful than "overheating issue".
  • 3
    What you did — materials, procedure, time taken.Be specific. "Applied one layer self-amalgamating tape and two 40 mm hose clamps to split at lower radiator hose, 60 mm from inlet stub" gives the workshop exactly what they need to see.
  • 4
    Photographs — before and after repair, from multiple angles.A photograph is worth three paragraphs of description. Take more than you think you need. WhatsApp to workshop contact in advance if connectivity allows.
  • 5
    Condition on departure — is the repair holding? Any residual symptoms?Note any residual noise, smell, or handling change. If the repair appeared sound at departure, note that too — it is evidence that the procedure was correct.
  • 6
    Mark it clearly as TEMPORARY with the required permanent repair.Write "TEMP — requires hose replacement" or "TEMP — requires TIG weld to chassis bracket". Do not let the word "temporary" become ambiguous over time.
Transfer to R8 Service History
Every field fault event — whether repaired or not — must be transferred to R8 (Service History) on return. Record it in the Repairs & Events section with the same detail used in the Fault Log. This ensures the service history reflects the vehicle's actual state at all times, and that workshop contacts (Dirk / Dup Diesel, Cape Transmissions, Suspension City) have a complete record when they next see the vehicle.

9 — Pre-Departure Skills & Tool Audit

This checklist audits your readiness to execute field repairs before you leave for a remote area. It is not a mechanical inspection — it is a capability audit. Complete it before any trip where recovery or workshop support is more than 150 km away.

  • 1
    JB Weld and Pratley Putty — in date and unopened or sealed.Expired product sets soft. Check the mixed remaining in tube kits — partially used two-part epoxy does not store reliably beyond 12 months once opened.
  • 2
    Self-amalgamating tape — at least two rolls, tested not brittle.Tape that has dried out will not amalgamate. Test by pulling a 30 cm length and stretching to 50% — it should stretch without tearing.
  • 3
    Hose clamp set — correct sizes for coolant and turbo hoses.Measure the upper and lower radiator hose OD and turbo inlet OD. Carry 2× clamps per hose size. Check stainless grade — cheap mild steel clamps corrode and snap.
  • 4
    Spare alternator belt — correct part number for 1HZ.Mark the part number on the belt bag. The 1HZ uses a V-belt, not a serpentine. Replacement in the field takes 20 minutes. Carry one belt minimum.
  • 5
    Spare thermostat — 82°C rated, complete with new gasket.Weighs 80 g, costs R50. The field thermostat removal procedure (Section 4) works without a spare — but a replacement allows return to normal operation.
  • 6
    Spare lift pump — correct part for 1HZ.Carry one sealed, with a new paper gasket. See Section 4 for replacement procedure. The 1HZ lift pump is the single most field-serviceable component in the fuel system.
  • 7
    Coolant hose — at least upper and lower radiator hose.The upper hose is the most frequent failure point. Carry it folded in the tool bag. A hose splice kit (two pieces of appropriately sized silicon hose and four clamps) covers unexpected sizes.
  • 8
    Spare rear leaf spring U-bolts — correct size for OME CS058R pack.Fitted January 2026. Carry four bolts, matching nuts, and flat washers. Torque specification: 90 Nm.
  • 9
    Gun Gum exhaust putty — fresh tube.Expires — check date. Carry a 400 g tin. Confirm applicator nozzle is clean and open.
  • 10
    Electrical repair kit — wire, terminals, fuses, multimeter.Multimeter is the single most useful diagnostic tool on the 76. Carry spare AA batteries for it. Confirm multimeter continuity and DC voltage functions are working.
  • 11
    Torque wrench — 0–150 Nm range, accessible.Not at the bottom of the tool bag. Suspension bolts and U-bolts require correct torque. Guessing by feel on a 76 at this mileage is not good enough.
  • 12
    Floor jack and axle stands — rated for 2,160 kg minimum, inspected.Test the jack on a level surface before departure. Inspect the axle stand locking mechanism. A jack that fails under the vehicle during a field repair creates an emergency from a breakdown.
  • 13
    Brake cleaner — at least one full 400 ml aerosol.Your primary surface prep tool for any adhesive repair. Without it, no JB Weld, Pratley, or Gun Gum repair will bond correctly to a vehicle surface.
  • 14
    Fencing wire — 3 m coil, galvanised.Carry a fresh coil. Wire fatigues with repeated bending — used wire from the previous trip may snap when you need it most.
  • 15
    Spare fastener bag — M6/M8/M10/M12 bolts, nuts, and washers.Check after each trip that the bag has been restocked. Lost hardware is the most common bush stranding cause after tyre issues.
  • 16
    Can you bleed the 1HZ fuel system from memory?If not, review Section 4 Fuel — Bad Fuel card and the R24 Fuel System Guide before departure. This is a 10-minute skill that prevents a 3-day wait.
  • 17
    Can you replace the alternator belt in under 30 minutes?Practice it once at home with the engine cold. The routing is simple on the 1HZ but unfamiliar hands in the dark in the rain will take 90 minutes.
  • 18
    Do you have GPS coordinates for the nearest workshop in each country on your route?Compile this list from T4A, iOverlander, and your overlanding network before departure. Coordinates, not just town names. Store in both phone and dedicated GPS device.
  • 19
    PLB / Garmin inReach registered and tested.Self-test the PLB per manufacturer instructions. Confirm COSPASS-SARSAT registration is current. Test inReach message send/receive with a non-SOS message.
  • 20
    Has the Fault Log been cleared (previous entries transferred to R8)?Start each trip with a fresh log. Carry-over entries create confusion about whether a recorded item has been addressed. Transfer all previous entries to R8 before departure.
