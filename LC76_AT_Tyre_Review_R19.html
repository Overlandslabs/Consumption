Why 3-Ply Sidewall Matters for Africa

African overland routes combine the worst of multiple worlds for tyres: sharp basalt and quartzite rock edges that slash sidewalls at low pressure, heavy sand corrugations that flex the carcass continuously, and long tar transits that cook under-spec rubber. The sidewall — not the tread — is typically the failure point. A 3-ply (or equivalent CoreGard/reinforced) sidewall dramatically reduces pinch flats, slashing, and bruising when aired down to 1.2–1.5 bar on soft terrain.

On the 1HZ low-boost turbo at ~2,500 kg loaded, you also need a genuine Load Range E (10-ply rated) tyre — not a passenger-car load index in the same size. Load rating 123/120R (1,550/1,220 kg per tyre) is the target.

Note on "3-ply sidewall" terminology: This refers to three structural plies in the sidewall construction specifically — separate from total ply rating. BFGoodrich CoreGard technology achieves equivalent protection through thicker, race-derived rubber rather than ply count alone. Both approaches are valid; all tyres in this shortlist meet the 3-ply/equivalent standard.

05 — Technical Notes

What Actually Matters — Beyond the Marketing

Tyre selection for a loaded LC76 expedition involves several technical decisions that go beyond brand preference. These notes cover the key factors specific to your vehicle and use case.

Load Range E vs D — Why It Matters for the LC76

The 1HZ LC76 at full overland load sits around 2,400–2,500 kg. At GVM (2,850 kg), four Load Range E tyres at 123 load index give a combined theoretical capacity of 6,200 kg — a comfortable 2.2× safety margin. Load Range D at 119 load index (1,360 kg per tyre) gives 5,440 kg combined — still adequate on paper, but the margin compresses significantly under heat, sustained speed on corrugations, and load shifting. On a two-year expedition across East Africa, LR-E is the right call. Reserve LR-D only for tyres you're using for pre-departure daily driving.

3-Ply Sidewall — What the Term Actually Means

The term is used inconsistently in the industry. In a modern radial LT tyre, "3-ply sidewall" refers to three structural cord plies running through the sidewall carcass specifically — separate from total ply rating, which includes belt plies across the tread crown. Most LT tyres are 2-ply sidewall carcass. The 3-ply construction adds a full third carcass ply wrapping around the bead, through the sidewall, and into the shoulder — directly where sidewall cuts and pinch flats happen on African terrain.

BFGoodrich's CoreGard achieves equivalent protection through a different route — thicker, harder rubber compound derived from Baja race development rather than an extra ply layer. The Falken Wildpeak's Outer Apex adds a secondary bead apex as an internal heat shield. All approaches are valid; the goal is the same: resistance to sidewall splitting at low pressures on sharp rock.

BFGoodrich CoreGard Technology

Both KO2 and KO3 use CoreGard rubber in the sidewall — a thicker, harder compound derived from BFG's Baja T/A racing programme. It achieves equivalent or superior puncture and split resistance to a 3-ply construction through material quality rather than ply count. The computer-modelled sidewall profile deflects protruding objects away from the carcass. Claimed 20% tougher than the previous KO sidewall in internal split tests. This is arguably the toughest sidewall in the AT segment.

Tread Depth — Why It Matters More Than You Think

Factory tread depth determines not just wear life but how long grip and self-cleaning performance remain consistent. Most AT tyres lose meaningful traction in mud and soft terrain once worn below 6–7/32″. Starting with 18/32″ (Wildpeak AT3W LT) vs 15/32″ (KO2, AT3G) gives significantly more usable performance life before grip degrades — important if you're running one set for 80,000+ km across the full expedition without replacement.

Tread depth also affects heat resistance: a thicker tread block retains more thermal mass and resists chunking and tearing at high sustained speeds on corrugated gravel. This is why the KO3's new gravel compound specifically addresses the chipping seen on the KO2 at highway speed on African gravel.

Tread Compound — Silica vs Standard Rubber

Silica-based compounds (Cooper AT3 LT, Falken Wildpeak, BFG KO3) deliver better wet grip and longer wear life, particularly on tar. They remain more flexible at lower temperatures and resist cracking under UV and ozone exposure — relevant for a vehicle that may sit for extended periods between expedition legs. Standard rubber compounds (some variants of the AT3G) can harden and crack faster in harsh African conditions if the vehicle is parked in the sun for extended periods. Silica compound is the preferred choice for any tyre spending significant time in equatorial Africa.

Stone Ejectors and Drilling Resistance

Stone drilling — where a small stone lodges in a tread groove and works its way through the carcass over time — is a significant failure mode on African gravel roads. It causes slow punctures and internal carcass damage that isn't visible externally. Stone ejector ribs (Cooper AT3 LT, Maxxis AT811, KO3) and stone ejector grooves (Wildpeak AT3W) are specifically engineered to prevent this. If you're driving extended periods on sharp quartzite or basalt gravel (common in Zambia, Tanzania, and the Drakensberg foothills), stone ejectors are not a marketing feature — they're functional and measurably reduce this failure mode.

Dunlop AT3G — Verify Before You Buy

Important: The AT3G is sold in SA in both SUV (2-ply) and LT (3-ply/10PR) configurations. Multiple SA retailers list the AT3G as "2-ply construction" for standard SUV sizes. You must specifically purchase the LT 265/75R16 10PR 123/120Q variant — this is the one with 3-ply construction and the WLT (white letter) sidewall. The standard 265/75R16 116T version is almost certainly 2-ply. If in doubt, ask the retailer to confirm "LT 10 ply rated" on the invoice before purchase.

Tyre Age and Storage — The Overlooked Risk

All tyres have a manufacture date stamped on the sidewall (DOT code — last 4 digits are week/year, e.g. 2824 = week 28, 2024). African UV, heat, and ozone conditions accelerate rubber degradation significantly faster than European or North American norms. A tyre older than 5 years at the time of fitment is a risk on a two-year remote expedition regardless of tread depth. Always check the DOT code when purchasing and reject tyres manufactured more than 2–3 years prior. This is especially important when sourcing tyres in-country during the expedition — older unsold stock in hot climates is a real problem.

Airing Down — Pressures for the LC76

At overland load (~2,400 kg laden), correct airing-down targets for LR-E tyres:

Tar / highway: 2.8–3.0 bar  |  Hard gravel/corrugations: 2.0–2.2 bar  |  Rocky terrain: 1.4–1.6 bar  |  Soft sand: 1.0–1.3 bar

All LR-E tyres in this shortlist handle the full range safely at the vehicle's load. The 3-ply or equivalent sidewall is critical at 1.0–1.3 bar — at these pressures the sidewall bulges significantly and any sharp edge that makes contact will cut straight through a 2-ply tyre. Never air below 1.0 bar on an LR-E LT tyre without bead locks. Always re-inflate before returning to corrugated gravel or tar — sustained driving on corrugations below 1.8 bar generates damaging heat in the sidewall.

Rotation and Wear — Expedition Planning

Running five tyres (four plus spare) on a consistent rotation schedule extends tyre life substantially. On an even rotation, a set of five tyres will last 25% longer than four — a full tyre's worth of extra life. For an 80,000 km expedition, this means one rotation every 12,000–16,000 km including the spare. Track tyre wear depth at each service. Replace all five when any tyre reaches 4/32″ tread depth — don't run a mixed worn/new set on expedition. The handling imbalance in sand and on corrugations is significant and dangerous.
