Service Highlights — Apr 2026 @ 308,248km

⚠️ 1 · Cylinder Head Oil Seepage — Apr 2026
Slow oil weep at rear of head noted by PW Auto Centre. Chemical block test passed — head gasket intact. Identify exact source before expedition.

⚠️ Slow oil weep noted at head · 308,248km

Oil seepage noted at the rear of the head by PW Auto Centre. Chemical block test (Radiator King, Apr 2026) confirmed no combustion gases in coolant — head gasket is intact. The seepage is almost certainly a top-end seal, not a head-to-block breach.

Probable sources — in order of likelihood for a turbocharged 1HZ:

1. Rocker/valve cover gasket — the most common source of top-end oil sweating on the 1HZ, particularly with a turbo fitted. The gasket hardens with age and weeps. Mechanics familiar with turbocharged 1HZs specifically flag this. Fix: genuine Toyota rocker cover gasket + front and rear rubber grommets under the cover.
2. Half-moon seal (part 11183-54011) — aluminium plug at the rear of the head under the rocker cover. Should be sealed with silicone on assembly; can weep if not, or if sealant has aged. Fix: remove rocker cover, reseal with silicone.
3. Crankcase pressurisation via PCV routing — with a turbo fitted, if the PCV breather feeds back into the intake post-intercooler rather than to the turbo inlet, boost pressure can back-pressurise the crankcase and push oil out at every top-end seal. Worth verifying the SAC setup with Dirk.

4. Oil gallery feed restrictor (part 11448-17010) — there is a small restrictor plug at the rear of the block where the oil gallery passes through to the head. Located close to the edge of the head gasket. Known to seep on high-km 1HZs; described as fairly common by rebuilders. Fix: replace restrictor and re-torque.
5. Intake manifold gasket — if crankcase is pressurised and breather feeds back into the intake, pressurised oil mist can push out at the manifold-to-head face. Less likely but worth noting if the above are ruled out.
6. Head gasket (external oil leak) — possible but least likely. The head gasket does have an oil gallery at the rear near the block edge; at high km this can seep. However the chemical block test confirmed no internal breach. If the seep is at the head-to-block face below the rocker gear oil port, escalate.

Action before expedition: Clean the entire area thoroughly. Run the engine to temperature and observe exactly where oil first appears. Rocker cover gasket + half-moon seal + grommet replacement is a straightforward service item and should be the first intervention — inexpensive and highly likely to resolve it. If seepage continues after that, investigate the oil gallery restrictor and PCV routing.

⚠️ 2 · Rear Hub Seal Leak — Apr 2026
Hub seal weeping — risk of oil tracking onto rear brake shoes. Shoes replaced Jan 2026 @ 293,949km.

⚠️ Rear hub seal weeping · 308,248km

The rear axle hub seals retain gear oil in the axle housing. A weeping seal allows oil to track onto the brake drum and rear brake shoes. On the LC76, the rear drums are the primary parking brake — oil-contaminated shoes lose friction coefficient completely.

Action: Check both rear drums for oil contamination before the expedition. A wet shoe requires immediate hub seal replacement and new shoes — do not drive. Confirm which side is leaking and monitor for progression. Rear brake shoes were replaced Jan 2026 @ 293,949km — contamination of those shoes within 14,299km would be a fast failure.

⚠️ 3 · Fat Knuckle Grease Leak — Apr 2026
Front knuckle grease escaping — birfield runs dry without grease. Knuckle kit replaced Feb 2025 @ 271,200km.

⚠️ Front knuckle grease escaping · 308,248km

The front axle birfield joints (constant velocity joints) on the LC76 run inside the steering knuckle housing, packed with grease. The knuckle seal retains this grease. Loss of grease means the birfield runs dry — once it is dry, wear accelerates rapidly and CV failure follows. The front knuckle/spindle overhaul kit was replaced Feb 2025 @ 271,200km — 37,048km ago.

Action: Confirm which side is leaking. Re-pack the knuckle with correct birfield grease (Molybdenum disulphide grease) and inspect the knuckle seal for damage. If the seal face is damaged, replace the seal before the expedition. Do not leave this for the field — a dry birfield in deep sand or river crossings accelerates to failure quickly.

Critical Inspections & Red Flags

⚠️ Daily/Weekly Visual Checks
Regular visual checks prevent catastrophic failures.

Daily/Weekly:

□ Viscous fan (should NOT spin freely when hot)
□ Boost leaks (listen for hissing)
□ Oil leaks (turbo, rocker cover, rear main seal)
□ Coolant level
□ EGT readings under load

Every 10,000km:

□ Blow-by check (oil filler cap @ idle)
□ Turbo shaft play
□ Intercooler boots (oil sweating)
□ Air intake (smoke test)

🚨 Red Flag Conditions — STOP DRIVING
These symptoms indicate imminent failure. Continued driving causes catastrophic damage.

STOP IMMEDIATELY if you observe:

❌ Rising EGT under same load • ❌ Sudden coolant loss • ❌ White smoke (head gasket) • ❌ Excessive black smoke • ❌ Loss of power + high temps • ❌ Metallic engine noises
