1 · System Overview

The 76's brakes are simple, robust and hydraulically split — know the five parts and the one quirk.

Front
Discs & Calipers
Ventilated front discs with floating calipers. Pads wear faster than the rear; they do most of the stopping work, especially loaded and downhill.
Rear
Drums & Shoes
Rear drum brakes with shoes and wheel cylinders. More tolerant of mud and water than discs, but the linings are vulnerable to oil contamination from a leaking hub seal (§6).
Master
Tandem Master + Booster
A dual-circuit (front/rear split) tandem master cylinder with a vacuum booster. The split means a single circuit failure still leaves partial braking — degraded, not zero.
LSPV
Proportioning Valve
A load-sensing proportioning valve (LSPV) at the rear axle varies rear brake pressure with axle load — more pressure when heavily laden, less when light, to resist rear lock-up. It has its own bleed nipple that must not be skipped (§4).
Shared
One Reservoir, Two Jobs
The fluid reservoir feeds both the brakes and the hydraulic clutch. This is the single most important system fact on this vehicle — see the box below.
The shared reservoir. Brakes and the Terrain Tamer hydraulic clutch draw from the same reservoir. A dropping fluid level therefore means a leak in either circuit — and a leak that empties the reservoir takes out both. Check the level daily; a spongy clutch pedal can be the first warning of a brake-side problem and vice versa. (R3, R13.)

2 · Fluid & Hydraulics

Right spec, changed on time, never contaminated.

ItemSpec / rule
Fluid specDOT 3 or DOT 4 (the factory spec is DOT 3; the vehicle currently runs DOT 4, which is fully compatible and has a higher boiling point — preferred for loaded, hot, mountain work). Never DOT 5 (silicone).
Change intervalTime-based — every 2 years regardless of mileage. Brake fluid is hygroscopic: it absorbs moisture, which lowers the boiling point and corrodes internals, with no visible change. You change it on the calendar, not on appearance.
Current stateFull fluid change Jan 2026 @ 293,949 km. Next due ~Jan 2028. (Owned by R8/R23; repeated here for the brake picture.)
Reservoir levelKeep between MIN and MAX. A falling level is a leak (brake or clutch circuit) until proven otherwise — never just top up and forget.
PaintBrake fluid strips paint. Wipe spills off bodywork immediately and flush with water.
Never mix DOT 5 (silicone) into a DOT 3/4 system. It will not mix, it contaminates the entire system and destroys the rubber seals. If the fluid history is unknown, flush completely with fresh DOT 4 rather than topping up blind. (R27.)

4 · Bleeding the Brakes

For a full fluid change, a spongy pedal, after any hydraulic work, or if the reservoir has run dry. The 76-specific trap is the LSPV.

Order — Furthest From Master First
RR→ LR→ LSPV→ RF→ LF

The principle is to bleed in order of descending distance from the master cylinder, ending at the nearest corner. The widely-used 70-series order is rear-right, rear-left, the LSPV bleeder, then right-front and left-front. Exact left/right ordering follows line length on a right-hand-drive vehicle — confirm the precise sequence against the vehicle's service manual; what matters most is that clean, bubble-free fluid reaches every point, the LSPV included.

Do not skip the LSPV bleeder. The classic 70-series mistake is bleeding the four wheels and leaving air trapped in the load-sensing proportioning valve on the rear axle — the result is weak or unbalanced rear braking that no amount of wheel bleeding will fix. If the LSPV nipple is rusted or seized, free it (penetrating oil, wire brush) before you start, not after.
Two-Person Method
1
Top the reservoir to MAX. Keep it between MIN and MAX throughout — never let it run dry, or you draw air back into the master and start over.
2
Fit a clear tube to the first bleed nipple, into a part-filled jar so you can see bubbles. Start at the point furthest from the master.
3
Assistant pumps the pedal a few times and holds it firmly down.
4
Crack the nipple ¼–½ turn; fluid and air escape and the pedal drops. Close the nipple before the assistant releases the pedal.
5
Assistant releases the pedal slowly. Repeat roughly five cycles per point — until the fluid runs clean and bubble-free.
6
Top the reservoir, move to the next point in sequence, and repeat. Do the LSPV in its place in the order. Finish at the corner nearest the master.
7
Reservoir to MAX, cap on. Test the pedal: firm and high, no sponginess. Test braking at low speed in a safe area before driving normally.
One-person options. A vacuum bleeder draws fluid from each nipple in the same order; a pressure bleeder pushes from the reservoir. Either is fine — the order and the "keep the reservoir wet" rule are unchanged. Carry a simple vacuum bleed kit and 500 ml of DOT 4 (R3).

6 · Hub-Seal → Shoe Contamination Monitor

An active monitor on this vehicle, surfaced here because it shows up as a brake fault.

Active monitor (Facts §3). The rear hub seal is on the watch list for leaking, and a leaking rear hub seal lets axle oil reach the rear brake shoes. Oil-contaminated shoes lose their friction — degreasing rarely restores them.

What you'll notice: rear braking that feels weak or grabby, a pull, or a faint burning smell under braking. A check at the drum confirms it.

1
At every rear wheel-off service, pull the drum and look at the shoe linings and the inside of the backing plate for an oil film or glazing.
2
Check the hub/axle seal as the source. A weeping seal is the cause — the shoes are the symptom.
3
If contaminated: replace the shoes and fix the seal. Do not just clean the linings and refit — the oil has soaked in and the friction is gone.

7 · Field Doctrine & Limits

The hard lines that keep a brake problem from becoming an accident. Cross-ref R27.

STOP CONDITION — A brake-line leak means the vehicle is not safely driveable until the line is replaced.
  • ✕Hose clamp on a brake line.It will not hold the pressure. This is not a field repair — it is a delay before failure.
  • ✕JB Weld / epoxy patch on a brake line.Will not hold. Brake hydraulic pressure is far beyond what a patch survives.
  • ✕Driving on a leaking circuit.The split system gives you partial braking only — move the vehicle to safety, gently, then fix it. Do not continue a journey on it.
  • ✕Anti-seize on disc or drum friction / mating surfaces.Compromises torque retention and contaminates the friction material. Keep all grease and anti-seize off rotors, drums and linings.
  • ✕DOT 5 silicone fluid.Never in this system (§2).
Do use anti-seize on caliper guide pins and slider hardware — it prevents seizure between dissimilar metals and keeps the caliper floating freely. The rule is simple: lubricant on the mechanism, never on the friction.

8 · Braking Technique

Brief — the full terrain treatment lives in R28.

Long descents: select a low gear and let engine braking do the work; brake in firm, intermittent applications rather than riding the pedal, which overheats fluid and fades the brakes. Clay mud: do not brake mid-section — locked wheels mean no steering and the mud packs the brakes; carry momentum and let off the throttle instead. For sand, water, rock and corrugation technique, see R28 Driving Conditions Guide.
