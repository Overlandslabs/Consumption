Expedition Systems Map
Subsystem couplings, failure cascades & the seams between them
2009 LC76 SW · 1HZ 4.2L Diesel · low-boost turbo (0.6 bar) · intercooler
Last updated: 12 July 2026
Sections 1. Why a Map 2. Systems & Couplings 3. Failure Cascades 4. Seams Register 5. Sensing & Feedback 6. Using the Map

Every other reference document in this library owns a part — cooling, brakes, tyres, money, comms. This one owns the connections. A two-year expedition rarely fails inside a single subsystem; it fails in the seams between them, where one small failure quietly starts a clock on something else. This map names the six systems the expedition runs on, shows how they couple, traces the cascades that follow the most consequential single failures, and lists the interfaces to watch. It is the trunk of the tree — the structure everything visible depends on.

§1 Why a Systems Map Parts vs whole · seams · second-order effects

Analysis is not enough. Taking the expedition apart — vehicle, crew, route, money, comms, paperwork — and understanding each part is necessary and this library does it thoroughly. But the essential properties of the expedition belong to the whole, not the parts. A vehicle that starts, a crew that is trained, a route that is mapped and insurance that is paid can still produce a failed trip, because failure lives in the interactions. The alternator does not end the expedition; the alternator → house charging → fridge → food → morale → decision-quality chain can.

Three habits this document encodes:

1 · Ask the second-order questionWhen anything fails, the first question is the obvious one — how do I fix this? The systems question comes immediately after: what has this failure started the clock on? Most field failures are survivable in themselves; what bites is the second thing that goes wrong while the first is being managed, on a timer nobody noticed had started.
2 · Trouble originates at interfacesIndividually engineered subsystems are usually sound; the places where two systems hand over to each other — belt to alternator, crew to machine, money to border, plan to weather — are where slack, ambiguity and neglect accumulate. §4 lists the expedition's seams and the signal to watch at each.
3 · Feedback beats predictionOn a 60,000+ km trip nothing can be forecast to the end; the honest alternative is to instrument the system and respond to what it says. §5 lists what the expedition measures, how often, and what trend triggers action. The library's oil-analysis programme, EGT/boost gauges, fuel logs and cost-per-km trending already exist for exactly this reason — this section simply gathers the listening apparatus in one place.

This document changes no doctrine. Every response it points to already lives in an owning document — R18 diagnoses, R38 decides, R29 evacuates. What R41 adds is the connective tissue: symptom → structure → consequence → owning document.

The expedition is a socio-technical system of six interacting subsystems. Each has an owning document family; none operates alone.

The coupling matrix. Read it as: the row system affects the column system through the named interface. The strongest couplings — the ones worth carrying in your head — are marked in amber.

Principal couplings — interface between systems
CouplingInterfaceHow it transmits failure
Vehicle → CrewWorkload & heat of field repairsA hard breakdown consumes crew energy and daylight; fatigue then degrades every later decision.
Vehicle → MoneyUnscheduled spendMajor repairs draw down the cash stock that also buys fuel, visas and margin. R8 shows unscheduled/renewal spend dominates lifetime cost.
Vehicle → PaperworkCarnetThe vehicle IS a customs liability. A written-off or abandoned vehicle does not end the obligation — disposal must happen under customs supervision or the carnet bond is called (R29 §8).
Crew → VehicleOperating skill & judgementMost vehicle damage is decision damage: speed on corrugations, a crossing not walked, a warning gauge unread. R37 exists to harden this interface.
Crew → CommsThe check-in disciplineHome's escalation tree (R38 §1) fires on silence. A missed check-in from forgetfulness triggers the same machinery as a real emergency.
Energy → VehicleCrank battery & alternatorThe house system is islanded from the crank side by design; the alternator is the single charge source for the Willard 650. See cascade C1.
Energy → CrewFridge, water, devices, navigationHouse-power loss degrades food safety, comms charging and navigation together — three crew-support functions on one bus.
Money → RouteFuel purchasing & border feesMoney access failure converts directly into a mobility limit and a visa-clock problem. See cascade C7.
Paperwork → RouteVisa validity windowsEvery entry stamp starts a countdown. Any delay — mechanical, medical, financial — spends visa days that were budgeted for travel.
Route → VehicleSurface, fuel quality, distanceCorrugation fatigues components; poor diesel attacks the freshly rebuilt injection system (R24); remoteness multiplies the cost of every failure above.
Route → CrewHeat, disease vectors, securityEnvironment loads the crew system continuously — malaria zones, heat stress, security vigilance (R17, R35, R36).
Comms → Crew (reverse)Home's picture of the expeditionComms failure doesn't just isolate the crew — it triggers escalation, worry and potentially a search on the home side. The coupling runs both directions. See cascade C6.
Why six, not oneThe instinct is to treat "the expedition" as the vehicle plus everything else. The matrix shows why that fails: of the twelve strongest couplings, only five have the vehicle on either end. The crew, money, paperwork and comms systems fail each other directly, without the vehicle ever being involved.
§3 Failure Cascade Register Trigger · chain · clocks started · chain-breaker

The ten most consequential single failures, each traced past the obvious first effect. Format: the chain (first, second, third order), the clocks started (the hidden timers), and the chain-breaker (the action or preparation that stops the cascade early). The owning documents carry the detailed response — this register carries the shape.

C1Alternator FailureVehicle → Energy → Crew
  • 1st orderEngine keeps running. The 1HZ's injection is fully mechanical — no electrical dependency once started. This is the deceptive part: nothing dramatic happens.
  • 2nd orderThe Willard 650 crank battery stops charging (alternator is its only source). The house bank stops receiving DC-DC charge; solar becomes the sole house input.
  • 3rd orderOnce the crank battery depletes below starting capability, the vehicle is immobile at the next shutdown. House loads (fridge, comms charging, navigation) survive on solar alone — degraded in cloud, dust or short winter days.
Clocks started: crank-battery reserve (days of starts, worsened by glow-plug draw on cold mornings); food safety once the fridge budget tightens; distance to a repair-capable town.
Chain-breaker: RA-4 rotating-electrics redundancy — spare alternator or brush/regulator kit carried (R3 ledger, EX-8). Doctrine: on alternator warning, do NOT shut down until parked somewhere recoverable; minimise starts; conserve crank battery absolutely. Emergency bridge: the house bank can jump the crank side in extremis (R32 §7 routine, R26 wiring).
Owns the response: R26 wiring · R32 crank care · R18 diagnosis · R3 spares
C2Tyre Casing DestroyedVehicle → Route → Paperwork
  • 1st orderA sidewall tear or run-flat destruction beyond plugging. Wheel change to the spare (R40 §2) — mobility restored, redundancy spent.
  • 2nd orderThe expedition is now running with zero tyre redundancy until the carcass is swapped onto the damaged wheel's rim (R40 §3–§5) or a replacement is sourced. North of the Zambezi, matching 265/75R16 stock is thin — sourcing can mean days and a detour.
  • 3rd orderThe sourcing detour spends visa days and budget; pushing on without redundancy converts the next puncture from an inconvenience into a mobility emergency in a remote area.
Clocks started: visa validity in the current country; distance to the next tyre-capable town (profile §serviceability); the risk-exposure window travelled without a spare.
Chain-breaker: the carried carcass (expedition only, matched to the fitted 265/75 set — RA-9) plus the pre-departure carcass-swap drill, both crew signed off in R37. The open owner decision on a second-spare / casing-repair strategy for East Africa is precisely a decision about how early this chain gets broken.
Owns the response: R40 procedures · R14 strategy · R37 drill sign-off · profiles §serviceability
C3Crew Member IncapacitatedCrew → everything
  • 1st orderIllness or injury takes one of two crew out of action — the expedition's single largest capacity loss, instantly halving every capability.
  • 2nd orderThe remaining crew member must simultaneously provide care, run comms escalation (R38 §1, R29 §7), drive, navigate and make decisions alone — the exact scenario RA-8 trains for.
  • 3rd orderIf evacuation follows, the vehicle and the healthy crew member are separated from the patient; the vehicle becomes a single-handed repatriation or long-stay problem (R38 §3), and the carnet clock keeps running regardless.
Clocks started: medical golden hours (condition-dependent); insurance case-opening requirements (R29 — insurer contact of record, first-call discipline); visa validity for a hospital stay; Bonitas day-85 lapse if the stay runs long.
Chain-breaker: RA-6 (WFA training both crew, screenings, prescribing-doctor kit) reduces the probability; RA-8 (Mary-Ann solo-capability, designed into the Karoo loop) and R37's no-single-point-of-failure rule make the second-order survivable. The SOS drill (R29 §7 / R10 §9) is rehearsed, not improvised.
Owns the response: R29 medevac · R10 emergency drill · R38 §1 escalation · R37 capability · R17 health
C4Timing Belt FailureVehicle — terminal branch
  • 1st orderThe 1HZ is an interference engine: belt failure means pistons meet open valves. This is not a roadside repair — it is engine destruction, bent valves at minimum.
  • 2nd orderThe expedition stops where the belt broke. Recovery is a flatbed to a capable town, an engine-out job of weeks, or repatriation (R38 §3).
  • 3rd orderCarnet disposal obligations, visa extensions, accommodation burn and the abort decision (R38 §2) all activate together.
Clocks started: every clock at once — this is the cascade the library works hardest to make impossible rather than survivable.
Chain-breaker: RA-3 — pre-departure belt + tensioner replacement (~T-3 months), removing the ~373,000 km interval from the expedition window entirely; the removed belt travels as the emergency spare. This is the textbook systems move: spend once at a workshop to delete a terminal branch from the field tree.
Owns the response: R23 pre-trip protocol · R4 engine · R38 §2–§3 if it ever happens
C5Cooling System FailureVehicle — fast escalation
  • 1st orderA hose lets go, the viscous hub gives up, or the radiator core fails — coolant temperature climbs. Caught at the gauge, this is a stop-and-fix.
  • 2nd orderDriven on, overheat becomes head-gasket failure within minutes — converting a R500 hose into a top-end rebuild far from home.
  • 3rd orderA cooked engine in a remote area follows the C4 terminal branch: recovery, repatriation, abort machinery.
Clocks started: minutes, not days — cooling is the fastest cascade in the register. The only clock that matters is how quickly the driver reacts to the gauge.
Chain-breaker: RA-5 (radiator core, viscous hub, hoses assessed and replaced on condition pre-departure) plus the standing doctrine: rising temperature = stop NOW, not at the next convenient spot. Carried hoses, Gun Gum and fencing wire (R3 §7) handle the field repair; the discipline handles the cascade.
Owns the response: R22 cooling · R18 diagnosis · R27 field repair · R3 spares
C6Comms BlackoutComms → Home → Crew (reverse cascade)
  • 1st orderThe inReach fails, is lost, or the subscription lapses; the planned satphone (once owned) is the voice backup — a blackout means both channels are down or the second is not yet purchased.
  • 2nd orderThis cascade runs backwards. The crew may be perfectly safe, but home cannot know it. A missed check-in starts the R38 §1 escalation tree on the home side — worry, then formal escalation, potentially a search enquiry.
  • 3rd orderResources are mobilised for a non-emergency; trust in the check-in system is damaged; and if a real emergency follows during the blackout, the layered SOS doctrine (R29 §7) is unavailable exactly when needed.
Clocks started: the home escalation clock — it starts at the first missed check-in whether or not anything is wrong. The crew must treat comms restoration with emergency-level urgency because of what silence does at the other end.
Chain-breaker: RA-10 — two independent channels (inReach owned; satphone planned pre-departure purchase with IERCC registration and a scheduled SOS test), scheduled verification, and an escalation tree at home that distinguishes "late" from "silent" with agreed grace windows. On any single-channel loss: divert to restore redundancy before continuing remote legs.
Owns the response: R16 comms kit · R38 §1 SOP · R29 §7 SOS doctrine · RA-10
C7Money Access FailureMoney → Route → Paperwork
  • 1st orderCards decline (fraud lock, network outage, forex control change) or an ATM corridor dries up. Purchasing stops.
  • 2nd orderFuel purchasing power is mobility: the vehicle's range shrinks to the tank plus the cash on hand. Camp fees, food and border fees compete for the same cash stock.
  • 3rd orderIf resolution takes longer than the visa window or the fuel range to the next reliable banking town, a money problem has become a paperwork problem and a route problem simultaneously.
Clocks started: cash-stock burn-down at daily spend rate; fuel range remaining; visa days in the current country.
Chain-breaker: R39's layered money doctrine — multiple cards on different networks, USD cash reserve in staged stashes, and the standing rule of refuelling and re-cashing before the stock runs low, not when it has. The cash reserve is sized as a stock: days of full autonomy, not an arbitrary sum.
Owns the response: R39 money doctrine · R35 cash security · profiles §money/fuel
C8Document Loss or TheftPaperwork → Route → Money
  • 1st orderPassport, carnet or vehicle papers lost or stolen. The expedition is legally frozen in place — no border can be crossed in either direction.
  • 2nd orderReplacement runs through embassies and issuing bodies at their pace, not yours: travel to a capital, accommodation burn, and visa extensions for the waiting time.
  • 3rd orderA lost carnet is the deep branch: the customs guarantee attaches to the document. Replacement mid-route is slow and the vehicle's legal status is ambiguous until resolved — it cannot simply be driven out.
Clocks started: visa validity (now being spent stationary); insurance/medevac cover continuity; embassy processing timelines.
Chain-breaker: R39's document doctrine — certified copies carried separately from originals, digital copies in offline and cloud storage, originals split between crew where sensible, and R35's security discipline on where documents live in the vehicle. The copies do not replace the originals but they collapse the replacement timeline.
Owns the response: R39 documentation · R15 borders · R35 security
C9Fuel ContaminationRoute → Vehicle → Money
  • 1st orderA load of dirty or water-laden diesel goes into the 145 L main tank. The water trap and filters are the engineered defence — they catch it if drained and watched.
  • 2nd orderIgnored, water and fines pass to the injection side. The Bosch VE pump and injectors — freshly rebuilt Jun 2026 — are precision components lubricated by the fuel itself; water attacks them directly.
  • 3rd orderInjection-system damage in the field is a tow-and-rebuild event: the single most expensive avoidable failure on a mechanical diesel, and one that consumes the filter spares stock even in its milder forms.
Clocks started: filter-stock depletion rate (each contamination event can consume multiple filters); distance to clean-fuel resupply.
Chain-breaker: R24's sourcing and filtration doctrine — buy from high-turnover stations, filter-funnel on suspect fills, drain the water trap as a routine (not a response), and carry filter spares sized to the leg (R3/EX-8 consumables ledger). The habit is the defence; the hardware only backs it up.
Owns the response: R24 fuel system · R18 diagnosis · R3 filter stock
C10Fatigue — The Slow CascadeCrew → Crew (the invisible stock)
  • 1st orderNothing fails. Days of heat, corrugation, border friction and camp routine slowly draw down the crew's reserve — a stock with no gauge on the dashboard.
  • 2nd orderDecision quality degrades before anyone feels "tired": the late push into the dark, the crossing not walked, the gauge not glanced at, the shortcut taken. Fatigue does not cause incidents directly — it causes the decisions that cause them.
  • 3rd orderThe incident that follows looks like a vehicle or route failure in the log, and the true root cause never gets recorded. Nearly every other cascade in this register becomes more likely as this stock depletes.
Clocks started: none visible — that is the danger. The stock depletes silently and recovers only with genuine rest.
Chain-breaker: R38 §5's structural defences — the 5–7 day rest rhythm, the no-night-driving rule, the either-crew veto on discretionary risk, and naming "get-there-itis" out loud. These are not comfort measures; they are the maintenance schedule for the system that operates every other system.
Owns the response: R38 §5 governance · R37 crew · R17 health · Manifesto
§4 The Seams Register Interfaces to watch · one signal each

The interfaces where systems hand over to each other — where responsibility is shared and therefore easiest to drop. Each seam gets one watch signal: the early tell that the interface is degrading.

Expedition seams & watch signals
SeamBetweenWatch signal
Belt & pulleysEngine ↔ alternator / coolingBelt condition & tension at every service stop; squeal or dust at the pulleys. One belt serves two cascades (C1, C5).
DC-DC charge pathCrank side ↔ house sideSmartShunt state-of-charge trend vs driving hours — a weakening alternator shows here first, before the crank battery complains.
Crew ↔ machineSkill ↔ hardwareAny task only one crew member can do. Per R38 §5 that is a governance risk to be scheduled out, not lived with.
Check-in disciplineCrew ↔ homeAny casually missed or late check-in — the protocol only protects if silence reliably means trouble (C6).
Vehicle ↔ carnetMachine ↔ customs lawEvery border stamp-in/stamp-out matched same-day; an unstamped exit is a latent bond claim (R39, R29 §8).
Money ↔ borderCash ↔ officialdomFee demands that don't match the profile's documented schedule — the tell for both stale data and solicitation (R15, R35).
Tyres ↔ surfaceVehicle ↔ routePressure discipline at every surface change (R14 / Tyre Pressure Calculator); sidewall checks at each fuel stop.
Fuel ↔ tankRoute ↔ vehicleWater-trap drain as routine; any hesitation or smoke change after a fill is treated as contamination until proven otherwise (C9).
Plan ↔ conditionsItinerary ↔ realityTwo consecutive days of schedule slippage — the tell that the plan, not the driving, needs to change (R38 §2 suspend logic).
Cover ↔ calendarInsurance ↔ timeThe R29 calendar rows — Bonitas day-85 lapse and licence/IDP expiry checks — reviewed at every rest day, not remembered under pressure.
The seam habitAt any interface event — a border, a fuel stop, a surface change, a handover of driving, a service — pause on the seam itself for ten seconds: what is being handed over here, and what would dropping it start? The vehicle's subsystems are engineered; the seams are only ever maintained by attention.
§5 Sensing & Feedback The listening apparatus · trend → trigger

The expedition cannot be predicted to its end; it can be instrumented. This is the consolidated list of what the system measures about itself, at what cadence, and what a bad trend triggers. Most of these instruments already exist in the library — this table is the loop that connects reading to action.

Feedback instruments — measure · cadence · trigger
InstrumentCadenceTrend that triggers action
Spectrographic oil analysis (RA-2)Every oil change (never deferred — owner policy)Rising wear-metal trend → workshop assessment before the next remote leg; feeds the ~360,000 km mid-expedition service decision (R38 §4).
EGT & boost gauges (fitted)Continuous while drivingEGT creep at familiar load → fuelling/filtration check (R21, R24); boost drift from 0.6 bar → turbo/plumbing inspection.
Coolant temperatureContinuous — the fastest loopAny rise above normal band → stop NOW (C5). No trend analysis; immediate response.

Feedback doctrineAn instrument that is read but never changes a decision is decoration. Each row above ends in an action because the loop — measure → compare → decide → act — is only closed by the act. The post-trip review (R23) is the same loop at expedition scale: the Karoo Winter Loop is its first live run.
§6 Using the Map Field habit · planning habit

In the field — when something fails, work R18 for the diagnosis and the owning document for the fix, then come here and read the failure's cascade card once. The card answers the question the fix does not: what clocks are now running, and what is the chain-breaker? Ten minutes with the right card converts a reactive day into a managed one.

In planning — before each major leg, read §4's seams register against the leg's profile: which seams does this leg stress? A remote northern leg stresses tyres↔surface, fuel↔tank and comms↔home; a border-dense leg stresses money↔border and vehicle↔carnet. Load the preparation where the stress will be.

Keeping it honest — this map is a model, and models earn their keep only by being updated. When the Karoo loop or the expedition produces a cascade this register did not predict, or a seam it did not name, the post-trip review (R23) amends this document. A systems map that never changes is a systems map nobody is dancing with.

What R41 does not ownNo procedures, no specifications, no doctrine changes. Diagnosis stays in R18, decisions in R38, evacuation in R29, spares in R3, and every subsystem with its owning guide. R41 owns only the connections — if a conflict ever appears between this map and an owning document, the owning document wins and this map gets corrected.
