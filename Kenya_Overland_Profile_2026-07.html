§02 Border Crossings Entry from Tanzania
Primary entry — Namanga OSBP
Tanzania → Kenya
One-Stop Border Post on the Arusha–Nairobi corridor. ~160 km SE of Nairobi, ~100 km N of Arusha. Private vehicles use a separate lane. Busy — 150+ trucks daily. Allow 1–3 hours. Arrive early.
Secondary entry — Isebania/Sirari
Tanzania → Kenya (western route)
Western crossing near Lake Victoria. Connects Serengeti/Mara Triangle. Less traffic than Namanga but smaller facilities. Useful if routing via western Tanzania.
Exit — Terminus
Kenya is the northern terminus
No onward overland exit planned. Vehicle return routing to SA to be resolved in EX-9 (licence-break workshop). Options include retracing through Tanzania or shipping from Mombasa.

Border procedure: Clear Tanzanian exit (passport stamp + TIP discharge) at the shared OSBP facility, then proceed to Kenyan immigration. Present passport (eTA-exempt, no form needed), vehicle registration, owner authority (police-certified affidavit — registered to Mary-Ann West), and COMESA Yellow Card or border third-party insurance. Vehicle receives a Temporary Import Permit (Form C32) — free for 14 days, extendable up to 90 days at KRA Customs offices. Alternatively, present the Carnet de Passages, which triggers a paid-up foreign permit valid for one month. No vehicle may leave the border station without a Form C32 or Carnet and a Foreign Permit.

§03 Vehicle Documents TIP · Carnet · insurance

Free 14-day permit at border

Valid in Kenya

Registered to Mary-Ann West. Neil carries a police-certified owner affidavit authorising him to operate the vehicle. Certified copies of the registration.

§04 Best Season & Timing Dry seasons · migration · park fees
Peak dry — best overland
Jul–Oct & Jan–Feb
Jul–Oct: Great Migration in the Mara (river crossings Aug–Sep). Jan–Feb: warm, dry, lowest park fees (Mara USD 100 vs USD 200). Good tracks.
Long rains — avoid
Mar–May
Heavy rainfall across most of the country. Unpaved roads become impassable. Parks difficult. Masai Mara's black-cotton soil is notorious.
Short rains
Oct–Dec
Less predictable. Manageable for overlanders but tracks can be muddy. Good for photography — green landscapes, dramatic skies. Calving season in Amboseli.
Cost note
Mara fees double Jul–Dec
Masai Mara: USD 100/day low season (Jan–Jun) vs USD 200/day peak (Jul–Dec). KWS parks (Amboseli, Tsavo, Nakuru) are flat year-round at USD 80–90/day.

Expedition timing note: If arriving from Tanzania during the dry season (Jul–Oct), the Mara migration will be at its peak but park fees are highest. A Jan–Mar arrival catches the dry season at lower cost and avoids the long rains. Seasonal routing will be resolved in EX-9.

§06 Parts & Serviceability 1HZ · tyres · Victron · fuel quality
Toyota / 1HZ Network CFAO Motors Kenya

Verdict: Excellent — the best network on the expedition route alongside South Africa. Toyota Kenya (CFAO Motors) operates 8 dealerships: Nairobi (Uhuru Highway, Ngong Road, Westlands), Mombasa, Eldoret, Kisumu, Nanyuki, and Nyeri. In addition, an extensive network of authorised service centres covers smaller towns including Narok, Kisii, Voi, Malindi, Marsabit, and Garissa. The Land Cruiser 70-Series has a massive installed base in Kenya — widely used by NGOs, safari operators, ranchers, and security services. 1HZ parts are widely available through both the official CFAO network and the aftermarket. Specialist 4x4 outfitters such as Robs Magic (Nairobi and Mombasa) stock suspension, recovery gear, and accessories for the 70-Series. Kenya is the strongest Toyota country on the expedition route.

Tyres 265/70R16 & 265/75R16

Buy in RSA — preferred strategy. 265/70R16 and 265/75R16 are available in Kenya through tyre dealers and 4x4 outfitters in Nairobi and Mombasa, though the specific Maxxis AT811 pattern may not be stocked. Major tyre brands (BFGoodrich, Cooper, General Grabber) are represented. In an emergency, Kenya's tyre market is workable but expect higher prices than RSA. The planned 265/75R16 upgrade will be done in RSA before departure — carry a full matching spare.

Victron Support Strong presence

Kenya has multiple Victron distributors and installers. Center for Alternative Technologies (CAT, Nairobi) is a major Victron distributor and installer with years of experience in off-grid and commercial systems. Firstsun Technologies and SolarShop Africa are further authorised Victron dealers in Nairobi. Additional solar suppliers stocking Victron include TDK Solutions and Solar Store East Africa. Nairobi has a robust solar industry — replacement MPPT controllers, SmartShunts, or cable accessories are obtainable. For the MultiPlus or Orion Smart units, availability may require ordering through CAT. The RSA support base (Manjaro Industries / direct Victron SA) remains the primary technical resource.

Fuel Quality 50 ppm (waiver) / 10 ppm (standard)

Kenya adopted 10 ppm sulphur diesel (KS EAS 177:2025) in 2025, putting it at Euro 5/6 equivalent — the cleanest in East Africa. The temporary 50 ppm waiver (Apr–Oct 2026) reverts to the earlier EAC standard. Even at 50 ppm, this is better than most African countries. The 1HZ runs comfortably on 50 ppm diesel without any fuel-system concerns. Stick to branded stations (Shell, TotalEnergies, Rubis) and avoid jerry-can fuel in remote areas if possible.

§07 Roads Tarmac · murram · 4x4 tracks
Nairobi – Mombasa (A109/SGR)
~480 km · Good tarmac
Upgraded dual carriageway for much of the route. Heavy truck traffic. Alternative: the scenic A8 via Taveta.
Nairobi – Namanga (A104)
~160 km · Good tarmac
Main Kenya-Tanzania road. Well maintained. Moderate traffic.
Nairobi – Narok – Masai Mara
~270 km · Tarmac to Narok, murram beyond
The Narok–Sekenani road is graded murram, corrugated in dry season, impassable black-cotton mud in rains. 4x4 essential beyond Narok.
Nairobi – Nanyuki – Samburu
~300 km · Tarmac to Nanyuki, tracks beyond
Good tarmac through the highlands to Nanyuki. Samburu access roads are murram/4x4 track. Scenic route via Mt Kenya.
Isiolo – Marsabit – Moyale
~530 km · Now tarmac but remote
The "northern highway" — recently upgraded to tarmac but passes through banditry-prone and arid terrain. Travel in convoy recommended. Very long fuel gaps. Security escort may be required for some sections.
Inside national parks
Murram / 4x4 tracks
Park roads range from well-graded murram (Amboseli) to rough 4x4 tracks (Mana Pools equivalent — Meru, northern Tsavo). Black-cotton soil in the Mara becomes impassable in rain. Tyre pressures: drop to 1.8–2.0 bar on soft surfaces.

Drive-left (LHD traffic). Kenya drives on the left — same side as SA. The LC76 is RHD, so no visibility disadvantage. Speed limits: 110 km/h highways, 50 km/h urban. Enforcement by speed cameras on major roads and KWS rangers inside parks. Avoid all night driving — poor lighting, pedestrians, livestock on roads, and elevated crime risk.

§08 Security Advisories · crime · no-go zones
US State Dept
Level 2 — Exercise Increased Caution
Due to crime, terrorism, civil unrest, kidnapping. Level 4 (Do Not Travel) for specific border zones.
Do Not Travel zones
Somalia border counties
Garissa, Wajir, Mandera, Tana River, Lamu (county), coast north of Malindi — al-Shabaab terrorism and kidnapping. Nowhere near standard safari routes.
Northern banditry
Turkana, West Pokot, Marsabit, Baringo
Armed banditry, cattle rustling, cross-border incursions near the Ethiopian and South Sudanese borders. If routing through northern Kenya, travel in convoy, avoid night movement, check local security.
Nairobi
Petty crime — normal precautions
Avoid Eastleigh and Kibera. Armed carjacking occurs but is declining (127 cases in 2025 vs prior years). Use secure parking. Don't display valuables. Avoid walking after dark in unfamiliar areas.

Core safari areas (Masai Mara, Amboseli, Tsavo, Samburu, Laikipia, coast south of Malindi) are safe for tourists. Dedicated ranger posts at all park entry points. Over 2 million international visitors in 2025. Tourist-related incidents in designated safari areas are statistically negligible. The no-go zones are hundreds of kilometres from standard tourist routes. Protests in Nairobi can flare — check for planned demonstration dates before scheduling city time. Register with the SA High Commission in Nairobi on arrival.

§09 Health Malaria · vaccinations · medical
Malaria
HIGH — year-round in lowlands & coast
P. falciparum dominant. High-risk zones: Lake Victoria basin, coast (Mombasa–Diani–Watamu), lowland safari areas. Low risk: Nairobi (altitude ~1,700 m), central highlands above 2,500 m. Prophylaxis essential for coast and safari legs.
Yellow fever
ICVP MANDATORY — arriving from Tanzania
Vaccinate before leaving SA. Certificate valid 10 years. Enforcement at border.
Other vaccinations
Hep A, Hep B, Typhoid, Tetanus
Routine for East Africa travel. Cholera vaccine recommended if visiting Lake Victoria shore. Rabies pre-exposure if camping in wildlife areas.
Medical facilities
Good in Nairobi, limited elsewhere
Nairobi: Nairobi Hospital, Aga Khan University Hospital, MP Shah — high-quality private care. Mombasa: reasonable. Safari areas and rural Kenya: limited — medevac insurance essential (R29). Hospitals require upfront payment.
Water
Treat or use bottled
Tap water is not reliably potable outside Nairobi's better hotels. Use the LC76's water system (R12) and purification.

§10 Camp Safety & Wildlife Big Five · hippo · camp protocols

Kenya is a Big Five country with large, free-roaming populations of lion, leopard, elephant, buffalo, and both black and white rhino. Hippo and crocodile are present in rivers and lake margins throughout. The primary camping threat is nocturnal wildlife — elephant, hippo, and buffalo moving through camp at night. The Masai Mara, Amboseli, and Samburu all have high densities of large predators and herbivores.

Elephant
Very high density — Amboseli, Tsavo, Samburu
Amboseli's elephants are habituated but not tame. Tsavo's "red elephants" can be aggressive. Never camp between elephants and water. Stand-off distance R36.
Hippo
Rivers, lakes, Mara River
Africa's most dangerous large animal. Never camp within 100 m of hippo water. Night grazing routes are invisible — use designated campsites only in hippo territory.
Lion & leopard
Present in all major parks
The Mara has one of the highest lion densities in Africa. Leopard present but secretive. Food must be stored in the vehicle. Camp in designated sites. Follow R36 after-dark protocols.
Mandatory ranger — Masai Mara
All campers must hire 2 rangers for overnight
Masai Mara requires two armed rangers for all overnight campers at private campsites. Arranged at the gate. Additional USD cost per night.

Outside parks, campsites are available at lodges, conservancies, and purpose-built overland sites. The OzTent RV4 ground-tent setup works well at all Kenyan camping sites.

§14 Southern Circuit — Highlights Amboseli · Tsavo · Masai Mara

Amboseli National Park: Iconic views of Kilimanjaro towering over elephant herds. 392 km² of savannah, wetland, and dry lakebed. Known for large-tusked elephants and excellent predator sightings. The Amboseli elephants are among the most studied in the world. Camping at designated KWS sites. Access via Meshanani or Kimana gates from the Nairobi–Namanga road.

Tsavo East & Tsavo West: Combined 22,000 km² — the largest protected area in Kenya. Tsavo East is open savannah with "red elephants" (dust-bathed in the red laterite soil), the Galana River, and the Yatta Plateau (world's longest lava flow). Tsavo West is more scenic: Mzima Springs (underwater hippo viewing), Shetani lava flows, and a rhino sanctuary. The Nairobi–Mombasa road bisects the two parks. Self-drive friendly with reasonable tracks.

Masai Mara National Reserve: Kenya's premier safari destination and the northern end of the Serengeti ecosystem. The Great Migration (Jul–Oct) brings ~1.5 million wildebeest and hundreds of thousands of zebra. The Mara River crossings (Aug–Sep) are the iconic spectacle. Outside migration, the Mara has year-round resident wildlife including big cats at extraordinary density. Self-driving now requires a mandatory park ranger escort — arranged at the gate, ~USD 40/day. Black-cotton soil makes the reserve impassable in heavy rain.

§15 Northern Circuit — Highlights Samburu · Laikipia · Mt Kenya

Samburu National Reserve: Semi-arid landscape along the Ewaso Ng'iro River in the northern Rift. Home to the "Samburu Special Five" — reticulated giraffe, Grevy's zebra, Beisa oryx, Somali ostrich, and gerenuk — species not found in southern Kenya. Leopard and lion sightings excellent. Self-drive friendly but roads are sandy and rocky. 165 km² — compact and wildlife-dense. Managed by Samburu County (separate fee system from KWS). Access from Isiolo (~30 km south).

Laikipia Plateau: A private-conservancy model — over 50 ranches and conservancies between Mt Kenya and the northern deserts. Some of Kenya's highest wildlife densities per unit area. Ol Pejeta Conservancy (home to the last two northern white rhinos). Lewa Wildlife Conservancy (black rhino stronghold). Laikipia is a pioneer in community conservation and pays its way through tourism. Some conservancies allow self-drive; others are guided only — check in advance.

Mount Kenya National Park: Africa's second-highest peak (5,199 m). The montane forest and moorland zones are spectacularly beautiful. Point Lenana (4,985 m) is a non-technical trek. The park surrounds the mountain at elevations above 3,200 m. Access from Nanyuki (north) or Chogoria (east). Cool climate — welcome relief from the lowland heat. KWS managed.

§16 Coast — Highlights Mombasa · Diani · Watamu · marine parks

Mombasa: Kenya's second city and main port. Fort Jesus (UNESCO World Heritage), Old Town with Swahili architecture, and a working harbor. Vehicle access to Mombasa Island is via causeway. Traffic is congested. Good services — fuel, ATMs, Toyota dealer (CFAO Mombasa), parts, tyres.

Diani Beach & the south coast: Kenya's premier beach destination, south of Mombasa via the Likoni ferry (avoid — safety concerns flagged by multiple advisories) or the new Dongo Kundu bypass road. White sand, warm Indian Ocean. Marine parks at Kisite-Mpunguti (snorkelling with dolphins). Camping options at backpacker-oriented sites. Malaria risk — coast is high-transmission year-round.

Watamu & Malindi: North coast. Watamu Marine National Park for snorkelling and turtle conservation. Malindi has a more local feel. Do not travel north of Malindi — the coastline from Malindi northward into Lamu County is under the Level 4 advisory. Lamu Island itself is accessible but the surrounding county is high-risk.

§18 Route Guide — Namanga to Terminus Indicative routing through Kenya
Leg 1

Namanga → Nairobi

  • Distance: ~160 km via A104
  • Surface: Good tarmac
  • Time: 2–3 hours (traffic near Nairobi)
  • Fuel at Namanga or Kajiado en route
  • Nairobi: resupply, vehicle service if needed (CFAO Toyota), SIM card, M-Pesa registration
Leg 2

Nairobi → Amboseli

  • Distance: ~240 km via Emali
  • Surface: Tarmac to Emali, murram approach
  • Time: 4–5 hours
  • Kilimanjaro views on clear mornings
  • 2–3 nights for elephants and game drives
Leg 3

Amboseli → Tsavo

  • Distance: ~200 km to Tsavo West (Chyulu gate)
  • Surface: Mix of tarmac and murram
  • Time: 4–5 hours
  • Mzima Springs, Shetani lava, red elephants
  • Cross into Tsavo East via internal road
Leg 4

Nairobi → Masai Mara

  • Distance: ~270 km via Narok
  • Surface: Tarmac to Narok, murram beyond
  • Time: 5–6 hours (road conditions variable)
  • Fuel and last resupply at Narok
  • Entry via Sekenani or Talek gate
  • 3–4 nights — more for migration season
Leg 5

Nairobi → Samburu / Laikipia

  • Distance: ~350 km to Samburu via Nanyuki
  • Surface: Good tarmac highlands, murram to reserve
  • Time: 5–6 hours
  • Mt Kenya views. Nanyuki for resupply
  • Ol Pejeta / Lewa en route (booking required)
Extension

Nairobi → Mombasa / Coast

  • Distance: ~480 km via A109
  • Surface: Good tarmac (upgraded dual carriageway)
  • Time: 6–7 hours
  • Passes through Tsavo — combine with Leg 3
  • Diani via Dongo Kundu bypass (avoid Likoni ferry)
  • Watamu/Malindi for marine parks — do not go north of Malindi
