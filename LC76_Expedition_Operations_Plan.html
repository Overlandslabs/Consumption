PART A · RUNNING THE EXPEDITION

1 · Communications & Escalation SOP

A written, agreed routine so that home always knows where we are, and so a genuine silence triggers help without us having to ask for it.

The point of a check-in routine is not to chat — it is to create a known pattern. When the pattern is kept, home does nothing. When the pattern breaks, home acts, on a schedule we agreed in advance, without waiting for a request that a stranded or injured crew may be unable to make. Two people alone in remote country cannot be their own safety net; the anchor at home is the net.

The Anchor

One nominated person at home is the anchor — the single point that holds our plan, our check-in log, and the authority to escalate. A named backup covers the anchor's own travel or illness.

  • 1Primary anchorName: ANCHOR — NAME · Phone: ANCHOR PHONE · Email: ANCHOR EMAIL. Holds the route plan, the check-in log, and this SOP. Reachable at short notice.
  • 2Backup anchorName: BACKUP — NAME · Phone: BACKUP PHONE. Steps in if the primary is unreachable; holds the same documents.
  • 3What the anchor holdsA copy of this plan, the current leg's route and intended waypoints, passport / vehicle / insurance copies (per R35 and R39), the medevac & insurer details of record (R29), and the running check-in log.
The Fail-Safe Layer (RA-10)

Every layer above depends on something — Starlink on power, account status and a clear sky; the phone on coverage; the sat messenger on its subscription and charge. The fail-safe layer is the device that works when everything else is dead: a PLB or inReach-class device, registered, subscription current, carried on the person (not packed in the vehicle — a burning or submerged vehicle takes packed devices with it). Chartered as RA-10: verify before the Karoo loop that the device exists, its registration and emergency contacts are current, a live test message has been sent and received, and one named crew member owns keeping it charged and current. Device detail and setup live in R16; this SOP only requires that the layer exists and is verified.

The Check-In Routine

Baseline cadence is a scheduled check-in every three days, sent by the satellite messenger (device and setup in R16). Where phone signal exists it may substitute; the sat device is the primary because it does not depend on coverage. The scheduled clock is CHECK-IN TIME (LOCAL) — a fixed time makes a missed check-in obvious rather than ambiguous.

Every check-in carries four things, kept short: position (the device sends coordinates automatically), status (a preset "all well" or a written note), next intended waypoint, and the next check-in time. If the plan changes — a longer remote leg, a deliberate off-grid stretch — tell the anchor before going quiet, and agree a revised next-contact time. A planned silence is not an emergency; an unplanned one is.

Two signals, unambiguous. Agree exactly two routine messages so there is never doubt: a preset OK ("all well, on plan") and a preset HELP / SOS (device SOS to the emergency service and the insurer per R29). Nothing in between should be improvised under stress. Anything that is not a clear OK is treated by the anchor as a reason to pay attention.
Escalation — When We Go Quiet

If a scheduled check-in is missed, the clock starts. The anchor works a fixed, pre-agreed ladder — no judgement calls required in the moment, because they were made here, calmly, in advance.

CHECK-IN ON PLAN → MISSED +48h · ANCHOR WORKS ALL CHANNELS → SILENT +72h · ANCHOR ACTIVATES MEDEVAC / INSURER
0Scheduled check-in missed. The anchor notes the missed slot and waits, without alarm, for the agreed grace window — a single missed message is often just weather, terrain, or a flat device.
1+48 hours of silence → the anchor acts. Works every channel: sat messenger, both mobile numbers, email, any lodge / camp / contact on the intended route, and other overlanders if known. Records each attempt with a timestamp in the check-in log.
2+72 hours of silence → the anchor escalates formally. Contacts the medevac provider and insurer (details of record in R29) with our last known position, intended route, vehicle description (SA-plated LC76 SW, registered to Mary-Ann West), and the crew and medical picture. From here the response is driven by R29's procedures.
3Contact restored. The crew confirms status and a revised check-in time; the anchor stands the escalation down and logs it. Any real incident feeds the post-trip review (R23).
Tune the windows to the leg. 48h / 72h suits normal remote travel. For a known deep-remote stretch with no realistic re-contact for days, agree longer windows with the anchor beforehand so a planned silence never triggers a costly false alarm. For town or coastal legs with reliable signal, they can be tightened. The routine is a default, not a straitjacket — but every change is agreed with the anchor in advance and written in the log.

PART B · ENDING THE EXPEDITION WELL

2 · Abort & Suspend Criteria

Deciding before we leave what would make us pause, and what would make us stop — so the decision under pressure is a recognition, not an argument.

Two distinct actions, deliberately kept separate:

Reversible
Suspend
A pause. Hold position, retreat to a safe point, or wait out a problem — without ending the expedition. Most situations resolve at this level. A suspend can always be lifted once the trigger clears.
Terminal for the leg
Abort
End the current leg or the expedition — turn for home, or begin vehicle repatriation (§3). A larger decision, reached jointly, and never taken in the heat of a single bad hour.
Who Decides

Authority is deliberately asymmetric, and safety always wins:

  • 1Either crew member may call a SUSPEND — alone, immediately, without debate.If one of us judges a situation unsafe, we stop. The other supports the call without argument in the moment; we talk it through once we are safe, not before. There is no override of a safety call. The more cautious judgement carries.
  • 2ABORT is a joint decision.Ending the expedition is made by both of us together, calmly, ideally slept on where circumstances allow. Neither ends it unilaterally.
  • 3An unresolved suspend escalates to abort.If a suspend cannot be safely resolved within a reasonable window — the vehicle cannot be recovered, the medical situation does not stabilise, the region does not settle — it becomes an abort decision. Drifting in a stalled suspend is itself a hazard.
Thresholds By Domain

Guidance, not a rigid formula — but agreeing it now removes the "is this bad enough?" argument later. Read SUSPEND as "pause and reassess" and ABORT as "this ends the leg unless it clears".

The bias is toward stopping. "Eco not ego." No destination, schedule, or sunk cost is worth pushing a genuine threshold. A suspended or aborted trip with both crew and the vehicle safe is a good outcome — it can be resumed. The failure mode to guard against is talking ourselves past a line we already agreed was a line.
Emergency numbers. The medevac 24-hour activation line and the insurer's assist line — the numbers this section's medical and repatriation decisions depend on — are held in §1 (Emergency Contacts) and in R29. Keep them where both crew can reach them without a document search.
Minute Every Suspend & Abort

Keep it simple: a dated line in the expedition journal for every suspend and abort — what triggered it, what we decided, and how it resolved. It removes hindsight argument, and it feeds directly into the post-trip review protocol (R23). This is the same discipline that runs through the rest of the library: decisions on the record.

3 · Vehicle Repatriation & Disposal

If we stop early, the LC76 still has to be dealt with — lawfully, and with the Carnet honoured. This section frames the options; the paperwork detail lives in R39.

Carnet first. A vehicle travelling under a Carnet de Passages must leave the country it entered under, or the temporary-import duties it guarantees fall due. That single fact drives every option below — the vehicle cannot simply be abandoned or casually sold abroad. The Carnet issuer, indemnity, and the duty mechanics are owned by R39; consult it and the issuer before acting. The vehicle is registered to Mary-Ann West, which shapes who can authorise disposal or shipping.

Four broad routes home, chosen on the situation — crew health, vehicle state, where we are, and cost:

OptionWhen it fits / what it involvesWatch-outs
A · Drive it out The vehicle is serviceable and the crew able. Drive the shortest safe corridor back toward RSA, or to a port — using the border process in R15. The simplest and usually cheapest option. Depends on a roadworthy vehicle and fit crew. Route and border feasibility per R15 and the profiles.
B · Ship it Crew must leave quickly (medical, family) but the vehicle is intact. Drive or transport it to a suitable port, arrange sea freight through a clearing / shipping agent, and fly the crew out. Common East-African exit ports are on the Kenyan and Tanzanian coast. Cost and lead time; agent and paperwork via R39. Carnet must be discharged correctly at export.
C · Secure storage, resume later The break is temporary (this is also the licence-break case, §4). Place the vehicle in bonded or secure storage, fly out, and return to continue. Preserves the trip. Storage security, insurance while stored (R29), and Carnet validity window — the Carnet has an expiry.
D · Lawful disposal abroad Last resort — the vehicle is a write-off or genuinely cannot be moved. Any sale or disposal must settle the Carnet duty / import position first, or run through the insurer's total-loss process (R29). Never informal. Carnet duties can be substantial. Owner (M-A West) authority and R39 / issuer guidance are mandatory.
Decide the trigger, not the detail, now. We cannot know in advance which port or agent we would use — that is a decision for the moment, made with the Carnet issuer and R39 in hand. What we can settle now is the principle: crew safety comes first, the Carnet is honoured, and the owner authorises any disposal. The rest is executed against R39 when the time comes.

5 · Crew Governance

How two people run a long expedition together well — decision rights, rest, and a simple rule for disagreeing about avoidable risk. Kept in the spirit of the Manifesto: partnership, safety, simplicity.

Decision Rights
  • 1Day-to-day callsPace, camp choice, and driving decisions for a leg sit with whoever is driving or leading that leg, in consultation with the other. Kept light — most of the trip runs on ordinary agreement.
  • 2Strategic callsRoute changes, rest-day timing, and any abort (§2) are made jointly. These are the decisions worth slowing down for.
  • 3Safety callsEither of us can call an immediate halt or suspend at any time — see §2. Safety authority is not shared by committee; it belongs to whoever sees the danger.
Disagreement On Discretionary Risk

The rule for any avoidable risk — a hard trail we could bypass, a dubious shortcut, a late push into the dark, a river crossing we are unsure of:

Either of us can veto. To proceed, we both have to agree. If one says no to an avoidable risk, we take the cautious option — no argument needed in the moment. The more careful call wins by default. We can revisit the decision later, calmly, at a rest stop; but we do not talk each other into a risk one of us has already refused.

This applies to discretionary risk — things we choose. It is different from a committed situation already unfolding, where the priority is to get out safely together and second-guessing mid-crossing is its own hazard. The veto is a tool for the decision point, before commitment.

Rest, Pace & Fatigue
  • 1A rest / admin day roughly every 5–7 travelling daysFlexible, not rigid — taken when fatigue, the vehicle, laundry, resupply, or a good spot calls for it. Long expeditions are lost to burnout as often as to breakdown. Town days handle resupply, fuel, water, cash (R39), and vehicle checks.
  • 2No night drivingSet up before dark. Night driving multiplies every risk — animals, people, road, fatigue — and is a standing owner value, not a case-by-case call (echoes R35, R28).
  • 3Share the loadSwap driving; don't push exhausted to make a destination. Tiredness and "get-there-itis" are the quiet cause behind many avoidable decisions — name it and stop.
Redundancy — No Single Point Of Failure

The most important governance rule on a two-person trip is that neither of us is indispensable. Both crew must be able to operate the vehicle, the Victron system end-to-end, the comms, and the recovery gear — so a decision is never forced by one person being the only one who can. Closing that gap is the whole purpose of R37; this plan simply depends on it. Where a skill still sits with one crew, that is a governance risk to be scheduled out, not lived with.

Disagreements that don't resolve. If a discretionary-risk disagreement can't be settled, the cautious option stands and we move on — then revisit at the next rest day if it still matters. The goal is not to win; it is to keep both crew and the vehicle safe and the partnership intact for a two-year trip. Minute the significant ones alongside the suspend/abort log (§2) for the post-trip review (R23).
