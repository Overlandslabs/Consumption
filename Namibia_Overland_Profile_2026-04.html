South African passport holders enter Namibia on a visa-free basis — the Visitor's Entry Permit is stamped at the border. The important catch is that the 90-day allowance is cumulative for the calendar year, not per visit. If you exit to Botswana for a week and return, you're not reset. For a 30-day trip this is no issue. Plan the dates you'll be in Namibia and confirm the officer stamps the correct number of days at entry. Note: bring a proof of onward travel (even a loose printed itinerary works).

Note on 2025 Namibia visa policy changes Since April 2025, Namibia introduced visa requirements for 33 nationalities that were previously exempt (including UK, US, Germany, France, Australia). This does not affect SA passport holders who remain fully visa-free. It is relevant if you're travelling with non-SADC companions.
02 Border Crossings From SA · Into Zambia/Botswana

The planned route enters at Vioolsdrif / Noordoewer from the Northern Cape, traverses west to east across Namibia, and exits into Zambia at Ngoma Bridge or Sesheke / Katima Mulilo area. Key borders for this trip are covered below.

— From South AfricaEntry

Noordoewer / Vioolsdrif (SA–Namibia)

24-hour border · GPS 28°45'S 17°33'E · Sealed road both sides (N7/B1)

The main SA–Namibia crossing for vehicles coming from Cape Town or Springbok. 24-hour operation makes it convenient. The Orange River forms the border here — bridge crossing. Namibian side requires paying the CBC road tax and obtaining third-party insurance (Motor Insurance Certificate) at the RFA/Africon office.

  • SA exit: stamp out at Home Affairs, declare foreign currency above ZAR 25,000
  • Namibia entry: stamp in, request 30+ days, pay CBC road tax (see Section 03)
  • Third-party insurance: obtain Motor Insurance Certificate at border — valid 30 days, free
  • RFA office: pay Cross Border Charge here — flat fee per entry
  • ZA sticker: must be affixed to rear of vehicle before arrival
  • Finance letter: if vehicle is still under finance, carry bank letter of authority with travel dates
  • Typical crossing time: 45–90 min if not busy; weekend mornings can queue longer
— Into Botswana / Zambia (Exit)Exit Routes

Ngoma Bridge (Namibia–Botswana)

07:00–18:00 · GPS 17°55'S 24°44'E · Sealed road Namibia side, good gravel Botswana side

The key crossing for the Caprivi–Chobe corridor. Sits inside the KAZA transfrontier area. Botswana issues a TIP at this crossing and you'll drive through Chobe National Park immediately after — have your park fees ready. The crossing is efficient and straightforward.

  • Namibia exit: cancel TIP / CBC, stamp out
  • Botswana entry: 30-day visa-free for SA; vehicle TIP issued at border
  • CBC for Botswana: separate road tax payable at Botswana border
  • Chobe NP: immediate park fees if transiting through the park road to Kasane

Wenela / Sesheke (Namibia–Zambia)

06:00–18:00 · GPS 17°28'S 24°17'E · Ferry or bridge crossing

Crosses the Zambezi River at Katima Mulilo / Sesheke. This is the standard overland crossing into southern Zambia for Caprivi-routed overlanders heading north. The KAZA UniVisa ($50 USD) covers both Zambia and Zimbabwe and is available at this crossing — highly recommended if Zimbabwe is also on the itinerary.

  • SA passport: Zambia single-entry $50 USD or KAZA UniVisa $50 USD (covers Zim too)
  • Vehicle: TIP issued at border, usually 30 days
  • Carbon tax: road tax payable at Zambia border in USD
  • Third-party insurance: purchase at border (compulsory)
  • KAZA UniVisa tip: only available at certain ports — Wenela / Sesheke is one of them; confirm current availability before routing
Important — border hours vs your driving day Ngoma Bridge and Wenela both close at 18:00. With a 400 km/day driving comfort zone, factor in that you may need a short day before a border to avoid arriving after hours. Noordoewer is 24-hour, so no constraint on the entry day.

Veterinary Cordon (Red Line) & Meat Movement Critical for Route Planning

The Veterinary Cordon Fence (colloquially the Red Line or Vet Line) runs roughly east–west across Namibia, separating the foot-and-mouth disease-free southern zone from the northern communal farming areas. It runs approximately from the Skeleton Coast at Ugab River east through Otjiwarongo and onwards. Critically: Etosha National Park sits just above the vet line, and the cordon gates at Etosha's Anderson, Von Lindquist, and Galton Gates are active checkpoints.

Raw meat rules at Etosha vet cordon gates Raw red meat (beef, lamb, pork, goat) is NOT permitted to be carried southward past the cordon gates. Meaning: if you buy raw meat in the north (e.g. Opuwo, Rundu) and try to bring it back south through Anderson Gate, it will be confiscated. Consume or leave all raw red meat north of the line before returning south. Moving northward (SA → Namibia → Etosha → Caprivi) is generally fine.

Biltong, droëwors, and commercially vacuum-packed cured meats are treated differently to raw meat and are generally permitted across the cordon, but this is subject to inspector discretion. The safest approach: carry biltong and cured provisions for the southern leg, and plan to buy and consume fresh meat locally in the north. Opuwo and Katima Mulilo both have butcheries.

Recommended Off-Season Windows Quiet & Good Conditions

Window 1 — Late April to Late May (Primary Recommendation)

30–40 days · Post-rains · Pre-European peak · Optimal for Kaokoland + Caprivi

The rains have stopped, Kaokoland vegetation is still green, riverbeds are accessible, and the whole country is yours. Etosha hasn't yet filled with Europeans. Temperatures are pleasant (25–32°C days, 10–16°C nights in the Kunene). The Epupa Falls are flowing from recent rains. Van Zyl's Pass and the Marienfluss are passable. Community camp availability is open. NWR sites easier to book than July.

  • Trade-off: Kunene region can still have occasional soft ground in April — confirm track conditions via T4A community reports before departure
  • Book NWR (Etosha) 2–3 months ahead for this window: reservations@nwr.com.na
  • This window aligns with the 2028 departure planning horizon

Window 2 — Late September to Early November (Secondary)

30–35 days · Post-European peak · Pre-rains · Hot but quiet

The Europeans have gone home, SA schools are back, and Namibia is empty. Etosha waterholes in late October are arguably the country's best game viewing — animals are completely desperate for water and the floodlit waterholes at night camps are extraordinary. Kaokoland is passable but very hot (35–40°C). The Caprivi is dry and accessible. Main trade-off is heat — this is not pleasant for camping in direct sun.

  • October is arguably Namibia's single best game month — Etosha waterhole activity peaks
  • Kaokoland heat requires early starts (on road by 06:00) and camp by 14:00
  • November rains can start — be ready to abandon Kaokoland tracks if storms develop

Namibia is not Angola — diesel is roughly on par with SA pricing. The main constraint is availability in Kaokoland, not price. The Namibian government regulates fuel pricing monthly (Walvis Bay serves as the benchmark; inland towns carry a small premium for transport). Fill up whenever you pass a pump in the north — don't assume the next town will have stock.

Kaokoland fuel gap — critical planning For the northern Kaokoland circuit (Epupa → Van Zyl's Pass → Marienfluss → Hoarusib), the effective fuel-loading point is Opuwo (last reliable diesel before the circuit), with secondary supply at Ruacana and sometimes Oncocua. The Seven Rivers route from Kunene River Lodge to Palmwag is approximately 1,000 km — you need a full tank plus at least 80–100L carry capacity (2×20L Desert Fox cells plus a jerry can if possible). Fuel at Opuwo before committing to the circuit.

07 Security Low Risk Overall · Regional Notes

Namibia is one of the safest overlanding destinations in Africa. It has a functioning police service, a professional border force, and no tradition of facilitating bribes. Remote areas are genuinely remote — the risk is isolation rather than hostility. Urban areas require standard city caution (Windhoek, Katima Mulilo).

Namibia's Security Positives

  • Very low violent crime outside major urban centres
  • Police and officials professional — not bribery-oriented
  • Remote Kaokoland: essentially zero human-safety risk
  • Welcoming people, high English fluency in towns
  • Well-maintained vehicle registration and inspection system (roadblocks are routine checks, not shakedowns)
  • Caprivi locals are peaceful, good community relationships with overlanders

Genuine Risks

  • Vehicle break-ins possible at tourist viewpoints (Sossusvlei, Fish River Canyon) — don't leave valuables visible
  • Windhoek: standard urban opportunist crime near backpacker areas; park in secure lots
  • Katima Mulilo: border town — slightly higher petty crime; secure camp recommended
  • Remote breakdown risk in Kaokoland is serious — no cell coverage, no rescue services; satellite communicator is mandatory
  • Landmine: residual risk exists along the Angolan border (Kunene River area). Stay on established tracks. Risk is low on known routes but zero off-track compliance is required.
Landmine awareness — Kunene / Angola border zone The Kunene River forms the Namibia–Angola border. Mine clearance in this area has been extensive but is not 100% complete. Do NOT leave established tracks, campsites, or riverbank paths in this area. Epupa Falls and Kunene River Lodge are on cleared, established ground — stay on the paths. The risk is low on known routes; it is the reason you never go off-track in this zone.

Namibia presents three distinct camp environments: Etosha (fenced NWR camps — most controlled), Kaokoland communal conservancy camps (unfenced, big-5 country, desert conditions), and Caprivi river camps (unfenced, elephant and hippo corridors). Each has a different risk profile for a ground tent setup.

Hippos, Crocs & Water Hazards River Camps

Kunene River (Epupa Falls area): Large crocodiles. Don't swim, wade, or collect water by kneeling at the river edge. The Kunene is one of Namibia's most croc-dense rivers. Admire from above the bank — the view down the falls and upstream from Epupa camp is excellent without getting near the water. Caprivi rivers (Kwando, Chobe, Zambezi): Hippos graze 3–5 km from water at night and return before dawn — their path runs through river camp areas. Don't position a ground tent between the river and the camp interior. If you hear hippo grunting, stay in your tent. They are not aggressive unless you block their path.

Park & Camp Rules Namibia-Specific

Etosha gates open at sunrise and close at sunset — typically 06:00–18:30 depending on the season. You must be back at camp before gate closing time. No off-road driving in Etosha (unlike SA parks, tracks are not graded for off-road departures). Kaokoland communal conservancy camps: stay on established tracks — this is not just a wildlife rule but a landmine compliance rule near the Kunene. Firewood: collection banned in Namibian national parks; buy at camp reception. Drones: Namibia requires a permit from the Civil Aviation Authority of Namibia (CAAN) for commercial use; recreational drones in national parks are restricted — see Section 10 for details.

Snakes, Scorpions & Smaller Hazards Bush Hazards

Namibia has a full complement of dangerous snakes, though encounters are uncommon in established camps. Puff adders are the biggest risk — they're slow, cryptic, and don't move out of the way. Shake boots every morning. Cape cobras and spitting cobras (Mozambique spitting cobra is common in the Caprivi) are present. Scorpions are abundant in the Namib and Kaokoland rocky terrain — shake sleeping gear, check shoes. Black mamba is present but rarely encountered in camp conditions. Antivenom: Windhoek Mediclinic. Remote area protocol: satellite communicator SOS + keep the victim still, as you likely have hours before help arrives. In any remote camp, the anti-venom logistics mean your job is to keep the person calm and stable until medevac, not to rush overland.

Drones, Satellite & GPS Legal Status

Drones: Namibia does not have a blanket drone ban like Botswana, but the Civil Aviation Authority of Namibia (CAAN) requires registration and permits. Recreational drones are generally tolerated in communal areas but are prohibited in national parks and within 5 km of airports and military installations. Flying over wildlife is illegal. If you intend to fly in Namibia (Kaokoland landscapes, the Marienfluss, the Caprivi), research current CAAN permit requirements well in advance — rules are evolving. As a ground rule: don't fly over or near wildlife or military facilities; check with camp managers in communal conservancies.

Satellite communicators: Garmin inReach or ZOLEO are fully legal and widely used by overlanders. No registration required. Essential for Kaokoland. GPS units / T4A: Tracks4Africa (T4A) is the standard for southern Africa overlanding. The Namibia dataset is comprehensive including Kaokoland tracks, community camps, water points, and fuel stops. Download maps offline before departure — Kaokoland has no data for live routing. Starlink: Starlink portable (Flat High Performance dish) is legal in Namibia and increasingly used in remote camps. Check Starlink's coverage map for active service zones — good coverage of main Namibia but check Kaokoland specifically.

11 Money & Currency NAD · ZAR Parity · SADC Rates

Park-by-Park Detail For This Route
Primary Park

Etosha National Park

  • Size: 22,270 km² — large central salt pan with surrounding bush and game-rich edges
  • Why go: Outstanding waterhole wildlife viewing, particularly late dry season. Floodlit night waterholes at Okaukuejo and Halali are genuinely spectacular.
  • Self-drive: Yes — extensive gravel road network throughout the park
  • Best camps: Okaukuejo (lit waterhole — black rhino common at night), Halali (mid-park, quieter), Namutoni (eastern, fort-shaped, proximity to different habitat), Olifantsrus (western, elephant concentration)
  • Ground tent: Excellent — all camps fenced, ablutions good, shade trees at pitches
  • Vet cordon: Anderson Gate, Von Lindquist Gate, Galton Gate — raw meat not south
  • Book: reservations@nwr.com.na — 2–3 months ahead (off-season)
  • Best timing for this trip: Arrive late April–May — pre-peak, post-rains, good availability
Primary — Kaokoland

Kunene Region / Kaokoland (Communal Conservancies)

  • Size: ~49,000 km² — not a formal national park but a network of 38+ communal conservancies
  • Why go: The signature Namibia 4×4 experience. Van Zyl's Pass, Marienfluss valley, Epupa Falls, Himba culture, desert-adapted lion and elephant. One of Africa's last true wilderness corridors.
  • Self-drive: Yes — highly recommended. Guides available at Epupa and Opuwo if wanted but not required.
  • Key camps: Epupa Falls Rest Camp (riverside, spectacular), Van Zyl's Pass Community Camp (basic, memorable), Marienfluss Syncro Camp, Purros Community Camp (elephant corridor), Hoarusib Valley
  • Ground tent: Good at established sites. Unfenced — full wildlife awareness required. Hard ground at some sites; carry extra pegs.
  • Booking: Most camps walk-in and pay cash locally. Epupa Falls Rest Camp and Kunene River Lodge should be booked in advance.
  • Fuel: Fill at Opuwo before any circuit. Carry minimum 120L.
Caprivi Highlight

Bwabwata National Park (Mahango + Buffalo Core)

  • Size: ~6,100 km² — two separate core areas connected by communal land
  • Mahango Core: Okavango floodplain access. Excellent birding. Large elephant herds. Vehicle accessible — excellent self-drive game drive loop along the river.
  • Buffalo Core: Namibia's best sable and roan sightings. Large buffalo herds (unusual for Namibia). Lions follow the buffalo. Very quiet — almost no other visitors.
  • Self-drive: Yes for Mahango loop. Buffalo Core has open track driving.
  • Camps: No NWR camping inside. Stay at Nunda Lodge (Divundu — Okavango River views, good facilities) for Mahango. Nambwa Tented Lodge for Buffalo Core (premium but worth it; nambwa.com).
  • Ground tent: Nambwa has campsites — unfenced, buffalo territory
Caprivi — Wild

Mudumu National Park

  • Size: ~1,010 km² — Kwando River floodplain, mopane woodland, elephant migration route
  • Why go: The least-visited park in Namibia. 2–3 vehicles per day maximum. Thick sand tracks, elephants everywhere, deafening night chorus. Raw Africa.
  • Self-drive: Yes — 4×4 only. Thick sand. No marked tracks — use T4A.
  • Camps: Nakatwa campsites (inside park — basic, river edge). Also Mukolo Camp outside park (6 campsites with river views, ablutions, electricity — mukolocamp.com).
  • Ground tent: Nakatwa is flat ground, no shade trees. Bring a shade structure. River edge is exceptional. Unfenced — elephants, hippos, buffalo through camp.
  • Book: Mukolo pre-book. Nakatwa: pay at park gate.
Optional

Nkasa Rupara (Mamili) NP

  • Size: ~320 km² — Linyanti/Chobe floodplain, two islands (Nkasa + Rupara)
  • Why go: Hippo pods, wetland birdlife, mokoro trips possible, extreme remoteness
  • Access: Rupara Island accessible in dry season. Nkasa: flood-dependent.
  • No camping inside: Use Rupara Rest Camp (3 km from park, managed by Nkasa Lupala lodge) — has camping and self-catering units
  • Consider: Worth 1–2 nights if time allows. Adds to the Caprivi circuit nicely.

13 Kaokoland — Highlights Northwest Namibia's Wild Frontier

Kaokoland (officially the Kunene Region) is the reason serious overlanders come to Namibia. It occupies the northwest corner — from the Skeleton Coast inland across ancient rocky plateaus to the Kunene River on the Angolan border. It is not a national park but a network of communal conservancies managing their own wildlife. There are essentially no formal tourist facilities outside a handful of lodges. This is where the trip earns its character.

— Epupa Falls & Kunene RiverThe Destination
Must Visit

Epupa Falls

  • What: The Kunene River plunges through a 1.5 km series of cascades dropping 60 m total into a gorge — the tallest individual drop is 37 m. Angola is on the far bank.
  • Setting: Dense palm grove (Makalani palms), fig trees, and baobabs clinging to the gorge cliffs. One of southern Africa's most dramatic campsites.
  • Camp: Epupa Falls Rest Camp — river-edge pitches between the palms. Book ahead. Ablutions good. Bar on-site.
  • Do: Sunrise over the falls (short walk from camp), Himba village visit with a local guide, swim in the pool above the falls (croc-clear section — confirm with camp manager)
  • Entry fee: No entry fee to the falls area. Camp fee only.
  • Timing: April–May post-rains the river is running hard — spectacular. October–November the water level drops but still impressive.
Cultural

Himba Villages

  • What: The Himba are a semi-nomadic pastoralist people who maintain traditional dress, ochre body paste, and cattle-herding lifestyle largely intact.
  • Approach: Always engage via a local guide or ask permission at the village before entering. Don't photograph without asking and offering something in return (small goods or money).
  • Where: Villages throughout Kaokoland. Most accessible near Opuwo, Epupa, and along the Kunene corridor. Organised visits via Opuwo lodges or community guides.
  • Cattle stampede: Near Himba villages, be aware that cattle return from long-range grazing at dusk or night — move your camp if you're in their path.
— Van Zyl's Pass & MarienflussThe 4×4 Objective
Expert 4×4

Van Zyl's Pass

  • What: A 12 km rocky descent off the escarpment into the Marienfluss Valley. The pass itself is technically demanding — narrow ledges, rock steps, sheer drops on the outside. Getting to the top from Otjitanda is arguably harder than the descent.
  • Difficulty: Expert 4×4. Low range, diff lock engaged, rock steps that will ground a poorly-equipped vehicle. Do NOT attempt in a high-clearance 2WD, soft roader, or alone.
  • Solo policy: Single-vehicle descent is possible but strongly discouraged. Recovery in this location is a multi-day exercise. Two vehicles minimum recommended.
  • LC76 suitability: The 1HZ + R151F + OME suspension combination handles Van Zyl's. Low range, centre and rear diff locks engaged for the rocky sections. Approach from Etengwa / Otjitanda side.
  • Community camp: Van Zyl's Pass Community Camp at the top — basic facilities, NAD cash payment
Iconic

Marienfluss Valley

  • What: A wide grass valley between towering mountain ranges — the Otjihipa and Hartmann mountains flanking a sand plain covered in fairy circles (unexplained circular clearings). Sweeping, silent, otherworldly.
  • Wildlife: Herds of springbok, oryx, ostrich. Desert elephant move through. Possible desert lion.
  • Drive: Open sand plain — deflate to 1.3–1.5 bar. Easy driving compared to the pass but long distances without fuel or facilities.
  • Camp: Syncro Camp at the river end. Community campsites in the valley — walk-in, NAD cash.
  • Distance consideration: From Van Zyl's Pass top to exit at Rooidrom is 150–200 km of slow track driving. Allow 2 full days.
Desert Wildlife

Purros & Hoarusib River

  • What: The Hoarusib River drains west from the Kaokoland plateau through Purros community and into the Skeleton Coast. It is a critical corridor for desert-adapted elephants.
  • Purros Camp: Community campsite on the river. Night-time elephant visits are essentially guaranteed in dry season — the camp sits directly on the main elephant route to water.
  • Skeleton Coast access: The Hoarusib riverbed can be driven west towards the coast — eventually reaching the Skeleton Coast NP. Permit required to enter the NP proper.
  • Driving: Sandy riverbed — 4×4 required, deflate tyres.
— Ruacana Falls & OpuwoGateway Town

Ruacana Falls: The Kunene River's larger waterfall (120 m drop, 700 m wide at flood). Powerful in the rainy season, sometimes barely flowing in the dry season when most water is diverted for hydroelectric generation. Worth a stop but don't plan a full day around it in the dry season. Opuwo: The capital of the Kunene Region — a genuine frontier town with a petrol station, supermarkets, ATM, a couple of lodges, and a lively market. Stock up on everything here. Kaoko Mopane Lodge has decent camping with good facilities. This is your last proper resupply before the Kaokoland circuit.

— The Lone Men of KaokolandStone Men · Treasure Hunt
Kaokoland Cult Attraction

The Lone Men (Stone Men) of Kaokoland

  • What they are: A series of life-size human sculptures made from local rock, appearing throughout the remote Kaokoland landscape. Each depicts a man in a different posture — sitting, standing, kneeling, crawling, hanging from a rock ledge. Each has a numbered aluminium disc attached with a brief message indicating where the figure is "going."
  • Numbers: A sculpture numbered at least 37 has been recorded; in practice fewer than 20 have been reliably documented by the overlanding community. Artist is anonymous and the total count unknown — new figures continue to appear.
  • Where to look: Scattered across the triangle bounded by Purros in the south, Van Zyl's Pass in the east, Otjinungua (Kunene River) in the north, and the Skeleton Coast Park in the west. You're in the right zone throughout the Kaokoland circuit. One notable one — "The Hanging Man" — is reportedly on the D3707 between Sesfontein and Orupembe, hanging from a rock overhang.
  • GPS coords: Not published by design — finding them is the point. The overlanding community actively withholds precise coordinates to preserve the treasure-hunt nature. Load the Overlanding GPS Logs & Tracks group on Facebook before departure — some pins are shared there. T4A community notes occasionally flag sightings.
  • How to find them: Scan rock outcrops and open plains as you drive the Kaokoland tracks, especially on the approaches to Van Zyl's Pass, along the Hoarusib riverbed, and between Sesfontein and Purros. They blend into the landscape — made from the same rock as their surroundings. Stop if you see a stone circle (each figure is surrounded by a ring of smaller stones).
  • Etiquette: Don't move them. Don't remove the aluminium disc. Don't publish precise GPS coordinates publicly. Photograph and log your sighting — the overlanding community is collectively mapping them.
  • Himba take: The local Himba people are reportedly unperturbed by the figures' presence — they have simply become part of the landscape.
14 Caprivi Strip — Highlights Namibia's Green Eastern Corridor

The Caprivi Strip (now officially the Zambezi Region) is Namibia's geographic anomaly — a narrow 450 km strip extending east, bordered by Angola, Botswana, Zambia and touching Zimbabwe at the Chobe-Zambezi confluence. It is green, river-threaded, and completely different from the desert heartland to the west. It's where Namibia's five perennial rivers converge and where you transition from desert overlanding to the water-world of the Okavango, Kwando, Linyanti, and Zambezi. It's also where you cross into Zambia heading north.

— The Caprivi Parks CircuitWater, Elephants & Quiet
Best Caprivi Park

Bwabwata NP — Buffalo Core Area

  • Why it's special: The only place in Namibia where you'll reliably see large buffalo herds alongside rare sable and roan antelope — species that are common in Zambia and Botswana but unusual this far west. Lions follow the buffalo.
  • Access: Turn south off B8 approximately 10 km east of Divundu. Self-drive open tracks through mopane woodland.
  • Virtually no other visitors: Genuinely one of Namibia's quietest game areas despite excellent game.
  • Stay: Nambwa Tented Lodge (premium, superb river location, strong conservation ethic) — book ahead.
Okavango Gateway

Bwabwata NP — Mahango Core Area

  • What: The Okavango River floodplain at its most accessible. A game drive loop along the river gives exceptional hippo, elephant, buffalo, and birding sightings.
  • Access: West of Divundu on the road toward Botswana (Shakawe border). Day visit or camp at Nunda Lodge nearby.
  • Crossing option: Mohembo / Shakawe border (06:00–18:00) is accessible from here into Botswana's Okavango Panhandle — an option if detouring through Botswana before Zambia.
Hidden Gem

Mudumu National Park

  • What: The least-visited park in the country. Kwando River floodplains, thick mopane, and elephant migration route from Botswana into Angola.
  • Game: Elephants everywhere — frequently blocking tracks. Hippos and crocs on the Kwando. Sitatunga and red lechwe (wetland antelope, unusual).
  • Birds: 400+ species recorded. African fish eagle, saddle-billed stork, pel's fishing owl.
  • Advice: Go here for the experience, not the facilities. Nakatwa campsites are basic. Mukolo Camp outside the park is more comfortable.
Four Corners Gateway

Katima Mulilo & Kazungula

  • What: Katima Mulilo is the largest town in the Caprivi. Gateway for Zambia crossing and the quadripoint where Namibia, Botswana, Zambia, and Zimbabwe meet.
  • Kazungula Ferry: The four-country confluence point. Botswana and Zambia are accessible from here — the Kazungula road bridge (completed 2021) now replaces the old ferry.
  • Services: ATMs (draw before Zambia), fuel, supermarkets (Choppies), basic vehicle parts. Last big provisioning stop before Zambia.
  • Stay: Caprivi Houseboat Safari Lodge or Zambezi Mubala for a river camp experience before crossing.

16 Western Namibia Route Guide Vioolsdrif → Windhoek → Etosha → Kaokoland

The western circuit covers the entry from SA, the transit to Windhoek, north to Etosha, and then the main event: the Kaokoland circuit from Kamanjab or Etosha through Damaraland, Palmwag, Opuwo, Epupa Falls, Van Zyl's Pass, Marienfluss, and exit south through Purros and Palmwag. Allow 15–18 days for a thorough Kaokoland circuit after the Etosha visit.

Route A — Full Western Circuit (18–22 days from SA border)

Vioolsdrif → Windhoek → Etosha → Palmwag → Opuwo → Epupa → Van Zyl's → Marienfluss → Purros → Skeleton Coast → Swakopmund → Windhoek
  • Day 1–2: Vioolsdrif crossing → drive north on B1 toward Springbok/Aus. Namibia's southern desert. Camp at Fish River Canyon (Hobas NWR camp, 10 km from viewpoint) — worth one night. Optional: Ai-Ais hot springs.
  • Day 3–4: Continue north B1 → Windhoek. Provision, service check, ATM, fuel, shopping. Spend 1 night in Windhoek for admin and rest.
  • Day 5–7: Windhoek → B1 north → Otjiwarongo → Outjo → Etosha (Anderson or Okaukuejo Gate). 3 nights Etosha — Okaukuejo (night waterhole), Halali (day drive), Olifantsrus (western elephant circuit). Fill tank at Okaukuejo or Halali fuel station.
  • Day 8: Exit Etosha at Galton Gate west → Kamanjab → begin Damaraland entry via C40. Camp at Palmwag or Khorixas restcamp.
  • Day 9: Damaraland — Twyfelfontein (UNESCO rock art, half-day), Burned Mountain, Organ Pipes. Drive north toward Sesfontein.
  • Day 10: Sesfontein → Opuwo (C43). Opuwo: full fuel, groceries, ATM, water. Stock up for 5+ days self-sufficient. Camp Kaoko Mopane Lodge.
  • Day 11–12: Opuwo → D3700 → Kunene River Lodge (200 km — allow full day). First look at the Kunene. Camp on the river.
  • Day 13–14: Kunene River Lodge → Epupa Falls (90 km). 2 nights at Epupa Falls Rest Camp — sunrise, Himba village, explore.
  • Day 15: Epupa → Etengwa → Van Zyl's Pass approach. Camp at Van Zyl's Community Camp (top of pass).
  • Day 16: Van Zyl's descent into Marienfluss. Slow descent — allow 4–6 hours. Camp in Marienfluss valley (Syncro Camp or community site).
  • Day 17: Marienfluss valley drive → south via Rooidrom landmark → Purros Community Camp (Hoarusib River). Elephant territory.
  • Day 18–19: Purros → Skeleton Coast NP (permit required) → coastal drive south → Torra Bay / Terrace Bay (NWR fishing camps) → exit south through the Skeleton Coast to Henties Bay → Swakopmund.
  • Day 20: Swakopmund (rest day, provisions, vehicle check).
  • Day 21–22: Swakopmund → Windhoek (B2, 370 km). Or extend west for Sossusvlei.

Route B — Compressed Western Loop (12–14 days)

For a tighter schedule or pre-Caprivi positioning
  • Skip Fish River Canyon (do on return if time)
  • Windhoek 1 night → Etosha 2 nights → Palmwag → Opuwo → Epupa (2 nights) → Van Zyl's → Marienfluss (1 night) → Purros → Kamanjab → Windhoek
  • Omit Skeleton Coast coastal drive — save for a dedicated future trip
  • Allows 14–16 days for Caprivi and Zambia transition

17 Eastern Caprivi Route Guide Windhoek / Etosha → Rundu → Caprivi Strip → Zambia

The Caprivi leg is the eastern run: from Windhoek or Etosha east on the B8 highway to Rundu (fast road, do in a day), then the parks circuit through Bwabwata, Mudumu, and optionally Nkasa Rupara, before exiting into Zambia at Wenela / Sesheke. Allow 7–10 days for a thorough Caprivi exploration.

Route C — Full Caprivi Circuit (8–10 days)

Windhoek → Rundu → Bwabwata → Mudumu → Katima Mulilo → Zambia crossing
  • Day 1: Windhoek → Rundu via B1/B8 (~700 km, sealed, fast). Fill fuel in Rundu. Camp at Kaisosi River Lodge or N'Kwazi Lodge (on the Okavango river — beautiful sunset).
  • Day 2: Rundu → Divundu (~210 km on B8). Divundu is the junction for Bwabwata Mahango. Afternoon: Mahango game drive loop along the Okavango (2–3 hours).
  • Day 3: Divundu area → Buffalo Core Area via B8 east → turn south. Full day in Buffalo Core for sable, roan, buffalo. Camp Nambwa Tented Lodge (book ahead).
  • Day 4: Nambwa → B8 east → C49 south → Mudumu NP entrance (~100 km). Self-drive Mudumu tracks. Camp Nakatwa or Mukolo.
  • Day 5: Full day Mudumu — morning and late afternoon drives. The middle of the day is for camp rest. River-edge bird list is outstanding.
  • Day 6: Optional — Nkasa Rupara loop (+80 km return from C49). Otherwise continue east on B8 toward Katima Mulilo.
  • Day 7: Katima Mulilo — provision, ATM, fuel, vehicle check. Camp Caprivi Houseboat Lodge or riverside camp for a final Namibia night on the Zambezi.
  • Day 8: Katima Mulilo → Wenela border (17 km) → Zambia crossing. Into Zambia and heading for Livingstone / Kafue.

Route D — Combined Entry Option via Botswana

Optional detour — Mahango → Botswana (Panhandle) → re-enter Caprivi

If time allows, crossing into Botswana at Mohembo/Shakawe after the Mahango visit, spending 2–3 days in the Okavango Panhandle (excellent mokoro and birding), and re-entering Namibia at Ngoma Bridge allows you to combine the Caprivi strip with a taste of Botswana. This is a natural routing if Botswana is the next country on the itinerary after Zambia — exit Zambia at Livingstone and loop back through Chobe and Kasane rather than retracing.

18 Essential Phrases English · Afrikaans · Oshivambo

English is widely spoken in Namibia — it is the sole official language (since independence in 1990). Afrikaans is a first language for many Namibians and is useful particularly in towns and with border officials of older generations. In Kaokoland, Oshivambo (Ovambo language group) and Otjiherero (Himba/Herero) are the local languages. A few words of Oshivambo or Otjiherero go a long way in the north. This document lists practical field phrases in Afrikaans (useful throughout) and Oshivambo (for the Kaokoland/Caprivi zone).
