02 Border Crossings From Namibia · Eastern · Northern Approaches

Angola shares land borders with Namibia to the south, DRC to the north and east, Zambia to the east, and Congo-Brazzaville to the north via Cabinda exclave. For overlanders approaching from southern Africa, the Namibia crossings are the overwhelmingly preferred entry points. Zambian and DRC crossings carry significantly higher risk and complexity.

— From Namibia (Southern Approach)Primary Route
Primary — 4×4 Vehicle

Oshikango (NAM) ↔ Santa Clara (ANG)

  • Main commercial and tourist crossing between Namibia and Angola
  • Fully sealed road both sides; 24-hour operation
  • Most overlanders and all SA-plated vehicles use this crossing
  • Currency exchange possible at the border — essential (Kwanza only inside Angola)
  • Immigration and customs process typically 1–2 hours on a quiet day
  • Hours: 24hr (Angola side is 1 hour ahead of Namibia)
Exit Route — South Circuit

Calueque / Ruacana (ANG–NAM)

  • The standard exit for the southern circuit (Iona/Cunene loop)
  • Unsealed approach on the Angola side — 4×4 essential
  • Quieter than Santa Clara; process typically faster
  • Hours: 07:00–18:00 Angola time
  • Fuel at Xangongo ~50 km inside Angola before crossing

Santa Clara Border — Step-by-Step Process

Oshikango (NAM) → Santa Clara (ANG) · Allow 1–2 hours
  • Exit Namibia normally at Oshikango immigration window
  • On Angola side: enter parking — visit small office on left of warehouses first (immigration / visa stamp)
  • Then: small office at exit between the two warehouses for TIP + road tax
  • Road tax payment: given an account number, pay at bank in large building beside the office
  • Carnet holders: present Carnet for stamp — no TIP form, no payment
  • Non-carnet: TIP costs ~USD 9 per month (official rate in AOA)
  • Documents needed: passport, vehicle registration, driver's licence — have copies accessible
  • Exiting Angola at any land border: single hut handles everything — hand in TIP, get exit stamp
Currency tip at Santa Clara The official e-visa fee (for non-SA passports) is USD 120 but billed in AOA at ~36,600 Kz. If you pay in Namibian Dollars, the border officer converts at the AOA rate — effectively paying closer to USD 70 equivalent. Offer NAD first. Relevant if travelling with non-SA companions.
— Eastern & Northern CrossingsComplex — Research First
Eastern — High Risk

Chavuma (ZAM) ↔ Cazombo (ANG)

  • Remote Zambezi River crossing in far eastern Angola
  • Road from Cazombo westward passes through Moxico province — active landmine risk
  • Very limited services; full self-sufficiency essential
  • Not recommended for independent overlanders without prior specialist intelligence
Northern — Avoid

DRC–Angola Crossings

  • Lunda Norte crossings: banditry risk, primarily mining industry traffic
  • Kongo Central / Matadi corridor: requires prior arrangement, procedures not standardised
  • Multiple permit requirements; significant delays common
  • Not recommended without local fixer and specialist preparation
Cabinda Province — Do Not Enter The Cabinda exclave (separated from mainland Angola by DRC) is subject to ongoing FLEC separatist activity. Kidnappings of foreign nationals have occurred. All major travel advisories classify Cabinda as high risk. No overland route justifies transiting Cabinda independently.

04 Best Season & Timing Dry Season Focus · Off-Peak
Overland Season
May – Oct
Dry season. All tracks open. Iona and Cunene accessible. Landmine displacement risk minimised. Temperatures moderate in the south.
Wet Season
Nov – Apr
Secondary roads impassable, mines shift in floods, bridges wash out. Deep south 4×4 tracks effectively closed. EN1 corridor remains passable.
Best Off-Season Window
Late Apr – Jun
Rains just ended, tracks drying, temperatures pleasant. Minimal tourist presence. Aligns well with a post-Namibia northward sequence.
Kalandula Falls Peak
Nov – Apr
Falls at maximum volume in wet season — spectacular but access tracks to Pedras Negras and Uíge become very challenging. Trade-off decision.

Angola does not have the same European peak-season dynamic as Namibia — tourist volumes are low year-round. The relevant constraint is purely practical: the 4×4 tracks that define the southern circuit (Iona interior, Foz do Cunene, coastal desert) are impassable in the wet season and carry elevated landmine displacement risk following rain events. May–October is the only viable window for the southern circuit. The northern EN1 corridor (Luanda–Lubango–Huambo) is accessible year-round.

06 Roads Right-Hand Traffic · Landmine Rule · Checkpoints

Angola drives on the right — the LC76 is RHD, so overtaking requires extra care. The main corridor from Santa Clara to Lubango and from Lubango to Luanda is largely sealed (Chinese-built post-2002) and in reasonable condition. Away from the main arteries, roads deteriorate rapidly. The deep south — Iona, Namibe desert tracks, Foz do Cunene — is 4×4-only territory.

07 Security Landmines · Regional Risk · Checkpoints
Landmines — the primary overland hazard in Angola Extensive minefields remain from the 27-year civil war (ended 2002), concentrated in southern provinces: Cuando Cubango, Bié, Benguela, and Moxico. During the rainy season, mines shift and can resurface outside known fields. Watch for PERIGO MINAS warning signs — red triangle, skull. Never leave established tracks or tarmac in rural areas under any circumstances.

09 Camp Safety & Wildlife Ground Tent · Iona · Cunene River

Angola's camping environment divides into two quite different settings: established bush camps in the Lubango/Namibe/Baia dos Pipas zone (generally low wildlife risk, focus on security against theft), and the deep south — Iona NP and the Cunene River corridor — where the environment itself is the hazard rather than human crime.

Camp Rules — Angola-SpecificIona & General

Iona NP: Wild camping banned since May 2024 — use designated campsites (12 sites managed by African Parks). No open fires inside the park except at designated fire rings. Waste must be carried out entirely. Night driving rule: As noted in Section 06, do not drive at night anywhere in Angola. Time your movements to be in camp before dark. Landmine boundary: Never leave the established camp perimeter or vehicle tracks at any remote site in this region. The track is the only verified cleared ground.

Snakes & Smaller HazardsDesert South

Southern Angola (Namib extension zone) has puff adders, cape cobras, and horned adders in the rocky desert terrain. Standard protocol: shake boots every morning, check sleeping gear. Scorpions are common in the rocky Namibe desert — more prevalent than in the grassy north. Antivenom: not reliably available outside Luanda — your first aid kit should include anti-venom protocols and the satellite communicator is your evacuation trigger.

10 Communications SIM · Coverage · Satellite

Angola's mobile network is concentrated along main urban corridors. Southern Angola — the Iona region, Namibe desert, Cunene River area — has significant coverage gaps. Unitel has the widest national coverage; Africell is expanding rapidly with 4G/5G in urban centres. For remote southern travel, a satellite communicator is non-negotiable.

Drones & Photography RestrictionsLegal Risk

Drone permits in Angola are difficult to obtain and rarely approved for foreign nationals. Effectively prohibited in most areas without specific authorisation from the Angolan aviation authority (INAVIC). Do not fly drones near military installations, government buildings, airports, or along the border zones. Photography of any government infrastructure (bridges, buildings, checkpoints) is illegal regardless of whether you are on foot or in a vehicle.

11 Money & Currency Kwanza · Cash Strategy · AOA Only in Parks

Angola uses the Kwanza (AOA). It cannot be purchased outside Angola — obtain on arrival only at the border, bank, or ATM. There is no SADC tier pricing; Angola's parks charge flat fees in Kwanza only (government regulation since May 2024).

12 National Parks & Camping Iona · Kissama · African Parks

Angola is rebuilding its national park system after four decades of civil war devastation. Two parks are currently accessible to overlanders: Iona in the deep south (the primary 4×4 destination) and Kissama near Luanda. Both are managed by African Parks.

Primary Destination

Iona National Park

  • 15,150 km² — largest park in Angola; part of the Namib Desert transfrontier
  • Managed by African Parks since 2019 — improving rapidly
  • 12 designated camping areas — wild camping no longer permitted (May 2024)
  • Fees: Kwanza only — no foreign currency accepted (May 2024 government regulation)
  • Must carry all water, food, and fuel for entire park visit
  • Angolan giraffe reintroduced 2023–2024; desert elephant, springbok, gemsbok, brown hyena
  • Access via Oncocua (from Namibia via Ruacana / Calueque border)
  • Contact African Parks before arrival: africanparks.org — confirm fees, site availability, access conditions
Near Luanda

Kissama (Quiçama) National Park

  • 9,960 km² — 70 km south of Luanda; day trip or overnight
  • Wildlife: elephant, giraffe, zebra, buffalo, various antelope
  • Lodge available for overnight stays; guided game drives available
  • Infrastructure improving but still basic — not comparable to SA parks
  • Self-drive permitted; guided drive recommended (poor signage)
  • Worth a day visit if routing through Luanda
Iona logistics — plan carefully in advance Contact Iona NP management via African Parks (africanparks.org) before arrival to confirm current camping fees, site availability, and access conditions. The park communication infrastructure is still developing — having pre-arranged information prevents unpleasant surprises at the gate.
13 Southern Angola — Highlights Lubango · Serra da Leba · Namibe · Iona

The southern Angola circuit is the heartland of the overland experience — a dramatic progression from highland plateau to desert coast, ending in one of Africa's most remote wilderness areas. The region is anchored by Lubango in the highlands and Namibe on the coast, with the Namib desert, Iona NP, and the Kunene River mouth as the defining destinations beyond.

— Lubango & the Huíla Highlands
★★★★★ Base Camp

Lubango

  • 1,700 m elevation — cool, pleasant climate year-round; frost possible in winter
  • Angola's most liveable southern city: fuel, supermarkets, mechanics, accommodation
  • Strong Boer settler history — Afrikaner descendants from 1880s migration still present
  • Cristo Rei statue overlooks city from a hilltop — viewpoint over the valley
  • Best base for 2–3 night stay; day trips in all directions
  • Portuguese cuisine in local restaurants; fresh produce at the market
★★★★★ Must-See

Tunda-Vala Gorge

  • 17 km from Lubango — largely sealed road to the escarpment rim
  • 2,600 m volcanic fissure: sheer 1,000 m cliffs dropping to a green canyon below
  • Multiple viewpoints along the rim; short walking trail recommended
  • No entrance fee, rarely crowded — often the entire escarpment to yourself
  • Late afternoon light is exceptional — plan at least 2 hours on site
★★★★★ Must-See

Serra da Leba Pass

  • One of Africa's most spectacular paved mountain roads — hairpins descend 1,000 m to the coastal plain
  • Best at sunrise: mist fills the valleys while the road catches golden light
  • Viewpoint platform at the top; roadside vendors on the descent
  • Tarmac throughout — accessible to any vehicle. Toll Kz 150 (~R3)
  • 45 min from Lubango; on the direct route to Namibe
— Namibe Coast & Desert
★★★★ Coastal Hub

Namibe (Moçâmedes)

  • Coastal desert city with strong Portuguese character — tiled facades, shaded colonnades
  • Excellent seafood: crayfish, kingklip, mackerel — landed daily at the harbour
  • Cold Benguela current keeps temperatures moderate; sea fog common in mornings
  • Museu Regional: dinosaur fossils discovered in Namibe province on display
  • Fish Market (Mercado do Peixe) — spectacular early mornings
  • Day trips: Baia dos Pipas (30 min), desert coast north and south
★★★★ Beach Camp

Baia dos Pipas

  • Sheltered bay 30 min from Namibe — camping directly on the beach
  • Good surf break, sea fishing, flamingos wading at low tide
  • Flamingo Lodge nearby for those who prefer beds
  • Track access from Namibe — 4×4 preferred, standard vehicle possible in dry season
  • Bring all food and water; no services on the beach itself
★★★★★ Legendary

Baia dos Tigres

  • A sand spit cut off by water — accessible only at low tide or by boat
  • Namib dunes meeting the Atlantic; sea fog rolls in most mornings
  • Ghost town of rusted colonial fishing industry buildings — surreal atmosphere
  • One of the most remote isolated beaches in all of Africa
  • Tidal timing is critical — plan entry and exit carefully from Tombua
  • Tombua is the last fuel stop; Baia dos Tigres has nothing
— Iona, Cunene & The Deep South
★★★★★ Wilderness

Iona National Park

  • 15,150 km² — geologically one of the oldest deserts in the world
  • Gravel plains, petrified dunes, ancient camelthorn-lined dry riverbeds
  • Welwitschia plants over 1,000 years old survive in the park interior
  • Wildlife recovering: desert elephant, springbok, gemsbok, ostrich, brown hyena
  • Angolan giraffe reintroduced 2023–2024 — now resident in northern sections
  • 12 designated campsites; Kwanza only; contact African Parks before arrival
★★★★★ Remote

Foz do Cunene (Kunene River Mouth)

  • Where the Kunene River meets the Atlantic — lush riverine vegetation against stark Namib
  • Flamingos, hippos, crocodiles; spectacular Atlantic wave action at the river mouth
  • The sense of remoteness is total — 2-day drive from the nearest significant town
  • Camp on the river bank — bring everything including wood and water
  • Access from Oncocua via Iona/Espinheira track — demanding 4×4 territory
  • Large crocs in the Cunene — do not go near the water's edge
★★★★ Culture

Himba Communities — Oncocua Region

  • Semi-nomadic Himba people in traditional ochre-painted settlements
  • More isolated from tourism than Namibian Himba — encounters feel more authentic
  • Engage through a local guide; do not photograph without explicit permission
  • The Himba territory sits at the junction of the Iona and Cunene routes near Oncocua

Southern Angola Delivers

  • Virtually no other tourists — complete solitude
  • Genuinely dramatic landscapes at every turn
  • Very cheap diesel — even after subsidy reforms, ~3× cheaper than SA
  • Warm, hospitable people outside Luanda
  • Visa-free for SA passports — no pre-trip admin
  • Roads improving rapidly — better than reputation suggests
  • Iona: one of the last truly wild national parks in Africa
  • Baia dos Tigres: Africa's most remote beach experience

Genuine Challenges

  • Landmines — real and ongoing risk off tarmac
  • Portuguese only — zero English outside Luanda
  • Kwanza cannot be sourced before arrival
  • Medical evacuation insurance is not optional
  • Police checkpoint frequency and occasional bribery attempts
  • Cholera outbreak active — strict water hygiene required
  • No mechanical support outside provincial capitals
14 Northern Angola — Highlights Luanda · Kalandula Falls · Central Highlands

Northern Angola is a completely different experience from the desert south — lush tropical highlands, powerful waterfalls, colonial architecture, and a sprawling oil-rich capital. Infrastructure is improving rapidly but the north demands more urban navigation, greater security awareness in Luanda, and a higher budget.

— LuandaSecurity: High Risk
City Overview
Population ~9 million. One of Africa's most expensive cities. Oil wealth and extreme inequality side by side. Skyline of Chinese-built towers; colonial Portuguese architecture survives in the Baixa. Baía de Luanda is spectacular at sunset.
Security — High Risk
Carjacking, armed robbery, and bag snatching common. Lock all doors while moving. Do not use phone in public. Park only in guarded compounds. Use Uber or UGO for all city movement — never flag street taxis. Avoid Sambizanga, Cazenga, Palanca.
Vehicle Logistics
Do not drive your overland vehicle through Luanda city traffic. Park at a guarded guesthouse in Talatona or Miramar districts and use Uber for everything. Fuel available throughout.
What to See
Fortaleza de São Miguel (colonial fort, museum), Baixa de Luanda (harbour district), Ilha de Luanda (peninsula beaches, restaurants), Miradouro da Lua 40 km south — lunar clay cliffs on the Atlantic coast. Allow 2–3 days.
— Kalandula Falls & Malanje Province
Major Natural Highlight

Quedas de Kalandula

  • 105 m high, 400+ m wide — volume rivals Victoria Falls in wet season
  • Horseshoe-shaped cascade on the Lucala River; one of Africa's most impressive and least-known waterfalls
  • Short trail to base — swimming possible in dry season
  • Basic guesthouse at Kalandula town; no camp at falls itself
  • 360 km from Luanda via N230 — 4–5 hour drive; tarmac most of route
  • Combine with Pedras Negras for a 2-day loop from Malanje
Geological Landmark

Pedras Negras de Pungo Andongo

  • Ancient volcanic basalt formations rising 200–300 m from flat savanna — hauntingly surreal
  • Queen Njinga of Matamba ruled from this area in the 17th century
  • 70 km from Malanje; short unsealed track to site
  • 4×4 recommended; standard vehicle possible in dry season
  • No formal tourist infrastructure — bring everything
Central Highlands

Huambo

  • 1,700 m elevation — cool highland climate, architecturally interesting
  • Good fuel, accommodation, and services — useful mid-corridor stop
  • Colonial railway station architecture survives
  • Lubango → Huambo: ~300 km via EN1, approx 4 hours
  • Huambo → Luanda: ~600 km via EN1/EN2, approx 7–8 hours

16 Southern Angola Route Guide Santa Clara → Lubango → Namibe → Iona → Foz do Cunene

Two main entry routes and several circuit options for the southern Angola overland. All distances are indicative — allow generous time buffers. Roads have improved dramatically since 2002 but pace is still slower than Namibia, particularly south of Namibe.

Route A — Main Southern Circuit (10–14 days)

Oshikango → Lubango → Namibe → Iona → Foz do Cunene → Calueque exit
  • Day 1: Santa Clara border → Ondjiva → Cahama → overnight near Cahama (~350 km, tarmac)
  • Day 2: Cahama → Lubango. City tour: Tunda-Vala, Cristo Rei. Overnight Lubango. Fill fuel and provisions.
  • Day 3: Day trip — Serra da Leba Pass → Humpata → return to Lubango overnight
  • Day 4: Lubango → Serra da Leba → Namibe (~170 km, tarmac). Afternoon in Namibe.
  • Day 5–6: Namibe → Baia dos Pipas → Tombua → Baia dos Tigres (timing tides). Re-fuel at Tombua.
  • Day 7–9: Tombua → Oncocua → Espinheira → Iona NP (deep south camping circuit). Fully self-sufficient.
  • Day 10–11: Iona → Foz do Cunene → Oncocua exit
  • Day 12–13: Oncocua → Cahama → Calueque border → Ruacana (Namibia)
  • Fuel loads: Full tank Lubango, full + 40L jerry Namibe, full + 80–120L jerry Tombua for Iona/Cunene segment

Route B — Express South (6–8 days)

For tighter visa budgets or pre-arranged return
  • Skips deep Iona circuit — focuses on Lubango highlights and coastal desert
  • Lubango (2 nights) → Namibe (2 nights) → Tombua/Baia dos Tigres → Oncocua → Calueque
  • More relaxed pace — time at each location rather than transit driving
  • Suitable for overlanders doing Angola as part of a longer route with time constraints

Route C — Extended Northern Circuit (20–28 days)

Full Angola run combining south + north
  • Adds Luanda (2–3 days), Kissama NP day trip, Kalandula Falls, Pedras Negras
  • Luanda to Lubango via Huambo — excellent Chinese-built EN1 tarmac
  • Luanda: use guarded accommodation in Talatona, move by Uber — avoid driving in city
  • Budget the full 30 days — extension possible once in Luanda

🗺 Navigation — Southern Angola
OsmAnd Angola offline maps are the best free option. Download before leaving Namibia — no data inside Iona or Cunene. Waypoints on iOverlander and Tracks4Africa Angola dataset (limited coverage). Always cross-reference multiple sources before committing to an unmarked track.
📅 Best Season — Southern Angola
May to October (dry season) is the only viable window for all 🟠 and 🔴 rated tracks. Iona interior and Foz do Cunene effectively impassable in the wet season. The Namibe–Tombua coastal track and Humpata loop are year-round accessible.
17 Northern Angola Route Guide EN1 Corridor · Luanda · Malanje · Kalandula

The northern circuit uses the EN1/EN2 corridor — Chinese-built tarmac connecting Lubango through Huambo to Luanda, then northeast to Malanje. It is a fundamentally different experience: more urban, more expensive, but home to some of Angola's most underrated natural landmarks. Most northern routes are tarmac-dominant.

Route D — Full Northern Circuit (20–28 days from Namibia)

Santa Clara → Lubango → Huambo → Luanda → Malanje → Kalandula → return south
  • Days 1–5: Southern circuit to Lubango (see Route A or B above)
  • Days 6–8: Lubango → EN1 north → Huambo (~300 km, 4 hours). One night Huambo. Continue → Luanda (~600 km from Huambo — 2 days).
  • Days 9–11: Luanda (2–3 nights). Baixa, Fortaleza de São Miguel, Miradouro da Lua. Use Uber for all movement. Kissama NP day trip.
  • Days 12–13: Luanda → Malanje via EN230 (~360 km, 4–5 hours). Overnight Malanje.
  • Day 14: Malanje → Kalandula Falls (~70 km, 1 hour). Full afternoon at the falls. Overnight Kalandula guesthouse.
  • Day 15: Kalandula → Pedras Negras de Pungo Andongo loop (~150 km). Return to Malanje overnight.
  • Days 16–18: Malanje → Luanda → south on EN1 → Benguela → Lubango. Two long driving days.
  • Days 19–21: Return south circuit — Serra da Leba, Namibe day, Calueque border exit or repeat Iona if time remains.

Northern Circuit Adds

  • Kalandula Falls — one of Africa's great waterfalls, virtually unknown
  • Pedras Negras — surreal geology and Njinga kingdom history
  • Luanda — oil-boom capital with extraordinary colonial architecture
  • EN1 highland scenery Lubango to Luanda
  • Kissama NP wildlife without Iona's logistics
  • All 4×4 tracks moderate — more accessible than deep south

Northern Challenges

  • Luanda demands a completely different security mindset
  • Very expensive: accommodation and restaurants in Luanda
  • 2,200+ km extra distance from Lubango return to Malanje
  • Minimum 7 additional days to do justice to the north
  • 30-day visa may be tight — apply for extension in Luanda if needed
  • Uíge tracks undocumented — limited current overland intelligence
