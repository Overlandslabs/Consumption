1 — Coverage Architecture

A two-year African overland cannot be covered by a single policy. Four distinct layers are required. Each layer fills gaps the others leave. Remove any one layer and you have an exposure that could financially end the trip or threaten your life.

1
Vehicle Insurance — Comprehensive + Cross-Border
Covers loss of or damage to the vehicle itself. SA comprehensive policies have significant limitations for long-duration Africa travel — most cap at 90 days outside SA. For a 2-year trip you may be forced to self-insure against write-off and carry third-party liability only.
90-DAY LIMIT RISK
2
Third-Party Liability by Country — Yellow Card (COMESA)
Legally required at most African borders. The COMESA Yellow Card is the regional third-party scheme covering 13 countries across East and Southern Africa. Botswana and Namibia require their own border policies. Not covered by your SA comprehensive policy — this is a separate purchase.
REQUIRED AT BORDERS
3
Health / Medical Insurance — SA Medical Aid (Bonitas + Genesis)
Your underlying SA medical aid covers emergency treatment outside SA for 90 days per trip only. Bonitas (Mary-Ann) includes R2.5m–R10m international emergency cover with ER24 partnership. Genesis (Neil) does not include international travel cover — this is a confirmed gap requiring alternative cover from departure date.
90-DAY TRIP LIMIT
4
Travel Insurance + Medical Evacuation
Fills what medical aid will not cover: evacuation logistics, repatriation of remains, political evacuation, trip duration beyond 90 days, adventure activity cover, and the cost of getting you to a facility. Must be long-duration capable (12–24 months).
GAP FILLER
5
Dedicated Medevac Membership (AMREF Maisha + inReach SOS + IsatPhone 2)
Travel insurance pays the bills. A medevac membership organises the helicopter. AMREF Flying Doctors (East Africa) and your Garmin inReach Mini 2 (global SOS/tracking via Iridium) and Inmarsat IsatPhone 2 (voice SOS via GEOS + direct voice calls to medevac providers) are the operational activation mechanisms. Use both devices simultaneously in a serious emergency — they operate on independent satellite networks and independent coordination centres.
ORGANISES THE AIRCRAFT
The critical insight: When a layer is missing, the one below it cannot compensate. A medevac membership without travel insurance means the aircraft arrives but you owe the costs. Travel insurance without a medevac membership means the money exists but no aircraft has been dispatched. SA medical aid alone caps at 90 days and excludes evacuation logistics. Get all five layers in place before departure.

3 — Third-Party Liability & the COMESA Yellow Card

Driving without valid third-party insurance in another country is illegal and leaves you personally liable for damages in a road accident. Third-party insurance is the minimum floor — it protects you from ruin if you injure or kill someone, or damage their property, in a collision.

COMESA Yellow Card — What It Is

The COMESA Yellow Card is a regional third-party motor vehicle insurance scheme recognised across 13 African countries. It provides the minimum legally required third-party liability cover in participating states — covering bodily injury and property damage you cause to third parties. It also covers emergency medical expenses for occupants of the insured vehicle arising from an accident. It does not cover damage to your own vehicle.

Yellow Card Coverage KEY MECHANISM

Countries covered: Burundi, DR Congo, Eritrea, Ethiopia, Kenya, Malawi, Rwanda, Sudan, Tanzania, Uganda, Zambia, Zimbabwe, and Djibouti. South Africa is not a COMESA member.

What it covers: Third-party bodily injury, third-party property damage, and emergency medical expenses for vehicle occupants — to the minimum legal limit of each participating country.

What it does not cover: Damage to your own vehicle, your own medical expenses beyond emergency first aid, theft, or any event not arising from a road traffic accident.

Duration: 30 to 365 days available. For a 2-year trip, purchase 365-day cards and budget for one renewal mid-trip.

Issue from SA: South Africa is not a COMESA member. However, South African residents can obtain a Yellow Card from Mukfin Insurance (Randburg), which operates via a reciprocal arrangement with a COMESA-issuer. Contact: +27 10 210 7093 or +27 11 886 0667. Also available in Lusaka, Zambia — the easiest and cheapest acquisition point on the route.

Cost & Practicalities VERIFY 2027/2028

Cost factors: Duration of cover, vehicle size/type, and countries included all affect the price. An indicative forum-reported price for a 4x4 covering all COMESA countries for 6 months was approximately USD 57 (around R1,000–R1,200 at April 2026 rates). Longer durations and larger vehicles cost more. Get a written quote from Mukfin before departure.

Before purchasing: You must have an underlying prime policy (your SA vehicle insurance) — the Yellow Card is issued as an endorsement to that policy.

Carry the card: The physical Yellow Card should be in the vehicle at all times. Some border officials and police officers will ask for it. It is standard and uniformly recognised across the scheme.

Tip — buy in Zambia: Zambia (Livingstone or Lusaka) is the most cost-effective and straightforward point to acquire the Yellow Card for East Africa. If crossing at Kazungula, go to Livingstone before proceeding north.

4 — Health Insurance: SA Medical Aid

SA medical aid is not designed for two-year Africa expeditions. It fills the hospital bill at a foreign facility — up to a limit, for 90 days. Beyond that, it does not pay, does not organise transport, and does not cover evacuation logistics. Know exactly what your scheme covers before you leave SA.

Mary-Ann — Bonitas
Bonitas International Travel Benefit 90-DAY TRIP LIMIT

Cover limit: R2.5m per family (BonStart) to R10m per family (upper plans) for medical emergencies. Confirm which plan Mary-Ann holds — limits vary significantly by plan tier. The R1.2m limit applies on Standard and Hospital Standard plans.

Emergency partner: Bonitas uses ER24 as its international travel partner for claims management and evacuation logistics.

Duration: 90 days per trip from departure from SA. A 2-year continuous expedition exceeds this — cover lapses after Day 90. The gap must be filled by travel insurance.

Activation: Must register for the international travel benefit prior to departure. Do this at least 2 weeks before departure date. Confirm registration confirmation received in writing.

Excluded: Treatment for pre-existing conditions, elective treatment, medical evacuation logistics (ER24 handles this as a service, but the costs beyond the medical treatment may need to be claimed through travel insurance), repatriation of remains, emergency dental beyond a low sub-limit, chronic medication supply outside SA.

Claims process: Notify Bonitas/ER24 as soon as possible. For hospitalisation requiring advance payment guarantee, call before admission where possible. Keep all original invoices and medical reports. Claim within 4 months of treatment.

Neil — Genesis
Genesis Medical Aid — International Cover NO INTERNATIONAL COVER

Critical gap: Genesis Medical Scheme does not include an international travel benefit. This is confirmed by Genesis's own documentation. Neil has no medical aid cover outside South Africa from the moment he crosses the border.

Action required: Neil must secure supplementary international medical cover before departure — either via a comprehensive long-term travel insurance policy with medical cover, or by switching to a medical aid scheme that includes international benefits (e.g. Discovery, Bonitas, Momentum) before the trip starts. A switch takes 3 months waiting period for new conditions — plan well ahead.

Options: (1) Switch to a medical aid with international cover before 2028 departure — Discovery, Bonitas, or Momentum all offer this. (2) Rely entirely on travel insurance for medical cover outside SA (World Nomads, SafetyWing, IMG Global). (3) Combination: keep Genesis for SA-based care, buy long-term travel medical insurance for the trip duration.

Recommendation: Address this gap in 2027 planning. Do not depart with Genesis as your only medical cover.

What SA Medical Aid Will NOT Cover (Either Scheme)
Regardless of which plan you hold, the following require alternative cover:
  • Medical evacuation logistics and organisation — ER24 may manage this under Bonitas, but the medevac membership organises the aircraft
  • Repatriation of remains to South Africa
  • Emergency dental treatment beyond a basic sub-limit (typically R500–R2,000)
  • Chronic medication supply for more than 5 months outside SA (check your plan)
  • Mental health treatment overseas
  • Any treatment after Day 90 of a continuous trip
  • Political evacuation (non-medical)
  • Pre-existing condition treatment
5 — Travel Insurance with Medical Evacuation

Travel insurance fills the gap above your medical aid: duration beyond 90 days, evacuation costs, adventure activities, political evacuation, and repatriation. For a 2-year Africa overland, long-duration cover is non-negotiable. Not all travel insurance policies are capable of this — most cap at 30–90 days.

Minimum requirements for this trip: Medical expenses minimum USD 500,000. Medical evacuation and repatriation explicitly included. Adventure activities covered (self-drive off-road, remote camping, water crossings). Policy duration 12–24 months, or renewable mid-trip without a return to SA. No "developed country only" restriction. No exclusion for Africa or sub-Saharan Africa. Clear definition of "remote area" cover. Political evacuation option.

6 — Dedicated Medevac Membership

A medevac membership is a subscription to an air rescue and medical evacuation service. It does not pay medical bills — it organises the aircraft, medical crew, and coordination to move you to a facility capable of treating you. This is operationally separate from travel insurance. Both are needed.

Travel insurance pays the bills. Medevac membership organises the helicopter. Without the membership, you rely on your travel insurer to arrange evacuation — which adds delay, approval steps, and uncertainty. A medevac membership bypasses this: one call activates the aircraft directly. In a serious trauma or time-critical event, that delay can be the difference between survival and death.

Understanding Global Rescue vs Garmin Response — Not the Same Thing
Garmin Response coordinates. Global Rescue rescues. This distinction matters in a serious emergency and is widely misunderstood.

Garmin Response (via inReach SOS) is a coordination relay bundled with your satellite subscription. When you press SOS, your GPS transmits via Iridium to Garmin's IERCC centre. A coordinator texts your device and contacts the most appropriate local emergency service or medevac provider. Garmin Response does not deploy anyone or fund anything — its job is to hand your case to the right local provider. Without a medevac membership, the local provider may not respond.

Global Rescue is a funded medevac membership — they take end-to-end ownership of your extraction. Founded 2004, staffed by military special operations veterans and physicians (Johns Hopkins Emergency Medicine partnership). Call them on the IsatPhone 2 (+1 617 459 4200, 24/7): they deploy resources, manage the evacuation, and take you to the hospital of your choice — including your home hospital in SA. No invoice, no deductibles. Accepts SA residents. For East Africa they typically subcontract AMREF's aircraft but manage the whole relationship themselves.

What Global Rescue offers that AMREF does not: (1) Security/political evacuation on higher-tier plans — extraction from civil unrest or conflict. Relevant for DRC, Ethiopia, Burundi legs. (2) Hospital of choice — AMREF flies to Nairobi; Global Rescue takes you where you need to go.

Cost (verify at globalrescue.com — prices increased April 2026): A 2-year continuous expedition requires 365-day trip coverage — significantly more than the standard 45-day annual plan. For two people with security evacuation, budget approximately USD 1,400–2,000 over two years. Multi-year plans available.

Field use: Activate inReach SOS immediately to begin tracking and alert Garmin Response. Simultaneously use the IsatPhone 2 to call Global Rescue or AMREF directly. Both channels operate in parallel — independent satellite networks, independent coordination centres.
Recommended structure for a 2-year Southern & East Africa expedition: AMREF Maisha Platinum/Diamond membership for the East Africa leg (Tanzania through Uganda/Kenya/Rwanda). Netcare 911 / ER24 reliance (via Bonitas partnership and TUFFSTUFF policy if active) for Southern Africa (SA, Namibia, Botswana, Zimbabwe). Global Rescue if political evacuation cover is desired for high-risk country legs. In a major emergency: activate inReach SOS immediately (begins tracking via Iridium), then use the IsatPhone 2 to call AMREF or Netcare 911 directly — voice coordination is faster than text in a crisis. Carry all control room numbers on the waterproof field card (Section 8).
7 — SOS Devices: Garmin inReach Mini 2 & Inmarsat IsatPhone 2

You are carrying two satellite SOS-capable devices: the Garmin inReach Mini 2 (data/tracking/SOS) and the Inmarsat IsatPhone 2 (voice/SOS). They operate on different networks, via different coordination centres, and serve different primary functions. Both are critical — neither replaces the other.

Garmin inReach Mini 2 — How SOS Works

Pressing the SOS button on your inReach initiates a 20-second countdown with audible alarm. Once confirmed, a distress signal travels via the Iridium satellite network (66 LEO satellites — 100% global coverage including poles) to Garmin Response, a 24/7 IERCC coordination centre. Garmin Response receives your GPS coordinates, device profile, and battery status, then initiates two-way text messaging with your device to assess the emergency. They contact the most appropriate local emergency services or medevac provider in your region. Garmin Response maintains a database of providers in 200+ countries.

inReach Subscription — Required SOS REQUIRES ACTIVE PLAN

SOS dispatch by Garmin Response requires an active satellite subscription. Freedom Plan (pay-per-use) and fixed monthly/annual plans available. For a 2-year expedition, monthly or annual plan is recommended.

Suspension risk: Garmin allows plan suspension up to 12 months — when suspended, SOS is completely disabled. Re-activate at least 48 hours before departure. Check subscription status before every major remote leg.

Profile: Keep emergency contact, blood type, and medical details updated in the Garmin Explore portal. Garmin Response reads this at activation.

Garmin SAR Insurance — Not Available for SA Residents USA/CANADA ONLY

Garmin offers optional SAR insurance (~USD 40/year standard) reimbursing up to USD 100,000 in SAR costs when SOS is activated. This plan is only available to US and Canadian residents — Neil and Mary-Ann cannot purchase it.

Alternative: Your travel insurance (World Nomads, SafetyWing, etc.) and AMREF medevac membership cover the costs that Garmin SAR would otherwise cover. The SOS button still activates Garmin Response to coordinate dispatch — the reimbursement mechanism is simply different.

Inmarsat IsatPhone 2 — How SOS Works

The IsatPhone 2 carries a dedicated orange SOS/Assistance button on the side of the handset. When pressed, a pre-configured distress message is automatically transmitted via the Inmarsat I-4 GEO satellite network, sending your GPS coordinates to GEOS (International Emergency Response Coordination Center), headquartered in Houston, TX, staffed 24/7/365. GEOS then calls you back directly on the satellite phone to confirm the nature of the emergency, then contacts the appropriate local rescue authority for your region.

GEOS registration is NOT automatic. The SOS button does not function until you register the device with GEOS. Registration is free for monthly postpaid plan holders. Prepaid SIM holders pay an annual fee. You must register before departure at geosalliance.com — provide your IMEI, device phone number, and IMSI (found at Menu › Settings › About). Update your emergency profile (next of kin, medical details, emergency contact number) in the GEOS portal. Test the SOS function after registration using GEOS's test procedure.
IsatPhone 2 Network Coverage NEAR-GLOBAL GEO COVERAGE

Network: Inmarsat I-4 geostationary satellites. Near-global coverage — sufficient for all of Southern and East Africa.

Limitation: GEO network. Must have clear line of sight toward the equator with antenna extended. Does not work in dense canopy, deep gorges, or indoors. Unlike Iridium (LEO), it does not cover extreme polar regions — not a concern for the Africa route.

Connection time: Network registration in under 45 seconds once antenna is extended. Requires at least 2 signal bars for voice calls.

Battery: Up to 8 hours talk time, 160 hours standby — longest battery life of any satellite phone. A significant operational advantage in the field.

IsatPhone 2 SOS Protocol REGISTER WITH GEOS BEFORE DEPARTURE

Activate SOS: Hold the dedicated orange SOS/Assistance button. A confirmation prompt appears — confirm to transmit. GPS coordinates are sent immediately to GEOS.

GEOS response: GEOS will call you back on the satellite phone number to confirm emergency type, your condition, and number of persons. Speak clearly — GEO voice quality on Inmarsat is excellent. GEOS then contacts local SAR authorities.

Voice advantage: Unlike the inReach (text-only), the IsatPhone 2 SOS results in a voice call back from GEOS. In a high-stress emergency, voice communication with a trained coordinator is faster and more reliable than composing texts.

Airtime note: GEOS callback is free under a monthly plan. The SOS activation message is also free. Confirm with your airtime provider before departure.

GEOS registration: geosalliance.com — select "Register a Device", choose Inmarsat ISAT 2. Enter IMEI, device phone number, and IMSI from Menu › Settings › About.

inReach SOS Field Protocol
1
Assess and confirm it is a genuine emergency requiring external assistance
Both inReach SOS and IsatPhone SOS initiate real search and rescue responses. Accidental activations waste emergency resources and may incur costs. On inReach: hold SOS button 3 seconds, then confirm on screen before transmission. On IsatPhone: hold orange SOS button, then confirm the prompt.
2
Activate inReach SOS — confirm on device screen
20-second countdown with alarm. Confirm activation. Automatic tracking begins — 60-second intervals for first 10 minutes, then 10-minute intervals to conserve battery. Keep device powered and in open sky view.
3
Simultaneously: use IsatPhone 2 to call AMREF (East Africa) or Netcare 911 (Southern Africa)
Voice call is faster than text. AMREF: +254 730 811 811 (East Africa). Netcare 911: 082 911 (Southern Africa). Have ready: your GPS coordinates (read from inReach screen or IsatPhone GPS), number of persons, nature of emergency, nearest identifiable landmark or track name.
4
Two-way messaging with Garmin Response (inReach)
Garmin Response will text your inReach. Reply to confirm emergency type, number of persons, medical condition, and whether self-rescue is possible. Be concise — satellite messages are slow. They will contact local emergency services on your behalf and can coordinate with AMREF or Netcare 911.
5
If GEOS calls back on IsatPhone 2 — confirm and brief
GEOS will call back on the IsatPhone 2 number to confirm the emergency. Provide: your full name, GPS coordinates, number of persons, nature of emergency (medical/vehicle/security), condition of injured, and your IsatPhone number for callback. GEOS then contacts regional SAR authorities independently of Garmin Response.
6
Stay put, stay visible, preserve battery on both devices
Rescuers navigate to your GPS coordinates. Do not move unless in immediate life-threatening danger. Mark position with any visible signal — mirror, bright cloth, fire smoke. Keep inReach tracking active. Keep IsatPhone charged and on standby for callback calls. Battery is your lifeline.
7
Cancel SOS only when emergency is fully resolved
Cancelling inReach SOS ends Garmin Response tracking. Cancelling IsatPhone SOS ends GEOS coordination. Only cancel when rescue is complete or you have confirmed self-rescue and communicated this to both centres. False-cancel causes confusion and may trigger a second response.

8 — Claim Procedures in the Field

A claim filed incorrectly or without the right documentation is a denied claim. These protocols apply when the event is happening — not after you are home. Know them before you need them.

Scenario A — Vehicle Accident, Damage, or Theft
1
Stop, secure safety, check for injuries first
Human life before vehicle. R10 Emergency Procedures if injury involved. Only then move to vehicle claim steps.
2
Document everything before moving the vehicles
Photographs of all vehicles, all damage, road surface, weather conditions, skid marks, position before impact. GPS coordinates. Timestamp on photos. More is better — you cannot go back.
3
Get a police report — this is mandatory for all insurance claims
In every country on this route, a police report is required for insurance claims. Go to the nearest police station. Get a reference number and ideally a stamped copy. If police come to the scene, get the officer's name and badge number. Do not leave the scene without a record of reporting.
4
Collect third-party details — do not admit fault
Name, vehicle registration, insurer (if they have one), contact number, witnesses. Do not say "I'm sorry" or "it was my fault." Under local law, an admission of fault is legally binding. Be polite and cooperative but say nothing about fault attribution.
5
Notify your SA insurer within 24–48 hours
Call the insurer emergency line. They will guide next steps. Keep notes of the call: time, person spoken to, claim reference number. Most SA policies require notification within a short window — exceeding it can invalidate the claim.
6
Do not authorise repairs until the insurer has assessed or provided written approval
Emergency safety repairs (e.g. securing a broken window) are usually fine. Major repairs without insurer approval can negate the claim. Get written authorisation by WhatsApp/email before proceeding. Keep all repair invoices.
Scenario B — Medical Event Requiring Treatment
1
Treat first — document later
Life-threatening emergencies take priority over paperwork. Get to the nearest capable facility. Notify your insurer/medical aid as soon as the patient is stable — most schemes require notification within 24 hours of hospital admission.
2
Pre-authorise if not an emergency
For non-emergency hospital admission, call Bonitas/ER24 or your travel insurer for pre-authorisation before admission. A payment guarantee from the insurer means the facility is paid directly — you don't go out of pocket. Expenses over R5,000 (Bonitas) or USD 150/€100 (Discovery) require pre-authorisation for direct payment.
3
Preserve all documentation
Every receipt, every doctor's note, every invoice. In the original language if possible — request an English translation. Photograph everything with date-stamped photos. A missing receipt is a denied line item.
4
Self-pay if pre-authorisation is not possible — claim on return
For out-of-hospital expenses below the authorisation threshold, pay and keep receipts. Claim within 4 months of treatment (Bonitas rule). Provide: patient name, diagnosis, provider details, date of service, treatment description, cost.
Scenario C — Medical Evacuation
1
Decide: is this a medevac event?
Medevac criteria: life-threatening condition or serious injury where local facilities cannot provide adequate care. Deteriorating condition with no improvement. Patient cannot be safely moved by road. When in doubt, call AMREF Control Room for advice — they assess and advise without obligation to dispatch.
2
Call AMREF Control Room immediately
+254 730 811 811 / +254 709 962 811 (24/7) — Use the Inmarsat IsatPhone 2 for this call — voice communication is faster and clearer than text in a crisis. Have ready: patient name, AMREF membership number, current GPS coordinates (read from inReach screen), patient condition, treatment received so far, nearest airstrip or landing area, contact number you can be reached on.
3
Simultaneously notify your travel insurer
Your travel insurer needs to know a medevac is occurring — they cover the costs. Get a claim reference number. AMREF covers the transport; your travel insurance reimburses AMREF's fees or pays the destination hospital directly.
4
Prepare the landing area — AMREF will advise
AMREF flies fixed-wing aircraft and requires a serviceable airstrip (not an open field). They will identify the nearest viable strip in their database. You may need to drive or be transported to that strip — coordinate with AMREF Control on timing and transport.
5
The non-patient partner: manage the vehicle and notify next of kin
In a solo-couple scenario, one of you may be evacuated and the other remains with the vehicle. The non-evacuated partner should: secure the vehicle and camp, retain all documents, notify next of kin, and coordinate with the travel insurer for vehicle repatriation or onward arrangements. Power of attorney between Neil and Mary-Ann is relevant here — pre-signed and witnessed before departure (see Section 9).

10 — Case Studies

Real-world events — drawn from overlanding accounts, published forum discussions, and documented incidents — that illustrate what can go wrong and what correct procedure looks like. Names are anonymised or circumstantial.

Vehicle Written Off in Remote Zambia
An SA couple hit a large animal at night on the Kafue road. Vehicle was totalled. SA comprehensive policy technically covered the write-off — but the agreed value had not been updated to include the R120,000 fitment package added pre-trip. The insurer paid market value for the base vehicle (R85,000), leaving the couple out of pocket by roughly R90,000 on their build. They were repatriated by road.
LESSON: Insure the full prepared vehicle value. Get agreed value written into the policy. Submit a full fitment schedule to the insurer before departure and get written confirmation of the agreed value.
Medical Evacuation from Tanzania — Correct Process
A solo traveller sustained serious abdominal injuries from a vehicle rollover in the Serengeti corridor. He had AMREF Maisha membership and a World Nomads policy. One call to AMREF Control Room (+254 730 811 811) resulted in a fixed-wing aircraft reaching him within four hours via a bush airstrip. AMREF's aircraft had an ICU-capable setup and a flight doctor. He was admitted to Aga Khan Hospital in Nairobi. World Nomads reimbursed the full cost including hospital bills. Recovery was complete.
LESSON: AMREF + comprehensive travel insurance is the correct dual-layer approach. One call activates the aircraft; the insurer pays the bill. The combination worked exactly as designed.
Theft at Border — Claim Denied
Equipment was stolen from a vehicle at a border camp. The owner's SA comprehensive policy covered personal belongings, but only if force entry was used or the vehicle was locked. The camp incident involved items left in an unlocked tent and vehicle. The claim for camera equipment and GPS devices worth R35,000 was denied on the grounds of inadequate precautions. The policy had a specific exclusion for unattended valuables in unlocked vehicles or accommodation.
LESSON: Read the theft exclusion clauses in your policy. "Unattended" and "unlocked" are standard denial triggers. Carry valuable electronics on your person or in a locked, concealed location. Know what your policy actually covers for theft outside SA.
Medical Aid Cap Exceeded in Nairobi
A South African traveller required emergency surgery in Nairobi following a road accident. Her medical aid international travel benefit covered up to R5m per trip — more than adequate. However, she had been in East Africa for 97 days continuously. Her 90-day trip benefit had lapsed seven days earlier. The medical aid rejected the claim. The couple had no travel insurance. They self-paid approximately R280,000 for the surgery and ICU stay.
LESSON: The 90-day trip limit is a hard stop. Travel insurance covering the full trip duration is not optional for a multi-month overland. Set a calendar reminder for the Day 85 mark — this is when you should check your position.
Vehicle Stuck — inReach SOS Misuse
A vehicle became seriously stuck in a riverbed in northern Botswana. The driver activated inReach SOS. Garmin Response coordinated with local emergency services. A helicopter was eventually dispatched. The vehicle was simply stuck — no injuries, no mechanical failure. The rescue cost exceeded USD 8,000. The traveller's travel insurance denied the claim because the policy covered medical emergencies only, not vehicle recovery. He had no SAR membership or equivalent.
LESSON: inReach SOS dispatches emergency rescue — it is not a vehicle recovery tool. If you are stuck but healthy, work the recovery tools in R9 Vehicle Recovery Guide first. Reserve SOS for genuine life-threatening emergencies. Know what your insurance will and will not reimburse before pressing the button.
Successful Theft Claim — Right Documentation
Catalytic converter and starter motor stolen overnight from a locked and alarmed vehicle at a campsite in Zimbabwe. The owner had a police report (obtained within hours from the nearest town), photographs of the tampered panels, and a pre-trip equipment inventory. The SA insurer approved the claim within two weeks. Total reimbursement: R18,000. Claim paid without dispute.
LESSON: Police report + photographs + pre-trip inventory = payable claim. None of these documents take more than a day to prepare before departure. Do it.
