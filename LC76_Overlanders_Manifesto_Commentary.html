The manifesto is written to be read on its own — in the field, in plain first-person voice, with no names and no apparatus. That is by design. This companion is where the scaffolding lives: one essay for each commitment, each able to stand alone, each naming the thinkers whose work runs underneath the plain words and showing what their philosophy lends to the road.

These essays are written lightly, but the ideas are load-bearing. Where a thinker appears more than once across the collection, each appearance draws on a different part of their work. Works are named in the text as they arise; a reference list closes the document. And one habit of the whole project carries over: where serious thinkers genuinely disagree, the essays stage the disagreement rather than smoothing it away. A closing coda steps back from the commitments altogether, to name the philosopher of the whole project's tempo: François Jullien.

◈ The Map · Seven Tensions, Held
The manifesto does not settle its deepest questions. It leans into both sides at once — the way you hold a vehicle on a camber.

Willing versus surrender

Do we make ourselves through decision, or unmake ourselves through letting go? Sartre and Arendt give us the self that is shaped in choices. There is no hidden true self, only the will and the choices that accumulate into character. Against them stand Simone Weil, with her idea of décréation and of attention as a self-emptying receptivity, and the Daoist wu wei of Lao Tzu: the best arrives when we stop gripping. Levinas and Han lean the same way. The manifesto refuses to choose: Commitment II surrenders the score, Commitment XIV insists we make ourselves road by road.

Escape versus belonging-with

Is the freedom of the road a freedom from others or a freedom held with them? The romance of overlanding — the wild, self-sufficient open road — pulls toward negative liberty, escape. Lea Ypi is the sharp corrector: real freedom is earned moral autonomy realised with others, not the consumer's freedom-to-leave. Ubuntu, Beauvoir's situated freedom, and Levinas's responsibility all place the other first. The preamble, Commitment X and Commitment XII carry the correction.

A world that welcomes, that is indifferent, or that is made

Three poles: Rosa's resonance and love of the world imagines a land that answers and meets us; McCarthy, Camus and Weil's love of necessity insist that the land is indifferent and doesn't care; Miéville and Glissant insist that the world is neither pristine nor innocent but historically made. Commitments IV and V hold all three.

Chosen hardship versus real affliction

Han tells us to embrace the negative against a society anaesthetised to all discomfort. Weil draws the hard line he doesn't: between suffering (that we choose and can end) and affliction (that is imposed and degrading and has no exit). Ypi adds that our mobility is a privilege that others are denied, and Benjamin warns that the longing for the untouched can itself be a kind of cult. Commitment XII is built on this.

Living it versus capturing it

Benjamin gives the project its theory of experience: deep, integrated Erfahrung that ripens into a story, against the reproduced image that strips a place of its aura, the information used up the instant it is shared. Weil's concept of attention and Han's critique of the achievement-image stand on the same side. The travel-writing ambition lives in the resolution: a story keeps unfolding; information does not. Commitment III.

Rootedness versus motion

Weil names uprootedness as the modern disease and rootedness as a need of the soul; Versfeld and Heidegger locate home in dwelling and the care of the ordinary. Overlanding is, on its face, voluntary uprootedness. The resolution the manifesto reaches for is that rootedness is a quality of attention, not of duration: you can be deeply rooted in a place you pass through, provided you do not uproot those who live there. Commitments VIII and XIV resolve it the only honest way.

Willing versus individuation

Do we make the self by choosing, or does the self emerge through relation? Sartre gives the manifesto its bracing formula — existence precedes essence, the self is the sum of its choices (Commitments IX and XIV). Gilbert Simondon supplies the corrective the manifesto keeps reaching for: the self is not chosen into being but individuated — it resolves out of prior tensions, stays forever incomplete, and is carried toward completion only through relation with what it moves through and with others, a fullness Simondon calls the transindividual. This is not the self-emptying of Willing versus surrender above: surrender is a letting-go of the self; individuation is the self's relational becoming. Commitment XIV makes itself road by road with Sartre; Commitment IX is made by what it moves through with Simondon. The manifesto leans both ways at once.

Beneath all seven run two more: process versus project (Huizinga, Suits and the critique of metrics — Commitments I, II and VII) and mastery versus not-knowing (Serres, Morin — Commitments VII and XIII). And beneath everything runs one more, treated in the closing coda: event versus transformation — Jullien's silent transformations. These are the substrate; the seven above are where the manifesto grows.

§ Preamble · Going Feral, Coming Home
Why the manifesto begins with a refusal — and why its freedom must be earned

The manifesto opens with a diagnosis so gentle it is easy to miss the accusation in it: the modern world has domesticated us. Nobody did this to us with malice. It happened through measurement, optimisation and prediction — through systems that, with the best of intentions, taught us that uncertainty is a risk and the unknown a threat. Going feral, then, is the deliberate recovery of capacities that were dulled by captivity.

But the preamble immediately complicates its own romance, and the complication comes from Lea Ypi. In Free: Coming of Age at the End of History (2021), Ypi — who grew up inside one failed promise of freedom and watched another arrive wearing a market's face — argues that the liberty that we usually think of is merely the freedom to exit: to leave, to choose, to consume. Real freedom is something more difficult, a moral achievement practised with other people and answerable to them. The manifesto takes her point on the chin. The freedom to leave is not yet freedom; it is only a departure. What we do on the road is where freedom is either earned or squandered.

Then comes the stranger move: the feral road leads home. The authority here is Martin Versfeld, the Cape Town philosopher who cooked his metaphysics in an iron pot and was an ecologist before the word had a movement. Across Food for Thought (1983) and Pots and Poetry (1985), Versfeld's great theme is that the largest questions live inside the smallest acts, and that every genuine journey — including the philosophical kind — is a homecoming. Home, for him, is not a building one occupies but a generosity one practises. A manifesto that leaves the house in order to learn how to dwell is a deeply Versfeldian document.

Finally, the preamble announces its method: the road hands out tensions, and it gets held. This is Edgar Morin's complex thought in working clothes — his insistence, in Homeland Earth (1993) and elsewhere, that reality is unitas multiplex, one and many at once, and that the honest thinker holds contradictions in productive relation rather than amputating one side. The image the manifesto reaches for is mechanical: a vehicle held on a camber, leaning into both forces at once. Everything that follows is written from that driving position.

I The Journey Is the Purpose
Bergson's two kinds of time, Lao Tzu's traveller, and the strange territory of the threshold

The first commitment rejects the logic of arrival, and its deepest resource is Henri Bergson's distinction between two kinds of time. In Time and Free Will (1889), Bergson argues that the time of clocks and schedules is a masquerade — a line chopped into identical, empty units. Lived time, durée, is nothing like it: it flows, thickens, lingers and races; it is qualitative through and through. An hour stuck in sand really is longer than a week on a tar road, and Bergson is the reason that this is not a metaphor. To measure a journey in texture rather than kilometres is to take his side in one of philosophy's oldest arguments.

The commitment's patron, though, is older: Lao Tzu, whose good traveller — with no fixed plans, not intent on arriving — embodies wu wei, the unforced action that many Western readers keep mistaking for passivity. It is nothing of the sort. Wu wei is the skill of moving with the grain of circumstances rather than against it, and on corrugated gravel it is simply good driving. Here is a rather fitting little anecdote: Versfeld, the Cape philosopher who is mentioned in the preamble, adored this book, the Tao Te Ching, so much that it inspired him to translate it into Afrikaans. The lineage of this manifesto loops through Africa more than once.

And then there is the moment the tarmac ends — which the commitment insists is not a change of surface but rather a crossing. The anthropology behind that claim begins with Arnold van Gennep's The Rites of Passage (1909) and matures in Victor Turner's The Ritual Process (1969): the liminal phase, betwixt and between, in which a person has left one status and not yet acquired the next. Turner noticed that this unsettled zone — disorienting, ambiguous, slightly dangerous — is precisely where transformation happens, because the structures that hold the self in place are suspended there. Every threshold crossing is a small rite of passage, which is why the speed drops and the world becomes real again.

By reading Bergson, Turner and Lao Tzu side by side, the commitment stops sounding like a travel preference and starts sounding like a metaphysics: time is lived rather than counted, the way is travelled rather than forced, and the in-between is not the boring bit before the destination. It is where the journey does its work.

II We Don't Keep Score
Play, the achievement society, and the careful guarding of a journey's futility

Ask what the trip is for and the manifesto declines to answer — cheerfully. The philosophical backbone of that cheerfulness is Johan Huizinga's Homo Ludens (1938), which makes the startling claim that play is older than culture: animals played before humans existed, and everything we are proudest of — language, law, poetry, ritual — bears play's fingerprints. For Huizinga, genuine play is free, stands deliberately outside ordinary life, and serves no material interest. It is autotelic: its purpose is internal to itself. A journey undertaken for nothing is not a journey that has failed to find a purpose. It is play in its purest, oldest form.

Bernard Suits clarified the point with a definition that remains relevant. In The Grasshopper: Games, Life and Utopia (1978), he argues that playing a game is the voluntary attempt to overcome unnecessary obstacles — choosing the harder path when an easier one exists, simply because the difficulty is the point. Nobody needs to cross the Makgadikgadi by the long route. That is exactly why we do. Suits calls the attitude that makes this intelligible the lusory attitude, and every overlander who has grinned at a terrible track recognises it instantly.

What gives this commitment its urgency is the world it refuses, and the diagnosis belongs to Byung-Chul Han. The Burnout Society (2010) describes the achievement-subject: the modern person who no longer needs a boss because they have internalised one — who exploits themselves voluntarily and experiences the compulsion as freedom. C. Thi Nguyen's Games: Agency as Art (2020) adds the mechanism: value capture, the quiet process by which rich, subtle values get replaced by the thin metrics that claim to measure them. Steps stand in for health, followers for friendship — and kilometres, badges and border counts stand in for travel. The commitment's strange phrase, guarding the journey's futility, is a defensive perimeter against exactly this. The moment that the trip becomes a project, the dashboard has won.

And when the plan collapses anyway, the smile at the broken U-bolt is on loan from Albert Camus. In The Myth of Sisyphus (1942) he argues that the world owes us no meaning, and that the honest response is neither despair nor false comfort but rather lucid persistence — pushing the stone, tightening the bolt, carrying on. Camus thought we should imagine his stone-pusher happy. Two days from anywhere, under a vehicle, the proposition can be tested empirically.

III The Journey Is Not Content
Walter Benjamin on aura, stories, and why information dies the moment it arrives

This commitment belongs almost entirely to Walter Benjamin, who saw our century coming from inside the 1930s. His famous essay on the work of art in the age of its technological reproducibility (1935) introduced the idea of aura: the unrepeatable here-and-now of a thing, its embeddedness in one place and one moment. Reproduction, he argued, strips that aura precisely by making the unique endlessly available. He was writing about paintings and film, but the analysis lands without modification on a waterhole at dusk: the photograph circulates, the aura does not. The place itself can only be met.

Benjamin's deeper distinction is between two kinds of experience, and German distinguishes them better than English: Erlebnis, the isolated jolt of an experience that happens and is over, and Erfahrung, experience that integrates into a life, accumulates, and ripens. In essays like Experience and Poverty (1933) he worried that modernity was producing people rich in jolts and poor in depth. The manifesto's claim that a truly memorable evening takes its time to become a story is Erfahrung in field clothes.

His most useful essay for travellers, though, is The Storyteller (1936). Here Benjamin contrasts the story with information. Information lives only in the instant of its newness — it must explain itself immediately, and it is consumed the moment it is shared. A story does the opposite: it withholds explanation, preserves its strength, and keeps unfolding in the listener for years. He notes that the traveller returning from afar was one of the storyteller's two archetypes. On this account, the choice between three stories and three thousand images is not sentimental arithmetic. It is a choice between two technologies of memory, one of which compounds and one of which depreciates.

The commitment is careful not to moralise the camera itself — we love photographs, and Benjamin loved film. What is highlighted is the performance: the journey staged for an audience that isn't there, the lens leading rather than following. Byung-Chul Han's The Transparency Society (2012) names the modern endpoint, a world where everything exists to be exhibited and nothing is allowed to remain quietly itself. Against that, the commitment makes a small, radical promise: some of the best moments will leave no evidence at all. Presence first; the camera waits its turn — and the story, unlike the Instagram post, will still be paying interest in twenty years.

IV Attention Is Wild
Simone Weil's generosity, Humboldt's measuring heart, and the ladder from curiosity to wonder

When the manifesto calls attention wild, it makes two claims: that attention is a survival skill that the smoothed world has allowed to waste away and that it is something far greater than a skill. The second claim is Simone Weil's. In her essay on the right use of school studies (1942), Weil argued that attention is not the muscular effort of concentration we were taught at school — brows furrowed, will engaged. It is almost the opposite: a suspension of the self, a waiting, an availability that lets the thing before us appear as it is rather than as we need it to be. Her letter to the poet Joë Bousquet compresses the whole doctrine into one line: "Attention is the rarest and purest form of generosity." To read sand, sky and elephant well is, on this account, already an ethical act.

The commitment's paragraph about wonder carries Alexander von Humboldt, the nineteenth century's greatest traveller-scientist. Humboldt climbed Chimborazo with forty instruments and came down with poetry; his Personal Narrative and the vast Cosmos (1845–62) insist that measurement and feeling are not rivals but partners — that knowing the world more precisely should make it more astonishing, not less. He learned this style of travelling-with-feeling while he was still young, and on the arm of veteran Georg Forster as they sailed with Captain Cook. These writings profoundly influenced Charles Darwin, who carried the Personal Narrative aboard the HMS Beagle. Wonder, for Humboldt, was not a mood; it was a method, and a moral stance toward a world that exceeds us.

The contemporary cyberneticist Francis Heylighen has mapped the mechanism: curiosity is the itch of a small, local gap in our understanding; awe arrives when the gap turns out to be vast; wonder is what happens when we stay with the vastness instead of closing the question. The manifesto's sequence — friction sharpens, attention ripens, wonder refuses to treat the world as a warehouse — walks his ladder rung by rung. Curiosity asks what; wonder asks how it is that anything is at all, and declines to hurry the answer.

Proust supplies the destination. The real voyage of discovery, he wrote in In Search of Lost Time, consists not in seeking new landscapes but in having new eyes. The commitment quietly radicalises him: new eyes are not given by travel. They are trained — by sand, weather, engines and silence — which is why the wild is where attention goes to be re-wilded.

V The Land Is Not Empty
McCarthy's indifferent country, Miéville's trained unseeing, and the land as a party to the journey

This commitment holds two hard truths in one hand. The first is that the land does not care about us. Cormac McCarthy is its uncompromising voice: the landscapes of Blood Meridian (1985) and The Crossing (1994) are ancient, vast and supremely indifferent, owing the traveller nothing — not safety, not meaning, not even hostility. This is not nature-writing's consoling wilderness. It is the desert as it actually is at two in the afternoon, and the manifesto's refusal to call the land our friend is a refusal of sentimentality. We love it because it is real, not because it loves us back. Simone Weil would have understood completely: her love of the order of the world is precisely a love of a necessity that grants no exceptions.

The second truth is political: the land is not empty. The phrase carries the full weight of the most damaging idea in the history of travel — terra nullius, nobody's land, the legal fiction that emptied inhabited country so that it could be claimed. China Miéville's fantasy The City & the City (2009) supplies the sharpest contemporary tool for thinking about this. In his twin cities, citizens are trained from birth to unsee the other city's people even as they walk among them — an enforced, habitual not-noticing. It is the perfect metaphor for the colonial gaze that looks at occupied land and sincerely sees wilderness. To keep our eyes open, to refuse to unsee, is the whole ethical labour of this commitment.

The metaphysics underneath — the elephant as a process, the tree as a network — comes from Alfred North Whitehead, whose Process and Reality (1929) argued that the world is not made of static things but of events and relations, each thing a happening rather than an object. The commitment's instinct to see the Makgadikgadi as alive with invisible process is Whitehead's universe rendered at ground level.

Finally, the commitment performs a quiet inversion through François Jullien, whose work on Chinese landscape painting — The Great Image Has No Form (2003) — recovers the Chinese word for landscape, shanshui, mountain-water: not a view framed for a spectator but a living system of interacting forces that one lives within. The windscreen makes us spectators; getting out makes us participants. Landscape is not something we look at, the commitment says; it is something we live from. That is the difference between a picture and a place, and it is the difference between a tourist and a guest of the land.

VI Worlds Within Worlds
Lugones's world-travelling, Glissant's Relation, and the invention called "Africa"

There is no such thing as "Africa" — only worlds, layered and plural. The philosophical engine of this commitment is María Lugones, the Argentine-American thinker whose essay Playfulness, "World"-Travelling, and Loving Perception (1987) gives the manifesto one of its central verbs. A "world", for Lugones, is a complete way of life with its own rules and its own sense of what a person is. To world-travel is to shift, willingly and humbly, from one to another — and crucially, to become a different self in each, because we are not the same person in a border post and a cattle kraal. She insists this travelling be done playfully, not as conquest or fieldwork but as openness to surprise, including the surprise of one's own foolishness. Arriving underqualified, always, is the correct posture.

The warning against the single golden "Africa" of the brochures has a precise philosophical name in Édouard Glissant, the Martinican poet-philosopher. His Poetics of Relation (1990) opposes the colonial fantasy of root identity — singular, pure, territorial — with what he calls Relation: identity as a weave of connections, irreducibly multiple. The flattening eye that turns a thousand worlds into one scenic backdrop is committing what Glissant regarded as a kind of violence: the erasure of multiplicity. To see worlds within worlds is to honour Relation over root.

The closing image — admitted as guests, but only if we knock — quietly raises the philosophy of hospitality that Kant opened in Perpetual Peace (1795) with his right of visitation, and that Derrida pressed much further in his late seminars on hospitality. The traveller, in this lineage, has a limited and conditional right: to be received decently, yes, but as a guest who knocks, not as a colonist who claims. Everything in this commitment turns on the knock — the small gesture that distinguishes a visitor from an invader.

VII Slow Is Not Lazy — Slow Is Deep
Rosa on a world that answers back, Han on lingering, and the itinerary as a hypothesis

The rule is simple — stop and stay, not stop and go — but its justification is one of the most interesting in contemporary social theory. Hartmut Rosa's Resonance (2016) and Social Acceleration (2005) argue that modern life is defined by speed, and that speed produces a specific pathology Rosa calls alienation: a mute relationship to a world that no longer answers back. His alternative is resonance — a relationship in which self and world genuinely affect one another, in which something out there reaches us and we reach it. Resonance, crucially, cannot be forced or scheduled; it can only be made available to. Three nights in one place is an availability strategy. The first evening shows the surface; by the third day the place has begun to answer.

Byung-Chul Han sharpens the same point in The Scent of Time (2009), a defence of lingering against a culture that has lost the capacity for it. For Han, the contemplative pause is not wasted time but the very thing that gives time a scent, a depth, a duration worth inhabiting. The manifesto's slowness is Han's lingering, executed in the medium of gravel and camp chairs.

The commitment's quiet structural claim — that the biggest changes make no noise — introduces the theme the closing coda will develop through François Jullien: silent transformation, the kind that only events seem to announce after the fact. Deserts creep and fences advance below the threshold of event-perception, and slowness, with its returns year after year, is the only instrument fine enough to register them.

Finally, the itinerary is a hypothesis, not a contract. Behind that lies Edgar Morin again, and the strategy of complex thought: in a complex, partly unknowable system, a rigid plan is a liability, and the intelligent response is to prepare thoroughly and then adapt continuously — to hold the plan loosely enough that reality can revise it. The phrase we are never behind schedule is not a refusal of planning. It is the recognition that a plan made in a complex world is a wager, and that the moments worth keeping are almost always the ones the wager didn't foresee.

VIII Every Camp Is a Home We Make
Heidegger's dwelling, Bachelard's hut, Versfeld's pots, and the threshold as an honest address

Home is not what we left behind, the commitment says, because we never left it behind — we remake it nightly. The deepest source here is Martin Heidegger's late essay Building Dwelling Thinking (1951). Heidegger distinguishes merely occupying a space from genuinely dwelling in it, and argues that dwelling is the fundamental character of human existence: to dwell is to care for and cultivate, to let things be what they are, to gather a world around oneself. We must first learn to dwell, he says, before we can build well. The camp ritual — table here, lamp there, coffee ground for the morning — is dwelling in his exact sense: the practice of care that turns borrowed ground into a place.

Gaston Bachelard's The Poetics of Space (1958) adds the phenomenology of intimate places: how a corner, a hut, a small pool of lamplight becomes psychologically vast, a centre from which a world is organised. The hermit's hut, for Bachelard, is an image of concentrated solitude and shelter that the human imagination returns to again and again. Two chairs and a fire generate exactly this — a centre of intimacy improvised nightly under an enormous sky.

The African philosophical key, though, is once again Martin Versfeld. In Food for Thought he develops a whole metaphysics from cooking and eating: the preparation of a meal with attention is, for him, a paradigm of dwelling, care and communion. There is no need to import European philosophers to dignify a camp kitchen; a Cape Town thinker already built the cathedral. When the manifesto says home is an activity and the architecture optional, it is speaking almost pure Versfeld.

Finally, the commitment redeems the in-between. Drawing again on the liminality of van Gennep and Turner from Commitment I, it makes a bolder claim: the threshold is not a gap between real places but the honest address — the place that admits what life actually is, transition all the way down. A life without thresholds, it suggests, is a life that has mistaken one of its stations for the whole journey. The overlander, by contrast, has made a home of motion itself, and learned the one skill the commitment names: to be in motion without being lost.

IX The Road Makes Us
Whitehead's process metaphysics, Lugones's plural selves, and the self as an ecology

If Commitment V used process philosophy to see the land, this commitment turns the same lens on the traveller. The claim that we are made by what we move through is a direct application of Alfred North Whitehead's Process and Reality (1929): there are no self-contained substances that exist first and relate afterwards; to be is to be in relation, and the self is a series of events constituted by everything it takes in. The driver, the vehicle, the gravel, the stranger and the fire are not separate things in proximity. They are, in Whitehead's terms, the actual occasions out of which a self is composed from one moment to the next.

Gilbert Simondon gives this process a name and a mechanism. His individuation is the idea that no individual — crystal, organism, or person — is a finished substance; each is the ongoing resolution of tensions in a "pre-individual" field, never fully completed, always carrying a charge of the not-yet. The traveller is individuated, in his sense, by the very encounters the commitment lists: the gravel, the breakdown, the stranger's kindness do not happen to an already-finished self but help constitute one. And because that completion comes only through what exceeds the single person — the others met, the collective around the fire — Simondon calls its fullest form the transindividual. "We are made by what we move through" is, in his vocabulary, an individuation that is never ours alone.

The contemporary philosopher Bayo Akomolafe — Nigerian, writing at the edge of post-humanist thought — gives this an African and ecological inflection. His These Wilds Beyond Our Fences (2017) refuses the bounded, sovereign individual entirely: we are, he writes, with the world, entangled and co-emergent, made and remade in the meeting. The gravel that changes us, and is marked by us in turn, is Akomolafe's intra-active world in miniature.

The commitment's second move — that the road makes more than one of us, a patient diplomat at the border, a reader of sand in the dunes, a storyteller at the fire — returns to María Lugones. Her plural self is not a fracture to be healed but a richness to be inhabited: we are many, and each "world" calls a different one of us into being, and none is the counterfeit of some hidden true self. We keep them all. Against a therapeutic culture forever hunting for the one authentic self beneath the masks, Lugones and this commitment make a quietly liberating claim: the masks, plural and shifting, are who we are. The road simply gives more of them room to live.

The folk line the commitment quotes — the way of the road is the rule for all upon it — turns the metaphysics into an ethic. If we are made by what we move through, then how we move through matters, because we are also, always, part of what is making everyone else on the road.

X Self-Reliance Is Not Independence
Beauvoir's bound freedom, Ypi's earned freedom, and ubuntu as an ontology of persons

This commitment dismantles the proudest myth of overlanding — self-sufficiency — and it does so honestly, from the inside. We lean on satellite communications, imported fuel and a global supply chain; the comfortable story of total independence is just a story. The philosophy that replaces it begins with ubuntu, condensed in the Nguni maxim umuntu ngumuntu ngabantu: a person is a person through other persons. As articulated by thinkers such as Mogobe Ramose in African Philosophy Through Ubuntu (1999) and by Desmond Tutu in No Future Without Forgiveness (1999), this is not a slogan about being nice; it is an ontology. Personhood is not the starting point but an achievement, brought into being and sustained through relationship. On a dirt road in Zambia, self-reliance that pretends otherwise is not just arrogant — it is metaphysically false.

Simone de Beauvoir supplies the existentialist version. In The Ethics of Ambiguity (1947) she breaks with the caricature of the solitary, sovereign chooser and argues that my freedom is bound up with the freedom of others — that I cannot will myself free while willing or ignoring others' unfreedom, because freedom is a shared condition or it is a sham. The traveller who depends on the communities they pass through, and who travels with gratitude rather than entitlement, is living Beauvoir's bound freedom rather than the adolescent fantasy of the unencumbered self.

The commitment's sharpest turn is its last: even our freedom is not our own work. This is Lea Ypi's argument from Free again, now fully stated. Liberty is not the consumer's freedom-to-leave, exercised alone and answerable to no one; it is moral autonomy, earned in relationship and practised under obligation. The open road does not set us free. It gives us the room to become free — and becoming free, for Ypi as for this manifesto, is harder, slower and more demanding than simply driving away. The empty fridge restocked from the people we meet is a small daily enactment of the larger truth: we are threads in an economy and a moral community, not sovereigns passing above them.

XI Take Off the Sunglasses
Levinas's face, Lugones's loving perception, and Glissant's right to opacity

The first line of defence is a handshake, not a locked door — and the philosophy that justifies this apparent recklessness is among the most demanding in the book. Emmanuel Levinas, who survived a Nazi labour camp while his family was murdered, built an entire ethics on the human face. In Totality and Infinity (1961) he argues that the face of the other person addresses me before I understand them, before I categorise them, making a claim on me that precedes all my theories and calculations. The face says, in effect, do not harm me — and in that address ethics is born, prior to knowledge. To stop the engine, remove the sunglasses and meet the face is to put oneself in the exact position Levinas thinks makes us human.

The two ways of seeing a person come straight from María Lugones. Arrogant perception files people into types — the locals, the guide, the man at the gate — and learns nothing, because it has already decided. Loving perception travels toward them, far enough into their world to glimpse how they see themselves, and it accepts that it will sometimes get things wrong. Willingness to be a fool — to mispronounce the greeting, misread the custom, miss the joke, and stay open anyway — is, for Lugones, not a social risk to be minimised but the very condition of crossing into another world at all.

The commitment then makes a move that protects the people it meets from the traveller's appetite to understand them. Drawing on Édouard Glissant's Poetics of Relation, it affirms the right to opacity — Glissant's insistence that others are not obliged to be transparent to us, that we can respect people without claiming to see through them, and that the demand for full comprehension is itself a soft form of domination. Their opacity, the commitment says, isn't a wall; it is their depth. This is the rare ethic that asks us to know others well enough to honour what we cannot know about them.

The closing recognition — that no encounter here is neutral, that how we arrive and how we leave both matter — sets up Commitment XII, where the privilege that makes our arrival possible is examined without flinching.

XII Our Hardship Is a Privilege
Weil's line between suffering and affliction, the unequal passport, and the cult of the untouched

This is the manifesto's most morally exacting commitment, and it turns on a distinction drawn by Simone Weil that almost nothing else in popular travel writing dares to make. In her essay on the love of God and affliction (1942), Weil separates ordinary suffering from what she calls le malheur — affliction: a condition that is imposed rather than chosen, that degrades and isolates, that crushes the soul along with the body, and from which there is no exit. Overlanding hardship is the opposite on every count. It is chosen, it ennobles rather than degrades, and it has an exit — the full tank, the working card, the open road home. We are never more than one decision from ending it. To call our discomfort by the same name as affliction would be, in Weil's terms, a category error and a small obscenity.

The commitment then widens from suffering to mobility itself, and here Lea Ypi returns a third time with her hardest point. The freedom to cross borders is not distributed evenly; our passports are keys that most of the world is never handed. The very mobility this manifesto celebrates is, for the majority of humanity, a locked door. A document that did not say this plainly would be dishonest about its own conditions of possibility, and the commitment says it plainly: our mobility is a privilege many are denied.

There is a subtler trap the commitment also avoids, and Walter Benjamin named it. The longing for the untouched, the pristine, the authentically remote can curdle into a kind of cult — a consumer's appetite for places and peoples imagined as outside history, which is itself a way of not seeing them as Commitment V warned. To dramatise our hardships, to collect them like trophies, is to feed that cult. The commitment's response is the oldest virtue in the book: to hold the privilege lightly, to let the clear sight of it become a reason for humility and for care toward the people whose home we are only passing through. The reward for telling this truth is not guilt; it is proportion.

XIII The Vehicle Is a Collaborator
Simondon's conductor, his concretization and his associated milieu, Ravaisson's grace of habit, and Illich's convivial tools

The claim that we travel alongside the Land Cruiser — neither commanding it nor merging with it — is the deliberate philosophical position of Gilbert Simondon, who in On the Mode of Existence of Technical Objects (1958) made it the centre of a whole philosophy of technology. Simondon's starting point is that our culture misreads machines in two opposite but equally mistaken ways: as the hostile robot that will one day rise against us, or as the docile slave placed "in the service of man." Both errors, he argues, spring from the same root — non-knowledge of the machine. The machine, for Simondon, is "the stranger inside which something human is locked up," and the cure for our unease with it is neither fear nor mastery but acquaintance: knowing, from within, how the thing actually works.

His governing image is the orchestra. Open machines, he writes, need the human being "in the same way musicians in an orchestra need the conductor" — and the conductor leads only because he plays the piece as intensely as the players do, and is "tempered or hurried by them" in turn. The human is "among the machines that operate with him": not above them as master, not beneath them as servant, and — this is the correction the commitment makes to its own first instinct — not fused with them either. To "become one with" the vehicle would be to collapse exactly the relation Simondon wants to keep open; the cyborg fantasy of human-machine fusion is, for him, just the old master–slave picture wearing new clothes. Reading the Land Cruiser's note, its heat, its small new vibration is the conductor's listening: leading and being led at once.

The deeper engine of the commitment, though, is Simondon's idea of concretization — his answer to the question of what makes a machine a good one. A primitive or, in his word, abstract machine is an assembly of separate parts, each charged with a single job and bolted to the next, so that any one sub-system can fail on its own and take the rest down with it. A concrete machine is one that has evolved in the opposite direction: its parts have become plurifunctional, folded into one another until the functioning of the whole produces what once required bolted-on systems. Simondon's own example is the engine in which "the functioning itself implies cooling," rather than depending on an autonomous cooling system that can break down by itself. This is not a metaphor for the old naturally-aspirated diesel that carries us; it is literally what such an engine is. Decades of refinement have made the 1HZ a concrete object in Simondon's exact sense — a long technical lineage matured toward internal solidarity, with little left that can fail in isolation. Concretization is the philosophical reason the simple, evolved engine is the one you trust far from help: not because it is crude, but because it has become coherent.

The second idea is the associated milieu. A true technical individual, Simondon argues, "creates its own associated milieu" — a mixed technical-and-natural world that conditions its working and is conditioned by it in turn, the way oil and water circulate in and around a turbine that cannot run without them. The Land Cruiser does not cross a landscape so much as bring such a world into being: the heat, the dust, the dragged air, the corrugation it answers to. Gravel is not its obstacle but its element — which is why the dragon-slayer with no dragons is not at rest but bereft, deprived of the very milieu in which it becomes fully itself. Here the commitment quietly rejoins Commitment IX: the vehicle, like the traveller, is made by what it moves through.

The driving itself — soft right foot, never forcing a line, comfortable retreating — is an ethic of skill that Félix Ravaisson captured in Of Habit (1838). Ravaisson's beautiful idea is that habit, properly formed, is not the death of attention but its transformation into grace: the practised skill sinks below deliberate effort and becomes a kind of second nature, a way the body knows the world directly. The driver who has driven well for long enough no longer calculates the line; they feel it. Good overlanding is the slow cultivation of exactly this grace.

The commitment's restraint — back off, go around, turn back, rarely need the recovery gear — has a political philosopher behind it too. Ivan Illich, in Tools for Conviviality (1973), distinguished tools that enlarge human capacity from those that dominate and de-skill their users, and argued for keeping technology within the bounds where it serves rather than enslaves. A vehicle driven gently, within its limits and ours, is Illich's convivial tool; a vehicle flogged across terrain in a contest of conquest is the opposite. The dragon-slayer image makes the point with a smile: the machine and the wild need each other, and the respect runs both ways — we respect the dragon, and the dragon respects us.

XIV Stay Long Enough to Be Changed
Weil's roots, Sartre's self-making, and Rosa on the change we cannot command

The final commitment states the wager of the whole manifesto: movement is not transformation, and it is entirely possible to drive ten thousand kilometres and arrive home unchanged. Transformation needs duration, attention and a willingness to be unsettled — and the commitment's account of how change actually arrives is its most philosophically careful passage.

The self that is capable of being changed is, first, a self that is made rather than found. Jean-Paul Sartre's Existentialism Is a Humanism (1946) gives the manifesto its blunt formula: existence precedes essence, nobody is born who they are, and we make ourselves through what we do. Nobody is born an overlander; one becomes one, decision by decision, road by road. Against any notion that travel merely reveals a self that was waiting there all along, Sartre insists that the self is the cumulative work of its own choosing.

Sartre is not the last word here, and the manifesto knows it. Where Sartre makes the self the product of sovereign choice, Gilbert Simondon would say this describes only the surface — the psychic individual taking up decisions — and misses the deeper process by which a self comes to exist at all. For Simondon the self is individuated rather than chosen: it resolves continuously out of its relations, never finished, which is precisely why a journey can still change it. The two thinkers pull in different directions — willed self-making against relational becoming — and the commitment holds both: nobody is born an overlander; we make ourselves road by road is Sartre, while we are made by what we move through is Simondon. The manifesto does not reconcile them. It travels in the tension between them.

If Simondon loosens the grip of pure choice, Simone Weil loosens it further, and from another side. In The Need for Roots (1949), written for the rebuilding of a shattered France, Weil names uprootedness as the great sickness of the modern age and rootedness as the most important and least recognised need of the human soul. The commitment performs a delicate resolution of its own central tension here: overlanding looks like voluntary uprootedness, yet it claims to deepen the self. The reconciliation is that rootedness is a quality of attention, not of duration — one can be profoundly rooted in a place one passes through, provided one attends to it fully and does not uproot those who live there. This single line carries more moral weight than any other in the manifesto.

Finally, the account of how change is noticed — not in the summit revelation but in the uneventful middle, seen only afterwards, the way we notice one morning that we have aged — belongs to Hartmut Rosa and points toward the coda. Rosa's late work on the uncontrollability of the world (2018) argues that the experiences that genuinely transform us are precisely the ones we cannot manufacture, schedule or command; they can only be made available to, and recognised in retrospect. We do not change at the lookout point. We discover, somewhere down the road, that we already have. The wish to visit Africa twice is the manifesto's last word on this: a place that refuses to let us stay the same is worth returning to, not to repeat it, but to find out who we have become since.

∴ Coda · The Silent Transformations of the Road
François Jullien, and the changes that make no noise

One philosopher stands slightly apart from the commitments, because his thought describes the tempo of the whole enterprise rather than any single principle. François Jullien is a French philosopher and sinologist who spent his career letting Chinese thought interrogate the unspoken assumptions of the European tradition. His most useful idea for this manifesto is silent transformation.

In The Silent Transformations (2009) he names what our event-hungry culture is least equipped to see: that the most important changes do not happen as events at all. A person ages, a climate shifts, a love grows or fades, a desert advances — none of these announces itself; each proceeds continuously, globally, silently, and is noticed only after it has already happened, when some belated event finally makes it visible. Western thinking, organised around dramatic moments and decisive actions, keeps missing the slow and the continuous. Chinese thought, attuned to process and the gradual maturing of situations, sees it.

This idea quietly threads through the manifesto. It is why slowness matters (Commitment VII): only a slow, returning traveller registers the silent transformations of a landscape. It is why transformation cannot be hunted (Commitment XIV): the change is silent and continuous, and the revelatory moment merely announces what the uneventful middle already accomplished. And it is the deepest meaning of the whole journey: we do not change at a stroke, at a summit, in a flash. We are transformed silently, by the road, over time — and like everyone who has ever truly travelled, we will only realise it later.

This, finally, is why the manifesto holds its tensions rather than resolving them. A silent transformation cannot be forced to a conclusion; it can only be lived through and, in time, recognised. The road does not hand out answers. It hands out tensions — and, if we travel slowly and attentively enough, it hands out, much later and almost without our noticing, a changed self that has been quietly becoming itself the whole way along.
