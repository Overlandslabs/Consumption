01 · Scope & Method

Chartered by the owner, 21 July 2026: “are they up to date, are they current with all relevant info.”

This is a different audit class from its two predecessors. The June 2026 audit (F1–F17) and the July 2026 post-install audit (F18–F37) were coverage audits — does a document exist for this topic, and is it structurally sound? Both passed the library. Neither would have caught what triggered this one.

The defect class this audit exists to find. On 21 July the owner flagged R42 as thin. It existed, it validated clean, its masthead was current — and it carried no fire blanket, no extinguisher sizing, and an instruction to operate a battery isolator that is not fitted to the vehicle. A document can be present, structurally perfect and confidently wrong. Coverage testing cannot see that. This audit tests the contents.

02 · Verdict

The library is in good condition. Its weakness is not what it says — it is the seams between documents, and the lag between an event and every document that event touches.

Thirty-seven findings were raised. One was critical, five high, the balance medium and low. Thirty-one were remediated within the audit sessions; four remain open and are routed; two were logged as acceptable scope. No finding indicated a document that should not exist, and no finding contradicted the library's doctrine — the doctrine is sound. The failures were of propagation: a correction landing in one document and not its neighbours, a fitted component never reaching the document that would send you looking for it, an upgrade completed and never entered in the upgrade tracker.

Three structural patterns account for nearly every finding, and are more useful than the findings themselves:

Pattern 1 — the owning document is the last to be updated

R42 was expanded with corrected fire doctrine on 21 July. R10 §3, which R42 explicitly delegates the in-the-moment drill to, still carried the superseded version — including “do not use water on an electrical fire” against a 210 Ah LiFePO4 pack, where water in volume is the correct response. The new document was right and the document a person would actually open in an emergency was wrong. Same shape: R14 never learned about the TPMS fitted in May 2025; R31 never learned about the July 2026 electrical install.

Pattern 2 — documents promise content they do not carry

R1's own subtitle advertises “torque values at a glance”; the document contained no torque figure at all. R23's plan depended on a post-trip review protocol that had never been written. R2 and R23 both stated task counts that did not match their own contents. These are not cosmetic: a reader who trusts the subtitle stops looking.

Pattern 3 — authoritative values marooned in one file

The LiFePO4 commissioning charge profile existed only in Facts.txt — not in R7, the document that owns the house system and the one a workshop would be handed. Values that exist in exactly one place are values that will be lost, contradicted, or reinvented.

04 · Critical & High Findings in Detail

F38 · CRITICAL · R10 §3 — the wrong drill in the right place

R42 §2 was rewritten on 21 July with corrected doctrine: the M-series battery switch is the one-second isolation action, no crank isolator is fitted to this vehicle, and on a venting LiFePO4 cell water in volume is correct because dry powder addresses surface flame only while a self-oxidising cell continues to re-flash. R42 explicitly delegates the in-the-moment drill to R10 §3. R10 §3 had not been touched since 11 July and instructed: “disconnect battery if safe to do so” and “do not use water on electrical fire.”

Under stress a crew opens the emergency document, not the equipment document. The library's newest and most carefully reasoned fire doctrine sat in the file nobody would reach for first. Remediated: both the engine-fire and electrical-fire procedures now carry the M-series step and the no-isolator fact; a LiFePO4 danger box states the water doctrine, the re-flash expectation and the total-loss perimeter response; a §3 ownership note directs prevention, sizing and equipment questions to R42.

F39–F41 · HIGH · R18 — three absences in a 91 KB troubleshooting guide

No occurrence of “timing belt” or “cambelt” anywhere in the file, on an engine where belt failure bends valves. The field-recognition signature — instantaneous stop at speed, then cranking noticeably faster and freer than normal — and the instruction that follows from it (stop cranking, because every further revolution adds damage) were absent. No EGT content, despite a fitted pyrometer being the earliest warning instrument the vehicle has. And a charging-fault tree that predated the July electrical install, so a crew troubleshooting “house not charging” would be sent to the alternator rather than the Orion.

Remediated: a timing-belt card now leads the no-start section; a §3B EGT group carries R21's thresholds and the EGT×boost cross-diagnosis; a charging note separates the crank and house paths and routes house faults to the Orion, with fuse ratings and layout deferred to R26.

F42 · HIGH · the ledger figures that half-propagated

EX-12 closed on 10 July with the ledger at 315 records and R810,838.32. The index card was corrected on 21 July. Facts §2 and the Library Register — the two authoritative sources — still read 314 and R810,872 as at June. The wrong numbers lived in the files that other work would be checked against.

F51 · HIGH · a protocol the plan depended on and nobody had written

The Karoo Winter Loop plan describes the return as the “first live execution of the R23 post-trip review protocol.” No such protocol existed: it is chartered inside EX-8, which has not been executed. The dependency could not have been met, and the failure would only have surfaced on the day. Remediated by owner charter (OI-35): R23 §7 was built — seven review tasks, a sign-off table, and propagation rules that route findings into R3, R37, EX-8 and the Open Items Register. It matters that this exists before departure, because it determines what gets logged during the trip.

F65 · MEDIUM · a fabricated specification, disclosed

The Victron charging note added to R18 in Session A stated that the Orion is protected by a “30A fuse on the alternator cable.” R26, which owns the wiring, gives no Orion fuse rating anywhere — it says only “check Orion input and output fuses.” The figure was invented to complete a sentence. It was plausible, it was specific, it read as authority, and it was not sourced.

It survived four sessions before the Session-D self-audit caught it. Remediated: generic wording, ratings deferred to R26, and validation now asserts the string is absent. Standing control adopted: any specific numeric specification introduced into a library document must be traceable to Facts, the owning R-document, or a manual — and self-audit of prior-session edits is now a step in every audit session.

F71 · HIGH · six profiles without a parts section

§06 Parts & Serviceability — dealer networks, independent specialists, parts availability and lead times — exists in the four profiles built in July 2026 (Mozambique, Malawi, Zimbabwe, Kenya) and in none of the six built earlier: Angola, Namibia, Botswana, Zambia, Kalahari and Tanzania. That is the entire opening leg of the expedition and the whole first dry-season run north. The retrofit is already chartered inside EX-8; this audit confirms it is outstanding and raises its priority, since a breakdown in Namibia in month two is served by nothing.
