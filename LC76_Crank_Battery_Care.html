Sections 1. System 2. Battery Types 3. Voltmeter 4. Check Schedule 5. Heat & Vibration 6. Charging 7. Home / Short Trips 8. Trouble Signs 9. Replace & Emergency

The crank (starting) battery has one job: deliver a high-current burst to turn the 1HZ over. On this vehicle it is electrically separate from the auxiliary/house battery, isolated by the Victron Cyrix. The 1HZ runs 22.4:1 compression, so cold-cranking current peaks high — a tired crank battery shows up as a slow, laboured start long before it leaves you stranded. This document covers how to look after it, what to check and when, how to read it with the multimeter you carry, and how to tell when it is in trouble.

⚠ Read this first — current system status This guide describes the current lead-acid dual-battery system as fitted: a crank battery plus a 105 Ah lead-acid auxiliary, joined by a Victron Cyrix 120 A voltage-sensitive relay and watched by a BM2000 dual-battery monitor. The all-Victron LiFePO4 upgrade is imminent but not yet installed. When it is, the auxiliary side changes — the crank battery and everything in this document about it does not. A note in §6 flags what shifts at that point.
S1 The System & the Crank Battery's Job Architecture · Role

The electrical system is a two-battery setup with the batteries kept apart. The crank battery sits under the bonnet and does nothing but start the engine and run the vehicle's own systems. The auxiliary battery runs camp loads — fridge, lights, charging. The Cyrix relay links them only when one side is being charged (engine running or mains/solar in), so a flat fridge can never leave you unable to start.

Why the 1HZ is hard on a crank battery High compression and indirect injection mean a big starting draw (roughly 600–700 A peak on a cold start). Add under-bonnet heat from the SAC turbo, long stationary spells between trips, and corrugation vibration — and the crank battery lives one of the harder lives in the vehicle. Expect roughly 3–5 years from a quality flooded battery, 5–7 from a good AGM — heat and deep discharge cut both figures sharply.
S2 Best Battery Types for the 1HZ Selection · CCA · Form factor

The 76 takes a tall N70ZZ-class case (JIS 80D26R / D31R family), positive terminal on the right. Target roughly 650–720 CCA and a reserve capacity strong enough to also feed the T-Max winch without bottoming out. Because camp loads sit on the auxiliary, the crank battery is doing near-pure starting duty — which keeps the choice simple. Three tiers are worth knowing, cheapest first.

Buying discipline Check the manufacture date code on the case — buy stock under 6 months old. Keep the receipt: there is a R150–R300 recycling deposit refund on the old unit at any major SA supplier.

S3 Using the Voltmeter Multimeter · BM2000 · Readings

You carry a multimeter, and the BM2000 gives an at-a-glance reading on both batteries. The multimeter is the more accurate tool and the one to trust for diagnosis. Voltage is a proxy for state of charge only when the battery has rested — measured straight after driving or charging it reads falsely high (surface charge). Let it sit at least 2 hours, ideally overnight, for a true resting reading.

How to Take Each Reading
  1. Resting / SoC: meter to DC volts (20 V range). Engine off, rested. Red probe to +, black to −. Read and compare to the table above.
  2. Alternator check: probes still on the terminals, start the engine, hold ~1500 rpm with everything off. Reading should climb to 13.8–14.4 V.
  3. Cranking (load) test: probes on terminals, have someone crank the engine while you watch the dip. A healthy battery holds above ~9.6 V through the crank.
  4. Parasitic draw: everything off, key out, doors closed, bonnet light disabled. Meter to DC amps (10 A jack), disconnect the negative lead, put the meter in series between post and lead. Wait 30 min for modules to sleep, then read. Target < 50 mA; over 100 mA means chase the drain (Multi Media Unit, alarm, dashcam, tracker).
  5. BM2000 cross-check: note both battery voltages on the monitor for a quick day-to-day view — but verify anything suspicious with the multimeter.
Amps mode safety Never touch the meter probes across the terminals (start the engine) while the meter is set to amps — it will blow the meter's internal fuse instantly. Amps mode is only ever used in series, engine off.
S4 Check Schedule Daily · Weekly · Monthly · Pre-trip

On the trail, the 1HZ is your first instrument: any slowing of the cranking speed is the earliest warning and is worth investigating the same day. The checks below scale from a glance to a proper service.

Daily / each start
Listen to the crank
Engine should spin briskly and fire promptly. Any sluggishness, hesitation, or slow churn → investigate before the next start.
Daily / fuel stop
Quick visual
Terminals clean, dry and tight; case not swollen or cracked; hold-down secure; no white/green corrosion bloom; no smell.
Rotten-egg (sulphur) smell = overcharge or internal fault — stop and diagnose.
Weekly
Resting voltage
Multimeter or BM2000 first thing in the morning before starting. Want ≥ 12.5 V. Below 12.4 V → recharge / drive longer that day.
See §3 voltage table.
Monthly
Terminals & hold-down
Clean terminals with baking-soda & water paste, rinse, dry, apply dielectric/petroleum grease. Re-torque hold-down clamp (≈5–8 Nm). Wipe the case top.
A dirty case top creates a slow surface-leak discharge path.
Monthly (flooded only)
Electrolyte level
If a non-sealed flooded battery: check level, top up plates with distilled water only. Sealed (SMF) and AGM batteries — skip, never open.
Pre-trip
Full health check
Resting voltage, cranking-voltage test, alternator output, and an annual parasitic-draw test (§3). If the battery is over 4 years old, replace before departure regardless of test result.

S5 Heat & Vibration — the Two Killers Mitigation

Heat

Life halves per +10°C

Post-turbo under-bonnet temps at the battery can reach 70–80°C. Battery life roughly halves for every 10°C above 25°C case temperature — this is the single biggest factor you control.

Fit a reflective heat shield between battery and engine block (Thermo-Tec or similar, ~R150–R300). Park nose out of direct sun in summer.

Vibration

Torque, don't hand-tighten

Corrugation shakes plates loose and cracks internal welds. The hold-down must be properly torqued (≈5–8 Nm), not finger-tight.

Fit rubber matting under the battery if not factory-fitted. Re-check the clamp after every long corrugated section (≥100 km).

The cheapest reliability upgrade you can make A heat shield costs less than a tank of diesel and can add a year or more to crank-battery life on a turbo 1HZ. If only one thing from this section gets done, make it the heat shield.
S6 Charging Discipline Drive · Mains · Solar · Maintainer

A crank battery wants to live near full charge. It is topped up by the alternator while driving, and by the DEFA SmartCharge at home. The Cyrix relay shares charge to the auxiliary only once the crank side is up to voltage, so the crank battery always gets priority.

Never sit below 12.4 V Sulphation — hard crystals on the plates that permanently reduce capacity — begins as soon as a lead-acid battery sits part-discharged. Recharge promptly. A single deep discharge below 10.5 V can permanently damage a lead-acid battery even if voltage later recovers.
When the LiFePO4 upgrade is installed The auxiliary side changes — Cyrix and the lead-acid 105 Ah give way to the Victron stack (Orion-Tr Smart DC-DC, SmartSolar MPPT, Smart BMS, BMV-712). The crank battery's role and every check in this guide stay the same. Two practical changes: (1) the DC-DC charger draws from the crank/alternator side only while the engine runs — it does not discharge the crank at rest; (2) the BMV-712 monitors the lithium house bank, not the crank, so keep a simple voltmeter (or the retained BM2000 reading) on the crank for at-a-glance health. AGM-mode charging limits (max ~14.7 V) still apply to an AGM crank battery regardless of the house chemistry.
S7 Care at Home & on Short Trips Between expeditions

The vehicle doubles as a home run-around, so the crank battery spends much of its life on short hops and standing spells — which is, paradoxically, harder on it than a long expedition. Short trips never fully recharge; long stands let it self-discharge and sulphate.

Short school-run / town use

Frequent short drives leave the battery chronically part-charged. Roughly once a fortnight, either take one longer run (30+ min at road speed) or put it on the DEFA SmartCharge overnight to bring it back to a true 100%. Check resting voltage monthly — if it is drifting down despite regular use, the alternator may not be keeping up on short trips, or a parasitic drain is at work.

Standing > 2 weeks (between trips)
  1. Best: leave the DEFA SmartCharge connected on float. It holds the battery at 100% indefinitely without overcharging.
  2. Alternative: disconnect the negative terminal to stop all parasitic drain. You lose radio presets and the MMU clock, but the battery holds charge far longer.
  3. Either way: a lead-acid battery left at part charge in the heat is being slowly ruined. Self-discharge runs ~5%/month for flooded, ~3%/month for AGM at 25°C — and much faster when hot.
  4. On return: check resting voltage before the first start. Below 12.2 V means damage has started; recharge fully and watch it over the next few weeks.
✓ Simple home rule If it's parked more than a fortnight, it's on the charger or the negative is off. That one habit is the difference between a battery that lasts its full life and one that quietly dies in the driveway.

Catch a failing crank battery before it strands you. Any one of these warrants a check the same day.

Quick decision tree
Slow crank, but resting voltage is good (≥12.5 V)?
Suspect connections (clean/torque terminals) or a starter issue — not the battery's charge. Re-test cranking voltage.
Resting voltage low, recovers fully after a charge?
Charge being lost — check for parasitic drain and confirm alternator output. Battery may be fine.
Resting voltage low, won't hold charge after charging?
Battery losing capacity — load-test it. If it fails with a healthy charging system, replace.
Cranking voltage drops below ~9.5 V?
Weak battery under load — replace soon, even if resting voltage looks acceptable.
Under 2 years old but failing, charging system healthy?
Heat fatigue or a warranty fault. Push the warranty claim and fit a heat shield on the replacement.
Get numbers, not opinions Before replacing on a "battery's done" verdict, have it load-tested and the charging system checked — a battery condemns only at <70% of rated CCA with a healthy alternator. Premature failure with a good charging system points to heat or a warranty fault, not a battery you simply need to keep buying.

If the crank battery is flat in the field
  1. ARB lithium jump pack — primary self-rescue. Follow the pack's polarity and connection sequence; let it start the engine, then drive long enough to recharge (30+ min at road speed).
  2. HD jumper cables from another vehicle if no pack — standard sequence, dead battery negative to a chassis earth point, not the battery's own negative post.
  3. Do NOT try to start off the auxiliary battery by bridging the Cyrix. The relay and wiring are not rated for cranking current, and once the LiFePO4 is fitted, the BMS will not permit reverse flow to the crank at all. The jump pack and cables are the only sanctioned methods.
  4. Once running, find the root cause (§8) before the next stop — a flat battery in camp is a warning, not a one-off.
Carry the means to self-rescue The ARB jump pack only helps if it is itself charged. Top it up before every trip and check it monthly at home — it is your single-point fallback for a dead crank battery in a remote place.
