! Critical Field Notices — Malawi Read Before Entering
▲Five things that shape — or wreck — this leg
Fuel crisis is the defining problem. In 2026 Malawi has among the world's most expensive diesel (~MWK 6,700/L) and chronic shortages — dry pumps, queues for hours to days. Do not rely on Malawi for diesel. Enter with a full tank + both cells full and refuel at the borders (Moz/Zambia/Tanzania). Forex crisis. Severe USD scarcity; the kwacha is stronger at the official rate than the street. Carry clean USD; expect cash-only realities and ATM caps. Lake Malawi = bilharzia. The lakeshore is the whole trip's highlight and a schistosomiasis hotspot. Treat ALL lake freshwater contact as a risk; distrust "it's safe here" claims. Test on return. COMESA covers you. Unlike Mozambique, the R39 COMESA Yellow Card is valid third-party cover in Malawi — but a road-access tax (historically ~US$51/30 days) still applies on entry. Never drive after dark; mind the radar. Livestock, pedestrians, unlit vehicles, rainy-season washouts. Police run radar speed traps nationwide — pay any fine on the spot and get a receipt.
Scope of this profile Profile 008 covers the whole of Malawi with Lake Malawi as the organising spine — South, Central and North. It completes the EX-4 §18 handoff by covering both Mozambique entries evenly: the Tete-corridor crossings into the south/centre (Mwanza/Zóbuè, Dedza) and the northern lakeshore entry (Chiponde/Mandimba). Onward exits — north to Tanzania and west to Zambia — are carried as forward cross-references (§18) to the existing Tanzania (006) and Zambia (004) profiles and the route workshop (EX-9).
01 Entry & Visas SA visa-free 90 days · reciprocity regime

For a South African passport holder, Malawi is visa-free for tourism — historically up to 90 days under the SADC exemption. Because South Africa admits Malawians without a visa, SA nationals remain exempt under Malawi's new reciprocity-based regime (in force 3 January 2026), which ties exemptions to how each country treats Malawians. Regional-bloc membership alone is no longer a guarantee, so the standing advice is: verify your status close to travel.

2026 change — verify before travelling On 3 Jan 2026 Malawi revoked the 2024 blanket waiver and moved to reciprocity, with an eVisa system (evisa.gov.mw) as the default route for non-exempt nationalities (most non-SADC travellers now need a visa or visa-on-arrival). SADC nationals who remain exempt are not required to use the eVisa for short stays, but exemptions "may change without notice." Confirm at evisa.gov.mw or the Malawi High Commission before each entry — and re-check if the trip is split around a licence-break re-entry.

03 Vehicle Documents COMESA valid · road tax · TIP · triangles

The good news after Mozambique: Malawi is a COMESA member, so the Yellow Card is valid third-party cover — no need to buy local insurance at the border if you're carrying it (obtain it in RSA pre-departure or in a COMESA country; see R39 §2). A separate road-access / toll tax still applies on entry, and the vehicle needs a TIP.

04 Best Season & Timing Dry May–Oct · wet/floods Nov–Apr

The cool dry season (May–October) is the driving season: firm roads, clear lake water, big skies on the plateaus, and the lowest mosquito load. The wet season (November–April) is hot and humid, with heavy rains that flood the lakeshore plain, wash out secondary roads and trigger landslides on the escarpments; cyclones spilling in from the Mozambique Channel reach southern Malawi.

Timing against the expedition A dry-season traverse dovetails with the whole route's seasonal pattern (verify rains in EX-9) and keeps the escarpment roads (Nyika, Livingstonia, Zomba) drivable. It also means firmer lakeshore tracks and a lower malaria load. Given the fuel situation, a shorter, well-planned traverse beats a long lingering one.
05 Fuel Crisis: dear & scarce · don't rely on Malawi

This is the section that governs the whole leg. In 2026 Malawi has been at or near the top of Africa's fuel-price table — a MERA adjustment on 1 April 2026 put diesel around MWK 6,687/L (~USD 3.9 at the official rate) — combined with a chronic forex-driven supply crisis: most filling stations dry, queues of hours to days, and government selling gold reserves to pay suppliers. Plan the traverse as if Malawi has no reliable diesel.

Quality: Malawi's imported diesel is broadly low-sulphur and fine for the 1HZ, but during shortages you may be pushed onto old, low-turnover or informally-sourced stock — the classic contamination (water/dirt) risk. Run the water-separating pre-filter, drain it, and treat suspect fills with caution to protect the freshly-overhauled injection system (Jun 2026).

Do not gamble the tank Treat "half a tank" as empty in Malawi. If you pass a station with diesel and reasonable queue, take it. Never commit to a remote leg (Nyika, northern lakeshore) without the range to exit the country on what you're carrying.

06 Parts & Serviceability 1HZ · tyres · Victron · self-reliance

The expedition-readiness view. Malawi has a solid official Toyota presence and deep 70-Series familiarity, but it is landlocked, second on the route, and import-starved by the forex crisis — so stock of anything specialist is thin and slow, and the South-African support base is now a genuine haul away (back through Mozambique or Zambia, not a quick border hop). Net posture: carry more, rely less. This feeds the R3 consumables ledger (EX-8).

1HZ / Toyota parts & workshopsCFAO Mobility Malawi

Toyota is CFAO Mobility Malawi (formerly Toyota Malawi Ltd) — market leader, the official Toyota/Hino distributor, with full Sales-Parts-Service (3S) branches in Blantyre and Lilongwe, a Parts-Service (2S) branch in Mzuzu, parts outlets at Dwangwa and Nchalo, and a mobile service van in some districts (central line +265 891 002 444). Toyota is the most common marque in the country and the 70-Series is everywhere, so general mechanical help and common consumables are realistic in the main centres. Genuine 1HZ injection parts (VE-pump internals, injectors, nozzles) are not to be expected — carry them (R3).

07 Roads M1 spine · lakeshore M5 · escarpments

The M1 is the tarred national spine running the length of the country (Blantyre–Lilongwe–Mzuzu–Karonga). The M5 lakeshore road hugs the lake through the central and northern shore. Both carry pothole stretches and heavy trucks. The character-defining drives are the escarpment climbs — Zomba Plateau, the Livingstonia hairpins, and the road up to Nyika — which are steep, narrow and treacherous when wet.

Driving discipline Radar speed traps (§03) are a revenue point through towns — keep to limits. Heavy pedestrian, cyclist and livestock traffic on the road edge at all hours; bicycle-taxis wobble into the lane. No night driving. On the escarpments, use low range on descents and yield to trucks.

08 Security Peaceful · petty crime · protest volatility

Malawi is one of the more peaceful, welcoming countries in the region — the "warm heart of Africa" is more than a slogan, and violent crime against travellers is comparatively rare. The realistic risks are petty theft, opportunistic and occasionally armed residential/vehicle crime in the cities, and the volatility of protests — the last sharpened by economic hardship and the fuel/forex crisis. Government advisories sit at a US Level 2 (increased caution).

09 Health Malaria · LAKE BILHARZIA · thin facilities

Two things dominate. Malawi is a year-round malaria country — prophylaxis and bite avoidance are essential. And Lake Malawi is one of the world's best-documented bilharzia (schistosomiasis) hotspots — the very water that is the trip's highlight. Medical facilities are weak outside the main cities, and evacuation means South Africa, which from Malawi is a long haul — R29 medevac cover is not optional.

Lake Malawi & bilharzia — the honest position Schistosomiasis is present throughout the lake; Malawi is historically the single most common source of traveller schistosomiasis, and health authorities (CDC/WHO) advise treating all lake freshwater contact — swimming, wading, watersports, even washing — as a risk, and to distrust local or lodge claims that a spot is "bilharzia-free." There's no preventive drug or vaccine. If you do enter the water, know you're accepting the risk: praziquantel treatment is given ~6–8 weeks post-exposure, and testing on return home is sensible even without symptoms.

10 Camp Safety & Wildlife Lake camps · hippo/croc · park game

Most camping is in established lakeshore and park camps rather than open wilderness. The wildlife hazards concentrate in the water and the parks: hippos and crocodiles along the Shire River and quieter lake shores, and reintroduced elephant, buffalo, lion and rhino in the African Parks reserves (Liwonde, Majete, Nkhotakota).

Lakeshore camps

11 Communications TNM/Airtel · sat for gaps

12 Money & Currency Kwacha · forex crisis · carry USD

The currency is the Malawian kwacha (MWK / K). The forex crisis is central: hard currency is scarce and highly valued, the official rate is stronger than the street, and the kwacha's real purchasing power is uncertain. Cards work in city hotels and larger supermarkets, but Malawi is otherwise a cash economy; ATMs exist in the towns but can cap withdrawals and run dry. Carry clean USD — some fees (road tax, COMESA) are USD-denominated, and USD is your reliable fallback.

13 National Parks & Camping African Parks reserves · lake · plateaus

Malawi punches above its size: three reserves are run by African Parks to a high standard (Majete, Liwonde, Nkhotakota), the lake has a UNESCO-listed national park, and the north holds two wild montane parks. Fees are modest and generally paid at the gate; confirm current rates with the managing body (DNPW or African Parks) rather than trusting a figure that dates quickly.

Big Five · African Parks

Majete Wildlife Reserve

  • Southern, Lower Shire; the flagship rewilding success — Big Five.
  • Well-run gate, roads and camps; good self-drive + community lodge.
  • Malaria area; low, hot. Dry-season game viewing best.
River · African Parks

Liwonde National Park

  • Along the Shire River — elephant, hippo, croc, rhino, reintroduced lion/cheetah.
  • Boat safaris; excellent birding. Easy add-on to the south/central route.
  • Gateway near Liwonde town off the M1.
Lake · UNESCO

Lake Malawi National Park

  • At Cape Maclear (Chembe), Nankumba Peninsula — world's first freshwater-fish-diversity World Heritage site.
  • Snorkelling among cichlids — but bilharzia applies (§09). Beach camps.
  • Access via Monkey Bay off the M5.
Montane · DNPW

Nyika & the North

  • Nyika NP — high rolling montane grassland, roan/eland/leopard, cool climate; the standout northern drive.
  • Vwaza Marsh (elephant, hippo) and Nkhotakota WR (African Parks). Kasungu NP in the centre.
  • Remote — self-sufficient on fuel/supplies (§05). Escarpment access.
Fees & booking African Parks reserves (Majete/Liwonde/Nkhotakota) and DNPW parks charge entry + vehicle fees at the gate; verify current rates and any pre-booking (some camps book out). Lake Malawi NP fees at Cape Maclear. Confirm close to travel.

17 South & Central Route Guide Entry → Blantyre/Lilongwe → Liwonde → Cape Maclear

In from the Tete corridor

Mwanza or Dedza · 5–8 days · fuel in hand

Enter on a full tank, sort documents and the road tax, then work the southern highlights and drop to the lake at Cape Maclear — all while guarding the fuel margin.

  • Cross at Mwanza/Zóbuè (→ Blantyre) or Dedza/Calomué (→ Lilongwe) — COMESA card, TIP, road tax, receipts.
  • Blantyre / Lilongwe: SIM, kwacha + USD discipline, provisions; try to fuel but assume scarcity.
  • Liwonde NP: Shire-river safari; optional Majete (Big Five) to the south.
  • Zomba Plateau for a cool night; Mulanje with registered guides if wanted.
  • Drop to Cape Maclear (Lake Malawi NP) via Monkey Bay — beach camp, snorkel with the bilharzia caveat (§09).
