South African passport holders enjoy visa-free entry under the SADC free-movement arrangement. Immigration will stamp the passport for up to 90 days; confirm the endorsed period at the counter and check it matches your intended transit. Carry printed proof of onward travel (exit to Zambia via Chirundu) and at least one night's camp booking. Yellow fever vaccination certificate required only if arriving from a WHO-designated risk country — Malawi is not a yellow-fever country, so this should not apply on this leg. However, the onward Kenya leg makes carrying the ICVP essential regardless (see R39 §4).

KAZA UniVisa — USD 50 for multiple entries between Zimbabwe and Zambia within 30 days. Available at Victoria Falls and Kazungula borders only. Not relevant for the Nyamapanda→Chirundu routing, but useful if the licence-break or routing shifts to include Vic Falls (EX-9).
§02 Border Crossings Entry & exit posts on this leg
Entry

Nyamapanda (Moz → Zim)

  • Hours: 06:00–18:00 (some sources say 06:00–20:00 — confirm at the Moz side)
  • Moz side (Cuchamano): Exit stamp, hand in Moz TIP, exit form
  • Zim side: Immigration stamp + ZIMRA TIP (electronic — clerk processes). Pay carbon tax, road access fee, third-party insurance in USD
  • Warning: This border is known for officials requesting a Police Clearance Certificate for the vehicle. Carry one from the nearest SA police station (free) to avoid confrontations
  • Distance from Tete: ~140 km on EN103/EN1
  • Heavy truck traffic (Moz–Zim–Malawi trade corridor) — plan for queues
Exit

Chirundu (Zim → Zambia)

  • Hours: 06:00–22:00 (one of the busiest SA–East Africa corridors)
  • Zim side: Immigration exit stamp + hand in TIP
  • Zambia side: Immigration entry + KAZA/visa if applicable. See Profile 004
  • Key note: Chirundu is a One-Stop Border Post (OSBP) — both countries process at a single facility on the Zambian side
  • ~370 km from Mana Pools Nyamepi; ~180 km from Kariba town

§03 Vehicle Documents ZIMRA TIP, Carnet, insurance

ZIMRA issues a free Temporary Import Permit (TIP) at the border for foreign-registered vehicles. The TIP itself carries no charge, but carbon tax, a road access fee, and compulsory third-party insurance are payable on entry — all in USD cash. The TIP is valid for the endorsed period (typically matching the immigration stamp) with a maximum of 90 days. Extensions are available at any ZIMRA office, subject to additional carbon tax and insurance.

Required Documents
Checklist
Valid passports (both crew) · SA vehicle registration book · Police-certified owner affidavit (reg. M-A West) authorising Neil to drive · SA police clearance for the vehicle (strongly recommended for Nyamapanda) · Valid driver's licence (SA + IDP)
Carnet de Passages
Accepted
Zimbabwe accepts the Carnet, but border TIP is also issued alongside. If carrying a Carnet (per R39 §1), present it — it simplifies breakdown/accident reporting and Carnet stamps are needed for the onward route.
COMESA Yellow Card
Valid
Zimbabwe is a COMESA member. The Yellow Card provides third-party cover. If not accepted at the border, local insurance is ~USD 30/month.
Breakdown or Accident If the vehicle breaks down or is involved in an accident, report to the nearest ZIMRA office immediately, in addition to the police. Leaving Zimbabwe without the vehicle, or without notifying ZIMRA, is a serious offence — the TIP must be discharged.

§04 Best Season & Timing Dry season, rainy season, wildlife windows
Optimal Window
Jul – Oct
Dry season. Best wildlife viewing (animals concentrate at water). Mana Pools open Jul–Oct (sometimes Jun). Coolest night-time temperatures. Clear roads.
Avoid
Nov – Mar
Rainy season. Park roads impassable. Mana Pools access roads flood (Ruchomeshi river bridge washout 2025/26). Extreme heat (40°C+ in the valley). High malaria risk.
Shoulder
Apr – Jun
Transition. Roads drying out. Vegetation still lush. Mana Pools may open late May/Jun depending on flood recovery. Confirm with ZimParks.

The Zambezi valley's altitude (~350–500 m) means temperatures are significantly higher than the highveld. August to October daytime temperatures regularly exceed 35°C in the valley, rising above 40°C in October before the rains break. Night temperatures in Jul–Aug can drop to single digits. Hwange, at higher elevation (~1,000 m), is more moderate. The best compromise for the combined Hwange + Mana Pools routing is August to September — dry, wildlife concentrated, and both parks fully accessible.

Zimbabwe diesel is among Africa's most expensive — a 39% price surge in March 2026 pushed costs sharply higher. ZERA regulates prices fortnightly; check the current ZERA notice before travel. Most fuel stations only accept USD cash despite official dual-currency policy. ZiG payments may be available at some stations but expect poor rates or refusal. Fuel is generally available on trunk routes (Nyamapanda road, Makuti, Kariba, Hwange/Vic Falls corridor) but remote areas and park access roads have no fuel. Enter Zimbabwe with a full tank; top up at every opportunity.

Fuel Strategy Enter full from Mozambique. Tank at the first reliable station inside Zimbabwe. The 145 L main tank + 2 × 20 L Desert Fox cells give ~185 L total capacity — at ~15 L/100 km that is a ~1,200 km theoretical range, sufficient for any leg within the scope area. But do not rely on theoretical range — top up at every town with fuel.

§06 Parts & Serviceability 1HZ · tyres · Victron · fuel quality
Toyota / 1HZ Network Strong presence

Zimbabwe has the strongest Toyota network on the expedition route north of South Africa. Toyota by CFAO Zimbabwe operates official dealerships in Harare and Bulawayo with genuine parts departments. Croco Toyota (Harare/Mutare) is a major authorised dealer. Harris Auto provides Toyota-trained technicians in the northern suburbs of Harare. Multiple independent parts dealers operate in Harare's Southerton industrial area and in Kwekwe, Masvingo, and Gweru. The 70-Series (particularly the Land Cruiser pickup and troop carrier variants) has a large installed base in Zimbabwe — farmers, safari operators, and NGOs run them hard — so aftermarket knowledge and bush-mechanic familiarity are good.

1HZ-specific parts: genuine injector nozzles, filters, and gaskets are orderable through CFAO/Croco. However, stock is not guaranteed — Zimbabwe's import squeeze means lead times can be weeks. The Bosch VE pump is serviced by diesel specialists in Harare's industrial area. Carry the full R3 spares set. For emergency parts sourcing, Harare is the fallback — ~250 km from Nyamapanda, ~350 km from Makuti (Mana Pools turnoff), ~350 km from Hwange.

Tyres Buy in RSA

The current 265/70R16 Maxxis AT811 (and planned 265/75R16 upgrade) should be purchased in South Africa before entry. Zimbabwe's tyre market is dominated by smaller sizes for sedans and light trucks. Larger 4x4 sizes are available in Harare but at a premium and with limited brand choice. Road hazards (potholes, corrugation, sharp stones on park roads) make tyre damage a real risk — carry the full-size spare and the repair kit.

Victron Support Present — Madison Solar

Victron Energy is officially represented in Zimbabwe by Madison Solar Engineering (Pvt) Ltd — an authorised distributor with trained installation staff. Zimbabwe's load-shedding crisis has driven strong solar/inverter adoption, so Victron products are well-known in the market. For the LC76's system (MultiPlus 12/500/20, SmartSolar MPPT 100/30, SmartShunt 500A, Orion Smart 12/12-50A), replacement units may be available from Madison or from the broader solar trade in Harare. However, RSA remains the primary support base. VictronConnect Bluetooth diagnostics and VRM remote monitoring work on Zimbabwe's mobile networks.

Fuel Quality 50 ppm — good

ZERA-regulated diesel is 50 ppm sulphur — the same standard as South Africa and fully compatible with the 1HZ fuel system. Quality at major-brand stations on trunk routes is reliable. Avoid buying fuel from roadside vendors or unmarked containers — adulteration is possible in rural areas.

§06 Verdict — Zimbabwe is the most serviceable country on the expedition route after South Africa. Strong Toyota network, 50 ppm fuel, a functioning Victron distributor, and deep 70-Series familiarity in the bush-mechanic community. Carry the full R3 set as always, but the RSA one-border fallback is less critical here than in Malawi or Mozambique. For emergencies, Harare is the regional hub.

§07 Roads Conditions, hazards, routing

Zimbabwe's trunk roads are tar but narrow — often single-lane each way with no hard shoulder. Heavy truck traffic (especially the Nyamapanda–Harare–Chirundu corridor, which carries Mozambique–Zambia freight) makes overtaking dangerous. Potholes are widespread and can be severe, particularly on the A1 south of Makuti and the Chinhoyi–Karoi stretch. Speed is not your friend — 60–80 km/h is realistic on many stretches. Do not drive after dark.

Mana Pools Access — 2025/26 Flood Damage The Ruchomeshi River bridge on the main Mana Pools access road was damaged in the 2025/26 floods. An alternative route exists via Mashumbi Pools (entering Mana from the west) but is longer and requires local knowledge. Confirm bridge/road status with ZimParks or a local operator before travel. Do not assume the direct route from Chirundu along the Zambezi to Nyamepi is passable — it frequently floods.

§08 Security US Level 2 · crime · roadblocks · official conduct
US Travel Advisory
Level 2
Exercise Increased Caution — crime and official harassment. Opportunistic crime in cities; home invasion, carjacking, smash-and-grab. Political rallies restricted and may escalate.
Tourist Areas
Generally Safe
Victoria Falls, Hwange, Mana Pools, Kariba — well-established tourism infrastructure. Professional guides. Sensible precautions apply.

Zimbabwe's security environment is manageable with standard precautions. The northern wilderness areas (Mana Pools, Kariba, Hwange) are well-policed by ZimParks rangers and present low crime risk. The main concerns are:

Roadblocks: Police roadblocks are common on all trunk roads. Have documents ready (passports, licence, registration, TIP, insurance). Be polite and compliant. Do not photograph police or military installations. Speed traps with radar are common — locals report strict enforcement of the 60/80/120 km/h limits.

Cash visibility: Avoid openly displaying large sums of USD. The cash economy means you will carry significant USD — apply the R35 concealment split (money distributed across multiple locations in the vehicle and on both crew members).

"Spot fines": Some officers may solicit unofficial payments. Per R35, the harm-reduction approach applies — safety over principle. Request a receipt and a supervisor if comfortable doing so; pay a small amount and move on if the situation escalates.

§09 Health Malaria · bilharzia · medical facilities
Malaria Risk
HIGH
Year-round in the Zambezi valley (below 1,200 m). P. falciparum >98% of infections. Prophylaxis mandatory. DEET 30–50% from dusk to dawn. Sleep under nets.
Mana Pools / Kariba
Very High
Zambezi valley is one of Zimbabwe's highest-risk malaria areas. Lake surface attracts mosquitoes at dusk. Walking safaris increase exposure.
Hwange
Seasonal
Higher elevation (~1,000 m). Risk Sep–May (wet season). Lower risk Jun–Aug (dry, cooler). Still carry prophylaxis.

Bilharzia (schistosomiasis): Present in many freshwater bodies. The Zambezi itself, Lake Kariba, and slow-moving rivers carry risk. Avoid wading or swimming in fresh water unless confirmed safe. Unlike the Lake Malawi profile (Profile 008), the Zimbabwe leg is less lake-focused, but the risk persists wherever standing water is encountered.

Medical facilities: Private clinics in Harare provide adequate malaria diagnosis and treatment. Victoria Falls has private medical facilities. In remote areas (Mana Pools, Kariba basin, Hwange interior), healthcare is limited. Carry rapid diagnostic tests (RDTs, USD 2–5 locally) and ACT treatment (Artemether-Lumefantrine) as per R17. Medevac coverage per R29 is essential.

Any Fever = Malaria Test Any fever during or after visiting the Zambezi valley — even months later — should prompt an urgent malaria test. Inform medical staff of Zimbabwe travel history. P. falciparum symptoms can appear 7 days to 12 months after exposure.

§10 Camp Safety & Wildlife Unfenced camps · big game · Zambezi hazards

Mana Pools is unique in Africa — visitors are permitted to walk unaccompanied in the park and to approach wildlife on foot at their own risk. This demands the highest level of R36 discipline. Camps at Nyamepi and Chitake Springs are unfenced. Elephant, buffalo, hippo, lion, leopard, hyena, and crocodile are all present. The R36 doctrine applies without exception: avoid not manage; tent is a shape not a wall; assume an apex predator within 200 m; water is the danger zone.

Hippo
Extreme
Zambezi riverbanks and pools. Most dangerous large animal in Africa. Never between hippo and water. Night river approaches lethal.
Elephant
Very High
Mana Pools elephants habituated but not tame. Bull elephants in musth. Albida trees attract large herds to camp areas. Give space.
Crocodile
Zambezi
Large Nile crocs throughout the Zambezi and in pools. Do not approach the water's edge. Canoeing guided by experienced operators only.

Hwange: Camps at Main Camp, Sinamatella, and Robins are partially fenced but wildlife wanders through. Elephant density is among the highest in Africa. Lion and wild dog are present. Kennedy Pan and other pans attract large concentrations during the dry season.

§11 Communications Mobile networks, satellite, connectivity

Mobile networks: Econet Wireless is the dominant network with the widest coverage. NetOne and Telecel also operate. Coverage on trunk roads and in towns is generally good (3G/4G). Coverage drops sharply in national parks and the Zambezi valley floor — do not rely on mobile comms at Mana Pools or in remote Hwange areas.

SIM cards: Econet SIMs are available at border posts, airports, and shops in major towns. EcoCash (Econet's mobile money platform) is widely used locally but transacts in ZiG — for overlanders, USD cash is simpler. Data bundles are affordable.

Satellite: The R38 check-in protocol applies. In areas without mobile coverage (most of the time in this scope area), satellite comms per R16 are the primary means. The 3-day check-in cadence with the home anchor per R38 §1 governs.

Zimbabwe is effectively a USD cash economy for overlanders. The ZiG (Zimbabwe Gold) exists alongside the USD in a dual-currency system, but most fuel stations, park fees, and service providers accept USD only. ZAR is accepted in border towns (Beitbridge, Mutare) at poor rates — not relevant for the Nyamapanda–Chirundu routing. Estimate USD cash needs for the Zimbabwe leg conservatively: park fees, fuel, food, camp fees, border fees — and carry the full amount in clean notes. The R39 §6 forex strategy and R35 concealment split apply.

§13 National Parks & Camping ZimParks booking, fees, campsites

Zimbabwe's national parks are managed by the Zimbabwe Parks and Wildlife Management Authority (ZimParks). All park entry requires payment of conservation fees — payable in USD cash at the gate. Camping in parks must be pre-booked through ZimParks or through accredited agents. Popular sites (Mana Pools Nyamepi, Hwange Main Camp) fill early in peak season (Jul–Oct) — book well in advance.

§14 The Zambezi Corridor — Highlights Mana Pools · Kariba · Chirundu · the escarpment

Mana Pools National Park

UNESCO World Heritage Site · Zambezi floodplain · walking safaris

Mana Pools is part of a larger boundary-less area stretching from the Kariba Dam east to the Mozambique border. "Mana" means "four" in Shona, referring to the four large ox-bow pools inland from the Zambezi — remnant meanders from when the river shifted northward. The landscape is stunning and varied: open woodland, riverine forest, floodplains, and the dramatic Rift Valley escarpment rising on the Zambian side. Mana is famous for its photogenic elephants (standing on hind legs to reach albida tree pods), large wild dog packs, and exceptional predator viewing.

  • Self-drive 4x4 access during the dry season (Jul–Oct typically). Confirm status of the access road and Ruchomeshi bridge with ZimParks
  • Walking safaris permitted without a guide — unique in Africa. Apply R36 discipline rigorously
  • Canoeing on the Zambezi via guided operators — hippo and crocodile territory
  • Nyamepi campsite — riverside, unfenced, elephant and hippo wander through at night
  • Chitake Springs — remote camp accessed via challenging 4x4 track. Predator concentration around the spring in the dry season

Lake Kariba & Matusadona

Africa's largest man-made lake · houseboats · tiger fishing

Lake Kariba (5,580 km²) was created by the Kariba Dam on the Zambezi in 1959. The lake provides a different tempo — houseboat safaris, tiger fishing, and sunsets over the drowned woodland. Kariba town on the southern shore is a resupply point (fuel, basic supplies) and access point for Matusadona NP. The dam wall itself is an impressive piece of mid-century engineering. Kariba Ferries operate crossings (useful for accessing Matusadona or linking to Zambia via the northern shore).

Chirundu & the Escarpment

Border town · one-stop border post · Zambezi crossing

Chirundu sits on the Zambezi at the Zambian border. The Chirundu OSBP (One-Stop Border Post) is one of the most efficient crossings in the region — both countries process at a single facility on the Zambian side. The road from Makuti descends the Zambezi escarpment via dramatic switchbacks with views across the valley. This is the exit point for this profile, linking to Profile 004 (Zambia).

§15 Hwange National Park — Highlights Zimbabwe's largest park · elephant country · the western extension

Hwange National Park covers over 14,600 km² of semi-arid Kalahari sandveld in western Zimbabwe — a blend of teak woodland, grassland, and open savanna. It supports one of Africa's largest elephant populations (estimated 40,000+) and over 100 mammal species including lion, leopard, wild dog, sable, and roan antelope. The park is divided into three main areas: Main Camp (south-east, most accessible), Sinamatella (north-west, quieter), and Robins (far north-west, remote).

Access
Main Camp Gate
Off the Bulawayo–Vic Falls road. Well-signposted. Gate hours ~06:00–18:00. The road from Makuti/Kariba via Karoi–Hwange is ~500 km on tar (poor condition).
Road Warning
Corrugation
Main Camp → Robins internal road is severely corrugated. "Everything will rattle and shake, crack, break or fall off." Reduce speed to 40–50 km/h. Deflate tyres per R14.

Kennedy Pan Loop: One of the best game drives in southern Africa. Large herds of elephant, buffalo, and predators concentrate at the pan in the dry season. Sable and roan antelope are Hwange specialities. Artificial pumped waterholes sustain wildlife through the dry season — a legacy of the original park management, now maintained by conservation organisations.

Routing note: Hwange sits in the far west of the scope area, ~500 km from the Zambezi corridor. Including Hwange adds significant distance but accesses Zimbabwe's flagship park. The Makuti → Karoi → Hwange road is tar but poor. Alternatively, if the licence-break routing (EX-9) includes Victoria Falls, Hwange is a natural add-on from the Vic Falls side.

§17 Route Guide — Nyamapanda to Chirundu Indicative routing through the scope area
Leg 1

Nyamapanda → Makuti

  • Distance: ~520 km via A2/A1
  • Surface: Tar, narrow, potholed, heavy trucks
  • Time: Full day — start early, aim to reach Makuti by afternoon
  • Fuel at Nyamapanda (if available) or first reliable station en route
  • Transit the highveld — altitude rises to ~1,200 m then descends
  • Chinhoyi Caves: optional overnight if needed (campsite available)
Leg 2A

Makuti → Mana Pools

  • Distance: ~80 km from escarpment turnoff
  • Surface: Gravel → 4x4 track, river crossings possible
  • Time: 2–4 hours depending on conditions
  • Confirm access road and bridge status with ZimParks before committing
  • Alternative western entry via Mashumbi Pools if direct route closed
  • No fuel in the park — enter with full tanks
Leg 2B

Makuti → Kariba

  • Distance: ~70 km
  • Surface: Good tar descending the escarpment
  • Time: ~1 hour
  • Fuel and resupply in Kariba town
  • Base for Matusadona NP (boat access) and lake activities
  • Kariba Dam wall — worth a visit
Leg 3

Makuti/Kariba → Chirundu

  • Distance: ~180 km from Kariba; ~120 km from Makuti
  • Surface: Tar, escarpment descent, dramatic views
  • Time: 2–3 hours
  • Chirundu OSBP — exit Zimbabwe, enter Zambia
  • Onward to Lusaka (~130 km on good tar) per Profile 004
Extension

Makuti → Hwange

  • Distance: ~500 km via Karoi–Hwange road
  • Surface: Tar, poor condition, corrugated sections
  • Time: Full day
  • Significant western detour — adds 2–3 days minimum
  • Consider routing via Vic Falls if EX-9 licence-break includes it
  • Fuel at Karoi, Hwange town
