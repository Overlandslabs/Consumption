The auxiliary (house) battery runs the camp side of the vehicle — fridge, lights, charging, the inverter. Unlike the crank battery, which delivers one short burst and is immediately recharged, the house battery is deep-cycled: drawn down over hours, then built back up. That working pattern, not age alone, is what wears it out. This interim document covers how to keep the current sealed lead-acid unit healthy until the LiFePO4 house battery is installed — at which point R26 (Electrical & Wiring) supersedes it.

⚠ Read this first — status & scope This is an interim document for the lead-acid house battery currently fitted. The planned 200 Ah LiFePO4 house battery is not yet installed. Lithium is managed in almost the opposite way to lead-acid — so once the upgrade is done, this document is retired and R26 takes over. Do not apply lead-acid rules to the lithium system or vice-versa.
S1 The System & the Aux Battery's Job Architecture · Role

The electrical system is a two-battery setup with the batteries kept apart by the Victron Cyrix. The crank battery starts the engine and runs the vehicle's own systems. The auxiliary battery carries every camp load — it is the one that gets discharged and recharged repeatedly, so it needs the most attention. The Deltec was fitted at the same time as the Willard crank battery (23 Jan 2025 @ 271,056 km), so both share a service-age baseline.

Why the house battery lives a harder life than the crank A crank battery is shallow-cycled — a few percent, then instantly refilled. The house battery is dragged down 40–60% every night on a fridge and slowly refilled the next day. Deep-cycle lead-acid tolerates this far better than a starting battery would, but it is still the abuse pattern that ends its life. How you manage state of charge decides whether it lasts 2 years or 6.
S2 The Golden Rule — State of Charge 50% floor · Recharge to 100%

Lead-acid dies of sulfation — hard lead-sulfate crystals that form whenever the battery sits below full charge, and which permanently reduce capacity. The governing principle is the opposite of lithium: lead-acid hates sitting partially charged. Two habits protect it.

Don't go below 50%

≈ 12.2 V rested

Treat half-charge as the floor for routine use, not 20%. Habitually pulling a lead-acid below 50% roughly halves its cycle life. The usable bank is therefore about 50 Ah of the 105 Ah, not the full nameplate.

Get it back to 100%

≈ 12.7–12.8 V rested

Return it to a genuine full charge as often as possible. A lead-acid left at 70–90% for weeks sulfates and loses capacity for good. This is the single most common way overland house batteries are killed.

The slow killer: chronic part-charge Fridge-only camping that never sees a full recharge — short drives, cloudy days, no mains — leaves the battery hovering at 80–90% for weeks. It still works, so nothing looks wrong, but capacity bleeds away quietly. The fix is to make sure it periodically reaches a completed full charge (see S4–S5), not just "topped up".
S3 Reading State of Charge Rested voltage · BM2000 caveat

The BM2000 shows live voltage on both batteries — but live voltage under load reads low (sag), and under charge reads high. For a true state-of-charge reading, take it rested: no charging and no significant load for about 2 hours, at roughly 25 °C.

Rested voltage → state of charge (12 V lead-acid, ~25 °C)
Rested voltageState of chargeAction
12.7–12.8 V100% — fullTarget after every charge
12.5 V~75%Healthy working range
12.2 V~50% — FLOORRecharge now; don't go lower in routine use
12.0 V~25%Damage zone — recharge fully ASAP

Don't trust voltage under load Run the fridge compressor or inverter and the displayed voltage drops half a volt or more — that is sag, not state of charge. Read the battery rested. Equally, a battery on charge can read 14 V and still be only 80% full: voltage is the destination, current acceptance is the proof it has arrived (see S4).

Temperature compensation These figures are for ~25 °C. When hot (Kalahari, engine bay), the charge voltage should come down — roughly 20 mV per °C above 25 °C for a 12 V battery — or the battery gasses and loses water it can't replace. Quality chargers do this automatically; verify the DEFA and the solar controller are temperature-compensated.
S5 Does the DEFA Also Charge the Aux? Cyrix behaviour · Field test

Short answer: almost certainly yes — but probably not to a true 100%, and it depends on which battery the DEFA is connected to. Here is the mechanism, then a two-minute test that settles it for your specific install.

The Cyrix is a voltage-sensitive relay (VSR). It watches voltage and combines the two batteries when it sees charging voltage (closes at roughly 13.7 V), and isolates them again when voltage falls (opens around 12.8 V) so camp loads can never flatten the starter. It does this regardless of where the charge comes from — alternator, solar, or the DEFA on mains.

If the DEFA is connected to the CRANK battery
DEFA charges the crank; once it holds charging voltage, the Cyrix combines and the aux charges through the relay. But the aux sits below the DEFA's regulation point by the voltage drop across the relay and cabling — so it may never complete absorption and can chronically rest at ~90–95%.
If the DEFA is connected to the AUX battery
DEFA charges the aux fully to its proper profile; once combine voltage is reached, the crank also tops up. This is the better arrangement for house-battery health.
Which one is it on this vehicle?
To be confirmed — this is a wiring fact, not a spec. Run the field test below, then record the result (S11).
Field test — does one charger charge both?~5 min · BM2000
  1. Vehicle off, batteries rested ≥2 h. Note both voltages on the BM2000.
  2. Plug in the DEFA on mains. Wait 2–5 minutes.
  3. Both readings climb together (toward 13.5–14.5 V) → the Cyrix is combining; one charger is servicing both batteries.
  4. Only one climbs, the other stays put → they are isolated for that session and the aux needs its own charge.
  5. Record the outcome and the DEFA connection point in this document and in Facts.txt §8.
Practical recommendation Whatever the test shows, make sure the aux periodically reaches a completed full charge, not just a top-up. Best options, in order: (1) if the DEFA is on the crank, occasionally connect it directly to the aux for a full multi-stage charge every few weeks; or (2) let the Hardkorr 200 W solar, connected to the aux, finish the absorption on a sunny day. Do not assume a crank-side DEFA fully services the house battery — it usually doesn't.
S6 Sealed-Battery Specifics What you must NOT do

The Deltec 1250 is sealed. That simplifies maintenance — there is nothing to top up — but it also removes your margin for error on overcharging, because you cannot replace lost electrolyte.

No water top-ups

Sealed means no removable cell caps and no distilled-water service. Don't try to open it. This is one fewer field chore than a flooded battery.

No equalisation

Never run an equalisation / desulfation overcharge (>15 V) on a sealed unit. It boils off water that can't be refilled and permanently dries the battery. Disable any "recondition" mode on chargers.

Keep the charge voltage capped Sealed batteries gas and lose moisture if held too high. Stay within the S4 profile (absorption ≤ ~14.7 V, float ~13.6–13.8 V), and make sure temperature compensation is active in hot conditions. Heat + overcharge is the fastest way to kill a sealed lead-acid.
S7 Heat & Vibration The overland killers

Two environmental factors do more damage than cycling itself on an African overland trip.

Heat

Lead-acid life roughly halves for every 10 °C above 25 °C. Keep the battery ventilated and out of direct engine-bay and exhaust heat. Check its mounting location stays as cool and airflow-fed as practical.

Vibration

Corrugations shake active material off the plates and loosen terminals. Confirm the clamp/box is genuinely tight at every pre-trip check, and that cable lugs are torqued — a loose lug arcs, heats and fails.

S8 Inverter Load Discipline Sinetech 1200 W

The Sinetech 1200 W inverter is the heaviest single load on the house battery. At full output it pulls roughly 100–115 A from a 12 V battery (1200 W ÷ ~12.4 V ÷ efficiency). On a 105 Ah battery that is more than a 1C draw — brutal. Voltage sags hard, and sustained high loads age the battery quickly.

Use it for bursts, not sustained loads Kettle, laptop charger, short tool runs — fine. Running a high-wattage appliance for long stretches off this battery is not. Watch the BM2000: if voltage dives toward 12.0 V under inverter load, stop and recharge before going lower. Prefer running heavy 230 V loads while the engine is running so the alternator carries some of the draw.
